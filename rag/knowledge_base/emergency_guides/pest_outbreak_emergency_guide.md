# Pest Outbreak Emergency Guide

## Overview
This guide addresses response to significant, fast-moving pest outbreaks (as distinct from routine pest pressure managed through standard integrated pest management). It focuses on migratory and rapidly reproducing pests capable of causing severe, widespread damage in a short time.

## Recognizing an Emerging Outbreak vs. Routine Pest Pressure
- Routine pest pressure: localized, gradual buildup, generally manageable through standard scouting and threshold-based IPM described in individual pest documents in `pests/`.
- Emerging outbreak: rapid appearance of large pest numbers, damage spreading across a wide area within days, or reports of similar outbreaks in neighbouring farms/districts.

## Fall Armyworm Outbreak Response
1. Scout affected and neighbouring fields immediately to assess the extent of infestation (see `pests/fall_armyworm.md` for identification).
2. Prioritize treatment of young, vulnerable plants (seedling to knee-high stage), where damage potential is highest.
3. Apply appropriate control matched to infestation severity, favouring biological/biopesticide options for early instar larvae where infestation is still moderate.
4. Report significant outbreaks to local agricultural extension services, since fall armyworm outbreaks are actively monitored regionally and reporting supports coordinated response and early warning for neighbouring farms.

## Locust Outbreak Response
- Desert locust and other locust species can form large, mobile swarms capable of destroying entire fields within hours.
- **Do not attempt large-scale independent chemical control of a locust swarm**, since effective control requires coordinated regional monitoring and often specialized aerial or ground application managed by national or regional plant protection authorities.
- Immediate steps for individual farmers:
  1. Report sightings of locust swarms or hopper bands immediately to local agricultural extension offices or national plant protection/locust control authorities, since early reporting supports regional early warning and coordinated control efforts.
  2. Where feasible and safe, noise-making and physical deterrents can help move a settling swarm away from a specific field, though this does not address the broader outbreak.
  3. Harvest any crops at or near maturity as quickly as possible if a swarm is reported approaching the area, to salvage what can be saved.

## Rodent Outbreak Response
- Sudden rodent population booms can occur following certain climatic conditions (e.g., abundant food availability after good rains) and can cause severe crop and stored grain losses.
1. Assess the extent of field and storage damage.
2. Implement rodent-proof storage measures immediately (see `storage/grain_storage.md`) to protect harvested grain.
3. Use appropriate, safe rodent control measures in fields and storage areas, avoiding methods that pose risks to children, livestock, or non-target wildlife.
4. Report significant rodent outbreaks to agricultural extension services, since coordinated community-level rodent control is often more effective than individual farm efforts alone.

## Disease Epidemic Response (Rapidly Spreading Plant Disease)
1. Isolate and remove severely affected plants where feasible to slow spread, particularly early in an outbreak.
2. Avoid moving plant material, tools, or equipment between affected and unaffected fields without cleaning, to reduce mechanical spread.
3. Report suspected rapidly spreading disease outbreaks to agricultural extension services promptly, since some diseases (e.g., certain viral diseases, bacterial wilts) can spread across a wider area quickly and benefit from coordinated regional monitoring and response.

## General Principles for Outbreak Situations
- Coordinated, community-level, and regional response is often more effective than individual farm action alone for migratory pests (locusts, armyworm moths) and fast-spreading diseases.
- Prompt reporting to agricultural extension services and relevant plant protection authorities supports both the individual farm's response and broader regional early warning systems that protect neighbouring farms.
- Avoid indiscriminate, large-scale chemical application without guidance during an outbreak, since this can be ineffective against migratory pests, wastes resources, and may cause unnecessary environmental and health risk.

## When to Consult an Agricultural Extension Officer or Plant Protection Authority
- Immediately upon suspecting or confirming a significant pest outbreak (locust swarms, fall armyworm outbreak, rodent population boom) or rapidly spreading disease.
- Before undertaking any large-scale chemical control response to an outbreak, to confirm the most effective and safe approach given the specific pest/disease and local conditions.
- To access information on any regional early warning systems or reporting hotlines available for migratory pest outbreaks in the local area.
