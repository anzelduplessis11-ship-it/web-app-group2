# Crop Emergency Guide

## Overview
This guide provides immediate response steps for common crop emergencies. It is intended to help farmers take appropriate first actions while arranging further professional assessment. It does not replace on-site diagnosis by an agricultural extension officer or plant health specialist for severe or unclear cases.

## Sudden Widespread Wilting

### Immediate Steps
1. Check soil moisture at root depth: if dry, the cause may be drought stress; if waterlogged, the cause may be root asphyxiation/root rot.
2. Inspect the stem base and roots of a few affected plants for discolouration, softness, or foul odour, which may indicate a soil-borne disease (e.g., bacterial wilt, root rot).
3. Check for insect activity at the stem base (e.g., stem borer entry holes, cutworm damage).
4. If wilting is confined to a patch of the field rather than uniform across it, inspect for localized drainage issues, soil-borne disease spread, or nematode damage.

### When to Escalate
- Widespread wilting affecting more than 10-15% of the field within a short period (days) warrants prompt consultation with an agricultural extension officer, since this can indicate a fast-spreading disease requiring specific diagnosis and containment measures.

## Sudden Leaf Yellowing Across a Field

### Immediate Steps
1. Determine the pattern: uniform yellowing (often nutrient-related, particularly nitrogen) versus patchy/mosaic yellowing (often disease or pest-related, e.g., viral disease or pest feeding damage).
2. Check for recent waterlogging, drought stress, or recent herbicide application nearby, all of which can cause yellowing.
3. Inspect the underside of leaves and growing points for pests (aphids, whiteflies, mites).

### When to Escalate
- If yellowing is accompanied by clear mosaic/mottled patterning, distorted growth, or is spreading rapidly, consult an agricultural extension officer promptly, since this may indicate a viral disease requiring specific management (see relevant documents in `diseases/`).

## Suspected Pest Outbreak (Rapid Crop Damage)

### Immediate Steps
1. Identify the pest if possible using identification guides in `pests/` (size, colour, damage pattern, time of day most active).
2. Assess the extent of damage across the field (percentage of plants affected) to gauge severity.
3. Begin appropriate immediate control measures matched to the identified pest and severity (see specific pest documents), prioritizing biological and cultural methods where infestation is still limited.
4. If chemical control is warranted based on severity, confirm currently approved and locally registered products with an extension officer or regulatory authority before application.

### When to Escalate
- Rapid, severe damage affecting a large area, unfamiliar pest species, or damage that does not respond to standard management should be reported to an agricultural extension officer promptly, particularly since some outbreaks (e.g., fall armyworm, desert locust) require coordinated regional monitoring and response.

## Suspected Disease Outbreak (Rapid Crop Loss)

### Immediate Steps
1. Isolate and, where feasible, remove severely affected plants to slow spread, particularly for diseases known to spread by contact or short-distance splash (e.g., late blight).
2. Avoid working in affected areas when foliage is wet, to reduce the risk of spreading disease to unaffected areas via tools, hands, or clothing.
3. Document symptoms (photograph if possible) and the pattern of spread across the field for extension officer consultation.

### When to Escalate
- Any suspected outbreak causing rapid plant death or spreading quickly across a field should be reported to an agricultural extension officer as soon as possible, given the potential for severe yield loss if intervention is delayed.

## Severe Weather Damage (Flood, Hail, Wind, Frost)

### Immediate Steps
1. Assess the extent of physical damage (broken stems, defoliation, flooding depth and duration) across the field.
2. For flooded fields, assess drainage options to remove standing water as quickly as possible, since prolonged waterlogging causes root damage and disease risk.
3. For frost-damaged crops, avoid immediate harvest or handling of visibly damaged tissue until full extent of damage is clear, typically 24-48 hours after the event.
4. Document damage for any applicable insurance, government disaster relief, or cooperative support programs, where available.

### When to Escalate
- Significant crop loss from severe weather should be reported to local agricultural authorities, both to access any available support programs and to contribute to regional damage assessment.

## General Principles for Crop Emergencies
- Act promptly but avoid panic-driven decisions (e.g., indiscriminate pesticide application) without basic diagnosis, since incorrect treatment wastes resources and may worsen the problem.
- Document the problem (photos, notes on pattern and progression) to support accurate diagnosis by an extension officer or plant health specialist.
- Contact an agricultural extension officer promptly for any emergency involving rapid spread, unclear cause, or significant potential crop loss, since early professional input often makes the difference between a contained problem and a severe loss.

## When to Consult an Agricultural Extension Officer or Plant Health Specialist
- For any crop emergency involving rapid spread, unclear cause, or where initial response steps do not halt the problem within a short period.
- Before applying any chemical control in an emergency situation, to confirm the correct product and approach for the specific problem identified.
- To access information on any available disaster relief or crop insurance support following severe weather events.
