# Livestock Disease Outbreak Emergency Guide

## Overview
This guide provides immediate actions for farmers who suspect a contagious or fast-spreading disease outbreak among livestock. Rapid, sudden illness, unexplained deaths, or symptoms consistent with a known serious livestock disease require urgent, careful action, since delayed response can allow disease to spread through a herd, to neighbouring farms, or in some cases to people. This guide supports immediate first response. It does not replace diagnosis and instruction from a veterinary officer or animal health authority, whose involvement is essential for any suspected serious or notifiable disease.

## Recognizing a Possible Contagious Disease Outbreak
- Warning signs include sudden illness or death affecting multiple animals within a short period, symptoms spreading progressively through the herd, unusual severity or rapid progression of illness, and reports of similar symptoms on neighbouring farms.
- Symptoms that should raise particular concern include sudden high fever, severe diarrhoea, blistering or sores around the mouth, feet, or udder, sudden lameness affecting multiple animals, unexplained abortions across several animals, neurological signs (staggering, uncoordinated movement, unusual aggression or apathy), and sudden death with no clear prior illness.
- Distinguish, where possible, an isolated illness in a single animal from a pattern suggesting spread through the group; the latter requires a faster and more cautious response.

## Immediate Actions Upon Suspicion
### Immediate Steps
1. Isolate the affected animal(s) from the rest of the herd or flock immediately, using a separate enclosure with its own water and feed source where possible.
2. Stop all movement of animals onto or off the farm, and avoid moving animals between different groups or paddocks on the farm, until the situation is assessed.
3. Restrict the movement of people, vehicles, and equipment between the affected animals and the rest of the herd, since disease can be spread mechanically on boots, clothing, tools, and vehicle tyres.
4. Record what is observed in detail: which animals are affected, the order and timing of symptom onset, specific symptoms, and any recent changes (new animals introduced, recent markets or gatherings attended, contact with other herds or wildlife).
5. **Contact a veterinary officer or animal health authority immediately**, describing the symptoms and situation as accurately as possible, and follow their specific instructions for the suspected disease.

### What NOT to Do
- **Do not move, sell, or transport any animals from the farm once a contagious disease is suspected**, even if a buyer is available or a market day is approaching, since this risks spreading the disease to other farms, markets, and regions.
- **Do not attempt to treat symptoms suggestive of a serious outbreak with home remedies or unauthorised medication before veterinary input**, since incorrect treatment can mask symptoms, delay accurate diagnosis, and allow the disease to spread further.
- Do not slaughter or sell a sick or dead animal for consumption or use, since this can spread disease to people (in the case of zoonotic diseases) or to other animals through contaminated meat, hides, or by-products.
- Do not allow visitors, buyers, or other farmers' vehicles onto the farm while a contagious disease is suspected or under investigation, without guidance from the veterinary authority.

## Isolation and Biosecurity Measures
1. Set up a dedicated isolation area as far as practical from the main herd, ideally with separate handlers where labour allows.
2. If separate handlers are not possible, always attend to unaffected animals first, then affected animals last, and change or thoroughly clean and disinfect footwear, clothing, and equipment between groups.
3. Provide a footbath or disinfection point at the entrance to isolation areas and, where the situation warrants, at the farm entrance itself.
4. Limit all non-essential visits to the farm until the situation is resolved or guidance is received from a veterinary officer.

## Reporting Requirements for Notifiable Diseases
- Many serious livestock diseases (including, in various regions, foot-and-mouth disease, anthrax, avian influenza, African swine fever, Newcastle disease, and others) are classified as notifiable, meaning that suspected cases must be reported to the relevant veterinary or animal health authority by law in most jurisdictions.
- Report suspected notifiable disease promptly even if uncertain of the exact diagnosis; veterinary authorities are equipped to investigate and confirm, and early reporting is central to containing outbreaks before they spread regionally.
- Follow all instructions from the veterinary authority regarding quarantine, movement restriction, and any required control measures, since these are typically legally binding once an official investigation begins.

## Protecting Human Health (Zoonotic Risk)
- Some livestock diseases can be transmitted to people (zoonotic diseases), including, depending on the specific disease, through direct contact with sick or dead animals, their blood, milk, or other body fluids.
- Use available protective measures (gloves, covering cuts or wounds, avoiding direct contact with blood, birth fluids, or discharge) when handling sick or dead animals, and wash hands and any exposed skin thoroughly afterward.
- **Seek urgent medical attention for any person who develops fever, illness, or unusual symptoms after contact with sick or dead livestock**, and inform the treating clinician of the recent animal exposure.
- Do not consume milk, meat, or other products from sick animals under any circumstances until cleared by a veterinary officer.

## Managing the Rest of the Herd/Flock
1. Monitor all remaining animals closely, multiple times per day if possible, for early signs of the same symptoms.
2. Maintain accurate records of the health status of all animals, since this information is valuable to the investigating veterinary officer.
3. Continue providing adequate feed and water to unaffected animals from separate, uncontaminated sources.
4. Follow any vaccination, treatment, or additional biosecurity instructions provided by the veterinary officer for the remaining herd.

## Carcass and Waste Management
- Do not move, bury, or dispose of any carcass from a suspected disease death without guidance from the veterinary authority, since carcasses may need to be examined, sampled, or disposed of according to specific protocols to prevent further disease spread.
- Where instructed to dispose of a carcass, follow the specific method advised (which may include deep burial, incineration, or other approved methods) exactly as directed.
- Manage manure, bedding, and other waste from affected animals separately from general farm waste until cleared by veterinary guidance.

## Movement Restrictions
- Once a notifiable or suspected serious disease is reported, expect and comply with any quarantine or movement restriction placed on the farm by the veterinary authority, which may restrict movement of animals, animal products, vehicles, or people on and off the property for a defined period.
- Movement restrictions, while disruptive, are central to preventing wider spread and protecting neighbouring farms, markets, and the broader livestock sector.

## Recovery and Restocking After an Outbreak
1. Do not reintroduce or restock animals until the veterinary authority confirms the farm is clear of infection and any required quarantine period has ended.
2. Thoroughly clean and disinfect all housing, equipment, vehicles, and handling areas as instructed before restocking.
3. Review and strengthen ongoing biosecurity practices (controlled farm access, quarantine for newly introduced animals, vaccination programs where relevant) to reduce the risk of future outbreaks.
4. Document the outbreak, response, and losses thoroughly, since this record may be required for compensation schemes, insurance, or future biosecurity planning.

## General Principles for Livestock Disease Outbreaks
- Early isolation and reporting are the most effective tools available to an individual farmer facing a suspected outbreak.
- Never move, sell, or slaughter animals from a farm under disease suspicion, and never attempt to diagnose or treat a suspected serious outbreak without veterinary input.
- Protect human health as a priority alongside animal health, particularly given the risk of zoonotic disease transmission.
- Cooperate fully with veterinary and animal health authorities, since coordinated regional response is essential to controlling serious livestock disease outbreaks.

## When to Consult a Veterinary Officer or Animal Health Authority
- Immediately upon suspecting any contagious or fast-spreading disease, sudden unexplained deaths, or symptoms consistent with a known serious livestock disease.
- Before taking any action to move, treat, slaughter, or dispose of a sick or dead animal.
- For guidance on isolation, biosecurity, and management of the remaining herd during an investigation.
- To confirm when quarantine restrictions can be lifted and it is safe to restock or resume normal operations.
- Urgent medical attention should be sought separately and promptly for any person showing illness after contact with sick or dead animals.
