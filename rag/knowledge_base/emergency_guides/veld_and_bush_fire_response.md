# Veld and Bush Fire Response Guide

## Overview
This guide provides immediate response and recovery steps for farmers facing an approaching, active, or recently extinguished veld, bush, or farm fire. Fires can spread extremely quickly, especially in dry conditions and wind, and pose serious danger to human life, livestock, structures, and land. This guide focuses on immediate safety and practical first response. It does not replace instructions from local fire and disaster management authorities during an active fire, or assessment by an agricultural extension officer during recovery.

## Immediate Actions During an Approaching or Active Fire
### Immediate Steps
1. Prioritize human life above all else. If a fire is approaching quickly or fire behaviour is unpredictable, evacuate people, especially children, elderly, and anyone with mobility or health limitations, well before the fire reaches the area.
2. Contact local fire services and disaster management authorities immediately upon noticing a fire that is spreading or approaching the farm, even if it appears to still be some distance away, since fire can move far faster than expected, particularly in wind.
3. Move livestock away from the fire's path toward already-burned ground, open fields without dry fuel, water bodies, or other safe areas, if this can be done quickly and safely.
4. If safe firebreaks exist (ploughed strips, roads, previously burned areas, rivers), move people, animals, and vehicles behind them.
5. Close off and, where possible, wet down structures (homes, barns, stores) in the fire's likely path if time and water supply allow, but only if this does not delay evacuation.

### Firefighting Safety Considerations
- Only attempt to fight a fire directly if it is small, you have a clear and safe escape route, and appropriate tools or water are on hand; do not attempt to fight a fire that is already large or spreading quickly.
- Be constantly aware of wind direction and changes in wind, since a shift in wind can trap people who are fighting or fleeing a fire.
- Avoid fighting fire alone; work with others and maintain communication.

### What NOT to Do
- **Do not attempt to outrun a fast-moving fire on foot across open, dry vegetation; move to already-burned ground, a firebreak, or another safe area instead**, since fire can travel faster than a person can run, particularly uphill or with wind assistance.
- Do not re-enter a building, vehicle, or field to retrieve possessions once a fire is close, since this significantly increases risk of injury or death.
- Do not attempt large-scale independent firefighting without appropriate equipment, training, and a clear escape route; wait for or coordinate with trained fire services where a fire is beyond a small, containable size.
- Do not release livestock into an area where the fire's path or wind direction is uncertain, since this can drive them directly into danger rather than away from it.

## Livestock Evacuation and Safety During Fire
1. If evacuation is possible, move animals calmly and steadily toward a known safe area; panicked, forced movement can cause animals to scatter unpredictably, including toward the fire.
2. Where evacuation is not possible in the time available, open gates and remove fencing barriers where safe to do so, to give animals the best chance to move away from the fire on their own.
3. Avoid leaving animals confined in small enclosures directly in a fire's likely path.
4. After the immediate danger has passed, account for all animals as soon as it is safe to do so, and assess for burns, smoke inhalation, and injury.

## Immediate Actions Once the Fire Has Passed
### Immediate Steps
1. Do not enter burned areas until local fire or disaster authorities confirm it is safe, since hidden hot spots, weakened trees, unstable ground, and smouldering material can pose ongoing danger for hours or days.
2. Check for and extinguish any remaining hot spots or smouldering material near buildings, fences, and fuel or chemical storage areas once it is safe to approach.
3. Provide immediate first aid for any burns to people or animals, and seek urgent medical or veterinary attention for anyone with significant burns or smoke inhalation.
4. Assess and secure water and feed sources for surviving livestock, since these may be affected by ash, contamination, or damaged infrastructure.

## Assessing Crop and Pasture Damage
1. Assess the extent of burned area and whether any crop or pasture remains viable, distinguishing between areas that were fully burned and those with only partial scorching.
2. For partially scorched crops, monitor over the following days for recovery versus continued decline before deciding whether to replant.
3. Avoid grazing livestock on freshly burned pasture until new growth is well established, since regrowth after fire is often highly palatable but can be limited in quantity and easily damaged by early grazing.
4. Do not harvest or consume any crop that was directly exposed to fire, smoke residue, or firefighting chemicals without confirming it is safe to do so.

## Assessing Livestock and Structure Damage
1. Examine all animals closely for burns, singed hair or wool, hoof damage, eye irritation, and signs of smoke inhalation (coughing, laboured breathing), even where injuries are not immediately obvious.
2. Isolate injured or severely stressed animals for closer monitoring and treatment.
3. Inspect fencing, water infrastructure, feed storage, and farm buildings for fire damage before returning animals to previously fenced areas, since damaged fencing can allow animals to stray into unsafe or still-hazardous areas.
4. Document all damage (photographs, notes on affected area and structures) for insurance, disaster relief, or extension officer consultation.

## Assessing Soil and Land Damage
- Severely burned land, particularly where fire burned hot and slow (heavy fuel loads), can suffer damage to soil structure and loss of organic matter and soil organisms.
- Burned, bare land is at significantly higher risk of erosion, especially before the next rains; where feasible, consider erosion control measures (contour barriers, temporary ground cover) on steeper or more exposed burned areas.
- Avoid heavy machinery or vehicle traffic on burned land where possible until assessed, since fire-damaged soil can be more vulnerable to compaction.

## Fire-Related Health Risks
- Smoke inhalation can cause delayed respiratory symptoms in both people and animals; seek medical or veterinary attention for anyone showing breathing difficulty, persistent coughing, or eye and throat irritation after fire exposure, even if symptoms seem mild initially.
- Ash and fire debris can irritate skin, eyes, and airways; use available protective measures (covering nose and mouth, protective clothing) when working in recently burned areas.

## Recovery Steps for Pasture and Cropland
1. Allow burned pasture adequate recovery time before grazing resumes, following guidance from an extension officer on typical recovery periods for the local vegetation and rainfall pattern.
2. Reseed or replant burned cropland once soil condition is assessed as suitable, prioritizing erosion control on any bare or sloped ground in the interim.
3. Monitor recovering land for weed encroachment, which often increases on burned ground before desired vegetation re-establishes.
4. Rebuild firebreaks and review fire prevention measures (see below) before the next dry season.

## Recovery Steps for Livestock
1. Continue monitoring previously exposed animals for delayed complications from smoke inhalation or burns for at least one to two weeks.
2. Provide supplementary feed and water while pasture recovers, since burned grazing land will not immediately support the herd.
3. Consult a veterinary officer regarding wound care for burn injuries and any additional supportive treatment needed for affected animals.

## Preventing Future Fire Risk
- Maintain firebreaks around fields, grazing land, and structures, particularly before the dry season.
- Avoid burning debris or vegetation during high wind or extremely dry conditions, and never leave any deliberate fire unattended.
- Coordinate with neighbours and local authorities on community-level fire prevention, particularly in areas prone to veld fires, since fires frequently spread across property boundaries.

## General Principles for Fire Emergencies
- Human life takes priority over livestock, structures, and crops in every decision during an active fire.
- Do not attempt to fight or contain a fire beyond your safe capability; contact fire and disaster authorities immediately and follow their instructions.
- Wait for confirmation that an area is safe before re-entering after a fire has passed.
- Document damage thoroughly to support insurance, disaster relief, and recovery planning.

## When to Consult Local Fire Services, Disaster Management, or an Agricultural Extension Officer
- Immediately upon noticing a spreading fire approaching the farm — contact fire and disaster management services first.
- For confirmation that burned areas, buildings, and land are safe to re-enter after a fire.
- For guidance on pasture and cropland recovery timelines, safe reseeding, and erosion control on burned land.
- For veterinary attention for any animal with burns, suspected smoke inhalation, or significant fire-related stress.
- To access disaster relief, insurance support, or coordinated community fire prevention programs where available.
