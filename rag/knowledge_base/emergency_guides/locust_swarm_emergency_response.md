# Locust Swarm Emergency Response Guide

## Overview
This guide provides immediate actions for farmers facing an approaching or active locust swarm, or hopper bands (wingless immature locusts moving on the ground). Locust swarms can travel long distances, form very large groups, and destroy standing crops within hours of arrival. Effective control of a locust outbreak requires coordinated regional monitoring and response by national or regional plant protection authorities; this guide focuses on what an individual farmer can do immediately to protect people, salvage crops, and support the wider response. It does not replace instructions from agricultural extension officers or plant protection/locust control authorities.

## Recognizing an Approaching Swarm or Hopper Band
- Adult swarms may appear as a dense, moving cloud in the sky, sometimes audible before clearly visible, and can settle over an area to feed, sometimes stripping vegetation within hours.
- Hopper bands (young, wingless locusts) move together on the ground and can be observed advancing across land before they develop wings; this stage can be an important window for reporting and limited control before a mobile swarm forms.
- Reports of swarms or hopper bands in neighbouring districts or along known migration paths should be treated as an early warning to prepare, even before a swarm is seen directly.

## Immediate Actions When a Swarm Is Reported Nearby
### Immediate Steps
1. **Report the sighting immediately to the local agricultural extension office or national plant protection/locust control authority**, including location, approximate size and direction of movement if known, and whether it is a hopper band or a flying swarm. Early reporting supports regional early warning systems and coordinated control efforts that benefit the whole area.
2. Assess which crops are at or near maturity and prioritize harvesting these as quickly as possible if a swarm is confirmed approaching, to salvage what can be saved before the swarm arrives.
3. Alert neighbouring farmers of the reported swarm so they can also prepare and report, since community-level awareness improves the effectiveness of the overall response.
4. Prepare noise-making tools (drums, metal sheets, other loud implements) and identify safe locations from which they could be used if the swarm approaches, without needing to enter fields during an active swarm.

### When to Escalate
- Any sighting of a locust swarm or hopper band should be reported promptly, regardless of size, since even small groups can indicate a larger developing outbreak that regional authorities need to track.

## Immediate Actions When a Swarm Arrives at the Farm
### Immediate Steps
1. Move people and livestock away from the immediate area where a swarm is actively feeding or settling, since large swarms can cause considerable disturbance and, in some cases, contain irritant substances on their bodies.
2. Where feasible and safe, use noise-making and physical deterrents (banging metal, loud noises, waving materials) to help discourage a settling swarm from remaining on a specific field, understanding that this addresses only the immediate field and not the broader swarm.
3. Close doors, windows, and openings to homes and stores where practical, since large swarms can enter buildings and contaminate stored food or living spaces.
4. Continue to document the swarm's presence, movement, and the area and crops affected, for reporting to the extension office or plant protection authority.

### What NOT to Do
- **Do not attempt large-scale independent chemical control of a locust swarm.** Effective control of a swarm typically requires coordinated regional monitoring and specialized aerial or ground application managed by national or regional plant protection authorities, and uncoordinated individual chemical use is often ineffective against a mobile swarm while posing unnecessary health and environmental risk.
- Do not enter a field where a dense swarm is actively settling or feeding if this can be avoided, and avoid unnecessary direct contact with large numbers of locusts.
- Do not assume a swarm that has moved on will not return; swarms can move back and forth over an area for a period before finally moving away, so continued vigilance and reporting remain important.
- Do not delay reporting a swarm while attempting individual control measures first; reporting should happen immediately alongside any other immediate action taken.

## Reporting to Authorities
- Provide as much detail as possible when reporting: date and time observed, location, estimated size of the swarm or hopper band, direction of movement, and crops or areas affected.
- Continue to report significant developments (swarm returning, new hopper bands appearing, spread to new areas) as they occur, since ongoing reporting supports the accuracy of regional monitoring.
- Follow any instructions issued by plant protection authorities regarding coordinated control activities, including any requests for farmer cooperation with organized spraying operations or monitoring efforts.

## Protecting Livestock During a Locust Event
1. Move livestock away from areas where control measures (including any officially coordinated chemical treatment) are being or will be applied, following guidance from the applying authority on safe distances and timing.
2. Do not allow livestock to graze recently treated areas until cleared as safe by the applying authority, since chemical control measures may carry a withholding period.
3. Monitor livestock for any unusual reaction if they have been in an area with a large locust presence, though direct harm to livestock from locusts themselves is generally limited; the greater risk to livestock in these situations is usually reduced grazing availability following heavy crop and pasture loss.

## Post-Swarm Field Assessment
1. Once a swarm has moved on, assess the extent of damage across all affected fields, distinguishing between total loss, partial defoliation, and unaffected areas.
2. For crops with partial damage, particularly cereals where growing points may survive, monitor for a period before deciding whether replanting is necessary, since some crops can show some recovery from partial defoliation.
3. Document damage thoroughly (photographs, estimated affected area, crop stage at time of damage) to support any disaster relief applications and regional damage assessment efforts.
4. Check stored produce and structures for locust presence or contamination following a heavy local swarm event, particularly if doors or openings were not fully secured.

## Recovery Steps
1. Where a crop is a total loss and season timing allows, consult an agricultural extension officer on suitable short-cycle replacement crops for the remainder of the season.
2. Assess soil and field condition before replanting; a locust event itself does not typically damage soil, but the loss of ground cover can increase short-term erosion risk on exposed land.
3. Rebuild depleted feed reserves for livestock if pasture was significantly affected, since grazing land can take time to recover after heavy locust feeding.
4. Participate in any continued regional monitoring or reporting requested by plant protection authorities, since locust populations can remain active in a region for an extended period and repeated waves are possible.

## Community and Regional Coordination
- Locust control is most effective as a coordinated, regional effort rather than isolated farm-level action, since swarms move across many farms and often across district or national boundaries.
- Support and participate in any community-level reporting networks, early warning systems, or coordinated control operations organized by agricultural extension services or plant protection authorities.
- Share accurate, prompt information with neighbouring farmers and local authorities, since early collective awareness improves outcomes for the whole area.

## General Principles for Locust Emergencies
- Report every sighting promptly, regardless of size, to support regional early warning and control efforts.
- Prioritize harvesting maturing crops when a swarm is confirmed approaching, to salvage what can be saved.
- Leave large-scale chemical control to coordinated regional plant protection operations rather than attempting independent large-scale control.
- Protect people and livestock from disturbance during an active swarm event, and avoid contact with treated areas until cleared as safe.

## When to Consult an Agricultural Extension Officer or Plant Protection/Locust Control Authority
- Immediately upon sighting a locust swarm or hopper band, regardless of size or apparent distance from the farm.
- Before undertaking any chemical control response, to confirm the appropriate, coordinated approach for the specific situation.
- For guidance on harvesting decisions when a swarm is confirmed approaching, and on replanting decisions following crop damage.
- To confirm safe timing before grazing livestock on land subject to official locust control treatment.
- To access information on regional early warning systems, reporting hotlines, and any disaster relief support available following significant locust damage.
