# Flood Emergency Response Guide

## Overview
This guide provides immediate and follow-up response steps for farmers facing flooding of cropland, grazing land, homesteads, or livestock housing. Flooding may result from heavy rainfall, river overflow, dam release, or poor drainage, and conditions can change quickly. This guide supports immediate decision-making while further help is arranged. It does not replace on-site assessment by an agricultural extension officer, veterinary officer, or disaster management authority, particularly where human safety, significant livestock loss, or large-scale crop damage is involved.

## Immediate Safety Priorities (Before Anything Else)
### Immediate Steps
1. Prioritize human life first. Move family members, workers, and children away from rising or fast-moving water before attending to crops, livestock, or property.
2. Move livestock to higher ground as early as possible, ideally before water levels rise significantly, since animals trapped by rising water are difficult and dangerous to move later.
3. Avoid walking, wading, or driving through moving floodwater at any depth. Even shallow, fast-moving water can sweep away people, vehicles, and animals, and floodwater frequently hides washed-out roads, ditches, and unstable ground.
4. Where it is safe to do so, switch off electrical supply to affected buildings (pump houses, stores, homesteads) to reduce the risk of electrocution.
5. Move stored produce, seed, agrochemicals, fuel, and important documents to the highest available dry point to limit contamination and loss.

### What NOT to Do
- **Do not attempt to rescue livestock or property from fast-moving or deep water without assistance.** Call for help and prioritize human safety over property.
- Do not allow children near floodwater, drainage channels, or dam spillways, even after the initial flood peak appears to have passed, since conditions can change rapidly and currents may be stronger than they appear.
- Do not drink or allow livestock to drink water from flooded areas without treatment, given the risk of contamination from agrochemicals, sewage, and animal waste carried by the water.

## Protecting Livestock During Flooding
### Immediate Steps
1. Move all livestock to the highest, driest ground available with access, if possible, to limited clean drinking water and dry feed.
2. Count and account for all animals as soon as it is safe to do so, and note any missing, trapped, or injured animals.
3. Separate any animals showing signs of injury, distress, chilling, or illness from the main group for closer observation.
4. Provide dry bedding or shelter where possible for young, weak, sick, or heavily pregnant animals, since prolonged cold, wet conditions substantially increase the risk of illness and death, particularly in newborns.
5. Where evacuation routes are limited or animals cannot be moved in time, prioritize moving the most valuable or vulnerable animals first and reassess as conditions develop.

### When to Escalate
- Any significant livestock deaths, injuries, or onset of illness following flood exposure should be reported to a veterinary officer promptly, since flood conditions increase the risk of certain infectious and environmental diseases and early assessment can prevent further losses.

## Floodwater Contamination Risks
- Floodwater often carries agrochemical residues, sewage, decomposing organic matter, and disease-causing organisms picked up as it moves across land, even when it appears clear.
- Do not use floodwater for drinking (by people or animals), irrigating crops close to harvest, or washing food crops, without prior treatment or confirmation that it is safe.
- Where floodwater has covered a borehole, well, or other drinking water source, treat that source as contaminated until it has been tested, or thoroughly flushed and disinfected, and use an alternative safe water source for people and animals in the meantime.
- Avoid direct skin contact with floodwater where possible, and wash thoroughly with clean water and soap after any necessary contact, since floodwater can carry pathogens that cause skin and gastrointestinal illness.

## Protecting Stored Produce and Farm Inputs
1. Move harvested grain, seed stock, fertiliser, and agrochemicals to elevated, dry storage as early warning of flooding allows.
2. If stored produce or inputs become wet or submerged despite precautions, treat them as compromised: wet grain is at high risk of rapid mould growth (see mould and aflatoxin guidance) and should not be mixed with dry, unaffected stock.
3. Do not use flood-affected seed for planting or flood-affected feed for livestock without inspection, since contamination and spoilage may not be visible.

## Assessing Crop Damage After Floodwaters Recede
### Immediate Steps
1. Assess how long the field remained under water. Brief flooding (under roughly 24-48 hours) is generally more survivable for most crops than prolonged submersion, which starves roots of oxygen and can cause rapid plant death.
2. Check for silt, debris, and sediment deposited on plants and soil, and clear heavy debris that is obstructing plant growth where feasible.
3. Assess soil structure once it is safe to access the field. Flooding often causes compaction and surface crusting that restricts future root growth and water infiltration.
4. Do not work waterlogged soil (ploughing, replanting, heavy machinery use) while it remains saturated, since this causes further structural damage and compaction that can affect the field for future seasons.
5. For crops at or near harvest that were submerged, do not harvest for human consumption or animal feed until food safety has been confirmed, given the risk of chemical or microbial contamination carried by floodwater.

### When to Escalate
- Significant crop loss, prolonged waterlogging with no clear drainage option, or uncertainty about the safety of flood-affected produce should be reported to an agricultural extension officer, both for technical guidance and to access any available disaster relief support.

## Assessing Livestock Health After Flooding
### Immediate Steps
1. Monitor animals closely for signs of respiratory illness (coughing, laboured breathing), lameness, foot rot, or skin conditions, all of which can increase after prolonged exposure to wet, muddy conditions.
2. Be alert to disease risks associated with flooding in the local area, which in some regions include exposure to old spore-contaminated ground (anthrax risk) or increased populations of biting insects capable of transmitting disease.
3. Provide clean, dry standing areas as soon as possible to reduce ongoing exposure to mud and contaminated water, which contributes to hoof and skin problems.
4. Inspect pasture before returning livestock to graze. Do not return animals to pasture that was submerged until it has had time to recover and any contamination risk has reasonably passed.

### When to Escalate
- **Any sudden, unexplained livestock deaths following flooding should be reported to a veterinary officer immediately, and the carcass should not be opened, moved, or handled unnecessarily,** since this may indicate anthrax or another notifiable disease requiring official investigation.
- Illness affecting multiple animals, or symptoms consistent with a vector-borne or infectious disease, warrants prompt veterinary consultation rather than individual treatment attempts.

## Drainage and Water Management
1. Where safe and feasible, create or clear drainage channels to remove standing water from fields as quickly as possible, since prolonged waterlogging significantly worsens root damage and disease risk.
2. Avoid diverting floodwater in ways that could worsen flooding on neighbouring land; coordinate with neighbours and local authorities where drainage decisions could affect shared land or infrastructure.
3. Inspect and clear irrigation infrastructure (canals, pumps, pipes, tanks) of silt and debris, and check for structural damage before returning it to use.

## Recovery Steps for Cropland
1. Once soil is workable and no longer waterlogged, assess whether replanting is feasible within the remaining growing season, in consultation with an extension officer regarding suitable short-cycle varieties if replanting is an option.
2. Seek guidance on soil condition before replanting, since flooding can alter nutrient availability, pH, and soil structure in ways that are not visible on inspection alone.
3. Monitor replanted or recovering crops closely for disease over the following weeks, since flood-stressed and previously waterlogged soils often show elevated pressure from root rots and certain bacterial diseases.
4. Document the extent of crop loss with photographs and field notes, including estimated affected area and expected yield impact, to support any disaster relief, insurance, or cooperative assistance applications.

## Recovery Steps for Livestock and Pasture
1. Reintroduce animals to recovered pasture gradually once it has had adequate time to recover and drain, monitoring closely for digestive upset.
2. Continue observing animals for delayed-onset illness for at least two to three weeks following flood exposure, since some conditions, including respiratory illness, hoof problems, and vector-borne disease, may not appear immediately.
3. Restock feed and mineral supplementation as needed, since flood-affected pasture may have reduced nutritional value in the short term.
4. Consult a veterinary officer on whether additional vaccination or preventive treatment is warranted, given the specific flood-related disease risks known in the local area.

## General Principles for Flood Emergencies
- Human safety comes first, followed by livestock, then crops and property.
- Treat all floodwater, and anything it has touched, as potentially contaminated until proven otherwise.
- Avoid rushing to replant, restock water sources, or resume normal operations before land and water have been properly assessed, since premature action often leads to repeated or compounded losses.
- Document damage thoroughly and promptly, since this record is often required to access disaster relief and insurance support.

## When to Consult an Agricultural Extension Officer, Veterinary Officer, or Disaster Management Authority
- Immediately for any risk to human life — contact local emergency and disaster management services first, before addressing crops or livestock.
- Promptly for sudden or unexplained livestock deaths, illness affecting multiple animals, or any suspicion of anthrax or another notifiable disease following flooding.
- For guidance on the safe use, testing, or disposal of flood-affected crops, stored produce, seed, and water sources.
- To access disaster relief programs, agricultural recovery support, or coordinated regional flood response where these are available.
- Before replanting, restocking water systems, or resuming irrigation from a source that may have been affected by floodwater.
