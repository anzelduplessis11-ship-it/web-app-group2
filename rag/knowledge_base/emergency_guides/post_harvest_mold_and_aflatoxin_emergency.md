# Post-Harvest Mould and Aflatoxin Contamination Emergency Guide

## Overview
This guide provides immediate actions for farmers who discover mould growth in stored grain or other produce, or who suspect aflatoxin contamination. Mould in storage can spread and worsen quickly, and some moulds produce toxins, including aflatoxins, that pose serious health risks to people and livestock even when the grain otherwise looks and smells acceptable. This guide supports immediate first response to limit health risk and further loss. It does not replace testing and guidance from an agricultural extension officer or food safety authority, which is essential before deciding whether affected grain can be used in any form.

## Recognizing Mould and Possible Aflatoxin Contamination
- Visible signs of mould include fuzzy or powdery growth (which may appear white, grey, green, yellow, or black), discoloured or dark, shrivelled kernels, and grain clumping together.
- Other warning signs include a musty, sour, or otherwise unusual odour from stored grain, unusually high moisture or heat felt within the grain mass (a sign of active mould growth and spoilage), and any history of the grain being stored wet, harvested late, or exposed to pest damage or previous water damage.
- Aflatoxin contamination cannot be seen, smelled, or tasted directly. Grain can be heavily contaminated with aflatoxin without obvious visible mould, particularly if contamination occurred in the field before harvest or if mould growth has since died back. Any grain with a history of mould growth, insect damage, drought stress during grain fill, or improper drying and storage should be treated as a possible aflatoxin risk even without visible mould.

## Immediate Actions Upon Discovery
### Immediate Steps
1. Stop using the affected batch of grain for human consumption or animal feed immediately upon noticing mould or any of the warning signs above, pending further assessment.
2. Isolate the affected grain physically from unaffected stock, using separate, clearly marked containers or storage areas, to prevent mould spores and moisture from spreading to unaffected grain.
3. Ventilate the storage area if enclosed, since damp, poorly ventilated conditions favour continued mould growth and can also pose an inhalation risk to people entering the space.
4. Check surrounding stock and storage structures for signs of moisture ingress, pest damage, or condensation, which may indicate the underlying cause of the mould problem and risk to remaining grain.

### What NOT to Do
- **Do not consume, sell, or feed to livestock any grain showing visible mould, or any grain suspected of aflatoxin contamination, until it has been assessed as safe.** Aflatoxin is not destroyed by ordinary cooking, boiling, or processing.
- **Do not attempt to salvage suspect grain by simply removing visibly mouldy kernels and using the remainder without further assessment**, since contamination is often distributed unevenly and invisibly through an apparently clean batch.
- Do not mix suspect or mouldy grain with clean, unaffected grain in an attempt to dilute the problem; this does not remove the toxin risk and instead contaminates the entire mixed batch.
- Do not burn large quantities of mouldy grain in enclosed spaces or without adequate ventilation, since this can produce harmful smoke; follow safe disposal guidance below.

## Human Health Precautions
- Avoid inhaling dust from mouldy grain when handling it; use a cloth or mask covering the nose and mouth where available, and handle affected grain outdoors or in a well-ventilated area.
- Wash hands thoroughly after handling mouldy grain, and avoid touching the face while handling it.
- **Seek medical attention for anyone who may have consumed heavily mould-contaminated grain or food**, particularly children, who are more vulnerable to the health effects of aflatoxin exposure, and describe the possible exposure to the treating clinician.
- Be aware that the health effects of aflatoxin exposure are often not immediately obvious, and repeated low-level exposure over time carries serious long-term health risks; this makes avoiding contaminated grain in the diet important even when no immediate symptoms are present.

## Livestock Feed Precautions
- Do not feed mouldy or suspect grain to livestock, including poultry, without confirming safety, since aflatoxin and other mould-related toxins can cause illness, reduced productivity, and residues in milk, eggs, or meat that can, in turn, pose a risk to people consuming those products.
- Dairy animals are of particular concern, since aflatoxin consumed by a lactating animal can be transferred into milk in a related form; do not feed suspect grain to dairy animals.
- Consult a veterinary officer or extension officer before feeding any grain of uncertain safety to livestock, particularly breeding, young, or lactating animals.

## Sorting and Salvage Assessment
1. Where the affected quantity is large and total loss would be severe, seek guidance from an extension officer or grain quality authority on whether any portion of the batch can be tested and confirmed safe for a specific limited use before making a final decision.
2. Do not assume that any sorting, cleaning, drying, or reprocessing method performed on the farm has removed contamination risk without confirmation, since aflatoxin is a stable toxin that is not reliably eliminated by common on-farm processing.
3. Recognize that visual sorting can reduce but does not reliably eliminate contamination risk in a batch with a history of mould exposure.

## Safe Disposal of Contaminated Grain
1. Dispose of confirmed or strongly suspected contaminated grain in a manner that prevents access by people, livestock, and pets, following any specific guidance provided by an extension or food safety officer for the quantity and situation involved.
2. Do not dispose of large quantities of contaminated grain in a way that could contaminate water sources or grazing land.
3. Keep disposal areas clearly separated from areas used for clean grain storage, handling, or livestock feeding.

## Preventing Further Contamination in Storage
1. Address the underlying cause of the mould problem before returning any grain to the affected storage structure, whether that is roof leaks, poor ventilation, high humidity, inadequate drying before storage, or pest damage.
2. Ensure all grain is dried to a safe moisture level before storage and verify this where testing equipment is available, since grain stored above safe moisture levels is at significantly higher risk of mould growth.
3. Clean and, where appropriate, treat storage structures thoroughly before introducing new or unaffected grain, to remove mould spores and reduce pest habitat.
4. Improve ventilation and moisture control in storage structures going forward, and inspect stored grain regularly rather than only at the point of use.

## Testing and Verification
- Where testing services for aflatoxin or grain quality are available through extension services, cooperatives, or regional laboratories, use them for any batch with a history of mould, pest damage, or improper storage, particularly before it is sold or used as feed for dairy or breeding animals.
- Follow the specific sampling method recommended by the testing service, since contamination is often unevenly distributed and a poorly collected sample can give a misleading result.

## Recovery Steps and Improved Storage Practices
1. Review harvest timing, drying practices, and storage conditions that may have contributed to the mould problem, in consultation with an extension officer.
2. Improve pre-storage drying and moisture testing practices for future harvests.
3. Consider improved storage structures or methods (properly sealed containers, elevated storage away from ground moisture, regular inspection routines) if recurring mould problems have occurred.
4. Maintain good pest control in storage areas, since insect damage to grain increases susceptibility to mould growth and subsequent toxin development.

## General Principles for Mould and Aflatoxin Emergencies
- When in doubt, do not use the grain for human food or animal feed until it has been assessed as safe; the health risks of aflatoxin exposure, particularly with repeated exposure, are serious and often not immediately visible.
- Visible mould removal or dilution with clean grain does not make a contaminated batch safe.
- Addressing the underlying moisture, drying, or storage problem is essential to prevent recurrence, not just managing the immediate affected batch.

## When to Consult an Agricultural Extension Officer or Food Safety Authority
- Immediately upon discovering significant mould growth in stored grain, or when any grain has a history of conditions that increase aflatoxin risk, even without visible mould.
- Before using, selling, or feeding to livestock any batch of grain with a history of mould, pest damage, or improper storage or drying.
- To access grain or feed testing services where available, particularly for grain intended for sale, dairy animal feed, or infant and young child food preparation.
- For guidance on safe disposal of contaminated grain and on improving storage practices to prevent recurrence.
- Medical attention should be sought promptly and separately for anyone, especially children, suspected of having consumed significantly mould-contaminated food.
