# Drought Emergency Response Guide

## Overview
This guide provides immediate and follow-up response steps for farmers facing severe drought conditions affecting crops, livestock, water supply, or pasture. Drought differs from most other emergencies in that it develops over weeks or months rather than hours, but decisions made early in a drought often determine whether losses remain manageable or become severe. This guide supports early, practical decision-making. It does not replace guidance from an agricultural extension officer, veterinary officer, or disaster/drought relief authority, particularly for decisions on destocking, emergency feed, or water allocation.

## Recognizing a Developing or Severe Drought Emergency
- Early warning signs include a delayed start to the rains, an extended dry spell during the growing season, visibly wilting or stunted crops beyond normal moisture stress, rapidly dropping water levels in dams, boreholes, or rivers, and pasture that is not regenerating after light rain.
- A drought becomes an emergency, rather than routine dry-season management, when water sources that are normally reliable are at risk of running dry, when crops are failing across a wide area rather than in isolated patches, or when available feed and grazing will clearly not meet livestock needs for the remainder of the dry period.
- Acting at the first clear signs of a developing emergency, rather than waiting until water and feed are critically short, preserves far more options for both crops and livestock.

## Immediate Priorities: Water
### Immediate Steps
1. Assess and prioritize all available water sources for household use, livestock, and any remaining irrigated crops, in that order of priority where supply is limited.
2. Reduce non-essential water use immediately (washing, non-critical irrigation) to extend the life of remaining supplies.
3. Check boreholes, wells, and storage tanks for leaks or inefficiencies and repair these promptly, since even small losses become significant when supply is limited.
4. Where multiple households or farms share a water source, coordinate use with neighbours and local authorities to avoid conflict and ensure equitable access, particularly for drinking water.

### What NOT to Do
- Do not allow livestock unrestricted access to a rapidly depleting water source without a management plan, since this can exhaust the source faster than necessary and leave no water for the following days.
- Do not draw down a shared community water source without coordinating with other users and, where relevant, local water or disaster management authorities.

## Immediate Actions for Crops Under Severe Drought Stress
### Immediate Steps
1. Assess each field or crop stage separately: crops close to maturity may still produce a partial yield with minimal further water, while very young or flowering crops are often the most vulnerable to total loss.
2. Where any irrigation water remains available, prioritize it for the crop stages most likely to respond, such as flowering and grain-filling stages, rather than spreading limited water evenly across all fields.
3. Apply mulch around surviving crops where material is available, to reduce soil moisture loss and moderate soil temperature.
4. Reduce competition for the limited available moisture by removing weeds promptly, since weeds compete directly with crops for scarce water.
5. For crops that are clearly beyond recovery, consider salvaging what is possible (for example, cutting for emergency livestock fodder) rather than leaving the crop to fail completely in the field, in consultation with an extension officer on suitability for feed.

### When to Escalate
- Widespread crop failure across a large area, or uncertainty about whether any intervention can save part of the crop, should be reported to an agricultural extension officer promptly, both for technical guidance and to support regional drought damage assessment and any relief programs.

## Immediate Actions for Livestock Under Severe Drought Stress
### Immediate Steps
1. Assess current feed and water reserves against the number of animals held, and calculate realistically how long these will last if the drought continues.
2. Begin reducing herd size or moving animals to available grazing well before feed and water are critically short, rather than waiting until animals are already in poor condition (see destocking guidance below).
3. Prioritize water and remaining good-quality feed for breeding females, young animals, and animals in the best condition to survive and remain productive, since attempting to sustain the entire herd equally on insufficient resources often results in higher overall losses.
4. Monitor body condition regularly (weekly or more often as conditions worsen) to identify animals becoming vulnerable before they reach a critical state.
5. Provide shade and reduce unnecessary movement of animals during the hottest part of the day, since heat stress compounds the effects of feed and water shortage.

### When to Escalate
- Rapid deterioration in body condition across the herd, deaths, or feed and water reserves projected to run out within days should be reported to a veterinary officer or agricultural extension officer immediately.

## What NOT to Do During Drought
- **Do not delay destocking decisions until animals are already in very poor condition,** since weak or emaciated animals are harder to sell, transport, or feed back to health, and losses escalate quickly once body condition drops sharply.
- Do not force-water or force-feed severely weakened animals without guidance, since reintroducing large amounts of feed or water too quickly to a severely undernourished or dehydrated animal can cause serious complications.
- Do not allow overgrazing of already stressed pasture in a desperate attempt to sustain the full herd, since this can prevent pasture recovery even after rains return, extending the impact of the drought.
- Do not use unfamiliar or untested emergency feed sources without checking for toxicity, since some plants and by-products that are normally avoided can be unsafe for livestock in certain quantities.

## Prioritizing Limited Water and Feed Resources
1. Rank animals by production value and likelihood of survival, and allocate resources accordingly rather than spreading them thinly across the entire herd.
2. Consider culling or early sale of the least productive, oldest, or least fit animals early in the drought, when they are still in reasonable condition and market value is less affected.
3. Explore any available community grazing schemes, fodder banks, or government drought relief feed programs, where these exist in the area.

## Emergency Feed and Fodder Management
1. Extend available feed using crop residues, and any locally recommended emergency feed sources, in consultation with an extension officer on appropriate proportions and safety.
2. Introduce any new or unfamiliar feed sources gradually, monitoring animals for digestive upset.
3. Store any purchased or gathered emergency feed carefully to protect it from spoilage and pests, since this feed represents a critical and limited resource.
4. Avoid feeding mouldy or spoiled material, even under emergency conditions, given the serious health risks of mould-related toxins (see post-harvest mould and aflatoxin guidance).

## Emergency Culling and Destocking Decisions
- Destocking (selling or otherwise reducing herd size) early in a developing drought, rather than as a last resort, generally preserves more value and reduces the risk of severe, uncontrolled losses later.
- Base destocking decisions on realistic projections of remaining feed and water against herd size, updated regularly as conditions change.
- Consult an agricultural extension officer or livestock marketing service where available for guidance on timing and options for emergency sale or relocation of animals.

## Recovery Steps After Drought Breaks
1. Reintroduce grazing to recovering pasture gradually and in a controlled manner once rains return, since pasture that regrows after severe drought stress is often vulnerable to damage from overgrazing before it is fully re-established.
2. Assess soil condition before replanting, since prolonged drought can affect soil structure and organic matter, and initial rains after a long dry period can also cause erosion on bare, drought-affected land.
3. Prioritize replanting with crop varieties suited to the remaining season length, in consultation with an extension officer, particularly where the drought has shortened the effective growing season.
4. Rebuild livestock feed reserves and monitor animal body condition and health closely during the recovery period, since animals that were significantly stressed by drought remain more vulnerable to illness for some time afterward.
5. Document losses (crop, livestock, and financial) thoroughly, since this record often supports access to drought relief, restocking assistance, or insurance programs where available.

## General Principles for Drought Emergencies
- Early, decisive action preserves more options than waiting for conditions to become critical.
- Water and feed allocation decisions should prioritize the animals and crops most likely to survive and remain productive, rather than attempting to sustain everything equally.
- Coordinate with neighbours and local authorities on shared water and grazing resources to reduce conflict and improve outcomes for the wider community.
- Avoid drastic, panic-driven decisions; where possible, base decisions on regular reassessment of actual water, feed, and animal condition data.

## When to Consult an Agricultural Extension Officer, Veterinary Officer, or Drought Relief Authority
- As soon as early warning signs of a developing drought emergency are recognized, to access guidance and any available early-response support.
- For guidance on destocking timing, emergency feed safety, and water allocation decisions.
- Promptly for rapid deterioration in livestock body condition, unexplained illness, or livestock deaths during drought.
- To access government or regional drought relief programs, fodder banks, water trucking schemes, or restocking assistance where available.
- Before replanting or restocking following a severe drought, to confirm suitable varieties, timing, and land condition.
