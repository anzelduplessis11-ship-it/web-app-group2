# Livestock Frequently Asked Questions

## Cattle

**Q: How much land or grazing do I need to keep cattle sustainably?**
A: Grazing capacity depends heavily on local rainfall, pasture type, and season, so there is no single figure that applies everywhere. Overstocking beyond what local pasture can support leads to degraded grazing land and poorer animal condition over time; a local extension officer or livestock advisor can help estimate a sustainable stocking rate for your specific area. See `livestock/cattle_management.md`.

**Q: What is the difference between raising cattle for dairy versus beef?**
A: Dairy production prioritises breeds and management (feeding, milking routine, calving intervals) that maximise milk yield, while beef production prioritises breeds and feeding regimes that maximise weight gain and carcass quality. Some smallholder systems use dual-purpose breeds that balance both to some degree, though typically with lower peak performance in either trait than specialised breeds.

**Q: How often should cattle be dewormed?**
A: Deworming frequency depends on local parasite pressure, season, and animal age, and is best guided by a local veterinary or extension recommendation rather than a fixed universal schedule. Overuse of dewormers can contribute to parasite resistance, so treating based on need (including faecal testing where available) is preferable to routine blanket treatment. See `livestock/cattle_health_and_disease.md`.

**Q: My cow is not producing as much milk as before. What could be wrong?**
A: Common causes include inadequate feed quantity or quality, stage of lactation (milk yield naturally declines over the lactation cycle), stress or heat, underlying illness (e.g., mastitis), or approaching the natural end of the lactation period before drying off. A sudden, sharp drop in yield combined with other symptoms (fever, abnormal milk, loss of appetite) should be checked by a veterinarian promptly.

**Q: How do I know if my cow is close to calving?**
A: Signs of approaching calving include udder enlargement (bagging up), relaxation and swelling around the tailhead and vulva, restlessness, and separation from the herd in the final hours. See `livestock/cattle_reproduction_and_calving.md` for a fuller description of the stages of calving and when intervention or veterinary help is needed.

**Q: What should I do if a cow is struggling during calving (difficult birth)?**
A: Prolonged, unproductive straining, a calf presented in an abnormal position, or more than roughly one to two hours of active labour without progress are signs that veterinary assistance is needed promptly, since delay increases risk to both cow and calf. Do not attempt significant manual intervention without experience or guidance, as this can cause injury.

## Goats and Sheep

**Q: What are the main differences in managing goats versus sheep?**
A: Goats are generally browsers (preferring shrubs, leaves, and varied vegetation) while sheep are primarily grazers (preferring grass), which affects suitable land and feeding strategy. Goats are often somewhat more resistant to certain internal parasites and harsher conditions, but both are susceptible to a range of shared diseases requiring similar vaccination and deworming attention. See `livestock/goat_and_sheep_management.md`.

**Q: How can I reduce losses from internal parasites in goats and sheep?**
A: Rotational grazing (avoiding continuous grazing of the same paddock), avoiding grazing very short pasture close to the ground where parasite larvae concentrate, and targeted deworming based on body condition or faecal egg counts rather than fixed calendar treatment are effective approaches. See `livestock/small_ruminant_parasite_control.md`.

**Q: At what age can goats or sheep be bred?**
A: Breeding readiness depends on the animal reaching adequate body weight and maturity rather than age alone, and breeding too early can harm the health and future productivity of the female. See `livestock/goat_and_sheep_management.md` for general guidance, and consult a local livestock extension officer for breed-specific recommendations.

**Q: My goats keep escaping or destroying fencing. What can I do?**
A: Goats are agile and persistent, so fencing needs to be higher and sturdier than what is typically adequate for sheep or cattle; electric fencing or well-constructed woven wire is commonly used. Adequate feed and space within the enclosure also reduces the incentive to escape in search of forage.

**Q: How do I recognise early signs of illness in small ruminants?**
A: Watch for reduced appetite, lethargy, isolation from the group, pale eyelid membranes (a sign of anaemia, often from parasites), abnormal droppings, nasal discharge, or laboured breathing. Small ruminants can decline quickly once visibly ill, so early veterinary consultation is important rather than a wait-and-see approach.

## Poultry

**Q: What is the difference between raising broilers and layers?**
A: Broilers are bred and managed for rapid weight gain and meat production over a short cycle (typically a matter of weeks), while layers are managed for sustained egg production over a much longer productive period, with different feed formulations for each. Using the wrong feed type for the bird's purpose (e.g., layer feed for broilers) will not support optimal growth or production.

**Q: How do I prevent disease outbreaks in my poultry flock?**
A: Key measures include maintaining clean housing with good ventilation, controlling visitor and equipment access (biosecurity), following a recommended vaccination schedule for diseases common in your region (such as Newcastle disease), and isolating new or sick birds before introducing them to the main flock. See `livestock/poultry_health_and_biosecurity.md`.

**Q: Why are my hens not laying eggs, or laying fewer than expected?**
A: Common causes include inadequate nutrition (especially protein and calcium), insufficient daylight hours, stress (overcrowding, predators, extreme heat), age (egg production naturally declines with age), moulting periods, or underlying disease. See `livestock/poultry_egg_production.md` for a more detailed troubleshooting guide.

**Q: How much space do chickens need for healthy production?**
A: Overcrowding increases stress, disease spread, and aggressive behaviour (including feather-pecking and cannibalism), while appropriate stocking density varies by system (free-range, semi-intensive, deep litter) and bird type. See `livestock/poultry_housing_guidelines.md` for general space recommendations by system type.

**Q: What vaccinations are recommended for backyard or smallholder poultry?**
A: Recommended vaccines vary by region depending on locally prevalent diseases, with Newcastle disease vaccination being widely recommended in many African contexts. Consult a local veterinary or extension service for the specific vaccination schedule recommended in your area, since disease pressure and available vaccines differ by location.

## Feeding and Nutrition

**Q: Can I feed my livestock only what I grow, without buying commercial feed?**
A: This is possible for some systems, particularly ruminants with access to adequate pasture and crop residues, but productivity is often limited without balanced supplementation, especially protein and mineral sources during dry seasons or peak production periods (lactation, growth, egg-laying). See `livestock/livestock_feeding_and_nutrition.md` for guidance on balancing home-grown and purchased feed.

**Q: What should I do during the dry season when pasture is scarce?**
A: Strategies include conserving feed in advance (hay, silage, crop residues stored from the wet season), reducing herd size before scarcity peaks if forage will not support current numbers, and using drought-tolerant fodder options where available. See `livestock/dry_season_feed_management.md` and `weather_and_climate_faqs.md` for related seasonal planning guidance.

**Q: Do livestock need mineral or salt supplementation even on good pasture?**
A: Many pastures, particularly in certain soil types, are deficient in specific minerals (such as phosphorus or trace minerals) that grazing alone does not adequately supply, so mineral licks or supplements are commonly recommended even where forage looks adequate. Signs of mineral deficiency can be subtle, so consult a livestock extension officer if you are unsure whether supplementation is needed for your area.

**Q: How do I introduce a new feed to my livestock without causing problems?**
A: Sudden feed changes, especially to grain-heavy or high-protein feeds, can cause digestive upset (such as bloat or acidosis in ruminants); a gradual transition over several days to a couple of weeks, mixing increasing proportions of the new feed with the old, is generally safer.

## General Husbandry and Health

**Q: How often should I consult a veterinarian versus handling a health issue myself?**
A: Minor, well-recognised issues with clear, low-risk remedies (e.g., routine hoof trimming, minor wound cleaning) can often be managed with basic training, but any case involving high fever, sudden death, unexplained multiple animal illness, difficulty breathing, or symptoms you do not recognise should be escalated to a veterinarian or livestock extension officer promptly, since delayed treatment of serious conditions increases risk of loss and, for some diseases, risk of spread.

**Q: What basic records should I keep for my livestock?**
A: Useful records include animal identification, birth/acquisition dates, vaccination and treatment history, breeding dates, and production data (milk yield, egg counts, weight gains). These records support better management decisions and are often required for accessing certain markets, insurance, or veterinary services. See `livestock/record_keeping_for_livestock.md`.

**Q: How do I reduce the risk of disease spreading between my farm and neighbouring farms?**
A: Basic biosecurity practices — limiting movement of visitors and equipment between farms, quarantining newly purchased animals before mixing them with the existing herd/flock, and reporting suspected notifiable diseases promptly — help limit spread. Certain diseases (e.g., foot-and-mouth disease, avian influenza) are subject to mandatory reporting requirements in many countries; consult local veterinary authorities if you suspect such a disease.

**Q: Is it worth investing in improved/exotic breeds versus local indigenous breeds?**
A: Improved or exotic breeds often have higher production potential (milk, meat, growth rate) but frequently require better feeding, housing, and health management to realise that potential, and may be less resilient to local heat, disease, or feed scarcity than indigenous or crossbred animals. The right choice depends on your management capacity, available resources, and market goals; a livestock extension officer can help assess what is realistic for your specific situation.

**Q: What should I do if I suspect a disease outbreak affecting multiple animals at once?**
A: Isolate affected animals where possible, avoid moving animals off the farm until the cause is identified, and contact a veterinarian or livestock extension/veterinary authority promptly, since rapid multi-animal illness can indicate a serious infectious disease requiring urgent professional diagnosis and, in some cases, mandatory reporting.

**Q: What housing or shelter do livestock actually need in a typical smallholder setting?**
A: At minimum, most livestock benefit from shelter that protects against rain, direct sun, and cold wind, with adequate ventilation to prevent respiratory disease from ammonia buildup and humidity. Requirements differ by species and age (young and newborn animals generally need more protection than mature, healthy adults); see `livestock/cattle_management.md`, `livestock/goat_and_sheep_management.md`, and `livestock/poultry_housing_guidelines.md` for species-specific detail.

**Q: How do I safely transport livestock to market or to a buyer?**
A: Avoid overcrowding during transport, provide access to water for longer journeys, handle animals calmly to reduce stress-related weight loss and injury, and check local regulations on movement permits or health certificates, which are required in many countries particularly for cross-border or long-distance movement. Excessive stress during transport can also reduce meat quality and increase disease susceptibility on arrival.

**Q: Is livestock insurance available, and is it worth considering?**
A: Livestock insurance products exist in some markets, covering risks such as disease-related death, theft, or, in some regions, drought-related losses through index-based schemes, but availability, cost, and terms vary considerably by country and provider. Check with local agricultural finance providers, cooperatives, or an extension officer for products available in your area, since FarmConnect does not track region-specific insurance offerings.

**Q: How do I decide the right time to sell or slaughter an animal for the best value?**
A: This depends on the animal's growth stage relative to its target market (weight, body condition, age expectations for the intended buyer type), and, for breeding females, weighing ongoing productive value against sale value. FarmConnect does not provide specific price guidance, since livestock prices vary by location, season, breed, and condition; check current listings on the live marketplace and consult local livestock traders or an extension officer for guidance on optimal timing for your specific animal and market.
