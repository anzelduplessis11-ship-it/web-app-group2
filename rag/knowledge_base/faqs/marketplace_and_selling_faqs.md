# Marketplace and Selling Frequently Asked Questions

## Listing Produce

**Q: What information should I include when listing my produce on FarmConnect?**
A: A good listing includes the crop or product name and variety, quantity available, unit of measure (kg, bag, crate, head), grade or quality description, location (for transport planning), and expected availability date if not yet harvested. Clear, accurate listings attract serious buyers and reduce time wasted on mismatched enquiries. See `marketplace/listing_best_practices.md` for a full checklist.

**Q: Should I list my produce before or after harvest?**
A: Listing shortly before or at harvest, once quantity and quality are reasonably certain, generally gives buyers more confidence than very early pre-harvest listings, which carry more uncertainty about final yield and condition. Some buyers do accept forward contracts for bulk or contract farming arrangements; see `marketplace/forward_contracts_and_bulk_orders.md` if this applies to your situation.

**Q: How often should I update my listing?**
A: Update your listing whenever quantity, price expectations, or availability changes, and remove or mark it as sold once your stock is committed. Stale listings showing produce that is no longer available can damage buyer trust in your account over time.

**Q: Can I list produce I don't have yet, expecting to harvest soon?**
A: This is possible for pre-order or forward-sale arrangements, but the listing should clearly state the expected harvest date and that the sale is provisional. Overstating certainty on quantity or timing that later cannot be met can lead to disputes and harm your reputation on the platform.

**Q: What photos should I use for my listing?**
A: Clear, well-lit photos of the actual produce batch being sold, taken close enough to show condition and grading detail, are more useful to buyers than generic stock images. Avoid using old photos from a previous season's harvest for a current listing.

## Grading and Quality

**Q: What does "grading" mean for produce, and why does it matter?**
A: Grading means sorting produce into quality categories based on defined criteria such as size, moisture content, colour, defect level, and uniformity. Higher grades typically command better offers and access to more buyers, including processors and exporters who often will not accept ungraded or mixed-quality lots. See `marketplace/product_grading_and_quality.md` for crop-specific grading standards.

**Q: My produce doesn't fit a single grade — some is high quality, some is not. What should I do?**
A: Sort and list mixed-quality produce as separate lots by grade rather than combining them into a single listing. Buyers generally pay more in total for clearly graded, separated lots than for one mixed lot sold at an averaged price.

**Q: How do I improve the grade of my produce before selling?**
A: Proper drying to the recommended moisture level, careful sorting to remove damaged, diseased, or foreign material, and clean, appropriate packaging are the main levers most smallholder farmers can control after harvest. See `storage/post_harvest_handling.md` and `marketplace/product_grading_and_quality.md`.

**Q: A buyer says my produce doesn't meet the grade I listed. What should I do?**
A: Ask the buyer to specify exactly which grading criteria are not met (moisture, size, defects, foreign matter) and, where practical, have this checked against an agreed standard or a neutral third party rather than relying only on one side's assessment. If the mismatch is genuine, renegotiating price or grade honestly is usually better for your long-term reputation than disputing a fair assessment.

**Q: Do I need special equipment to grade my produce accurately?**
A: Basic tools such as a moisture meter (for grains), sieves for sizing, and simple visual defect checklists cover most smallholder grading needs. Cooperatives or local buying centres sometimes have shared grading equipment available; check with local farmer groups or extension services.

## Negotiating and Pricing

**Q: How do I know what price to ask for my produce?**
A: FarmConnect does not set or publish fixed prices, since prices vary constantly by location, season, quality, and buyer. Check current listings and offers on the live marketplace for comparable produce and grade in your area, and cross-check with local market or cooperative price information before setting an asking price. See `pricing/pricing_principles.md` for general factors that influence price.

**Q: A buyer's offer seems very low. How should I respond?**
A: Compare the offer against other current listings or offers for similar grade and quantity on the marketplace before deciding, since a single offer is not necessarily representative of the market. It is reasonable to counter-offer with your reasoning (grade, transport distance, delivery timing) rather than accepting the first offer immediately.

**Q: Is it better to negotiate individually or sell as a group with other farmers?**
A: Collective marketing through a farmer cooperative or group can increase negotiating leverage, particularly for reaching larger buyers who prefer bulk volumes, and can reduce individual transport costs. This must be balanced against the coordination needed to align quality, timing, and payment terms across group members. See `marketplace/collective_marketing.md`.

**Q: Should I accept the first offer I receive?**
A: Not necessarily — comparing multiple offers where time allows generally leads to a better outcome, provided the produce can wait without quality loss. For perishable produce with limited shelf life, weigh the risk of spoilage from waiting against the potential benefit of a better offer.

**Q: How do I handle a buyer who keeps trying to lower the price after we already agreed?**
A: Confirm the agreed price and terms in writing through the FarmConnect messaging or order confirmation feature before delivery, so both sides have a clear record. Repeated attempts to renegotiate after agreement, especially close to or after delivery, are a warning sign and may warrant reporting the buyer through the platform's feedback or dispute process.

## Payment Safety

**Q: What is the safest way to receive payment for my produce?**
A: Using FarmConnect's in-platform payment or escrow feature where available is generally safer than informal cash arrangements with unfamiliar buyers, since it creates a verifiable transaction record and, with escrow, holds funds until delivery is confirmed. See `marketplace/payment_and_escrow_guide.md` for details on how payment protection works on the platform.

**Q: A buyer wants to pay only after collecting and reselling my produce. Is this safe?**
A: Payment-after-resale arrangements shift most of the risk onto the seller, since you have no guarantee of payment once the produce has left your control. This arrangement should generally be avoided with new or unverified buyers; if used at all, it is safer with long-standing, trusted buyers and ideally with some written agreement on payment timing.

**Q: A buyer is asking me to send produce before any payment or deposit. What should I do?**
A: Exercise caution with any request to deliver a full order before receiving payment or an agreed deposit, particularly from a buyer with no transaction history or verified profile on the platform. Requesting at least a partial deposit, or using the platform's payment protection features, reduces this risk.

**Q: How can I tell if a buyer's profile is trustworthy?**
A: Check the buyer's verification status, transaction history, and ratings/reviews from other farmers on the platform where these features are available. A new account with no history is not necessarily untrustworthy, but it warrants more caution, such as starting with a smaller trial order.

**Q: What should I do if a buyer's payment fails or bounces?**
A: Do not release or deliver produce based on unconfirmed payment claims; wait for confirmed payment through the platform or bank before finalising delivery. If a payment already made turns out to be fraudulent or reversed after delivery, report the incident through FarmConnect's support/dispute channel promptly.

**Q: Can I accept mobile money payments through FarmConnect?**
A: Supported payment methods, including mobile money where integrated, are listed in the platform's payment settings and may vary by region. See `marketplace/payment_and_escrow_guide.md` for the payment options available in your area.

## Dealing with Buyers and Farmers

**Q: How do I respond to buyer enquiries professionally?**
A: Respond promptly with accurate information about quantity, grade, price expectations, and availability, and avoid promising quantities or timelines you are not confident you can meet. Clear, honest communication builds the kind of reputation that leads to repeat business.

**Q: A buyer wants to inspect my produce before agreeing to buy. Should I allow this?**
A: Allowing reasonable inspection, especially for larger orders, is a normal part of building trust and reduces the chance of later disputes over quality claims. Agree on inspection terms (who bears cost/time, what happens if quality is disputed) before the buyer travels, where practical.

**Q: How do I handle multiple buyers interested in the same batch of produce?**
A: Communicate clearly and promptly to all interested buyers about availability status, and update or close your listing as soon as the produce is committed to avoid misleading other interested buyers. Overselling the same stock to multiple buyers can seriously damage trust and platform standing.

**Q: What should I do if a buyer becomes rude, abusive, or makes suspicious requests (e.g., asking to move the deal off-platform to avoid fees)?**
A: Keep communication professional, and use FarmConnect's reporting or blocking features for abusive behaviour. Requests to move a transaction off-platform specifically to bypass safety or payment protection features are a common warning sign and should generally be declined.

## Closing a Sale and Disputes

**Q: What should be confirmed before finalising a sale?**
A: Confirm quantity, grade, agreed price, payment method and timing, delivery/collection arrangements, and who bears transport cost, ideally recorded through the platform's order confirmation feature. Having these details agreed in writing reduces the chance of later misunderstanding.

**Q: Who is responsible for transport — the buyer or the seller?**
A: This depends entirely on what is negotiated and agreed for each specific transaction; there is no universal rule. Clarify and record this explicitly as part of closing the sale, since transport cost can materially affect the net price received.

**Q: What happens if the buyer doesn't collect the produce as agreed?**
A: Contact the buyer promptly through the platform to clarify the delay, and document the agreed collection date and any communication. If the produce is perishable and at risk of spoiling, you may need to sell to another buyer; FarmConnect's dispute process can help address any resulting disagreement over the original agreement.

**Q: How do I raise a dispute if a deal goes wrong?**
A: Use FarmConnect's dispute resolution or support feature, providing the order details, agreed terms, and any relevant communication or evidence (photos, messages). See `marketplace/dispute_resolution_process.md` for the steps involved and expected timelines.

**Q: Should I leave feedback or a rating after a transaction?**
A: Yes — honest feedback on both successful and problematic transactions helps build a more reliable marketplace for all users and helps other farmers and buyers make informed decisions.

**Q: I'm not sure whether a specific deal or contract term is fair or standard. What should I do?**
A: For unusual contract terms, large or complex transactions, or situations involving significant financial risk, it is reasonable to consult an agricultural extension officer, a farmer cooperative representative, or, for legal terms, appropriate advisory services before committing, rather than relying solely on marketplace guidance.
