# Storage and Post-Harvest Frequently Asked Questions

## Drying and Moisture Management

**Q: How dry does my grain need to be before storage?**
A: Safe storage moisture content varies by grain type but commonly falls in the range of 12-14% for cereals such as maize; pulses and oilseeds often require different thresholds. See `storage/grain_storage.md` for crop-specific safe moisture levels, and use a moisture meter where available rather than judging dryness by look or feel alone, since visual assessment is unreliable.

**Q: I don't have a moisture meter. How can I check if my grain is dry enough?**
A: Simple field tests include the "salt test" (comparing grain sealed with dry salt in a jar for shaking and clumping signs) or biting/shaking tests traditionally used by farmers, but these are less precise and reliable than a proper moisture meter. Where possible, accessing a moisture meter through a cooperative, buying centre, or extension office is recommended before committing large volumes to long-term storage.

**Q: What is the best way to dry grain after harvest?**
A: Sun-drying on a clean raised surface or tarpaulin (not directly on bare ground, which increases contamination risk), with regular turning for even drying, is the most common smallholder method. Solar or mechanical dryers, where accessible, can dry more evenly and quickly, particularly useful in humid conditions or when rain threatens field-drying. See `storage/post_harvest_handling.md`.

**Q: Rain fell on my grain while it was drying — what should I do?**
A: Re-spread and continue drying as soon as conditions allow, and check the affected grain closely for early mould development, since repeated wetting and drying cycles increase mould and mycotoxin risk. If mould is visible on a significant portion of the batch, do not simply mix it back with clean grain; see the food safety section below.

**Q: How do I know if my dried grain will store well, or if it needs more drying?**
A: Beyond checking moisture content against the recommended safe threshold for that grain, physical signs like grains that shatter (rather than dent) when bitten or pressed, and a low, hollow sound when a handful is shaken in a container, suggest adequate dryness, though a moisture meter reading remains more reliable than these traditional indicators.

## Storage Structures and Methods

**Q: What storage structure is best for a smallholder farmer with limited budget?**
A: Hermetic (airtight) storage bags or containers are widely recommended for smallholders because they starve storage insects of oxygen without chemical treatment and are relatively affordable, though they require grain to already be adequately dry before sealing. Improved granaries, metal silos, and traditional structures (improved with basic pest-proofing) are other options depending on scale and budget; see `storage/storage_structures_comparison.md`.

**Q: Are traditional storage structures (mud granaries, woven baskets) safe to keep using?**
A: Traditional structures can perform reasonably well if kept clean, raised off the ground, protected from rain and rodents, and used with grain that has been properly dried, but they generally offer less protection against pests and moisture than sealed modern alternatives. Improving a traditional structure (raising it, sealing cracks, adding a rodent guard) can meaningfully reduce losses without a full replacement.

**Q: How often should I inspect stored grain?**
A: Regular inspection — commonly recommended at least monthly, more frequently in humid conditions or during the early weeks after storage — helps catch developing insect infestation, mould, or moisture problems before they spread through the whole stock. Look for heat or moisture when reaching into the grain mass, unusual smells, live insects, and webbing or clumping.

**Q: Can I store different crops together in the same structure?**
A: Storing different crop types together is generally discouraged where avoidable, since they may have different safe moisture requirements, attract different pests, and make inspection and stock rotation more difficult. If space constraints require shared storage, keep crops in clearly separated, sealed containers within the structure.

**Q: How long can grain be safely stored before quality declines significantly?**
A: This depends on the crop, storage method, and conditions, but even well-stored grain gradually loses germination viability (for seed use) and some quality attributes over time; hermetic and cool, dry conditions extend safe storage duration considerably compared to poor storage. See `storage/grain_storage.md` for typical storage life expectations by crop and method.

## Pest and Spoilage Prevention

**Q: What are the most common causes of stored grain losses for smallholder farmers?**
A: The most common causes are insect pests (weevils, grain borers, moths), rodents, mould from inadequate drying or moisture re-entry, and poor storage hygiene (residue from previous harvests harbouring pests). Addressing drying, structure quality, and hygiene together generally reduces losses more effectively than tackling any single factor alone.

**Q: How can I control storage pests without using chemical pesticides?**
A: Hermetic storage (airtight bags/containers), thorough cleaning of storage structures between harvests, and traditional botanical methods (such as certain plant materials used as repellents in some regions) can reduce pest pressure without synthetic chemicals, though effectiveness varies. See `storage/storage_pest_management.md` for details on both non-chemical and chemical options.

**Q: Is it safe to use storage chemicals (fumigants, protectants) myself?**
A: Some storage protectants are approved and safe for smallholder use when following label instructions precisely regarding dosage, application method, and safe re-entry/consumption waiting periods, but fumigants in particular can be hazardous and are best applied by trained personnel. Always follow product label instructions exactly, and consult an extension officer if unsure which product is appropriate and safe for your situation.

**Q: I found weevils in my stored grain. What should I do?**
A: Assess the extent of infestation across the stock, since a localised infestation caught early can sometimes be managed by isolating and treating the affected portion, while widespread infestation may require broader treatment or re-drying and re-sealing the whole batch. See `storage/storage_pest_management.md` for treatment options, and consider consulting an extension officer for guidance on the safest and most effective response.

**Q: How do I keep rodents out of my grain store?**
A: Effective measures include raising storage structures off the ground with rodent guards, sealing gaps and holes, keeping the area around the store clear of vegetation and food debris, and using traps or approved rodenticides carefully where infestation persists. Rodents also pose disease and contamination risks beyond direct grain loss, so control is important for both quantity and safety reasons.

## Handling Perishables

**Q: How do I reduce post-harvest losses in fruits and vegetables, which spoil much faster than grain?**
A: Careful handling to minimise bruising and physical damage during harvest and transport, prompt cooling or shading after harvest, sorting out damaged produce before storage or sale (since damaged produce accelerates spoilage of surrounding produce), and minimising the time between harvest and sale are the main levers for perishables. See `storage/perishable_produce_handling.md`.

**Q: What is the best way to transport fresh produce to reduce losses?**
A: Using appropriate, well-ventilated containers (avoiding overfilled sacks that crush produce at the bottom), minimising handling and drop height during loading/unloading, and transporting during cooler parts of the day where possible all help reduce transport losses. Poor packaging and rough handling are among the largest controllable contributors to fresh produce loss.

**Q: Can I extend the shelf life of fresh produce without refrigeration?**
A: Options include evaporative cooling structures (such as simple charcoal coolers for vegetables), shade and ventilation to slow respiration and moisture loss, and careful sorting to remove any already-damaged or diseased produce that would otherwise accelerate spoilage of the batch. See `storage/perishable_produce_handling.md` for low-cost cooling approaches suited to smallholder conditions.

**Q: How do I know if fresh produce is still safe to sell after a delay in transport or market access?**
A: Visible signs of spoilage (soft rot, significant wilting, mould, off odours) indicate produce that should not be sold for consumption. Borderline cases should be assessed conservatively; when in doubt about food safety for produce intended for sale, it is safer to withdraw a batch than risk a buyer complaint or health issue.

## Food Safety in Storage

**Q: Is grain with some visible mould safe to sell or eat if I remove the mouldy parts?**
A: Visible mould is a warning sign of possible mycotoxin contamination (such as aflatoxin), which can be present in grain that does not show obvious mould, meaning visual sorting alone does not guarantee safety for the remaining grain. Significantly mould-affected batches should not be assumed safe based on removing visibly affected pieces; consult an agricultural extension officer or food safety authority regarding appropriate handling, testing, or disposal.

**Q: What crops are most at risk of aflatoxin contamination, and when?**
A: Maize and groundnuts are among the crops most commonly associated with aflatoxin risk, particularly when exposed to drought stress before harvest or inadequate drying and damp storage conditions afterward. Prompt, adequate drying and proper storage are the most effective preventive measures available to farmers; see `storage/grain_storage.md` and consult an extension officer if contamination is suspected in produce intended for sale or consumption.

**Q: Can I sell grain on FarmConnect that I suspect may have quality or safety issues?**
A: No — knowingly selling produce with suspected significant mould, contamination, or other safety issues is both a food safety risk to buyers and a violation of marketplace trust that can result in account penalties. Address suspected contamination through proper testing, treatment, or disposal guidance from an extension or food safety authority before offering produce for sale.

## Storage Decisions and Market Timing

**Q: Should I sell my produce right after harvest or store it and sell later?**
A: Prices for many crops are typically lower at peak harvest (when supply is high) and higher in the following lean period, so farmers with adequate safe storage capacity may benefit from selling gradually over time rather than all at once. This must be weighed against storage costs, the risk of storage losses (pests, mould) without proper storage conditions, and immediate cash flow needs. FarmConnect does not provide specific price predictions; check current listings on the live marketplace and local market information when deciding. See `pricing/pricing_principles.md`.

**Q: How do I calculate whether storing my crop is financially worthwhile compared to selling immediately?**
A: Consider the cost of storage (structure, chemicals, potential losses), the opportunity cost of cash tied up in stored produce, and the realistic likely price improvement based on typical seasonal patterns for that crop in your area, rather than assuming storage is always profitable. Where the numbers are uncertain or the decision is significant, consulting a farmer cooperative or extension advisor experienced in local market patterns can help with this assessment.
