# General Farming Frequently Asked Questions

## Seed and Planting

**Q: Should I use my own saved seed or buy certified seed each season?**
A: Certified seed generally offers more reliable germination, genetic purity, and freedom from seed-borne disease compared to farm-saved seed, particularly for hybrid crop varieties, which do not breed true from saved seed (offspring will not match the parent's performance). For open-pollinated varieties, farm-saved seed can be reasonable if the source crop was healthy and true-to-type, but germination testing before planting is recommended.

**Q: How do I decide when to plant if the rains are unpredictable?**
A: Monitor for a genuine, sustained rainfall onset rather than planting after a single early shower, which may be a false start. Consulting local seasonal forecast information where available, and considering staggered planting to spread risk, can help manage this uncertainty. See `planting_calendars/` documents for regional guidance and `weather/weather_impacts_on_farming.md` for further detail.

**Q: What is crop rotation and why does it matter?**
A: Crop rotation means changing the type of crop grown in a field from season to season, typically alternating between different plant families (e.g., cereals and legumes). It helps break pest and disease cycles specific to certain crops, balances soil nutrient use and replenishment (especially with nitrogen-fixing legumes), and can improve overall soil structure and long-term productivity.

## Soil and Fertiliser

**Q: How do I know if my soil needs fertiliser, and how much?**
A: A soil test, where available, provides the most reliable basis for fertiliser decisions, since nutrient needs vary by field history and soil type. In the absence of soil testing, crop-specific general guidelines (see `crops/` documents) provide a starting point, adjusted based on observed crop performance over time. See `soil/soil_testing_and_fertility.md` for details.

**Q: Can I rely only on manure/compost instead of buying fertiliser?**
A: Organic amendments improve long-term soil health and supply some nutrients, but generally at lower concentration and slower release than inorganic fertiliser. For high-yield-target commercial production, combining organic and inorganic sources (integrated soil fertility management) typically gives the best results. See `fertilisers/organic_fertilisers.md`.

**Q: Why does my crop look nutrient-deficient even though I applied fertiliser?**
A: Possible reasons include: incorrect fertiliser type or timing for the specific deficiency, soil pH extremes limiting nutrient availability despite adequate soil nutrient levels, waterlogging or drought limiting nutrient uptake by roots, or a different underlying issue (disease, pest damage) causing similar visual symptoms. See `troubleshooting/crop_troubleshooting_guide.md` for further diagnostic guidance.

## Pest and Disease Management

**Q: Is it better to spray pesticides regularly or only when I see a problem?**
A: Routine, calendar-based spraying regardless of actual pest presence is generally discouraged, since it wastes resources, increases the risk of pest resistance development, and harms beneficial organisms (natural pest enemies, pollinators). Regular scouting combined with threshold-based treatment (integrated pest management) is the recommended approach; see individual documents in `pests/` for crop-specific guidance.

**Q: My neighbour's field has a pest/disease outbreak — should I treat my healthy-looking crop preventively?**
A: This depends on the specific pest/disease, its spread mechanism, and current weather conditions favouring spread. For fast-spreading diseases favoured by current weather conditions (e.g., late blight during cool, wet weather), preventive fungicide application may be reasonable. For most pests, increased scouting frequency to catch early signs is generally preferable to blanket preventive chemical treatment. Consult the relevant `diseases/` or `pests/` document or an extension officer for guidance specific to the situation.

**Q: How can I tell if a crop problem is caused by a nutrient deficiency, disease, or pest?**
A: Consider the pattern (uniform across the field vs. patchy), presence of visible insects or lesions, and whether the problem is spreading over time. See `troubleshooting/crop_troubleshooting_guide.md` for a structured approach to narrowing down likely causes.

## Harvest and Storage

**Q: How do I know when my crop is ready to harvest?**
A: Each crop has specific physical maturity indicators (colour change, moisture content, texture) described in its individual document under `crops/`. Relying on these physical indicators, rather than calendar date alone, gives the most reliable harvest timing.

**Q: Why does my stored grain develop mould even though it seemed dry?**
A: Grain may not have reached the recommended safe storage moisture content (commonly 12-14% depending on the grain type), or moisture may have re-entered storage due to poor sealing, humid storage conditions, or condensation. See `storage/grain_storage.md` for detailed guidance and safe moisture thresholds.

**Q: Is it safe to eat mouldy grain if I sort out the visibly mouldy pieces?**
A: Visible mould is a strong warning sign of possible mycotoxin (e.g., aflatoxin) contamination, which can extend into grain that does not show obvious mould growth, since toxins can be present without highly visible mould in every case. Affected grain batches should not be assumed safe based on visual sorting alone; consult an agricultural extension officer or food safety authority regarding safe handling or disposal of significantly mould-affected grain.

## Market and Pricing

**Q: When is the best time to sell my crop for the best price?**
A: Prices for most crops are typically lowest at peak harvest time (when supply is highest) and higher during the pre-harvest/lean period. Farmers with safe storage capacity may benefit from selling gradually rather than all at once at harvest, but this must be weighed against storage costs, potential losses, and cash flow needs. See `marketplace/market_fundamentals.md` and `pricing/pricing_principles.md`. FarmConnect does not provide specific current price figures, since these change constantly and vary by location; consult local market information sources for current prices.

**Q: How can I get a better price for my produce?**
A: Improving product grade and quality (proper drying, sorting, clean handling), building a reliable and consistent supply relationship with buyers, accessing multiple market information sources before negotiating, and considering collective marketing through farmer cooperatives are all approaches that can support better pricing outcomes. See `marketplace/product_grading_and_quality.md` and `pricing/pricing_principles.md`.

## General Farming Practice

**Q: What is integrated pest management (IPM) in simple terms?**
A: IPM means using a combination of methods — regular field monitoring, cultural practices (crop rotation, sanitation), biological control (natural predators/parasites), and chemical control only when needed based on an established threshold — rather than relying on any single method, particularly routine chemical spraying, alone.

**Q: How often should I test my soil?**
A: Every 2-3 seasons is a reasonable general guideline, or after any major change in field management (e.g., switching crops, major fertility inputs), since soil fertility changes gradually over time with cropping and input use.

**Q: What should I do if I'm not sure what's wrong with my crop?**
A: Start with `troubleshooting/crop_troubleshooting_guide.md` to narrow down likely causes based on observed symptoms. If the problem is spreading rapidly, affecting a large area, or does not match a clear pattern, consult an agricultural extension officer or plant health specialist promptly rather than guessing at treatment.
