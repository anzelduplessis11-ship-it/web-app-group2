# Beekeeping (Apiculture) for African Smallholders

## Overview
Beekeeping is a low-land, low-water enterprise well suited to many African smallholder farmers, providing honey, beeswax, and other hive products for home use and sale, while also improving crop pollination on and around the farm. Compared with most other livestock, bees require little daily labour and no feeding under normal conditions, since they forage for themselves, but they do require correct hive management, careful handling for safety, and hygienic harvesting to produce good-quality honey. African honeybees are generally more defensive and more prone to swarming and absconding (leaving the hive) than the European honeybee strains often described in beekeeping literature from temperate countries, so management approaches adapted to local bee behaviour are important. This document covers hive types, colony management, honey harvesting and processing, and safety. General animal-health and biosecurity principles in `livestock/livestock_health_and_biosecurity.md` are written mainly with mammals and poultry in mind and apply only partially to bees; this document covers bee-specific pests, diseases, and colony health separately.

## Bees Kept in Africa
- The honeybee species kept across most of Africa is the **western honeybee (*Apis mellifera*)**, but in a number of regionally adapted subspecies (for example East African lowland and highland types, and West African types), which differ somewhat in temperament, swarming tendency, and productivity but share the same general biology.
- African honeybee populations are generally **more defensive (quicker to sting) and more prone to swarming and absconding** than the European bee strains widely described in commercial beekeeping guides from temperate regions. This affects hive siting, handling frequency, and protective equipment needs, all covered below.
- A colony consists of one queen (the only egg-laying female), a small number of male drones, and thousands of female worker bees that forage, build comb, care for brood, guard the hive, and produce honey.
- Most African beekeepers work with wild-caught or naturally occurring local bee populations that colonise hives on their own, rather than purchasing packaged bees or queens, though queen rearing and colony splitting (see below) are used by more advanced beekeepers to multiply colonies deliberately.

## Hive Types
- **Traditional/log and bark hives**: Simple hollowed logs, bark cylinders, or woven baskets hung in trees or placed on stands. They are cheap and use local materials and bee behaviour that colonies readily accept, but make inspection and clean honey harvesting difficult, and the whole comb is often destroyed at harvest, which reduces colony productivity from one season to the next.
- **Top-bar hives** (including designs sometimes called Kenya top-bar hives): A box or trough fitted with a row of movable wooden bars from which bees build comb downward. They allow individual combs to be lifted out for inspection and selective harvesting without destroying the whole nest, are relatively low-cost to build, and are widely promoted for smallholder beekeeping in Africa as a good balance between cost and manageability.
- **Frame hives** (such as Langstroth-style hives): Boxes fitted with removable wooden frames that can be reused, and often allow separate "honey supers" to be added above a brood area, keeping honeycomb separate from the brood nest. They generally give the highest yields and easiest large-scale honey extraction, but cost more to build or buy, require more precise construction, and may need purchased equipment (such as a centrifugal extractor) to get the full benefit.
- Whichever hive type is used, ensure it is well built, weatherproof, has a means of excluding rain and pests, and can be opened and inspected without excessive damage to the colony.

## Siting and Housing (Apiary Management)
- Site hives away from houses, footpaths, livestock housing, and areas where people or animals pass frequently or gather, since defensive bees can sting people or animals nearby; a barrier of vegetation, a fence, or simply distance and orientation of the hive entrance away from paths reduces this risk.
- Provide shade (natural or constructed) to prevent hives from overheating in strong sun, while allowing morning sun to encourage early foraging activity.
- Raise hives off the ground on stands and use ant-guards or grease/water barriers on the stand legs to protect against ants and other crawling pests, which are a common cause of colonies absconding.
- Ensure a reliable water source is available within easy flying distance, since bees need water to cool the hive and prepare larval food, especially in hot, dry periods.
- Space hives with enough gap between them to allow easy access for inspection and to reduce robbing behaviour (bees from one colony stealing honey from a weaker neighbouring colony).
- Keep the area around hives reasonably clear of tall grass and brush to reduce fire risk, ease access, and discourage pests, while retaining nearby flowering vegetation as a forage source.

## Colony Management
- Attract wild swarms into empty, well-prepared hives using traditional lures (such as beeswax or propolis rubbed inside the hive) placed in suitable locations, particularly during known swarming seasons in your area; alternatively, colonies can be started by splitting an existing strong colony or by purchasing a nucleus colony where available.
- Inspect hives only as needed rather than constantly, since frequent, disruptive inspection stresses African bee colonies and increases the risk of absconding or defensive stinging; many smallholder beekeepers inspect only occasionally outside of harvest, focusing checks on colony strength, presence of the queen (or eggs, which indicate a queen is present), and signs of pests or disease.
- Always use a smoker correctly before and during inspection: a few gentle puffs of cool smoke at the entrance and under the lid calms bees and reduces defensive stinging; avoid excessive smoke, which can taint honey or harm the colony.
- Recognise the difference between a strong, healthy colony (steady bee traffic at the entrance, calm but alert defence, brood comb with eggs and larvae, and stores of pollen and honey) and a weak or stressed colony (little activity, few bees, patchy or absent brood, robbing bees, or signs of pests), and take action (such as combining weak colonies, addressing pests, or providing shade or water) where a problem is identified.
- **Swarming and colony splitting**: Colonies naturally reproduce by swarming, when part of the colony leaves with the old queen to found a new nest, while a new queen takes over the original hive. Beekeepers can also deliberately split a strong colony into two by dividing brood, bees, and stores between two hives, allowing one part to raise a new queen, as a way to increase colony numbers under control rather than losing bees to uncontrolled swarming.
- **Absconding**: African bee colonies may abandon a hive entirely in response to repeated disturbance, pest problems, food shortage, poor hive conditions (heat, water leaks, ant invasion), or after repeated robbing; good hive siting, pest control, and minimal unnecessary disturbance reduce this risk.

## Feeding and Forage
- Under normal conditions, healthy colonies do not need to be fed, as they forage for nectar and pollen from surrounding flowering plants, crops, and trees.
- Ensure a reasonable diversity and abundance of flowering plants within foraging range where possible; diverse forage supports stronger colonies and more consistent honey flow across seasons.
- During periods of severe dearth (little flowering, for example in a prolonged dry season) a weak or newly established colony can sometimes be supported with a simple sugar-water solution as emergency feeding to prevent starvation, but this is a stop-gap measure, should not be relied on as the main feeding strategy, and should not be given close to a planned honey harvest, since it can dilute or contaminate honey quality.
- Avoid siting hives where nearby crops are likely to be sprayed with pesticides during flowering, since pesticide exposure can kill foraging bees or weaken the colony; coordinate with neighbouring farmers where possible (see `regulations/pesticide_safety_and_handling.md` for pesticide-safety principles relevant to protecting pollinators).

## Common Pests, Diseases, and Colony Problems
- **Ants**: A very common problem, invading hives to steal brood, honey, and stores, and a frequent cause of colonies absconding; controlled with ant-guards, grease, or water barriers on hive stands.
- **Wax moths**: Moth larvae that tunnel through and destroy comb, especially in weak colonies or stored empty comb; keeping colonies strong and storing empty comb carefully reduces damage.
- **Small hive beetle**: A beetle whose larvae can damage comb, consume brood and stores, and cause stored honey to ferment; strong colonies generally keep beetle numbers in check, so maintaining colony strength is the main defence.
- **Varroa mites**: External parasitic mites of honeybees that weaken colonies and can transmit viruses; presence and severity vary by region. If unusual colony weakness, deformed bees, or unexplained colony collapse is observed, consult a local beekeeping extension officer or experienced beekeeper, as mite management approaches are specialised and region-specific.
- **American foulbrood and other brood diseases**: Bacterial or fungal diseases of bee brood that cause patchy, discoloured, or foul-smelling brood and can seriously weaken or destroy a colony; some brood diseases are notifiable in certain countries because they can spread between apiaries on shared equipment. Suspected serious brood disease should be reported to a local beekeeping or agricultural extension officer, and affected equipment should not be shared or reused without professional guidance.
- **Robbing**: Bees from other colonies (or wasps) entering a weak hive to steal honey, which can rapidly deplete or destroy the robbed colony; reducing hive entrance size on weak colonies and avoiding spilling honey or syrup near hives helps prevent it.
- **Predators**: Birds, honey badgers, and other animals may raid hives for honey and brood; secure hive stands and siting reduce losses.

## Honey Harvesting
- Harvest only mature, capped honeycomb (comb where bees have sealed the cells with wax), which indicates the honey has been properly ripened and has low enough moisture content to store well; harvesting unripe, uncapped honey produces a product that ferments and spoils quickly.
- Harvest calmly, using smoke correctly, and work quickly and gently to minimise stress and disruption to the colony, ideally during cooler parts of the day (early morning or evening) when bees are calmer and fewer foragers are away from the hive.
- In top-bar and frame hives, remove only the honeycombs intended for harvest and leave brood comb and a reasonable portion of stores in place so the colony can continue to feed itself and recover; in traditional hives where the whole comb is typically removed, be aware this is harder on colony survival and productivity going forward.
- Avoid harvesting all stores at once, especially heading into a dearth period, as this can starve the colony; leave enough honey and pollen behind to sustain the colony until the next good forage period.

## Honey Processing and Hygiene
- Process harvested comb promptly in a clean, covered area away from dust, insects (especially other bees, which can trigger robbing), and strong odours that honey can absorb.
- **Extraction methods**: Crushing and straining (crushing capped comb and straining the honey through a clean cloth or sieve) suits top-bar and traditional-hive combs; a centrifugal extractor, which spins honey out of comb while leaving the comb largely intact for reuse, suits frame hives and increases comb reuse and future yields.
- Strain honey to remove wax fragments, bee parts, and debris, then store in clean, dry, food-safe, well-sealed containers, since honey readily absorbs moisture and odours from the environment and can ferment if too much moisture is present or containers are not sealed.
- Do not add water or other substances to harvested honey, and keep separate honey harvested at different times or from different colonies if quality or source needs to be tracked for buyers.
- Beeswax can be melted down, strained, and moulded for sale or further use once honey has been separated from the comb, providing an additional saleable product.

## Safety
- Wear appropriate protective clothing when working with bees, particularly with more defensive African bee colonies: a well-fitting veil to protect the face and neck, a full-coverage suit or overalls, gloves, and closed footwear, with all openings (cuffs, ankles, neck) sealed or tucked to prevent bees entering.
- Always carry and use a smoker correctly before opening a hive, moving calmly and avoiding sudden movements or loud noises that provoke defensive stinging.
- Work hives during cooler, calmer parts of the day where possible, and avoid opening hives in windy, stormy, or very hot conditions, when bees tend to be more defensive.
- Know and plan an escape route before starting hive work, and move away calmly (not by running and swatting, which increases stinging) if bees become aggressive; do not work alone in an isolated area when possible, in case of a serious stinging incident.
- Be aware that some people are severely allergic to bee stings and can experience a life-threatening reaction (such as difficulty breathing or swelling of the face and throat); anyone showing signs of a severe allergic reaction after stings needs urgent medical care.
- Keep bystanders, children, and animals well away from hives during inspection or harvest, and warn neighbours when working hives near shared paths or boundaries.

## Record Keeping
- Record the number and location of hives, dates of colonisation, splitting, or requeening, and the strength and condition observed at each inspection.
- Record harvest dates, quantities of honey and wax obtained per hive, and any pest or disease problems noticed, to track colony performance and identify hives or seasons that need attention.
- Keeping simple records over several seasons helps identify the best-performing hive types, sites, and management practices for your specific conditions.

## Expected Yields and Outputs
- Honey yield per hive varies widely depending on hive type, colony strength, forage availability, season, and management, and can differ greatly between a traditional hive with limited management and a well-managed top-bar or frame hive.
- Not every hive will be colonised or produce a harvestable surplus in a given season change with rainfall and flowering patterns, so beekeepers typically manage multiple hives to obtain a reliable overall harvest.
- Beeswax, propolis, and, in more developed operations, pollination services for surrounding crops are additional potential sources of value beyond honey itself.

## Common Mistakes
- Siting hives too close to houses, paths, or livestock, leading to stinging incidents with people or animals.
- Inspecting hives too often or too roughly, stressing colonies and increasing the risk of absconding or aggressive defence.
- Harvesting uncapped, immature honey, producing a product that ferments and spoils.
- Taking all the honey and stores from a hive, leaving the colony to starve, especially before a dearth period.
- Neglecting ant and pest control on hive stands, a common and preventable cause of colonies absconding.
- Working bees without proper protective clothing or without using a smoker correctly, increasing the risk of stings.
- Storing harvested honey in poorly sealed or non-food-safe containers, allowing it to absorb moisture or odours and ferment.

## Best Practices
- Choose a hive type matched to your budget, skills, and management capacity, recognising the trade-off between low cost (traditional hives) and easier management with higher yields (top-bar and frame hives).
- Site apiaries away from people and animal traffic, with shade, a nearby water source, and pest barriers on hive stands.
- Manage colonies calmly and only as often as needed, using smoke correctly and always wearing appropriate protective clothing.
- Harvest only mature, capped comb, leave adequate stores for the colony, and process and store honey hygienically in clean, sealed, food-safe containers.
- Watch for and manage common pests (ants, wax moths, small hive beetle) and seek expert advice promptly for suspected serious brood disease or unexplained colony collapse.
- Keep simple records of hive performance, harvests, and problems to improve management over successive seasons.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: My bees keep leaving the hive (absconding). What is causing this?**
A: Absconding is often linked to repeated disturbance during inspection, pest problems (especially ants), food shortage, or poor hive conditions such as heat, leaks, or overcrowding. Reduce how often you open the hive, control ants on the hive stand, ensure adequate shade and a nearby water source, and avoid harvesting all the stores at once. If absconding continues, consult a local beekeeping extension officer, as regional bee behaviour and pest pressure vary.

**Q: How do I know when honey is ready to harvest?**
A: Harvest only comb that bees have capped (sealed with wax), which shows the honey has ripened to a safe moisture level for storage. Uncapped honey has too much moisture and will ferment quickly if harvested and stored.

**Q: Is it dangerous to keep bees near my house?**
A: African honeybees are generally more defensive than the bee strains often described in general beekeeping guides, so hives should be sited a reasonable distance from houses, paths, and areas where people and animals regularly pass, with the entrance facing away from such areas where possible. Always use protective clothing and a smoker when working hives, and keep bystanders well away during inspection or harvest.

**Q: Should I feed my bees sugar water regularly?**
A: No. Healthy colonies normally forage for themselves and do not need routine feeding. Sugar-water feeding is only a stop-gap measure for a weak or newly established colony during a severe forage shortage, and should not be used as an ongoing practice or close to harvest, as it can affect honey quality.

**Q: What should I do if I notice my hive's brood looks patchy, discoloured, or has a bad smell?**
A: This can indicate a serious brood disease that may spread to other colonies on shared equipment. Do not move or share combs, hive parts, or tools from the affected hive to other hives, and contact a local beekeeping or agricultural extension officer promptly for diagnosis and advice.
