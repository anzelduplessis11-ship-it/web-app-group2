# Small-Scale Poultry Keeping (Chickens)

## Overview
Poultry keeping is one of the most accessible livestock enterprises for African smallholder farmers, requiring relatively little land and capital to start. Chickens provide eggs, meat, manure for the farm, and a source of income that can be sold in small quantities as cash is needed. Three broad production types are common at smallholder level: layers (kept mainly for egg production), broilers (fast-growing birds kept for meat), and indigenous or dual-purpose birds (hardy local breeds kept for both eggs and meat under low-input, often free-range or semi-scavenging systems). Each type has different housing, feeding, and management needs. Newcastle disease is the single most important disease threat to smallholder flocks in Africa and is the leading cause of poultry losses; its prevention through vaccination is emphasised throughout this document. For general animal-health principles, biosecurity, and safe use of veterinary medicines, see `livestock/livestock_health_and_biosecurity.md`.

## Production Types
- **Layers**: Selected egg-laying strains that begin laying at approximately 18-20 weeks and can lay productively for around 12-18 months under good management. They require consistent, balanced feed and are usually kept in more controlled housing systems.
- **Broilers**: Fast-growing meat strains that reach market weight in approximately 6-8 weeks under good feeding and management. They require high-quality feed and careful management of temperature and hygiene, and are sensitive to poor conditions.
- **Indigenous and dual-purpose birds**: Hardy local and improved dual-purpose breeds that tolerate scavenging, variable feed, and harsh conditions better than specialised commercial strains. They grow more slowly and lay fewer eggs, but survive well under low-input village conditions and are well suited to smallholder and free-range systems.

## Housing and Shelter
- Provide a dry, well-ventilated shelter that protects birds from rain, direct sun, cold night temperatures, predators, and theft.
- Ensure good airflow without direct draughts on the birds; poor ventilation leads to ammonia build-up from droppings and respiratory problems.
- Allow adequate floor space to avoid overcrowding; as a general guideline, deep-litter systems provide roughly 3-5 birds per square metre for layers and slightly more space per broiler as they grow. Confirm spacing for your specific system and breed with a local extension or animal-health worker.
- Use dry litter material (such as wood shavings, chopped straw, or rice husks) on the floor, kept clean and topped up or replaced when damp or soiled.
- Provide perches for roosting and nest boxes for layers (approximately one nest box per 4-5 hens) placed in a darker, quiet part of the house to encourage laying and reduce egg breakage.
- Site the house on well-drained ground, raised or sloped to prevent water pooling inside.
- Secure the house against predators (such as birds of prey, wild cats, dogs, snakes, and rodents) with proper walls, wire mesh, and a lockable door.

## Feeding and Nutrition
- Balanced nutrition is the largest factor determining growth, egg production, and disease resistance. Feed requirements differ by production type and age.
- **Chicks (starter phase)**: A high-protein starter feed supports early growth and immunity. Provide continuous access to feed and clean water.
- **Growers**: A grower ration with slightly lower protein supports steady development before laying or finishing.
- **Layers**: A layer ration with adequate protein, energy, and especially calcium (for strong eggshells) supports sustained egg production. Insufficient calcium leads to thin or soft shells.
- **Broilers**: High-energy, high-protein broiler feeds support rapid weight gain; broilers eat and grow quickly and must not run out of feed.
- Indigenous and scavenging birds obtain part of their diet from foraging (insects, seeds, kitchen scraps, greens), but supplementing with grain and a protein source improves growth and egg numbers, especially for confined birds or during dry seasons when scavenged feed is scarce.
- Locally available feed ingredients can reduce costs, for example maize or maize bran as an energy source (see `crops/maize.md`), grain by-products, oilseed cakes for protein, and greens for vitamins. Balance home-mixed rations carefully, as deficiencies reduce productivity.
- Provide grit (small stones) to free-range and grain-fed birds to aid digestion in the gizzard.
- Avoid sudden feed changes, mouldy or spoiled feed, and feed contaminated with aflatoxin, which can cause poisoning and reduced production. Store feed dry and use it before it spoils.

## Water
- Provide continuous access to clean, fresh drinking water at all times; water is the most essential and often most overlooked nutrient.
- Even short periods without water reduce egg production in layers and slow growth in broilers, and can be fatal in hot conditions.
- Clean drinkers daily to prevent build-up of dirt, algae, and disease organisms, and refill with fresh water.
- Position drinkers to reduce spillage onto litter, since wet litter promotes disease and parasites.
- Water needs rise significantly in hot weather; ensure supply keeps up during heat.

## Breeding
- Under natural mating, a general guideline is approximately one cock (rooster) to 8-12 hens for good fertility in indigenous and dual-purpose flocks; confirm ratios for your breed and system locally.
- Broody hens can incubate and hatch their own eggs naturally, which suits indigenous birds and low-input systems; a hen typically incubates eggs for about 21 days.
- Select breeding stock from healthy, productive birds with good growth, egg production, and disease survival, and avoid breeding from sickly or deformed birds.
- Avoid mating closely related birds over many generations, as this can reduce vigour and productivity; introducing new unrelated cockerels periodically helps maintain flock quality.
- Collect and store hatching eggs in a cool, shaded place and set them within about a week for best hatchability.

## Common Diseases and Health
- **Newcastle disease**: A highly contagious viral disease and the most important cause of poultry death in African smallholder flocks. It can wipe out most or all of an unvaccinated flock, often spreading rapidly. Signs can include sudden deaths, breathing difficulty, greenish diarrhoea, twisted necks or paralysis, drop in egg production, and dullness. There is no cure once birds are infected; prevention through vaccination is essential (see Vaccination and Biosecurity below). Suspected outbreaks should be reported to a veterinarian or local animal-health authorities.
- **Fowl pox**: A viral disease causing wart-like scabs on unfeathered skin (comb, wattles) or lesions in the mouth and throat. Spread is aided by biting insects such as mosquitoes.
- **Infectious bronchitis and other respiratory diseases**: Cause coughing, sneezing, nasal discharge, and reduced production, and are worsened by poor ventilation and ammonia.
- **Coccidiosis**: A parasitic gut disease, common in young birds on damp litter, causing diarrhoea (sometimes bloody), weakness, and death. Dry, clean litter and clean water reduce risk.
- **Internal and external parasites**: Worms, mites, lice, and fleas reduce productivity and comfort. Signs include weight loss, poor feathering, restlessness, and pale combs. Regular inspection and hygiene help control them.
- **Fowl typhoid and other bacterial diseases**: Cause illness, diarrhoea, and deaths and may require veterinary diagnosis.
- **Nutritional and management disorders**: Poor feeding, dirty water, overcrowding, and stress lower disease resistance and can mimic or worsen infectious disease.

Because many poultry diseases show similar signs (dullness, diarrhoea, drop in production, death), accurate diagnosis often requires a veterinarian or animal-health worker. See `livestock/livestock_health_and_biosecurity.md` for how to recognise a sick bird and general disease-control principles.

## Vaccination and Biosecurity
- **Newcastle disease vaccination is the single most important preventive measure for smallholder poultry.** Vaccinate according to a schedule appropriate for your area, including regular booster doses, because immunity does not last indefinitely and unvaccinated new birds are vulnerable. Heat-stable Newcastle disease vaccine formulations suited to village conditions exist and are widely promoted for smallholder flocks; confirm the appropriate vaccine, dosing, and schedule with a qualified veterinarian or animal-health worker.
- Other vaccinations (for example against fowl pox and other diseases) may be recommended depending on local disease pressure. This knowledge base does not name specific commercial vaccine products; consult a qualified veterinarian or animal-health worker to confirm which vaccines, at what ages and intervals, are appropriate for your flock and locality.
- Vaccines must be stored and handled correctly (many require cool storage, or the "cold chain") to remain effective; a vaccine that has overheated may not protect the birds.
- **Biosecurity** reduces the chance of disease entering and spreading:
  - Limit visitors and other people's birds from mixing with your flock.
  - Quarantine newly bought or returning birds separately for a period before adding them to the main flock, as new birds are a common way disease enters.
  - Keep housing, feeders, and drinkers clean, and control rodents and wild birds where possible.
  - Do not bring apparently healthy birds from a market with a known disease outbreak into your flock.
  - Dispose of dead birds safely (for example by deep burial or burning as advised locally) rather than leaving carcasses where other birds, animals, or people can contact them.
- See `livestock/livestock_health_and_biosecurity.md` for detailed biosecurity and vaccination-schedule principles.

## Common Mistakes
- Not vaccinating against Newcastle disease, or vaccinating once and never giving booster doses, leaving the flock exposed to devastating outbreaks.
- Adding newly purchased birds directly to the flock without quarantine, introducing disease.
- Overcrowding birds, which increases stress, disease spread, and cannibalism (feather and vent pecking).
- Allowing litter and drinkers to become wet and dirty, encouraging coccidiosis, worms, and respiratory disease.
- Feeding an unbalanced or mouldy ration, or letting birds run out of feed or water, reducing growth and egg production.
- Ignoring early signs of illness until many birds are affected, rather than isolating sick birds and seeking advice promptly.

## Best Practices
- Prioritise a regular Newcastle disease vaccination programme, including boosters, as the foundation of a healthy flock.
- Match the production type (layer, broiler, or indigenous/dual-purpose) to your resources, market, and management capacity; indigenous and dual-purpose birds are often the most resilient choice for low-input systems.
- Provide clean, dry housing with adequate space, ventilation, perches, and nest boxes.
- Feed a balanced ration appropriate to age and purpose, and always provide clean water.
- Practise good biosecurity: quarantine new birds, limit outside contact, keep facilities clean, and dispose of dead birds safely.
- Keep simple records of vaccinations, deaths, egg production, and costs to guide management decisions.
- Observe the flock daily so that sick birds and problems are noticed early.

## When to Consult a Veterinarian
- When several birds fall sick or die suddenly, or deaths increase over a short period, which may signal a contagious outbreak such as Newcastle disease that should also be reported to local animal-health authorities.
- When you see nervous signs (twisted necks, paralysis, tremors), severe breathing difficulty, or greenish diarrhoea spreading through the flock.
- To design an appropriate vaccination schedule for your area and to confirm which vaccines and any medicines are suitable and locally available.
- Before using any veterinary medicine, to confirm correct use and any withdrawal period before eggs or meat are consumed or sold (see `livestock/livestock_health_and_biosecurity.md`).
- When a disease problem is unclear, recurring, or not responding to basic management improvements.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: My birds are dying suddenly and quickly, some with twisted necks. What could it be?**
A: These signs are consistent with Newcastle disease, a highly contagious viral disease and the most common cause of sudden poultry deaths in African flocks, though other diseases can cause similar signs. There is no cure once birds are infected. Isolate the flock to limit spread, avoid moving birds, and contact a veterinarian or local animal-health authorities promptly, as outbreaks should be reported. For the future, a regular Newcastle disease vaccination programme with boosters is the key preventive measure.

**Q: How often do I need to vaccinate my chickens against Newcastle disease?**
A: Vaccination must be repeated with booster doses because protection does not last indefinitely, and any new birds also need to be vaccinated. The exact schedule and vaccine depend on your area and the products available locally. Confirm the appropriate schedule with a qualified veterinarian or animal-health worker.

**Q: Should I keep indigenous birds or improved commercial breeds?**
A: This depends on your resources and goals. Indigenous and dual-purpose birds are hardier, survive well under scavenging and low-input conditions, and need less feed, but grow slowly and lay fewer eggs. Specialised layers and broilers are far more productive but require better feed, housing, and management and are less forgiving of poor conditions. Many smallholders do well with hardy dual-purpose birds or a mix suited to their situation.

**Q: My layers are producing thin or soft-shelled eggs. Why?**
A: This is commonly linked to insufficient calcium in the diet, though age, heat stress, and some diseases can also contribute. Ensure layers receive a proper layer ration with adequate calcium and always have clean water. If the problem persists or spreads with other signs of illness, consult an animal-health worker.

**Q: Can I add chickens I bought at the market straight into my flock?**
A: This is risky, because new birds can carry disease that spreads to your existing flock. Keep newly bought birds separate (quarantined) for a period and watch them for signs of illness before mixing them in, and avoid buying birds from markets with known disease outbreaks. See `livestock/livestock_health_and_biosecurity.md` for biosecurity guidance.
