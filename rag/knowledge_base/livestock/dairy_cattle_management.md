# Dairy Cattle Management for Smallholders

## Overview
Dairy cattle keeping provides smallholder farmers with a regular income from milk sales, a valuable source of household nutrition, manure for crop fields, and offspring that can be sold or kept for herd growth. Unlike beef or dual-purpose cattle, dairy production depends heavily on consistent, high-quality feeding, careful breeding management, and strict hygiene at milking, because milk yield and milk safety respond quickly to small changes in management. Dairy cattle also share the general ruminant husbandry principles described in `livestock/goats_sheep_and_cattle.md` (feeding, grazing, water, and common diseases), and general animal-health and biosecurity principles in `livestock/livestock_health_and_biosecurity.md`. This document focuses on what is specific to dairy production: suitable dairy breeds and crosses, housing for milking herds, feeding for milk production, milking technique and milk hygiene, breeding for a productive herd, and the health problems most closely tied to dairying, including mastitis (see `livestock/mastitis_in_dairy_cattle.md`).

## Suitable Breeds for Africa
- **Exotic dairy breeds** such as Holstein-Friesian, Jersey, Ayrshire, and Guernsey are capable of high milk yields but require good-quality feed, reliable water, disease control, and closer management. They are generally less heat-tolerant and less resistant to local diseases and ticks than indigenous cattle, and perform poorly under low-input conditions.
- **Indigenous breeds** (various local zebu and other native cattle types) are well adapted to heat, poor-quality forage, and local disease and tick challenge, but produce relatively little milk beyond what a calf needs.
- **Crossbred dairy cattle** (crosses of exotic dairy breeds with indigenous cattle) are widely kept by smallholders because they combine reasonable milk yield with better heat tolerance, disease resistance, and ability to cope with variable feed compared with purebred exotics. The proportion of exotic blood can be matched to how well feed, water, and management can support the animal.
- Choose the breed or cross according to the feed, water, housing, and management you can realistically provide; a highly productive purebred animal kept on poor feed and in poor housing will underperform, fall sick more easily, and may cost more than it earns.

## Housing Requirements
- Provide a clean, dry, well-ventilated shelter that protects cattle from rain, strong sun, cold nights, and wind, with enough space to lie down comfortably without overcrowding.
- Many smallholder dairy herds are kept under **zero-grazing or semi-zero-grazing systems**, where animals are housed and fed cut fodder rather than grazed, especially where land is limited. This makes better use of small plots, reduces disease and parasite exposure, and allows careful collection of manure and urine for use on crop fields.
- Provide a separate, clean, dry area or stall for milking that is easy to clean and kept apart from manure and mud, since a dirty milking area is one of the biggest causes of contaminated milk and mastitis.
- Provide separate housing or pens for calves, for sick animals, and for cows close to calving, since mixing these groups with the main herd increases disease risk and stress.
- Keep floors well drained and clean bedding or flooring regularly; damp, dirty conditions promote foot problems, udder infections, and fly-borne disease.
- Provide shade in the resting/loafing area, as heat stress reduces feed intake and milk yield, particularly in exotic and high-grade crossbred cattle.

## Feeding and Nutrition
- Milk production places heavy demands on energy, protein, minerals, and water; underfeeding is the most common reason smallholder dairy cattle fail to reach their genetic potential for milk yield.
- **Forage base**: Good-quality forage (cultivated fodder grasses such as Napier/elephant grass, natural pasture, crop residues, and conserved fodder such as hay or silage where feasible) should form the bulk of the diet. See `livestock/livestock_nutrition_and_feeding.md` for general feed types and ration-balancing principles, and `livestock/goats_sheep_and_cattle.md` for grazing and fodder management shared with other ruminants.
- **Protein and energy supplementation**: Lactating cows, especially higher-yielding crossbred and exotic animals, usually need concentrate feed (such as dairy meal, cereal brans, and protein-rich supplements) in addition to forage to sustain milk yield, particularly in early lactation when milk output is highest. Introduce concentrates gradually to avoid digestive upset.
- **Minerals**: Lactating cows have high calcium and phosphorus needs to support milk production and avoid depleting body reserves; provide a suitable mineral supplement or lick alongside forage and concentrate feed, as deficiencies contribute to poor fertility, weak bones, and metabolic problems around calving.
- **Fodder conservation**: Plan for dry-season feed shortages by conserving fodder as hay or silage and by growing drought-tolerant fodder species, since a drop in feed quality or quantity causes an immediate and sometimes lasting drop in milk yield.
- **Water**: Lactating dairy cows need large volumes of clean water daily, since milk is mostly water; even short-term water restriction sharply reduces milk yield. Ensure constant access to clean water, especially in hot weather and during peak lactation.
- Avoid sudden feed changes, mouldy or spoiled feed, and excessive grain without adaptation, which can cause digestive upset, acidosis, or reduced milk fat.

## Milking and Milk Hygiene
- Milk twice daily at consistent times where possible, as regular milking intervals support steady yield and udder health; once-daily milking generally reduces total yield.
- Follow a clean, consistent milking routine:
  - Wash hands thoroughly with soap and water before milking, and keep fingernails short and clean.
  - Wash and dry the udder and teats with clean water before milking; do not milk into a dirty or wet environment.
  - Discard the first few streams of milk from each teat into a separate strip cup (not onto the floor or into the milk pail), and check for clots, blood, or watery milk, which are early signs of mastitis.
  - Milk quickly, calmly, and completely, as incomplete milking (leaving milk in the udder) increases the risk of mastitis and reduces future yield.
  - Dip or spray teats with a suitable teat disinfectant immediately after milking where available, as the teat canal remains open for a period after milking and is vulnerable to infection; confirm suitable products with an animal-health worker.
  - Milk healthy animals first and any cow with signs of mastitis or other infection last, using separate equipment or thoroughly cleaning equipment between animals, to avoid spreading infection.
- Use clean milking containers and equipment made of materials that can be properly washed (avoid containers that cannot be cleaned well), and wash and dry all milking equipment after every milking session.
- **Cool milk promptly** after milking and keep it in a clean, covered container away from dust, flies, and direct sun; milk left warm quickly develops high bacterial counts and spoils faster, reducing its value and safety.
- Do not sell or consume milk from a cow being treated with antibiotics or other medicines until the correct withdrawal period has passed, as residues can remain in milk and pose a food-safety risk; see `livestock/livestock_health_and_biosecurity.md` for withdrawal-period principles and confirm exact periods with a veterinarian.
- Do not use or sell milk from a cow with visibly abnormal milk (clots, blood, foul smell, watery appearance), as this indicates infection or illness.

## Breeding and Reproduction
- Heifers should reach adequate body weight and development, generally around 15 months of age for many dairy breeds and crosses under good feeding, before being bred, so that they calve down at an appropriate age and body size; breeding an underweight, poorly grown heifer too early leads to difficult calving and stunted lifetime production. Confirm target breeding weights and ages for your specific breed and conditions with a local extension or animal-health worker.
- Gestation length in cattle is approximately nine months (about 283 days).
- A well-managed dairy cow should ideally calve down roughly once a year (a calving interval of around 12-13 months); longer intervals reduce lifetime milk production and profitability.
- Provide a **dry period** of about 6-8 weeks before the next expected calving, during which the cow is not milked, allowing the udder tissue to rest and recover before the next lactation; failing to dry off a cow properly reduces the yield and health of the following lactation.
- Detect heat (oestrus) signs (restlessness, mounting behaviour, mucus discharge, reduced feed intake) promptly and breed at the correct time, whether by natural mating with a suitable bull or by artificial insemination where available; confirm timing and available breeding services locally.
- Select or source breeding bulls or semen from healthy, well-performing stock, and avoid repeated mating of closely related animals, which reduces vigour and productivity over generations.
- Provide extra care and good nutrition to cows in late pregnancy and around calving, ensure a clean, quiet calving area, and make sure the calf receives colostrum (the first milk) within the first few hours of life, as it provides essential early immunity that the calf cannot get any other way.
- Watch for signs of difficult birth (prolonged, unproductive straining, a visible but not progressing calf) and seek veterinary help promptly, as delayed assistance can cost the life of the calf, the cow, or both.

## Health Management
- Follow the general principles in `livestock/livestock_health_and_biosecurity.md`: observe animals daily, isolate sick animals, quarantine new stock, follow a vaccination programme suited to local disease risks, and use veterinary medicines correctly, always observing milk withdrawal periods.
- Pay particular attention to udder health, since mastitis is one of the most common and costly problems in dairy herds; check udders and milk at every milking (see `livestock/mastitis_in_dairy_cattle.md`).
- Watch for lameness, which reduces feed intake, movement to feed and water, and milk yield; check housing surfaces, hoof condition, and nutrition if lameness becomes common in the herd.
- Manage tick-borne diseases and internal parasites as described in `livestock/goats_sheep_and_cattle.md` and `livestock/tick_borne_diseases_in_livestock.md`, since exotic and high-grade dairy cattle are often more susceptible to tick-borne diseases than indigenous cattle.
- Be alert for signs of foot-and-mouth disease (fever, drooling, mouth and foot blisters, lameness), a highly contagious, notifiable disease affecting cattle among other cloven-hoofed animals; see `livestock/foot_and_mouth_disease.md` and report suspected cases to a veterinarian or animal-health authority immediately.
- Watch for signs of metabolic problems around calving, such as a cow unable to stand shortly after calving (which can indicate milk fever, a calcium-deficiency condition), and seek veterinary help promptly, as these conditions can be rapidly fatal without treatment.

## Common Diseases
- **Mastitis**: Udder infection causing abnormal milk, reduced yield, and sometimes visible udder swelling and illness; see `livestock/mastitis_in_dairy_cattle.md` for a detailed discussion.
- **Foot-and-mouth disease**: A highly contagious viral disease causing fever, mouth and foot blisters, drooling, and lameness; see `livestock/foot_and_mouth_disease.md`.
- **Tick-borne diseases** (including East Coast fever, babesiosis, anaplasmosis, and heartwater): Serious, often rapidly fatal diseases transmitted by ticks, causing fever, anaemia, weakness, and sometimes nervous signs; see `livestock/tick_borne_diseases_in_livestock.md`.
- **Internal parasites (worms) and liver fluke**: Reduce growth, body condition, and milk yield, especially in young and grazing animals; managed through grazing management and strategic deworming, as described in `livestock/goats_sheep_and_cattle.md`.
- **Milk fever and other metabolic disorders around calving**: Related to mineral imbalances at the start of lactation; prevention relies on correct mineral nutrition, particularly in late pregnancy.
- **Retained placenta and reproductive infections**: Can follow difficult calving or poor hygiene at calving, reducing future fertility if untreated.

## Record Keeping
- Record each cow's calving dates, breeding dates, and expected next calving, to plan dry periods and monitor calving intervals.
- Record daily or periodic milk yields per cow to identify underperforming animals, detect early drops in production (an early sign of illness or poor nutrition), and guide culling and breeding decisions.
- Record treatments given, including dates and any withdrawal periods, so milk is not sold or consumed while a withdrawal period is in effect.
- Record vaccination dates and due dates for boosters, and any cases of mastitis or other illness, to track recurring problems and inform culling decisions for chronically affected cows.

## Expected Yields and Outputs
- Indigenous cattle typically produce only enough milk for their own calf, often a litre or two per day beyond calf needs.
- Crossbred dairy cattle under smallholder management commonly produce a moderate daily yield, considerably more than indigenous cattle, depending on the level of exotic blood, feeding, and management.
- Purebred exotic dairy breeds under good feeding, housing, and health management can produce substantially higher daily yields, but require correspondingly higher inputs and closer management, and perform poorly if feed, water, or health care fall short.
- Actual yields vary widely with breed, individual animal, stage of lactation, feeding, health, and management; use your own farm records rather than general figures to judge and improve performance over time.

## Common Mistakes
- Choosing a highly productive exotic breed without being able to provide the feed, water, housing, and management it needs, leading to poor yields, poor health, and financial loss.
- Milking with dirty hands, dirty equipment, or a dirty udder, and failing to check foremilk, leading to contaminated milk and undetected mastitis.
- Failing to give cows an adequate dry period before calving, reducing the next lactation's yield and the cow's health.
- Underfeeding energy, protein, or minerals during peak lactation or late pregnancy, leading to poor yields, poor fertility, or metabolic disease around calving.
- Selling or consuming milk from a cow under treatment without observing the withdrawal period, or selling milk with obvious signs of infection.
- Delaying veterinary help for a difficult calving, a cow unable to stand after calving, or suspected foot-and-mouth disease.
- Not keeping records, making it hard to notice declining yields, missed dry periods, or recurring health problems early.

## Best Practices
- Match breed or cross to your actual feed, water, and management capacity, and improve inputs gradually as capacity grows.
- Provide consistent, adequate feeding year-round, including a dry-season fodder reserve, appropriate concentrate supplementation for lactating cows, and mineral access.
- Follow a clean, consistent milking routine at regular intervals, checking foremilk and cooling milk promptly to protect both cow health and milk quality.
- Manage breeding for a calving interval of around a year, including a proper dry period and good calving-time care with prompt colostrum feeding for calves.
- Follow a vaccination, deworming, and tick-control programme suited to local disease risk, designed with a veterinarian or animal-health worker.
- Observe cows daily, act quickly on early signs of mastitis, lameness, or illness, and always observe medicine withdrawal periods before selling or consuming milk.
- Keep simple records of yields, breeding, treatments, and vaccinations to guide day-to-day and long-term herd decisions.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Why is my crossbred dairy cow producing much less milk than I expected?**
A: Low yield is most often caused by underfeeding (insufficient energy, protein, or minerals), inadequate water, poor milking routine, subclinical mastitis, or an inadequate dry period before the current lactation. Check feeding and water first, examine milk and udders for signs of mastitis, and review your records for the length of the last dry period. If yield remains low after addressing these, consult a veterinarian or animal-health worker.

**Q: How long should I dry off my cow before she calves again?**
A: A dry period of about 6-8 weeks is generally recommended to let the udder rest and recover before the next lactation, supporting a healthier and higher-yielding next lactation. Confirm the best approach for your specific cow and system with an animal-health worker.

**Q: Is it safe to sell milk from a cow that was recently treated with medicine?**
A: Not until the withdrawal period for that specific medicine has passed. Selling or consuming milk during the withdrawal period can leave harmful residues in the milk. Always confirm and record the correct withdrawal period with a veterinarian when a cow is treated, and keep that milk separate until it is safe.

**Q: My cow's milk has clots in it and the udder feels warm and swollen. What should I do?**
A: These are signs consistent with mastitis, an udder infection. Do not sell or use this milk, isolate the affected quarter or animal from routine milking order (milk her last), and contact a veterinarian or animal-health worker for diagnosis and appropriate treatment. See `livestock/mastitis_in_dairy_cattle.md` for more detail.

**Q: Should I keep a purebred exotic dairy breed or a crossbred cow?**
A: This depends on the feed, water, housing, and management you can reliably provide. Crossbred cattle generally cope better with variable feed, heat, and local disease pressure while still giving a useful milk yield, making them a common and practical choice for smallholders. Purebred exotic breeds can produce much more milk but need consistently good feeding, housing, and health care to do so and perform poorly if these are lacking.
