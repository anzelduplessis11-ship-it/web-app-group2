# Livestock Health and Biosecurity

## Overview
Keeping livestock healthy is central to a productive and profitable smallholder livestock enterprise. Prevention (good feeding, clean housing, biosecurity, and vaccination) is almost always cheaper and more effective than treating disease after it strikes. This document sets out general animal-health principles that apply across poultry, small ruminants, cattle, and other farm animals: how to recognise a sick animal, how to keep disease out through biosecurity, how vaccination schedules work in principle, how to use veterinary medicines safely (including withdrawal periods), how to protect people from diseases that pass from animals to humans (zoonoses), and when to consult a veterinarian or animal-health worker. For species-specific guidance, see `livestock/poultry_keeping.md` and `livestock/goats_sheep_and_cattle.md`.

## Signs of a Healthy Animal
Knowing what is normal makes it easier to spot problems early. A healthy animal typically shows:
- Alertness, normal movement, and normal interaction with the group.
- A good appetite and normal drinking.
- A smooth coat or clean feathers, bright eyes, and clean nose, eyes, and rear.
- Normal breathing at rest, normal droppings/dung, and normal urination.
- Steady body condition (neither wasting away nor, for productive animals, sudden loss of condition).
- For productive animals: steady milk yield, egg production, or growth appropriate to age and feeding.

## Signs of a Sick Animal
Watch the herd or flock daily and look for changes from normal. Common warning signs include:
- Dullness, separation from the group, weakness, or reluctance to move or stand.
- Loss of appetite or reduced drinking, and a sudden drop in milk, egg production, or growth.
- Fever (an unusually hot animal) or, in some cases, unusually cold ears and extremities.
- Discharge from the eyes, nose, or mouth; drooling; or coughing, sneezing, or laboured breathing.
- Diarrhoea (sometimes bloody or discoloured), constipation, straining, or bloating.
- Pale or yellowish gums, eyelids, or membranes (a sign of anaemia or other serious disease).
- Lameness, swellings, sores, blisters, or wounds; skin problems, hair/feather loss, or intense scratching.
- Nervous signs such as trembling, circling, twisted neck, paralysis, or convulsions.
- Difficulty giving birth, retained afterbirth, or abnormal discharge.
- Sudden death, or several animals sick or dying over a short period.

Because many different diseases share the same general signs (dullness, fever, loss of appetite, diarrhoea, weight loss, death), the signs alone often cannot confirm a diagnosis. When signs are severe, spreading, or unclear, consult a veterinarian or animal-health worker. Sudden deaths or several animals affected at once may indicate a contagious outbreak or poisoning and should be treated as urgent.

## First Response When an Animal Falls Sick
- **Isolate** the sick animal from the healthy group where practical, to limit the spread of contagious disease. Handle or feed sick animals last, and wash your hands and clean equipment afterwards.
- **Provide comfort**: ensure the animal has clean water, appropriate feed, shade or shelter, and rest.
- **Observe and record** the signs, when they started, how many animals are affected, and any recent changes (new animals, new feed, weather, movements), which helps a veterinarian diagnose the problem.
- **Do not guess with medicines.** Avoid giving leftover or borrowed medicines without knowing the cause, as the wrong treatment can waste money, harm the animal, delay proper care, and contribute to drug resistance.
- **Seek advice promptly** when signs are serious, spreading, or unclear, rather than waiting until many animals are affected.
- **Handle carcasses safely**: dispose of dead animals by deep burial or burning, or as advised locally, rather than leaving them where other animals, people, or scavengers can contact them; do not consume or sell animals that died of unknown disease.

## Biosecurity
Biosecurity means the everyday practices that keep disease from entering your farm and from spreading within it. It is one of the most cost-effective ways to protect livestock.

**Keeping disease out:**
- **Quarantine new animals.** Keep newly bought or returning animals separate from the main herd or flock for a period, and watch them for signs of illness before mixing them in. Introduced animals are one of the most common ways disease enters a farm.
- **Buy carefully.** Source animals from healthy, reputable sources, and avoid buying from markets or areas with known disease outbreaks.
- **Control visitors and movement.** Limit contact between your animals and other people's animals, vehicles, and equipment, which can carry disease.
- **Control pests and wildlife** such as rodents and, for poultry, wild birds, where possible.

**Stopping disease spreading within the farm:**
- **Isolate sick animals** and handle them last.
- **Keep housing, feeders, and drinkers clean**, and keep bedding/litter dry; clean and, where appropriate, disinfect equipment and housing.
- **Practise hygiene**: wash hands and clean footwear and tools after handling sick animals or visiting other farms or markets.
- **Separate age groups** where possible, as young animals are more vulnerable to disease.
- **Dispose of dead animals and waste safely.**

## Vaccination Schedules (General Concept)
- Vaccination prepares an animal's immune system to resist specific diseases before it is exposed, and is one of the most important preventive tools, especially against contagious diseases that have no cure once an animal is infected (for example Newcastle disease in poultry and PPR in small ruminants).
- **Schedules matter.** Vaccines are given at particular ages and repeated as booster doses because protection builds up and then fades over time; a single dose is often not enough for lasting protection. New and young animals entering the herd or flock also need to be vaccinated.
- **The right schedule is local.** Which diseases to vaccinate against, and at what ages and intervals, depend on the diseases present in your area, the species, and the production system. A veterinarian or animal-health worker should design the schedule for your situation.
- **Keep the cold chain.** Many vaccines must be kept cool (refrigerated) from storage to use; a vaccine that has become too warm may not work, leaving animals unprotected even though they were "vaccinated."
- **Handle and give vaccines correctly**, following professional guidance on dose, route, and timing, and using clean equipment.
- **Keep simple records** of what was given, to which animals, and when, so booster doses are not missed.
- This knowledge base does not name specific commercial vaccine products; confirm which vaccines, schedules, and products are appropriate and locally available with a qualified veterinarian or animal-health worker.

## Safe Use of Veterinary Medicines and Withdrawal Periods
- **Use medicines only when needed and correctly.** Give the right medicine for the right condition, ideally based on a proper diagnosis, at the correct dose for the animal's weight, and for the full course advised. Guessing, underdosing, or stopping early can fail to cure the animal and promote drug resistance.
- **Follow professional and label guidance** on how much to give, how, and for how long. Store medicines correctly (cool and dry as directed) and do not use medicines that are expired or have been stored badly.
- **Antimicrobial resistance:** Misusing antibiotics (for example using them unnecessarily, at the wrong dose, or without a diagnosis) contributes to bacteria becoming resistant, which is a serious problem for both animal and human health. Use antibiotics responsibly and only under appropriate guidance.
- **Withdrawal periods are critical for food safety.** After treating an animal with certain medicines, there is a withdrawal period, a set time that must pass before its milk, eggs, or meat is safe to consume or sell. Consuming or selling products during the withdrawal period can leave harmful medicine residues in food. Always confirm and observe the correct withdrawal period for any medicine used in food-producing animals.
- **Confirm products and periods locally.** This knowledge base does not recommend specific commercial medicine products by brand. Confirm suitable, locally approved products, correct dosing, and the exact withdrawal periods with a qualified veterinarian or animal-health worker, and keep records of treatments.
- **Deworming and parasite treatments** follow the same principles: correct dosing, strategic timing, and attention to any withdrawal period. See `livestock/goats_sheep_and_cattle.md` for parasite-control guidance in ruminants.

## Zoonosis Awareness (Diseases Passing from Animals to People)
- Some animal diseases (zoonoses) can spread to people, for example through handling sick animals or carcasses, contact with birth fluids or manure, bites, contaminated milk or meat, or contaminated environments. Farmers, family members, and consumers can be affected.
- **Reduce risk with basic precautions:**
  - Wash hands with soap and water after handling animals, manure, carcasses, birth fluids, or animal products.
  - Wear protective covering (such as gloves) where practical when handling sick animals, assisting births, or handling carcasses.
  - Consume milk and meat only after appropriate treatment; boiling or proper cooking reduces the risk from many milk- and meat-borne diseases. Avoid consuming products from animals that are sick or died of unknown causes.
  - Keep living areas separate from animal housing where possible, and control pests.
  - Keep any wounds covered and seek medical care for animal bites or wounds contaminated by animals.
- **Some diseases affect both animals and people and can be severe.** If people who have been in contact with sick animals or animal products develop illness (for example fever, or severe symptoms), they should seek medical care and mention the animal contact. Suspected serious animal disease outbreaks should be reported to animal-health authorities, who also help protect public health.

## Record Keeping
- Keep simple records of animal numbers, births, deaths, illnesses and their signs, treatments (including dates and any withdrawal periods), vaccinations and booster due-dates, and production (milk, eggs, growth).
- Records help you spot problems early, avoid missing booster vaccinations, observe withdrawal periods before selling products, and give a veterinarian useful information for diagnosis.

## Common Mistakes
- Noticing sick animals late because the herd or flock is not observed regularly, allowing disease to spread before action is taken.
- Adding new animals to the herd or flock without quarantine, introducing disease.
- Vaccinating once and skipping booster doses, or letting vaccines get too warm, so animals are not actually protected.
- Using leftover, borrowed, or wrong medicines without a diagnosis, or underdosing, which wastes money, harms animals, and promotes drug resistance.
- Ignoring withdrawal periods and selling or consuming milk, eggs, or meat with harmful medicine residues.
- Handling sick animals or carcasses without hygiene precautions, risking spread of disease to other animals and to people.
- Not reporting suspected outbreaks of serious contagious diseases to animal-health authorities.

## Best Practices
- Observe animals daily and know what is normal, so illness is caught early.
- Prevent disease first: good feeding, clean and dry housing, clean water, and low stress build natural resistance.
- Practise biosecurity: quarantine new animals, isolate sick ones, buy from healthy sources, and keep facilities clean.
- Follow a vaccination schedule designed with an animal-health worker, keep the cold chain, and do not miss boosters.
- Use veterinary medicines responsibly, at correct doses and full courses, and always observe withdrawal periods.
- Protect people from zoonoses with hygiene, protective covering, and safe handling of animal products.
- Keep simple health and treatment records, and report suspected serious outbreaks to authorities.
- Seek professional advice promptly for severe, spreading, or unclear health problems.

## When to Consult a Veterinarian or Animal-Health Worker
- When an animal shows severe or worsening signs (for example severe breathing difficulty, inability to stand, severe bleeding, difficult birth, convulsions, or severe bloat), which are emergencies.
- When several animals fall sick or die over a short period, or there are sudden deaths, which may indicate a contagious outbreak or poisoning.
- When signs suggest a highly contagious or notifiable disease (for example blisters and lameness suggesting foot-and-mouth disease, or sudden flock deaths suggesting Newcastle disease); such suspected outbreaks should also be reported to animal-health authorities.
- To design vaccination, deworming, and parasite-control programmes suited to your area, and to confirm suitable, locally available products.
- Before using any veterinary medicine, to confirm correct diagnosis, dose, and the withdrawal period before milk, eggs, or meat is consumed or sold.
- When a disease problem is unclear, recurring, or not improving with basic management changes, or when you are unsure how to proceed.
- When people in contact with sick animals or animal products develop illness (they should also seek medical care and mention the animal contact).

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: How do I know if an animal is getting sick before it becomes serious?**
A: Observe your animals daily and learn what is normal for them. Early warning signs include dullness, separating from the group, reduced appetite or drinking, a drop in milk or egg production, changed droppings, discharge from the eyes or nose, and abnormal breathing. Catching these early, isolating the animal, and seeking advice when signs are serious or spreading gives the best chance of a good outcome.

**Q: I have leftover medicine from a previous illness. Can I use it on another sick animal?**
A: This is risky and generally not advisable. Without knowing the actual cause, the medicine may be wrong for the condition, and using it at the wrong dose can harm the animal, waste money, delay proper treatment, and contribute to drug resistance. It is better to confirm the problem and the correct treatment with a veterinarian or animal-health worker, and to check any withdrawal period before the animal's products are consumed or sold.

**Q: What is a withdrawal period and why does it matter?**
A: A withdrawal period is the time that must pass after treating an animal with certain medicines before its milk, eggs, or meat is safe for people to consume or sell. Ignoring it can leave harmful medicine residues in food. Always confirm and observe the correct withdrawal period for any medicine you use in food-producing animals, and keep records so you know when products are safe.

**Q: Why do vaccines need to be repeated with booster doses?**
A: Protection from a vaccine builds up and then fades over time, so a single dose is often not enough for lasting protection, and new or young animals entering the herd also need vaccinating. Repeating doses on the correct schedule keeps immunity strong. The right schedule depends on your area and species, so confirm it with a veterinarian or animal-health worker, and keep vaccines cool until they are used.

**Q: Can I catch diseases from my animals?**
A: Yes, some animal diseases (zoonoses) can spread to people through handling sick animals, carcasses, birth fluids, or manure, or through contaminated milk or meat. Reduce the risk by washing hands after handling animals, wearing protective covering when handling sick animals or assisting births, cooking meat well and treating milk (for example boiling) before consuming it, and avoiding products from sick animals. If someone who has been in contact with animals becomes ill, they should seek medical care and mention the animal contact.

**Q: When is an animal-health problem an emergency that needs a vet right away?**
A: Treat it as urgent when an animal cannot stand, has severe breathing difficulty, severe bleeding, convulsions, a difficult birth, or severe bloating, and when several animals are suddenly sick or dying. Signs suggesting a highly contagious or notifiable disease also warrant urgent professional advice and, where relevant, reporting to animal-health authorities.
