# Goats, Sheep, and Cattle for Smallholders

## Overview
Goats, sheep (together called small ruminants), and cattle are important livestock for African smallholder farmers, providing milk, meat, income, manure for crop fields, draught power (in the case of cattle), and a form of savings and security that can be sold when cash is needed. Small ruminants are often kept by households with limited land and capital because they are cheaper to buy, reproduce quickly, and are easier to manage than cattle. Cattle require more feed, water, and space but provide larger quantities of milk, meat, manure, and traction. All three are ruminants, meaning they digest fibrous plant material such as grass and crop residues through a specialised stomach system, so their feeding, grazing, and health management share many principles. This document covers feeding, grazing and fodder, water, common diseases, deworming, and when to call a veterinarian. For general animal-health principles, biosecurity, and safe use of veterinary medicines, see `livestock/livestock_health_and_biosecurity.md`.

## Choosing and Keeping Small Ruminants and Cattle
- **Goats** are hardy browsers that eat a wide range of shrubs, leaves, and grasses, tolerate dry conditions well, and are well suited to marginal areas. They reproduce quickly, often producing multiple kids.
- **Sheep** are mainly grazers preferring grasses, and generally do better in areas with adequate pasture; some breeds are adapted to drier zones.
- **Cattle** require more feed, water, and space, and grow and reproduce more slowly, but provide larger volumes of milk and meat, valuable manure, and draught power for ploughing and transport.
- Choose breeds and types adapted to local climate, disease pressure, and available feed. Indigenous and locally adapted breeds are often more disease-tolerant and better suited to low-input conditions than high-yielding exotic breeds, which need better feeding and management.

## Housing and Shelter
- Provide a simple, dry, well-ventilated shelter that protects animals from rain, cold nights, strong sun, and predators, and keeps them secure from theft.
- Keep the floor dry and clean; damp, dirty conditions promote foot problems, parasites, and disease.
- Allow enough space to avoid overcrowding, which increases stress and disease spread; provide separate space or pens for sick animals and for females about to give birth.
- Raise or slope the site for good drainage, and clean out manure regularly (manure can be composted for use on crop fields).
- House young animals warmly and protect them from damp and draughts, as the young are more vulnerable.

## Feeding and Nutrition
- Ruminants rely mainly on forage: grass, browse (leaves and shrubs), crop residues, and cultivated fodder. Good nutrition is the foundation of growth, milk production, fertility, and disease resistance.
- **Roughage/forage** forms the bulk of the diet: grazed pasture, browse, cut-and-carry grasses, and crop residues such as maize stover and legume haulms (see `crops/maize.md` and `crops/common_beans.md`).
- **Protein-rich fodder**, especially fodder legumes and tree/shrub leaves, improves growth and milk yield and is valuable in the dry season when grass quality is low.
- **Energy supplements** such as grain, bran, and by-products can be added for productive animals (for example lactating or fattening animals), particularly when forage is scarce, but should be introduced gradually to avoid digestive upset.
- **Minerals and salt**: Provide access to a mineral/salt source, as ruminants have important mineral needs (for example for bone, milk, and fertility) that pasture alone may not fully meet.
- Plan for the dry season, when natural feed becomes scarce and poor in quality. Conserving fodder (for example as hay or by storing crop residues) and establishing fodder plants help avoid the seasonal weight loss and deaths that occur when animals run short of feed.
- Avoid sudden diet changes, mouldy feed, and overfeeding of grain, all of which can cause serious digestive disorders such as bloat or acidosis.
- Introduce animals to fresh, lush pasture or large amounts of legumes gradually, as sudden access can cause bloat.

## Grazing and Fodder
- **Grazing management** protects both the animals and the land. Overgrazing reduces pasture productivity, promotes soil erosion, and increases the build-up of parasite larvae on heavily used pasture.
- Rotating animals between grazing areas, where land allows, helps pasture recover and reduces parasite burdens compared with continuous grazing of the same ground.
- **Cut-and-carry (zero-grazing or semi-zero-grazing)** systems, where fodder is harvested and brought to housed animals, suit areas with limited land and can reduce disease and parasite exposure while making better use of manure. Dairy cattle in densely populated highland areas are often kept this way.
- **Establishing fodder** (improved grasses and fodder legumes, and fodder trees/shrubs) provides higher-quality feed and a dry-season reserve; fodder legumes also improve soil fertility.
- Avoid grazing very early in the morning on dew-wet, lush legume-rich pasture without care, as this can raise bloat risk.
- Combine crop and livestock activities where possible: crop residues feed animals, and manure improves crop fields (see `sustainability/sustainable_farming_practices.md`).

## Water
- Provide access to clean, adequate drinking water daily; water needs are substantial, especially for cattle, lactating animals, and in hot weather.
- Insufficient or dirty water reduces feed intake, growth, and milk production, and can cause illness.
- Water sources shared by many animals can spread disease and parasites; keep watering points clean where possible and avoid stagnant, contaminated water.
- Cattle generally require considerably more water than small ruminants; plan water supply for the dry season, when shortages are most likely.

## Breeding and Reproduction
- Small ruminants reach breeding age and reproduce faster than cattle, and goats and some sheep often produce twins, allowing flocks to grow quickly.
- Select breeding males and females from healthy, productive, well-grown animals, and avoid breeding from sickly or deformed stock.
- Avoid mating closely related animals over many generations, which can reduce vigour and productivity; introducing new unrelated breeding males periodically helps maintain quality.
- Provide good nutrition to breeding females before and during pregnancy and while nursing, as poor condition reduces fertility, birth weights, and milk.
- Give birthing females a clean, quiet, sheltered place, and ensure newborns receive the first milk (colostrum) soon after birth, as it provides essential early protection against disease.
- Keep breeding males in good condition and, for cattle, consider locally available breeding services where appropriate; confirm options with an animal-health or extension worker.

## Common Diseases and Health
Ruminant diseases in Africa include highly contagious viral diseases, tick-borne diseases, internal parasites, and other conditions. Many require prompt veterinary attention and, for certain diseases, reporting to animal-health authorities.

- **Peste des petits ruminants (PPR)**: A highly contagious and often fatal viral disease of goats and sheep. Signs can include fever, discharge from the eyes and nose, mouth sores, difficult breathing, and diarrhoea, often with high death rates. There is no cure once animals are infected; prevention is through vaccination and biosecurity, and suspected outbreaks should be reported to a veterinarian or animal-health authorities.
- **Foot-and-mouth disease**: A highly contagious viral disease affecting cattle, sheep, goats, and other cloven-hoofed animals. Signs can include fever, blisters/sores in the mouth and on the feet, drooling, lameness, and reduced feeding and production. It spreads very easily. It is a notifiable disease in many countries and suspected cases must be reported to animal-health authorities; movement of animals is often restricted during outbreaks.
- **Tick-borne diseases**: Ticks transmit several serious cattle diseases (and some affecting small ruminants) that are major causes of livestock loss in Africa, including diseases affecting the blood and organs that can cause fever, weakness, anaemia, and death. Signs vary but often include fever, dullness, loss of appetite, and sometimes swellings, pale or yellowish membranes, or nervous signs. Tick control and, where appropriate, vaccination and prompt treatment are important; consult a veterinarian for diagnosis and treatment, as these diseases can be rapidly fatal.
- **Internal parasites (worms) and liver fluke**: Very common causes of poor growth, weight loss, diarrhoea, anaemia (pale membranes), swelling under the jaw ("bottle jaw"), and death, especially in young animals and on wet or overgrazed pasture. Managed through grazing management and strategic deworming (see Deworming below).
- **External parasites**: Ticks, mites, lice, and fleas cause irritation, skin disease, weight loss, and can transmit disease; regular inspection and control are important.
- **Contagious and respiratory diseases**: Various bacterial and viral diseases (including contagious diseases of the lungs and other conditions) cause coughing, breathing difficulty, fever, and deaths, and may require veterinary diagnosis and treatment.
- **Digestive disorders**: Bloat (gas build-up in the rumen, which can be rapidly fatal), acidosis from overfeeding grain, and poisoning from certain plants or mouldy feed are management-related emergencies.
- **Mastitis**: Infection of the udder in milking animals, causing swelling, pain, and abnormal milk; important in dairy animals and requires prompt attention.

Because many diseases share signs such as fever, dullness, loss of appetite, diarrhoea, or weight loss, accurate diagnosis often requires a veterinarian or animal-health worker. See `livestock/livestock_health_and_biosecurity.md` for recognising a sick animal and general control principles.

## Deworming and Parasite Control
- Internal parasites (worms) are one of the most common and costly health problems in grazing ruminants, especially in young animals and during and after the rainy season when pasture larvae are high.
- Signs of a heavy worm burden include weight loss, poor growth, rough coat, diarrhoea, pale gums or eyelids (anaemia), and swelling under the jaw.
- Deworm strategically rather than blindly: timing deworming to high-risk periods (such as around the rainy season and before it, or at key production stages) is generally more effective than random treatment. A veterinarian or animal-health worker can advise on appropriate timing and products for your area.
- Combine deworming with grazing management (rotation, avoiding overgrazing, and not grazing young animals on the most contaminated pasture) to reduce parasite pressure and slow the development of resistance to deworming medicines.
- Overusing or underdosing deworming medicines can lead to resistant worms that no longer respond to treatment; use the correct dose for the animal's weight and follow professional guidance.
- Control external parasites (especially ticks) through appropriate methods for your area, as tick control also reduces tick-borne diseases. Confirm suitable products and methods with an animal-health worker.
- This knowledge base does not name specific commercial deworming or tick-control products; confirm locally available and approved products, correct dosing, and any withdrawal periods before milk or meat is consumed or sold with a qualified veterinarian or animal-health worker (see `livestock/livestock_health_and_biosecurity.md`).

## Vaccination and Biosecurity
- Vaccination against key diseases (such as PPR in small ruminants, and other locally important diseases including certain cattle diseases) is an important preventive measure. The appropriate vaccines and schedule depend on the diseases present in your area; confirm these with a qualified veterinarian or animal-health worker.
- Practise biosecurity to keep disease from entering and spreading:
  - Quarantine newly bought or returning animals separately for a period before mixing them with the herd or flock, as introduced animals are a common source of disease.
  - Avoid buying animals from markets or areas with known disease outbreaks.
  - Isolate sick animals from healthy ones and handle them last.
  - Keep housing and watering points clean, and dispose of dead animals safely as advised locally.
- See `livestock/livestock_health_and_biosecurity.md` for detailed biosecurity and vaccination principles.

## Common Mistakes
- Failing to plan for the dry season, so animals lose weight or die when grass runs short instead of relying on conserved fodder and crop residues.
- Overgrazing pasture, which reduces feed, promotes erosion, and increases parasite burdens.
- Neglecting parasite control, or deworming randomly and incorrectly, leading to poor growth and drug-resistant worms.
- Ignoring tick control, allowing tick-borne diseases that can be rapidly fatal, especially in cattle.
- Adding newly bought animals directly to the herd without quarantine, introducing disease.
- Suddenly changing the diet or overfeeding grain, causing digestive disorders such as bloat and acidosis.
- Delaying veterinary help when animals show signs of a serious or spreading disease, allowing losses to grow.

## Best Practices
- Match breeds and animal numbers to your available feed, water, land, and management capacity; locally adapted breeds are often the most resilient choice.
- Provide good year-round nutrition, including planning and conserving fodder for the dry season, plus mineral/salt access and clean water.
- Practise sound grazing management (rotation where possible, avoiding overgrazing) and consider cut-and-carry systems where land is limited.
- Follow a strategic parasite-control and vaccination programme designed with an animal-health worker for your area's disease pressure.
- Practise biosecurity: quarantine new animals, isolate sick ones, and avoid animals from outbreak areas.
- Provide clean housing, care for breeding females and newborns (including colostrum), and use manure to benefit crop fields.
- Keep simple records of births, deaths, treatments, vaccinations, and production to guide decisions.
- Observe animals regularly so illness and problems are noticed and acted on early.

## When to Consult a Veterinarian
- When one or more animals show signs of a serious or highly contagious disease, such as fever with mouth or foot sores, blisters, drooling and lameness (possible foot-and-mouth disease), or fever with eye/nose discharge, mouth sores, and diarrhoea in goats and sheep (possible PPR). Suspected outbreaks of these diseases should also be reported to animal-health authorities, as some are notifiable and movement may be restricted.
- When several animals fall sick or die over a short period, suggesting a contagious disease.
- For tick-borne diseases or any illness with fever, severe weakness, anaemia (pale membranes), or nervous signs, which can be rapidly fatal and need prompt diagnosis and treatment.
- For an animal that is severely bloated, unable to stand, having difficulty giving birth, or in obvious distress, which are emergencies.
- To design appropriate deworming, tick-control, and vaccination programmes and to confirm suitable, locally available products, correct dosing, and any milk/meat withdrawal periods.
- When a health problem is unclear, recurring, or not improving with basic management changes.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: My goats and sheep have fever, runny eyes and noses, mouth sores, and diarrhoea, and some have died. What could it be?**
A: These signs are consistent with peste des petits ruminants (PPR), a highly contagious and often fatal viral disease of goats and sheep, though other diseases can cause similar signs. There is no cure once animals are infected. Isolate affected animals, avoid moving stock, and contact a veterinarian or animal-health authorities promptly, as suspected outbreaks should be reported. Vaccination and biosecurity are the main ways to prevent it in the future.

**Q: My cattle have blisters in the mouth and on the feet, are drooling, and have gone lame. What should I do?**
A: These signs are consistent with foot-and-mouth disease, a highly contagious viral disease of cloven-hoofed animals. It is a notifiable disease in many countries. Avoid moving animals, keep them separate from other herds, and report the suspected case to a veterinarian or animal-health authorities immediately, as movement controls and official response are usually required.

**Q: How often should I deworm my animals?**
A: Rather than deworming at fixed random intervals, it is generally more effective to time deworming to high-risk periods (such as around the rainy season) and key production stages, combined with good grazing management. The best timing and products depend on your area and animals. Confirm an appropriate deworming plan and suitable products with a veterinarian or animal-health worker, and always use the correct dose for the animal's weight.

**Q: My animals lose weight and some die every dry season. How can I prevent this?**
A: This is usually caused by feed and water shortages as pasture dries up. Plan ahead by conserving fodder (for example hay and stored crop residues such as maize stover and legume haulms), establishing fodder plants for a dry-season reserve, providing mineral/salt and adequate clean water, and reducing overgrazing. Managing parasites also helps animals hold condition through the dry period.

**Q: One of my cattle is suddenly very bloated on the left side and distressed. Is this urgent?**
A: Yes. Sudden severe bloat (gas build-up in the rumen) can be rapidly fatal and is an emergency. It can follow sudden access to lush pasture or overfeeding of grain. Contact a veterinarian or animal-health worker urgently. To prevent it, introduce lush pasture and grain gradually and avoid sudden diet changes.
