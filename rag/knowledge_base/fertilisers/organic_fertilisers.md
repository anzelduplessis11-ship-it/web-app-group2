# Organic Fertilisers and Soil Amendments

## Overview
Organic fertilisers improve soil fertility through the decomposition of plant or animal-derived materials, contributing both nutrients and long-term improvements to soil structure, water retention, and microbial activity. They are a central component of sustainable, integrated soil fertility management.

## Types of Organic Fertilisers

### Farmyard Manure
- Composed of animal dung, urine, and bedding material, decomposed for several weeks to months before use.
- Nutrient content varies significantly depending on animal type, feed quality, and how the manure was stored (well-covered manure retains more nitrogen than manure left exposed to rain and sun).
- Typical application rate: 5-10 tonnes/ha for field crops, incorporated into soil 2-4 weeks before planting to allow partial decomposition.
- Fresh, undecomposed manure can burn young plants and may introduce weed seeds or pathogens; well-composted manure is safer and more effective.

### Compost
- Produced by controlled decomposition of plant residues, kitchen waste, and manure under managed moisture and aeration conditions.
- Well-made compost is generally more stable and weed-seed-free than raw manure due to heat generated during decomposition.
- Typical application rate: 5-15 tonnes/ha depending on crop and soil condition.
- Improves soil structure, water-holding capacity, and supports beneficial soil microorganisms in addition to supplying nutrients.

### Green Manure and Cover Crops
- Fast-growing plants (often legumes) grown specifically to be incorporated into the soil while still green, adding organic matter and, in the case of legumes, fixing atmospheric nitrogen.
- Common green manure crops include various legume cover crops suited to local climate; consult local extension guidance for regionally appropriate species.
- Typically grown for 6-10 weeks then incorporated into soil before flowering/seed set, at least 3-4 weeks before the main crop is planted to allow decomposition.

### Crop Residues
- Leaving or incorporating crop residues (stalks, leaves, husks) after harvest returns organic matter and some nutrients to the soil.
- Residues with a high carbon-to-nitrogen ratio (e.g., mature cereal stalks) can temporarily tie up soil nitrogen during decomposition; balancing with nitrogen-rich residues or fertiliser can offset this effect.

### Biochar
- Charcoal-like material produced by heating organic material in a low-oxygen environment (pyrolysis).
- Improves soil structure, water retention, and can improve nutrient retention, particularly on sandy or degraded soils.
- Best used in combination with organic or inorganic nutrient sources, since biochar itself supplies few readily available nutrients.

## Advantages of Organic Fertilisers
- Build long-term soil organic matter, improving water retention and reducing erosion risk.
- Support beneficial soil microbial and fungal communities that aid nutrient cycling.
- Often locally available at lower direct cost, particularly for farmers with livestock.
- Reduce dependence on purchased inorganic inputs.

## Limitations of Organic Fertilisers
- Nutrient content is variable and generally lower per unit weight than inorganic fertilisers, requiring larger volumes to supply the same nutrient amount.
- Nutrient release is slower, which may not match rapid-uptake periods of fast-growing crops without supplemental inorganic fertiliser.
- Bulky material requires more labour and transport compared to concentrated inorganic fertilisers.
- Poorly composted material can introduce weed seeds, pathogens, or excess salts.

## Integrated Soil Fertility Management
- Combining organic amendments (for long-term soil health) with targeted inorganic fertiliser (for precise, rapid nutrient correction) generally produces better results than either approach alone.
- Organic matter improves the efficiency of applied inorganic fertiliser by improving soil structure and microbial activity, meaning combined approaches can sometimes reduce the total inorganic fertiliser needed for a given yield target.

## Application Best Practices
- Apply organic materials well before planting (2-4 weeks minimum) to allow initial decomposition and reduce risk of nutrient tie-up or plant damage.
- Incorporate into the soil rather than leaving on the surface, to reduce nutrient loss and speed decomposition.
- Test or estimate nutrient content of organic sources where possible, since application rates based on nitrogen content alone can under- or over-supply other nutrients.

## Common Mistakes
- Applying fresh, undecomposed manure directly to young plants, risking root and stem burn.
- Assuming organic fertilisers alone can fully replace inorganic fertiliser for high-yield-target commercial production without adjusting application volumes accordingly.
- Storing manure or compost uncovered, leading to significant nutrient loss (particularly nitrogen) before application.

## When to Consult an Agricultural Extension Officer
- To access soil and manure/compost nutrient testing services where available, for more precise application rate planning.
- When designing a green manure or cover crop rotation suited to local climate and cropping system.
- If integrating organic and inorganic fertiliser strategies for a commercial-scale operation, to optimize both cost and yield outcomes.
