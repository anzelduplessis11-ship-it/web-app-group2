# Fertiliser Basics

## Overview
Fertilisers supply essential plant nutrients that may be insufficient in the soil for optimal crop growth. Effective fertiliser use requires understanding nutrient roles, timing, application methods, and matching application to actual crop and soil needs rather than fixed formulas alone.

## Primary Macronutrients

### Nitrogen (N)
- Role: drives vegetative growth, leaf development, and overall plant vigour; a component of chlorophyll and proteins.
- Deficiency symptoms: yellowing (chlorosis) starting from older, lower leaves, stunted growth, pale green colour overall.
- Excess symptoms: excessive vegetative growth, delayed maturity, increased susceptibility to lodging and some diseases, reduced flowering/fruiting in some crops.
- Common sources: urea, ammonium nitrate, ammonium sulfate, and compound NPK fertilisers.
- Nitrogen is highly mobile in the soil and prone to leaching (especially in sandy soils and high rainfall) and volatilisation losses, so split applications through the season are generally more efficient than a single large dose.

### Phosphorus (P)
- Role: essential for root development, energy transfer within the plant, and flowering/fruiting.
- Deficiency symptoms: stunted growth, dark green or purplish leaf discolouration (especially in maize and some other crops), delayed maturity.
- Common sources: single superphosphate, triple superphosphate, diammonium phosphate (DAP), and compound NPK fertilisers.
- Phosphorus is relatively immobile in soil, so it is typically applied at or before planting, close to where roots will establish (basal application).

### Potassium (K)
- Role: regulates water balance within the plant, supports disease resistance, and is important for fruit/grain quality and root crop bulking.
- Deficiency symptoms: yellowing and browning (scorching) along leaf margins, weak stems, poor fruit/grain fill.
- Common sources: muriate of potash (potassium chloride), sulfate of potash, and compound NPK fertilisers.
- Particularly important for root and tuber crops (e.g., cassava, potato) and fruiting crops (e.g., tomato).

## Secondary Nutrients and Micronutrients
- **Calcium**: important for cell wall structure; deficiency contributes to disorders such as blossom end rot in tomato.
- **Magnesium**: central component of chlorophyll; deficiency causes interveinal yellowing on older leaves.
- **Sulfur**: needed for protein synthesis; deficiency symptoms resemble nitrogen deficiency but typically appear first on younger leaves.
- **Zinc**: important in many cereal crops, especially rice; deficiency causes stunted growth and bronzing of leaves.
- **Boron**: important for pollination and fruit/seed set; deficiency can cause poor grain fill or fruit deformities.
- Micronutrient deficiencies are more likely on heavily cropped soils, sandy soils, or soils with unusual pH extremes.

## Organic vs. Inorganic Fertilisers
| Aspect | Organic Fertilisers (manure, compost) | Inorganic Fertilisers (mineral/synthetic) |
|---|---|---|
| Nutrient release | Slow, depends on decomposition | Fast, immediately plant-available |
| Soil structure benefit | Improves organic matter, water retention, microbial activity | Minimal direct soil structure benefit |
| Nutrient content precision | Variable, depends on source material | Precise, labelled nutrient content |
| Cost and availability | Often lower cost if farm-produced; bulky to transport | Can be costly; requires cash purchase |
| Best use | Long-term soil health building, combined with inorganic for balanced nutrition | Precise correction of specific nutrient deficiencies |

Combining organic and inorganic fertiliser sources (integrated soil fertility management) generally gives better long-term results than relying on either alone.

## Application Methods
- **Basal application**: fertiliser incorporated into the soil at or before planting, typically supplying phosphorus and a portion of nitrogen and potassium.
- **Topdressing**: fertiliser (usually nitrogen) applied to the soil surface near the plant during the growing season, timed to key growth stages.
- **Banding**: fertiliser placed in a band near but not touching the seed/seedling row, reducing the risk of seedling scorch while keeping nutrients accessible to developing roots.
- **Foliar application**: nutrient solutions sprayed directly onto leaves, useful for rapid correction of certain micronutrient deficiencies but not a substitute for adequate soil fertility.

## Timing Principles
- Apply phosphorus-containing basal fertiliser at or before planting, since phosphorus needs to be near developing roots and does not move readily through soil.
- Split nitrogen applications across 2-3 timings matched to crop growth stages (e.g., at planting, at vegetative growth, and at flowering/reproductive stage) to reduce losses and match nutrient uptake patterns.
- Apply potassium in line with crop demand, often increasing proportion during flowering and fruit/grain filling stages.

## Matching Fertiliser Rates to Soil and Crop Needs
- Soil testing, where available, provides the most reliable basis for fertiliser recommendations, since nutrient needs vary significantly with existing soil fertility.
- In the absence of soil testing, crop-specific general guidelines (see individual crop documents in `crops/`) can be used as a starting point, adjusted based on observed crop performance and local extension advice.
- Over-application wastes money, can harm the environment (e.g., nitrogen runoff into water bodies), and can damage crops (fertiliser burn), while under-application limits yield potential.

## Common Mistakes
- Applying all fertiliser at planting instead of splitting nitrogen applications, leading to nutrient losses and reduced efficiency.
- Placing fertiliser in direct contact with seed, causing germination damage ("fertiliser burn").
- Using a single fixed fertiliser rate across all fields regardless of soil fertility differences.
- Ignoring micronutrient deficiencies that limit yield even when N, P, and K are adequate.

## When to Consult an Agricultural Extension Officer
- Before making significant fertiliser investment decisions on an unfamiliar field, to access soil testing services if available.
- If crop symptoms suggest a nutrient deficiency but do not respond to standard corrective fertiliser application, since other factors (soil pH, waterlogging, disease) may be limiting nutrient uptake.
- To confirm currently available and appropriately priced fertiliser products in the local market.
