# Biofertilisers and Biochar

## Overview
Biofertilisers (living microbial inoculants) and biochar (a stable carbon material) are two distinct but complementary tools for building soil fertility without relying solely on mineral fertiliser. Biofertilisers work by introducing or boosting beneficial microorganisms that help plants access nutrients already present in the soil or air, while biochar works mainly as a soil conditioner that improves the physical and chemical environment for roots and microbes. Both fit within an integrated soil fertility management approach alongside organic and inorganic fertilisers; see `fertilisers/organic_fertilisers.md` and `fertilisers/fertiliser_basics.md` for related context.

## Part 1: Biofertilisers

### What Biofertilisers Are
Biofertilisers are products containing live microorganisms — bacteria, fungi, or a combination — that, when applied to seed, soil, or roots, establish a relationship with the plant that improves its nutrient supply. Unlike conventional fertilisers, biofertilisers do not supply nutrients directly in large quantities; instead, they enhance the plant's own ability to capture nutrients from the soil, air, or organic matter.

### Rhizobium Inoculants

#### How They Work
Rhizobium bacteria form a symbiotic relationship with legume roots, colonising root nodules where they convert atmospheric nitrogen (N2) into a form the plant can use (biological nitrogen fixation), in exchange for carbohydrates from the plant. This can substantially reduce or, on well-nodulated fields, eliminate the need for nitrogen fertiliser on legume crops.

#### Crops That Respond
- Common beans, soybean, groundnut, cowpea, green gram, chickpea, and other grain and forage legumes.
- Different legume species generally require different rhizobium strains; a strain effective on soybean will not necessarily nodulate beans or groundnut, so product selection must match the crop.

#### Application Method
- Rhizobium inoculant is typically supplied as a powder, liquid, or gel-based carrier and applied to seed shortly before planting (seed inoculation), following the product's specific instructions.
- Inoculated seed should generally be planted the same day and kept out of direct sunlight and away from contact with fresh fertiliser or agrochemicals, which can kill the live bacteria before planting.
- Seed treated with certain fungicides may need a compatible inoculant formulation or careful sequencing, since some seed treatments are toxic to rhizobium; check product compatibility.

#### When Inoculation Helps Most
- Fields where the target legume has not been grown in several years (rhizobium populations naturally specific to that legume may have declined).
- New land or fields with a history of poor legume nodulation and pale, stunted legume growth despite adequate other conditions.
- Soils with very low organic matter or that have been heavily cropped without legume rotation.
- Inoculation is less critical, though still often beneficial, on fields with a strong recent history of successfully nodulating the same legume crop.

#### Limitations
- Rhizobium inoculants are living organisms and are sensitive to heat, direct sunlight, and expired storage; check expiry dates and store as instructed (often cool and out of sunlight).
- Very acidic soils, waterlogging, and severe nutrient deficiencies (particularly phosphorus, which nodules need) can all reduce nodulation success even with a good inoculant.
- Inoculation supplies nitrogen but not phosphorus, potassium, or other nutrients the legume crop still requires.

### Mycorrhizal Fungi

#### How They Work
Mycorrhizal fungi form a symbiotic association with plant roots, extending a network of fine fungal threads (hyphae) into the soil far beyond the reach of the root system itself. This effectively expands the plant's root absorption area, improving uptake of phosphorus in particular, along with some micronutrients (zinc, copper) and, to a lesser extent, water.

#### Crops That Respond
- Most field and horticultural crops form mycorrhizal associations, including maize, cassava, many vegetables, and fruit trees.
- Some plant families (notably brassicas such as cabbage and kale, and some others) do not form typical mycorrhizal associations and will not benefit from inoculation.

#### Application Method
- Commercial mycorrhizal inoculants are applied at or before planting, commonly as a seed coating, root dip for transplants, or granular application placed near the root zone/seed.
- Because the fungi need to physically contact and colonise young roots, placement close to the root zone at planting is more effective than surface broadcasting after the crop is established.

#### When Mycorrhizal Inoculation Helps Most
- Soils with low native phosphorus availability, particularly where soil testing shows phosphorus is limiting.
- Degraded, eroded, or heavily disturbed soils where natural mycorrhizal populations may be reduced.
- Transplanted vegetable and tree crops, where early root establishment strongly affects later performance.

#### Limitations
- High rates of soluble phosphorus fertiliser can suppress mycorrhizal colonisation, since the plant has less incentive to support the fungal relationship when phosphorus is already freely available; inoculation and heavy phosphorus fertilisation are generally not combined.
- Fungicide seed treatments and some soil fumigants can reduce inoculant survival; check compatibility.
- Benefits are more consistent on phosphorus-poor soils and less noticeable where soil phosphorus is already adequate.

### Other Biofertiliser Types (Brief Overview)
- **Phosphate-solubilising bacteria/fungi**: help convert soil phosphorus that is chemically locked up into forms plants can absorb; most useful on soils with high total phosphorus but low available phosphorus.
- **Azospirillum and other free-living nitrogen-fixing bacteria**: associate loosely with cereal and grass roots and can contribute modest amounts of fixed nitrogen, though generally less than legume-rhizobium symbiosis.
- **Plant growth-promoting rhizobacteria (PGPR)**: a broader category of beneficial root-zone bacteria that can improve nutrient availability, root development, and in some cases resistance to certain soil pathogens.

### General Biofertiliser Best Practices
- Buy from a reliable supplier and check the expiry date; live organisms lose viability over time, especially under poor storage (heat, direct sun).
- Match the specific product/strain to the target crop; a generic "biofertiliser" label without species/strain information may not be effective for a given legume or crop.
- Do not mix biofertiliser directly with chemical fertiliser, strong fungicides, or fresh manure at application unless the product label specifically confirms compatibility.
- Biofertilisers complement but do not fully replace balanced soil fertility management; phosphorus, potassium, and micronutrient needs generally still require other inputs.

## Part 2: Biochar

### What Biochar Is
Biochar is a stable, carbon-rich material produced by heating organic material (wood, crop residues, husks, manure) in a low-oxygen environment, a process called pyrolysis. Unlike ordinary ash or partially burned residue, biochar retains a porous structure and is resistant to further decomposition, allowing it to persist in soil for a long time (years to decades, depending on soil conditions).

### How Biochar Improves Soil Fertility
- **Improved water retention**: the porous structure holds water and makes it more available to plant roots, particularly valuable on sandy or degraded soils with poor natural water-holding capacity.
- **Improved nutrient retention (cation exchange capacity)**: biochar's surface chemistry can bind and hold onto positively charged nutrients (such as potassium, calcium, magnesium, and ammonium-nitrogen), reducing leaching losses and making them more available to roots over time.
- **Habitat for beneficial soil microorganisms**: the porous structure provides physical shelter for beneficial bacteria and fungi, which can support nutrient cycling and root health.
- **Long-term soil carbon building**: because biochar resists decomposition, it contributes to stable, long-lasting soil organic carbon rather than the short-term boost provided by fresh organic matter.
- **Possible liming effect**: many biochars are mildly to moderately alkaline and can contribute a small pH-raising effect on acidic soils, though this varies by feedstock and production method.

### What Biochar Does Not Do
- Biochar itself supplies very few directly available plant nutrients; it should not be treated as a fertiliser replacement.
- Applying biochar alone to a nutrient-poor soil, without additional organic or inorganic fertiliser, generally does not produce a yield benefit and can in some cases temporarily reduce nutrient availability (particularly nitrogen) as the biochar's surfaces bind nutrients before releasing them.
- Fresh, poorly made biochar (incompletely pyrolysed, retaining unstable tar-like compounds) can be less beneficial and, in some cases, mildly harmful to seedlings; well-made, fully charred biochar is preferred.

### Combining Biochar with Nutrient Sources ("Charged" Biochar)
- Biochar is most effective when combined with a nutrient source before or during application — commonly by mixing it with compost, manure, or liquid fertiliser and allowing it to absorb nutrients ("charging") before soil application.
- Charging biochar with nutrient-rich material before use in the field generally gives faster, more reliable benefits than applying uncharged biochar and adding nutrients separately afterward.

### Application Guidance
- Biochar is typically incorporated into the topsoil rather than left on the surface, to maximise contact with roots and reduce loss to wind.
- Application rates vary widely by soil type and production scale; general field trials have used rates from roughly 0.5-5 tonnes/ha for annual crops, but exact rates should be guided by local trial results or extension advice given cost and labour involved in production and transport.
- Benefits are generally more pronounced on degraded, sandy, or acidic soils with low natural fertility than on already fertile, well-structured soils.
- Biochar production requires care to avoid excessive smoke and safety hazards; simple pit or drum kiln methods are commonly used at smallholder scale, but should be done away from dwellings and dry, flammable vegetation.

### Limitations and Considerations
- Producing biochar requires biomass that might otherwise be used for other purposes (fuel, animal bedding, mulch); the trade-off should be considered at farm level.
- Benefits typically build over multiple seasons rather than appearing immediately in the first year of application.
- Quality varies significantly depending on feedstock and production temperature/method; poorly produced material may deliver fewer benefits than well-made biochar.
- As with any soil amendment, biochar's effect depends on existing soil conditions — it is not a universal fix for poor fertility and works best as part of a broader integrated soil fertility management plan.

## Combining Biofertilisers and Biochar
- Biochar's porous structure can serve as a favourable habitat for the same beneficial microorganisms introduced through biofertilisers, and some research suggests combining biochar with microbial inoculants can improve inoculant survival in soil, though results vary by product and site.
- Both approaches work best as complements to, not replacements for, a balanced overall fertility programme that includes organic matter and, where needed, mineral fertiliser to correct specific nutrient shortfalls.

## Common Mistakes
- Treating biofertiliser inoculants as a fertiliser that supplies large amounts of nutrients directly, rather than a tool that improves the plant's own nutrient capture.
- Using an expired or improperly stored rhizobium/mycorrhizal inoculant, resulting in poor or no live organism survival at planting.
- Mixing biofertiliser directly with chemical fertiliser or strong seed treatment chemicals without checking compatibility, killing the beneficial organisms before they can establish.
- Applying uncharged, "raw" biochar to nutrient-poor soil and expecting an immediate yield boost without pairing it with compost, manure, or fertiliser.
- Assuming all crops benefit equally from mycorrhizal inoculation, when some plant families (e.g., brassicas) do not form the same symbiotic relationship.

## When to Consult an Agricultural Extension Officer
- To identify the correct rhizobium strain or mycorrhizal product suited to a specific legume or crop and confirm local product availability.
- Before large-scale biochar production, for guidance on safe, efficient production methods and appropriate application rates for local soil conditions.
- If nodulation or biofertiliser establishment appears poor despite correct application, since soil acidity, phosphorus deficiency, or waterlogging may be limiting factors.
- To access any locally available trial data on biochar or biofertiliser performance for the specific crops and soils in the area.
