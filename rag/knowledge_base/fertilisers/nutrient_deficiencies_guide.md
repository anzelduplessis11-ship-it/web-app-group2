# Nutrient Deficiencies Guide: Visual Diagnosis

## Overview
Crops that lack essential nutrients show characteristic symptoms in their leaves, stems, and overall growth. Learning to recognise these visual signs helps farmers respond to fertility problems, but visual diagnosis alone is not conclusive. Many deficiency symptoms overlap with each other and with symptoms of drought, waterlogging, pests, and disease. A soil test (see `soil/soil_testing_and_fertility.md`) is the most reliable way to confirm which nutrients are actually limiting. For general fertiliser guidance, see `fertilisers/fertiliser_basics.md`.

## How to Read Deficiency Symptoms
- **Where symptoms appear first matters.** Mobile nutrients (nitrogen, phosphorus, potassium, magnesium) can move within the plant from old leaves to new growth, so deficiency symptoms typically appear first on older, lower leaves. Immobile nutrients (calcium, sulfur, zinc, iron, boron) cannot move as easily, so their deficiency symptoms typically appear first on younger, upper leaves and growing points.
- **Pattern matters.** Note whether yellowing (chlorosis) is uniform across the leaf, between the veins (interveinal), or along the margins, and whether tissue is dying (necrosis) or the leaf is simply pale.
- **Look at the whole plant and the field.** Poor growth in patches may reflect soil variation, drainage, or compaction rather than a whole-field nutrient shortage.

## Primary (Macronutrient) Deficiencies

### Nitrogen (N)
- Typically causes uniform yellowing of older, lower leaves first, later progressing up the plant; overall growth is stunted and pale.
- Common in heavily cropped soils, sandy soils, and after heavy rainfall leaches nitrogen from the root zone.
- Nitrogen is highly mobile in both soil and plant, so deficiency can appear and change quickly.

### Phosphorus (P)
- Often causes stunted growth with darker green foliage; older leaves may develop a reddish or purplish tint, particularly on stems and leaf undersides.
- Roots and early establishment are especially affected; symptoms are more common on cold or acidic soils where phosphorus is less available.
- Symptoms can be subtle and are easily confused with other stresses.

### Potassium (K)
- Typically causes yellowing and then browning/scorching along the margins and tips of older leaves, while the leaf centre stays greener.
- Plants may be weak-stemmed and more prone to lodging, drought stress, and some diseases.

## Secondary and Micronutrient Deficiencies

### Calcium (Ca)
- Affects new growth and growing points, since calcium is immobile; young leaves may be distorted, and growing tips can die back.
- In fruiting crops, associated with disorders such as blossom-end rot in tomato and related crops, often linked to uneven water supply that limits calcium movement.

### Magnesium (Mg)
- Causes interveinal yellowing (yellow between green veins) on older leaves first, since magnesium is mobile.
- Common on sandy, acidic, or heavily leached soils.

### Sulfur (S)
- Causes general yellowing that often appears first on younger leaves (unlike nitrogen), since sulfur is less mobile.
- Symptoms can resemble nitrogen deficiency, making soil testing especially useful to distinguish them.

### Zinc (Zn)
- Causes interveinal yellowing on younger leaves, shortened distance between leaves (giving a bunched or rosette appearance), and small, narrow leaves.
- Common on alkaline soils and where topsoil has been removed by erosion or land levelling.

### Iron (Fe)
- Causes distinct interveinal yellowing on the youngest leaves, with veins remaining green; in severe cases new leaves may be nearly white.
- Common on alkaline and waterlogged soils, where iron availability is restricted rather than absent.

### Boron (B)
- Affects growing points and reproductive structures; symptoms include death of growing tips, brittle or distorted young leaves, and poor flowering, fruit, or grain set.
- Both deficiency and excess can harm crops, so boron corrections require particular care.

## Symptom Reference Table
| Nutrient | Typical symptoms | Common correction |
|---|---|---|
| Nitrogen (N) | Uniform yellowing of older leaves first; stunted, pale growth | Nitrogen-supplying fertiliser and/or organic matter; legume rotation to build nitrogen |
| Phosphorus (P) | Stunting; dark green foliage; reddish/purplish older leaves and stems | Phosphorus-supplying fertiliser; correct acidity where it limits availability |
| Potassium (K) | Yellowing/scorching of older leaf margins and tips; weak stems | Potassium-supplying fertiliser; organic sources such as some crop residues and ashes |
| Calcium (Ca) | Distorted, dying young growth; blossom-end rot in fruiting crops | Lime (also raises pH); maintain even soil moisture |
| Magnesium (Mg) | Interveinal yellowing of older leaves | Magnesium-containing lime (dolomitic) or magnesium fertiliser |
| Sulfur (S) | General yellowing, often on younger leaves | Sulfur-containing fertilisers or organic matter |
| Zinc (Zn) | Interveinal yellowing and bunched, small young leaves | Zinc-supplying fertiliser; correct very high pH where possible |
| Iron (Fe) | Interveinal yellowing of youngest leaves, veins stay green | Improve drainage; correct alkalinity; iron sources where appropriate |
| Boron (B) | Dying growing tips; poor flowering/fruit/grain set | Small, carefully measured boron application (excess is toxic) |

Corrections above are general categories only. Confirm the specific, locally registered products, application rates, and timing with a soil test result and an agricultural extension officer.

## Why Symptoms Overlap and Soil Testing Confirms
- Several nutrients produce similar-looking yellowing, and the same nutrient can look different depending on crop, severity, and stage.
- Deficiency symptoms are frequently mimicked by drought, waterlogging, root damage, soil acidity or alkalinity (which locks up nutrients that are actually present), pests, and disease.
- Because over-application wastes money and can harm crops and the environment, visual symptoms should prompt a soil test rather than an immediate guess-based fertiliser purchase.
- For distinguishing deficiencies from pests, diseases, and environmental stress, see `troubleshooting/crop_troubleshooting_guide.md`.

## Common Mistakes
- Diagnosing a deficiency from a single symptom and applying fertiliser without confirming the cause.
- Treating yellowing as always meaning nitrogen shortage, when it may be sulfur, magnesium, iron, waterlogging, or root damage.
- Ignoring soil pH, which can make nutrients unavailable even when they are present in the soil.
- Correcting boron or other micronutrients by guesswork, risking toxicity from over-application.

## When to Consult an Agricultural Extension Officer
- To arrange a soil test and interpret the results in the context of the specific crop and field.
- When symptoms are widespread, severe, or do not match a clear single nutrient pattern.
- Before applying micronutrients, which are effective only in small, correct amounts and can be toxic in excess.
- To identify the locally registered fertiliser products and appropriate application methods for the identified deficiency.
