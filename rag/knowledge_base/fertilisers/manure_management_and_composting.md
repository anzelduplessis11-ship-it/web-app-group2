# Manure Management and Composting

## Overview
Animal manure is one of the most widely available organic fertiliser sources for smallholder farmers with livestock, but its value depends heavily on how it is collected, stored, processed, and applied. Poorly managed manure loses much of its nutrient value before it ever reaches the field and can introduce pathogens, weed seeds, or plant-damaging compounds. This document covers safe and effective manure collection, composting, and application practices. For a broader comparison of organic fertiliser types, see `fertilisers/organic_fertilisers.md`; for livestock-specific guidance, see the relevant file under `livestock/`.

## Why Manure Management Matters
- Fresh manure nutrient content is highly variable and much of its nitrogen is in unstable forms that are easily lost as ammonia gas or through leaching if left exposed.
- Poorly managed manure is a common source of weed seed spread, since undigested seeds from animal feed can pass through livestock and remain viable.
- Fresh or poorly treated manure can carry pathogens (bacteria such as E. coli and Salmonella, and parasite eggs) that pose a health risk if it contacts edible crop parts, particularly raw vegetables.
- Well-managed manure, by contrast, is a valuable, often low-cost nutrient and soil-conditioning resource that builds long-term soil organic matter alongside its direct nutrient contribution.

## Manure Nutrient Value by Source
Nutrient content varies significantly with animal species, diet, age, and how manure is collected and stored, so the figures below are general indicators only, not a substitute for local testing.
| Manure source | Relative nitrogen content | Relative phosphorus/potassium content | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Poultry manure | High | Moderate-high | Fast-acting; can burn plants if applied fresh or in excess; often mixed with bedding material |
| Goat/sheep manure | Moderate-high | Moderate | Drier, pelleted form; decomposes relatively quickly |
| Cattle manure | Moderate | Moderate | Common and widely available; benefits strongly from composting before use |
| Pig manure | Moderate | Moderate | Often wetter; needs adequate composting/drying |
| Horse/donkey manure | Moderate | Moderate | Frequently mixed with bedding (straw), affecting carbon-to-nitrogen ratio |

Composted manure of any source generally has more stable, though somewhat lower total, nutrient content than fresh manure of the same source, since some nitrogen is lost as part of the composting process while the remaining nutrients become more available and less likely to burn plants.

## Collection and Storage

### Collection Practices
- Collect manure regularly from housing, pens, or kraals rather than allowing it to accumulate for long uncontrolled periods, which improves hygiene for both animals and the eventual compost quality.
- Where possible, separate bedding material (straw, wood shavings) collection from pure manure collection, since bedding-heavy material has a different carbon-to-nitrogen ratio and may need different composting handling.
- Avoid collecting manure that has been contaminated with chemical residues (e.g., from certain veterinary treatments with withdrawal periods, or spilled fuel/chemicals in storage areas).

### Storage Practices
- Store manure under cover (roofed storage, tarpaulin, or a covered pit) to prevent nitrogen loss from rain leaching and sun-driven ammonia volatilisation; uncovered manure can lose a large share of its nitrogen value within weeks.
- Keep stored manure reasonably moist but not waterlogged; both very dry and very wet conditions slow the beneficial decomposition process and can increase nutrient loss in different ways (dry manure loses nitrogen as ammonia gas more readily; waterlogged manure loses nutrients through leachate runoff).
- Site manure storage away from wells, boreholes, and surface water sources to prevent contamination of drinking water and waterways through runoff or seepage.
- Turning or mixing stored manure periodically speeds even decomposition and reduces the risk of anaerobic (oxygen-free) conditions that produce stronger odours and less stable nutrient forms.

## Composting Manure

### Why Compost Rather Than Apply Fresh
- Composting stabilises nutrients, reducing the risk of nitrogen burn to young plants and making nutrients more gradually available over the growing season.
- The heat generated during active composting (when done correctly) kills many weed seeds and disease-causing organisms, reducing the risk of spreading them to the field.
- Composted material has better texture and is easier to spread evenly than fresh, wet manure.

### Basic Composting Process
1. **Select a site**: choose a shaded, well-drained location, ideally close to both the manure source and the field where compost will be used, to minimise transport effort.
2. **Layer materials**: build the compost heap or pit in alternating layers of manure, crop residues or other carbon-rich material (dry leaves, straw, stalks), and a thin layer of soil or already-finished compost to introduce decomposing organisms.
3. **Manage moisture**: the pile should be moist like a wrung-out sponge — not dry and not dripping wet. Water lightly during dry periods and cover during heavy rain to prevent waterlogging and nutrient leaching.
4. **Turn regularly**: turning the pile every 1-3 weeks introduces oxygen, which speeds decomposition and helps the pile heat evenly, including material that started on the outer edges.
5. **Monitor heat**: a well-built, actively decomposing pile should feel noticeably warm to hot in its centre during the early weeks; this heat is a sign that decomposition (and pathogen/weed seed reduction) is occurring effectively.
6. **Judge maturity**: finished compost is dark, crumbly, and earthy-smelling, with original materials no longer easily recognisable; this typically takes a minimum of 6-12 weeks under active management, though timing varies with climate, materials, and how often the pile is turned.

### Carbon-to-Nitrogen (C:N) Balance
- Effective composting depends on a reasonable balance between carbon-rich ("brown") material (dry leaves, straw, stalks) and nitrogen-rich ("green") material (fresh manure, green plant material, kitchen scraps).
- Too much carbon-rich material relative to nitrogen slows decomposition and can temporarily tie up soil nitrogen when the compost is eventually applied and continues to decompose.
- Too much nitrogen-rich material relative to carbon can lead to excessive ammonia loss (a strong smell is a sign of this) and a wetter, less well-structured pile.
- Mixing a reasonable variety of both material types, adjusted based on the pile's smell, moisture, and heating behaviour, is more practical at farm scale than measuring exact ratios.

### Composting Methods
- **Heap composting**: material piled above ground, turned periodically; simple and low-cost, suited to most smallholder situations.
- **Pit composting**: material layered in a dug pit, useful in hot, dry, or windy areas where it helps retain moisture; requires care to avoid waterlogging in high-rainfall periods.
- **Vermicomposting (worm composting)**: uses earthworms to process organic material into a nutrient-rich, fine-textured product; requires more careful moisture and temperature management but can produce a high-quality soil amendment on a smaller scale.

## Application Practices

### General Guidance
- Incorporate manure or compost into the soil rather than leaving it on the surface, to reduce nutrient loss (particularly nitrogen) to the air and improve contact with plant roots.
- Apply well ahead of planting — a minimum of 2-4 weeks for well-composted material, and longer for less fully decomposed material — to allow any remaining decomposition to occur before crop roots are established.
- Base application rates on the crop's nutrient needs and, where possible, the estimated or tested nutrient content of the specific manure/compost source, since rates based on a generic assumption can significantly under- or over-supply nutrients.
- Spread manure or compost as evenly as possible across the field to avoid patches of over-concentrated nutrients (and associated burn risk) next to under-fertilised areas.

### Fresh vs. Composted Manure
- Fresh, undecomposed manure carries a higher risk of root and stem burn from concentrated ammonia and salts, and a higher risk of introducing weed seeds and pathogens; it should generally not be applied close to planting or direct to growing crops.
- If only fresh manure is available and time before planting is short, apply it well in advance (several weeks minimum) and incorporate it into the soil to allow initial breakdown before the crop is established.
- Composted manure is safer for use closer to planting time and, for crops eaten raw (e.g., leafy salad vegetables), is strongly preferred over fresh manure due to food safety concerns.

## Food Safety Considerations
- Avoid direct contact between fresh manure (or manure-containing compost that has not fully matured) and edible parts of crops that will be eaten raw.
- Where fresh manure has been applied, allow an adequate time interval before harvest, particularly for crops in direct soil contact (root vegetables, leafy greens); well-composted, fully matured compost carries substantially lower risk.
- Wash hands and equipment after handling manure, and keep manure storage and composting areas separate from areas used for handling harvested produce.
- Avoid using manure from animals recently treated with certain veterinary medicines until any relevant withdrawal period has passed, since residues can persist in manure.

## Common Mistakes
- Applying fresh, uncomposted manure directly to young plants or shortly before planting, risking fertiliser burn, weed seed introduction, and food safety hazards.
- Storing manure uncovered and exposed to rain and sun, losing much of its nitrogen value before it is ever applied to the field.
- Building a compost pile with too much dry, carbon-rich material and too little moisture or nitrogen-rich material, resulting in a pile that never heats up or breaks down properly.
- Never turning the compost pile, leading to uneven, slow decomposition and incomplete pathogen/weed seed reduction.
- Assuming all manure sources have the same nutrient content and applying a single blanket rate regardless of animal type, diet, or storage history.
- Using manure or immature compost on crops eaten raw without adequate time or processing to reduce pathogen risk.

## When to Consult an Agricultural Extension Officer
- To have manure or compost tested for nutrient content where laboratory services are available, improving the accuracy of application rate planning.
- For guidance on composting methods suited to local climate (particularly in very wet or very dry regions where moisture management differs).
- If manure-related crop damage (burn, poor germination) occurs, to review both the manure's decomposition state and the application method used.
- For specific food safety guidance when using manure on crops intended for raw consumption or for certified/regulated markets.
