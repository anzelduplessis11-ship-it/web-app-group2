# NPK Ratios Explained

## Overview
NPK ratios describe the relative proportions of nitrogen (N), phosphorus (P), and potassium (K) in a fertiliser product, and are the single most important piece of information on a fertiliser label. Understanding how to read and compare these ratios allows farmers to choose products that match crop needs and growth stage, rather than buying by habit or by what is simply available. This document explains how NPK numbers work, how to interpret fertiliser labels and packaging, and how to select an appropriate ratio for common crop types and growth stages. For background on the roles of individual nutrients, see `fertilisers/fertiliser_basics.md`; for diagnosing existing deficiencies, see `fertilisers/nutrient_deficiencies_guide.md`.

## What the Three Numbers Mean
Every compound (blended) fertiliser bag displays three numbers separated by hyphens, for example 23-10-5 or 17-17-17. These numbers always appear in the same order and represent the percentage, by weight, of:
- **N** — total nitrogen content
- **P** — phosphorus content, expressed as phosphorus pentoxide (P2O5) in most countries, not elemental phosphorus
- **K** — potassium content, expressed as potassium oxide (K2O) in most countries, not elemental potassium

For example, a 50 kg bag labelled 15-15-15 contains 15% nitrogen, 15% P2O5, and 15% K2O by weight — that is, 7.5 kg of nitrogen, 7.5 kg of P2O5, and 7.5 kg of K2O, with the remaining weight made up of filler material, sulfur, or other carrier compounds.

- A **balanced ratio** (e.g., 15-15-15, 17-17-17) supplies roughly equal proportions of all three nutrients and is often used as a general-purpose or "starter" fertiliser when soil status is unknown.
- A **skewed ratio** favours one nutrient over the others, for example 23-10-5 (high nitrogen, used for topdressing leafy growth) or 8-24-24 (high phosphorus and potassium, often used as a basal/planting fertiliser).
- Some labels also list secondary nutrients (calcium, magnesium, sulfur) or micronutrients (zinc, boron) as additional numbers or a separate list; these matter for crops with known secondary or micronutrient needs but do not change how the core N-P-K numbers are read.

## Why P and K Numbers Are Not "Pure" Nutrient Content
A common source of confusion is that the P and K figures on most fertiliser bags are not the amount of elemental phosphorus or potassium, but the oxide forms (P2O5 and K2O) historically used in the fertiliser trade. This matters mainly for technical nutrient-balance calculations; for everyday farm decision-making, it is enough to know that:
- Higher numbers mean a higher concentration of that nutrient form in the bag.
- Ratios can be compared directly between products as long as both are labelled the same way (which is the case for the vast majority of commercial compound fertilisers).
- If converting to elemental content is needed (for detailed budgeting), P2O5 is roughly 44% elemental phosphorus and K2O is roughly 83% elemental potassium, but confirm exact conversion factors with a soil scientist or extension officer if precision matters for a specific calculation.

## Common Fertiliser Types and Their Approximate Ratios
| Fertiliser type | Approximate N-P-K | Typical role |
|---|---|---|
| Urea | 46-0-0 | Straight nitrogen source, used for topdressing |
| Ammonium sulfate | 21-0-0 (plus sulfur) | Nitrogen plus sulfur, useful on sulfur-deficient soils |
| Diammonium phosphate (DAP) | 18-46-0 | High-phosphorus basal fertiliser |
| Single superphosphate (SSP) | 0-18-0 (plus calcium, sulfur) | Basal phosphorus source |
| Triple superphosphate (TSP) | 0-46-0 | Concentrated basal phosphorus source |
| Muriate of potash (MOP) | 0-0-60 | Straight potassium source |
| Sulfate of potash (SOP) | 0-0-50 (plus sulfur) | Potassium source, preferred for chloride-sensitive crops |
| NPK compound (basal type) | e.g., 10-20-10, 8-24-24 | General basal/planting fertiliser |
| NPK compound (topdress type) | e.g., 23-10-5, 34-0-0 (urea) | Nitrogen-heavy topdressing fertiliser |
| Balanced NPK compound | e.g., 15-15-15, 17-17-17 | General-purpose, all-round fertiliser |

Exact formulations and product names vary by country and supplier; always check the specific label and confirm availability with a local agro-dealer or extension officer.

## How to Read a Fertiliser Label
- **Check the N-P-K numbers first** — this tells you the basic nutrient profile and role of the product (basal, topdress, or balanced).
- **Check the net weight of the bag** (commonly 25 kg or 50 kg) to calculate actual nutrient quantity per bag and per hectare.
- **Look for secondary and micronutrient listings**, often in smaller print, which may include sulfur (S), magnesium (Mg), zinc (Zn), or boron (B) — important on soils known to be deficient in these.
- **Check for a manufacture or expiry-related date and storage instructions**, since some fertilisers (especially those containing nitrate) can degrade in quality or become a fire/safety hazard if stored improperly.
- **Confirm the product is registered for sale in your country/region**, as recommended by the local regulatory authority; see `regulations/agricultural_regulations_overview.md`.

## Choosing a Ratio by Growth Stage
Nutrient demand shifts as a crop develops, and matching fertiliser ratio to growth stage improves both yield and nutrient-use efficiency.

- **Establishment/basal stage (at or before planting):** Young plants need strong root development, so a higher-phosphorus ratio (e.g., 10-20-10, 8-24-24, or straight DAP) is generally applied at or near planting, placed close to but not touching the seed.
- **Early vegetative growth:** As plants establish leaves and stems, nitrogen demand rises. A nitrogen-heavy topdressing (e.g., urea, ammonium sulfate, or a high-N compound) is commonly applied 2-4 weeks after emergence, depending on the crop.
- **Rapid vegetative growth (leafy crops, cereals before tasseling):** Continued nitrogen supply supports canopy development; a second topdressing is often applied at this stage for cereals and heavy feeders.
- **Flowering and reproductive stage:** Nitrogen demand typically declines relative to potassium, which supports flower retention, fruit set, and grain/tuber filling. A balanced or potassium-heavier ratio is often favoured here.
- **Fruit/grain filling and bulking (root crops, tubers, fruiting vegetables):** Potassium becomes especially important for quality, sugar/starch accumulation, and disease resistance; potassium-rich fertiliser (e.g., sulfate of potash) may be applied at this stage.
- **Maturation:** Fertiliser application generally stops before harvest to avoid delaying maturity, reduce residue risk in the crop, and avoid wasted input.

## Matching Ratios to Broad Crop Groups
These are general starting principles, not fixed prescriptions — always confirm with a soil test and local crop guidance (see the relevant file under `crops/`).

- **Cereals (maize, rice, wheat, sorghum, millet):** Typically benefit from a phosphorus-rich basal application at planting, followed by one or two nitrogen-heavy topdressings timed to key vegetative and pre-flowering stages.
- **Legumes (beans, groundnut, soybean, cowpea):** Generally need less nitrogen fertiliser since they can fix atmospheric nitrogen via root nodules (see `fertilisers/biofertilisers_and_biochar.md` for rhizobium inoculation), but still benefit from phosphorus and potassium at planting.
- **Root and tuber crops (cassava, potato, sweet potato, taro):** Benefit from balanced or potassium-heavier fertiliser, since potassium strongly influences tuber bulking and quality.
- **Leafy vegetables (kale, cabbage, spinach, lettuce):** Respond strongly to nitrogen, since the harvested product is vegetative leaf material; moderate, split nitrogen applications support continuous leaf growth without excessive nitrate accumulation.
- **Fruiting vegetables (tomato, pepper, eggplant, cucumber, okra):** Need balanced early growth followed by a shift toward potassium during flowering and fruit development; excess nitrogen late in the season can promote leafy growth at the expense of fruit set.
- **Perennial tree/fruit crops (mango, avocado, citrus, coffee, cocoa):** Nutrient needs shift with tree age and season; young trees prioritise balanced growth-stage fertiliser, while mature bearing trees generally need potassium-heavier fertiliser during fruit development, often split across multiple applications through the season.

## Using Ratios Alongside Soil Test Results
- A soil test (see `soil/soil_testing_and_fertility.md`) reports existing nutrient levels in the soil, which should be the primary basis for choosing both fertiliser rate and ratio.
- If soil phosphorus or potassium is already high, choosing a lower-P or lower-K ratio (or a straight nitrogen source) avoids wasted expenditure and reduces environmental loss.
- If soil testing is not available, a balanced ratio at planting followed by nitrogen topdressing is a reasonable general default for most annual crops, adjusted based on observed crop response in previous seasons.

## Common Mistakes
- Assuming a higher total percentage (e.g., 17-17-17 vs. 15-15-15) always means a "stronger" or better fertiliser without checking whether the ratio actually matches crop needs at that growth stage.
- Using a single balanced or high-nitrogen fertiliser for the entire season, ignoring the shift in nutrient demand toward potassium during flowering and fruiting.
- Confusing the P and K numbers on the bag (oxide form) with elemental nutrient content when doing detailed nutrient budgeting.
- Applying a basal-type high-phosphorus fertiliser as a topdressing, where it is far less effective since phosphorus does not move readily to where roots are actively growing later in the season.
- Buying fertiliser based only on price per bag without comparing actual nutrient content and ratio suitability for the crop.

## When to Consult an Agricultural Extension Officer
- To interpret a soil test result and translate it into a specific recommended NPK ratio and application rate for a given field and crop.
- Before purchasing fertiliser for an unfamiliar crop or field, to confirm which locally available product ratios best match known or suspected soil conditions.
- If crop growth or yield does not respond as expected to a chosen fertiliser ratio, since other factors (soil pH, micronutrient deficiency, moisture stress) may be limiting nutrient uptake despite an appropriate NPK choice.
- To confirm current, locally registered fertiliser products, their exact composition, and appropriate handling and storage practices.
