# Micronutrient Deficiencies and Correction

## Overview
Micronutrients — zinc, boron, iron, manganese, copper, and molybdenum, among others — are needed by crops in very small quantities but are just as essential as nitrogen, phosphorus, and potassium. A shortage of any one micronutrient can limit yield even when macronutrient supply is adequate. Because micronutrients are required in small amounts, the margin between deficiency and toxicity is narrow, so correction requires more care and precision than macronutrient fertilisation. This document provides a deeper, correction-focused look at the six micronutrients most commonly limiting to crop production; for a broader overview of all nutrient deficiency symptoms including macronutrients, see `fertilisers/nutrient_deficiencies_guide.md`, and for general fertiliser principles see `fertilisers/fertiliser_basics.md`.

## Why Micronutrient Deficiencies Occur
- **Soil pH extremes**: most micronutrients (except molybdenum) become less available to plants as soil pH rises above neutral (alkaline soils); molybdenum availability instead drops as soil becomes more acidic.
- **Sandy, heavily leached, or eroded soils**: naturally low in total micronutrient reserves, especially where topsoil has been lost.
- **Continuous cropping without replenishment**: intensive cropping over many years without organic matter or balanced fertiliser inputs can deplete micronutrient reserves.
- **High rates of macronutrient fertiliser without micronutrients**: rapid yield increases driven by N-P-K fertiliser can "mine" soil micronutrient reserves faster than they are naturally replenished, a phenomenon sometimes called induced deficiency.
- **Waterlogging or compaction**: alters root function and soil chemistry in ways that can reduce uptake of certain micronutrients (notably iron and manganese) despite adequate soil content.
- **High soil organic matter or peaty soils**: can bind certain micronutrients, notably copper, reducing their availability.

## Zinc (Zn)

### Role and Importance
Zinc is involved in enzyme activity, hormone regulation (including growth hormones), and normal leaf expansion. It is one of the most widespread micronutrient deficiencies in cereal production globally, especially in rice and maize.

### Deficiency Symptoms
- Interveinal yellowing (chlorosis) on younger to middle leaves, sometimes with a bronze or grey tinge in rice ("khaira" disease in rice is a classic zinc deficiency).
- Shortened internodes, giving plants a stunted, bunched, or rosette-like appearance.
- Small, narrow leaves, sometimes with irregular shape.
- Delayed maturity and reduced grain/fruit set in severe cases.

### Where It Is Common
- Alkaline and calcareous soils.
- Sandy soils and soils where topsoil has eroded.
- Soils with very high phosphorus levels, since excess phosphorus can interfere with zinc uptake.
- Rice grown under flooded (waterlogged) conditions.

### Correction
- Soil-applied zinc sulfate is a common correction, applied at planting or incorporated before planting; exact rates depend on soil test results and should be confirmed locally.
- Foliar zinc sprays can correct deficiency more rapidly during the growing season (see `fertilisers/foliar_feeding_guide.md`) and are often used when deficiency is identified after planting.
- On very high-pH soils, chelated zinc forms may remain plant-available for longer than simple sulfate forms.

## Boron (B)

### Role and Importance
Boron supports cell wall formation, pollen tube growth, and flower and seed development. It has one of the narrowest safe ranges of any nutrient — both deficiency and excess cause crop damage.

### Deficiency Symptoms
- Death or distortion of growing points and young leaves, since boron is immobile within the plant.
- Thickened, brittle, or curled young leaves.
- Poor flower, fruit, or seed set; hollow stems in some crops (e.g., hollow heart in root vegetables, hollow stem in broccoli/cauliflower-type crops).
- Cracked fruit or corky patches in some fruiting and root crops.

### Where It Is Common
- Sandy soils with low organic matter.
- Soils that have been heavily leached by rainfall or irrigation.
- Alkaline soils and soils that have received heavy liming without boron replacement.
- Periods of drought, which can worsen boron deficiency symptoms even where some soil boron is present.

### Correction
- Boron is typically corrected with small, carefully measured applications of borax or boric acid-based products, either soil-applied or as a foliar spray.
- Because the difference between a deficient and a toxic level of boron in soil is narrow, application rates should be based on soil test recommendations rather than estimation.
- Repeated small foliar applications are often safer than a single larger soil application, particularly on sensitive crops.

## Iron (Fe)

### Role and Importance
Iron is required for chlorophyll synthesis and several enzyme systems involved in energy transfer within the plant, even though it is not itself part of the chlorophyll molecule.

### Deficiency Symptoms
- Distinct interveinal yellowing on the youngest leaves, with veins remaining green — often called "iron chlorosis."
- In severe cases, new leaves can appear almost white or cream-coloured.
- Symptoms appear on new growth first because iron is immobile within the plant.

### Where It Is Common
- Alkaline and calcareous soils, where iron is present but converted to forms plants cannot readily absorb.
- Waterlogged or poorly drained soils, where root function and iron uptake are impaired even if soil iron content is adequate.
- Cold, wet conditions that slow root activity.

### Correction
- Improving drainage and aeration often resolves iron chlorosis linked to waterlogging without any product application.
- On alkaline soils, chelated iron (iron bound to an organic compound that keeps it plant-available at high pH) is generally more effective than simple iron sulfate.
- Foliar iron sprays can provide rapid, temporary greening, but do not correct the underlying soil condition (usually pH) causing the deficiency; addressing soil pH (see `fertilisers/lime_and_soil_ph_correction.md`) may be needed for a lasting fix on alkaline soils.

## Manganese (Mn)

### Role and Importance
Manganese is involved in photosynthesis and several enzyme systems, and works alongside iron in some plant processes, which can make the two deficiencies visually similar.

### Deficiency Symptoms
- Interveinal yellowing similar to iron deficiency, but manganese deficiency often shows fine grey or brown necrotic (dead) speckling within the yellowed areas, and tends to appear on young-to-middle leaves rather than only the very youngest.
- Reduced growth and, in severe cases, leaf drop.

### Where It Is Common
- Alkaline and well-limed soils, where availability drops sharply as pH rises.
- Sandy, low-organic-matter soils.
- Interestingly, manganese can become excessive (toxic) on very acidic soils, showing the opposite problem to most other micronutrients.

### Correction
- Manganese sulfate is a common soil or foliar correction; foliar application is often preferred since manganese availability in soil is strongly pH-dependent and soil correction alone may not be reliable on alkaline soils.
- Avoid over-liming soils that are already adequate in manganese, since raising pH further can induce deficiency.

## Copper (Cu)

### Role and Importance
Copper is involved in enzyme systems related to lignin formation (plant structural strength) and reproductive development. Deficiency is less common than zinc or boron deficiency but occurs on specific soil types.

### Deficiency Symptoms
- Young leaves may appear pale, twisted, or limp, sometimes described as "melting" or wilting appearance at the tips.
- Poor pollen viability and reduced grain/seed set in cereals ("reluctant heading" in some grain crops).
- Dieback of shoot tips in severe, prolonged deficiency.

### Where It Is Common
- Sandy soils and soils very high in organic matter (peaty or muck soils), since organic matter binds copper tightly.
- Newly cultivated or reclaimed land with naturally low copper reserves.

### Correction
- Copper sulfate or copper-chelate products can be soil-applied or used as a foliar spray, at carefully measured rates since copper can also become toxic to plants and soil organisms in excess.
- Because copper deficiency is less common and its correction carries a higher risk of over-application, confirming the diagnosis with a soil or tissue test before treating is particularly important.

## Molybdenum (Mo)

### Role and Importance
Molybdenum is required in extremely small amounts but plays a critical role in nitrogen metabolism, including the nitrogen-fixation process in legume root nodules and nitrate conversion in all plants.

### Deficiency Symptoms
- General yellowing that can resemble nitrogen deficiency, since molybdenum is needed for the plant to use nitrogen effectively.
- In legumes, poor nodulation and reduced nitrogen fixation, leading to overall poor growth and pale colour despite otherwise adequate soil nitrogen.
- In some crucifer crops (e.g., cauliflower), a distinctive symptom called "whiptail," where leaves become narrow and strap-like.

### Where It Is Common
- Acidic soils — unlike most other micronutrients, molybdenum availability decreases as soil becomes more acidic, so it is often deficient on the same soils where other micronutrients are excessive.
- Sandy, low-organic-matter soils.

### Correction
- Because molybdenum deficiency is strongly linked to soil acidity, liming acidic soils (see `fertilisers/lime_and_soil_ph_correction.md`) is often the most effective and lasting correction.
- Where a direct correction is needed, molybdenum is typically supplied as a very small seed treatment or foliar application (sodium or ammonium molybdate), since the total amount required per hectare is extremely small and easy to over-apply.

## General Correction Principles
- Confirm the deficiency with a soil test and, where available, plant tissue analysis before applying any micronutrient product — visual symptoms alone frequently overlap between micronutrients and with other stresses.
- Apply micronutrients at the specific small rates recommended for the product and confirmed soil condition; more is not better, and several micronutrients (boron, copper, manganese, zinc) are toxic well before macronutrient-equivalent rates.
- Foliar application (see `fertilisers/foliar_feeding_guide.md`) is often the fastest and safest way to correct an identified micronutrient deficiency during the growing season, though it may need to be repeated since it does not build soil reserves.
- Correcting underlying soil pH is frequently more effective and longer-lasting than repeated micronutrient applications, since pH extremes are the root cause of many micronutrient availability problems.
- Chelated forms of iron, zinc, and manganese generally remain available longer on alkaline soils than simple sulfate forms, though they are typically more expensive.

## Common Mistakes
- Applying a micronutrient blindly based on a general symptom description without confirming which specific nutrient is limiting.
- Over-applying boron, copper, or manganese in the belief that "more will help faster," risking toxicity that can be more damaging than the original deficiency.
- Treating a deficiency symptom caused by high soil pH with repeated product applications instead of addressing the underlying pH problem.
- Ignoring micronutrient status while investing heavily in N-P-K fertiliser, leading to yield plateaus that macronutrients alone cannot fix.
- Assuming all pale or yellow leaves indicate nitrogen deficiency rather than considering iron, manganese, sulfur, or magnesium as possibilities.

## When to Consult an Agricultural Extension Officer
- To arrange soil and, where available, plant tissue testing to confirm a suspected micronutrient deficiency before purchasing correction products.
- Before applying boron, copper, or manganese, given the narrow margin between an effective and a toxic rate.
- If deficiency symptoms persist after a correction application, since soil pH or drainage issues may need to be addressed directly.
- To identify locally registered micronutrient products, correct formulations (sulfate vs. chelate), and appropriate application rates and methods for the specific crop and soil.
