# Lime and Soil pH Correction

## Overview
Soil pH strongly influences how available nutrients are to crops, how well beneficial soil organisms function, and how susceptible plants are to certain diseases and toxicities. When soil pH drifts too far from the range most crops tolerate, nutrients that are physically present in the soil can become chemically "locked up" and unavailable, producing deficiency symptoms even where fertiliser has been applied correctly. This document explains how to recognise acidic and alkaline soil problems and how agricultural lime and other amendments are used to correct them. For nutrient-specific deficiency symptoms, see `fertilisers/nutrient_deficiencies_guide.md` and `fertilisers/micronutrient_deficiencies_and_correction.md`; for soil testing guidance, see `soil/soil_testing_and_fertility.md` and `soil/soil_types_africa.md`.

## Understanding the pH Scale
- Soil pH is measured on a scale from 0 (extremely acidic) to 14 (extremely alkaline), with 7 as neutral.
- Most field and horticultural crops perform best in a moderately acidic to neutral range, roughly pH 6.0-7.0, though tolerance varies by crop.
- Some crops (e.g., tea, pineapple, potato) tolerate or even prefer more acidic conditions, while others (e.g., some legumes, asparagus) tolerate more alkaline conditions.
- The pH scale is logarithmic, meaning each whole-number change represents a tenfold difference in acidity or alkalinity — a small-looking change in the number reflects a large change in actual soil chemistry.

## How Soil pH Affects Nutrient Availability
- **Acidic soils (low pH)**: phosphorus becomes chemically bound to iron and aluminium and less available; aluminium and manganese can become soluble enough to be toxic to roots; calcium, magnesium, and molybdenum availability decline; beneficial bacteria (including nitrogen-fixing rhizobium) function less effectively.
- **Alkaline soils (high pH)**: phosphorus becomes bound to calcium and less available; iron, zinc, manganese, copper, and boron availability decline sharply, often producing micronutrient deficiency symptoms even when total soil content is adequate.
- Nitrogen, in most mineral forms, is relatively less sensitive to pH than phosphorus and most micronutrients, though very low pH can still impair the microbial processes that convert organic nitrogen into plant-available forms.

## Why Soils Become Acidic
- **Natural leaching**: high rainfall gradually washes calcium, magnesium, and other basic nutrients out of the topsoil, especially in humid tropical regions.
- **Long-term use of certain nitrogen fertilisers**: ammonium-based fertilisers (urea, ammonium sulfate) acidify soil over time as the ammonium converts to nitrate in the soil.
- **Continuous cropping and residue removal**: repeated harvest removes basic nutrients from the field without replenishment.
- **Naturally acidic parent material**: some soils are inherently acidic due to the geology they formed from.

## Why Soils Become Alkaline
- **Naturally calcareous parent material**: soils formed from limestone or similar calcium-rich rock tend to be alkaline.
- **Low rainfall/arid conditions**: in dry climates, basic minerals (calcium, magnesium, sodium) are not leached away and can accumulate near the surface.
- **Irrigation with hard or saline water**: water high in dissolved calcium, magnesium, or sodium can raise soil pH and, in some cases, contribute to salinity problems over time.
- **Over-liming**: applying more lime than an already near-neutral or alkaline soil needs can push pH too high.

## Recognising a pH Problem
- **Test first**: a laboratory or field pH test is the only reliable way to confirm soil pH; visual symptoms alone are not conclusive since deficiency symptoms caused by pH can resemble true nutrient shortages.
- **Suggestive field signs of acidity**: stunted crop growth despite fertiliser use, poor legume nodulation, visible aluminium/manganese toxicity symptoms (root stunting, leaf curling), and naturally occurring acid-tolerant weeds or vegetation in the area.
- **Suggestive field signs of alkalinity**: micronutrient deficiency symptoms (iron chlorosis, zinc deficiency) appearing widely across a field despite otherwise reasonable fertility management, and, in arid areas, visible white salt crusting on the soil surface (which may indicate a related but distinct salinity problem).
- A soil test should always be used to confirm suspected pH problems before investing in correction; see `soil/soil_testing_and_fertility.md`.

## Correcting Acidic Soils: Liming

### What Agricultural Lime Is
Agricultural lime refers to ground limestone (calcium carbonate) or related materials applied to soil to neutralise excess acidity and raise pH. Common forms include:
- **Calcitic lime (ground limestone)**: primarily calcium carbonate; the most widely available and commonly used liming material.
- **Dolomitic lime**: contains both calcium and magnesium carbonate; preferred where soil is deficient in both pH and magnesium.
- **Hydrated (slaked) lime**: reacts faster than ground limestone but is more caustic to handle and can be more prone to over-application effects; requires careful handling.
- **Wood ash**: contains variable amounts of calcium, potassium, and other basic minerals and has a mild liming effect; useful in small quantities where available, though nutrient content is inconsistent and should not be treated as equivalent to purpose-made agricultural lime.

### How Liming Works
Lime reacts with acidic components in the soil, neutralising excess hydrogen and aluminium ions and gradually raising soil pH. This reaction happens over weeks to months, not instantly, because it depends on the lime dissolving and reacting throughout the soil profile.

### Determining How Much Lime to Apply
- The correct liming rate depends on both current soil pH and the soil's buffering capacity (its resistance to pH change, which is strongly influenced by clay and organic matter content) — two soils with the same starting pH can need very different lime rates.
- A soil test that reports "lime requirement" alongside pH gives the most reliable basis for calculating an application rate; general estimates without this information risk under- or over-correction.
- As a broad principle, sandy soils with low buffering capacity need less lime to shift pH than clay-rich or high-organic-matter soils, even to reach the same target pH.
- Because of these variables, this document intentionally does not give a fixed tonnes/ha figure — confirm the specific rate for a given field with a soil test and local extension guidance.

### Application Practices
- Lime works best when thoroughly mixed into the topsoil (incorporated by tillage) rather than left on the surface, since it reacts slowly and mainly where it makes contact with acidic soil.
- Apply lime well ahead of planting — several weeks to a few months where possible — to allow the reaction time to raise pH before the crop needs the benefit.
- Because the full reaction is gradual, lime is often applied every few years as part of a rotation rather than every single season; retesting pH periodically shows whether and when reapplication is needed.
- Avoid applying lime and fresh ammonium-based nitrogen fertiliser or manure at exactly the same time in direct contact, since the reaction between them can increase ammonia loss to the air, reducing nitrogen fertiliser efficiency; separate the two applications by some weeks where practical.

### Choosing Between Lime Types
| Lime type | Speed of reaction | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Ground limestone (calcitic) | Slow, steady | Most widely available; safe and easy to handle |
| Dolomitic lime | Slow, steady | Use where magnesium is also deficient |
| Hydrated lime | Fast | More caustic; requires careful handling and precise rates |
| Wood ash | Mild, variable | Useful supplement in small amounts; inconsistent composition |

## Correcting Alkaline Soils
Lowering soil pH is generally more difficult, slower, and more expensive than raising it, and repeated treatment is often needed since alkalinity is frequently tied to underlying parent material or water source rather than a one-time deficit.
- **Elemental sulfur**: soil bacteria gradually convert applied elemental sulfur to sulfuric acid, lowering pH over a period of months; this is one of the more common approaches for moderate, gradual correction.
- **Acidifying organic matter**: composted pine needles, certain composts, and general organic matter additions have a mild acidifying effect over time and improve overall soil buffering and structure, making pH easier to manage.
- **Ammonium-based nitrogen fertilisers**: as noted above, these have a mild acidifying side effect over repeated seasons, which can be a secondary benefit on mildly alkaline soils, though they should be chosen based on crop nitrogen needs first.
- **Improving irrigation water quality/management**: where alkalinity is linked to irrigation with hard or saline water, improving drainage and water management can help prevent further pH increases, though it does not reverse existing alkalinity on its own.
- **Choosing pH-tolerant crops or varieties**: on soils where full correction is impractical (e.g., naturally calcareous soils), selecting crops and varieties known to tolerate higher pH, and using targeted micronutrient correction (see `fertilisers/micronutrient_deficiencies_and_correction.md`), is often more practical than attempting large-scale soil acidification.

## Gypsum: A Special Case
- Gypsum (calcium sulfate) supplies calcium and sulfur but, unlike lime, does not significantly change soil pH.
- It is commonly used to improve the structure of compacted or sodium-affected (sodic) soils and to supply calcium on soils that do not need pH correction, rather than as a liming material.
- Gypsum should not be used as a substitute for lime where the actual problem is soil acidity, since it will not correct low pH.

## Monitoring After Correction
- Retest soil pH after a full season or two following lime or sulfur application, since reactions occur gradually and initial post-application readings may not reflect the final result.
- Continue routine soil testing every few years even after correction, since ongoing farming practices (fertiliser choice, crop removal, rainfall/irrigation) will continue to push pH gradually in one direction or the other.
- Adjust future lime or sulfur applications based on updated test results rather than repeating the same rate indefinitely.

## Common Mistakes
- Applying lime or sulfur based on guesswork or generic online rates without a soil test, risking over-correction (pushing pH too far in the opposite direction) or under-correction.
- Expecting an immediate pH change after liming; the reaction takes weeks to months to complete, especially with coarsely ground limestone.
- Using hydrated lime at rates intended for ground limestone, risking over-application due to its faster, more concentrated reaction.
- Treating gypsum as a liming material when the actual problem is low pH, leaving the acidity uncorrected.
- Ignoring soil pH entirely while troubleshooting apparent nutrient deficiencies, when pH correction may resolve the issue more effectively and permanently than repeated fertiliser applications.

## When to Consult an Agricultural Extension Officer
- To obtain a soil test that reports both pH and lime requirement, which is essential for calculating an appropriate, field-specific correction rate.
- Before applying hydrated lime or elemental sulfur at scale, given the greater risk of over-application compared with ground limestone.
- If crop symptoms suggest a pH-related nutrient lockup but do not improve after a correction attempt, since underlying soil type, drainage, or salinity issues may also be involved.
- To identify locally available lime, gypsum, or sulfur products and confirm quality (e.g., fineness of grinding, which affects how quickly ground limestone reacts).
