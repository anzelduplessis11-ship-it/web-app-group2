# Foliar Feeding Guide

## Overview
Foliar feeding is the application of nutrient solutions directly onto plant leaves, where they are absorbed through the leaf surface rather than through the roots. It is a fast-acting, targeted tool for correcting specific nutrient shortfalls or supporting crops during periods when root uptake is limited, but it is not a substitute for adequate soil fertility. This document explains when foliar feeding works well, how to apply it correctly, and which nutrients and crops respond best. For soil-applied fertiliser fundamentals, see `fertilisers/fertiliser_basics.md`; for micronutrient-specific correction details, see `fertilisers/micronutrient_deficiencies_and_correction.md`.

## How Foliar Feeding Works
- Leaves have a waxy outer layer (cuticle) that limits water and nutrient movement, but nutrients dissolved in a fine spray can enter through microscopic openings (stomata) and small imperfections in the cuticle, then move into leaf tissue and, to a more limited extent, to other parts of the plant.
- Absorption is generally faster than root uptake for the specific nutrients applied, making foliar feeding useful for rapid correction of an identified deficiency during a critical growth stage.
- Foliar-absorbed nutrients supplement, but do not replace, the much larger volume of nutrients a healthy root system draws from the soil over the whole season.

## What Foliar Feeding Is Good For
- **Rapid correction of a diagnosed micronutrient deficiency**, especially zinc, iron, manganese, and boron, where soil conditions (high pH, waterlogging) limit root uptake even when the nutrient is present in the soil.
- **Supporting crops during root stress**, such as waterlogged, compacted, or cold soil conditions when root nutrient uptake is temporarily impaired.
- **Supplementing nutrition during critical growth stages** (flowering, fruit set, grain filling) when nutrient demand peaks and soil supply or root uptake may not keep pace.
- **Overcoming poor nutrient mobility in soil**, since some micronutrients move very little through soil and may not reach roots even when adequate total quantity is present.

## What Foliar Feeding Is Not Good For
- **Meeting bulk nitrogen, phosphorus, or potassium needs.** Leaves can only absorb small amounts of nutrient per application, far less than the total quantity a growing crop needs of these macronutrients; attempting to meet the bulk of N-P-K demand through foliar spraying alone is inefficient and impractical.
- **Replacing a fundamentally deficient or poorly managed soil fertility programme.** Foliar feeding treats a symptom quickly but does not build long-term soil fertility or correct underlying problems such as poor soil structure, low organic matter, or incorrect pH.
- **Correcting severe, whole-field deficiencies as a permanent fix.** Repeated foliar treatment of the same problem season after season is usually a signal that the underlying soil issue should be addressed directly (e.g., liming, organic matter building — see `fertilisers/lime_and_soil_ph_correction.md`).

## Nutrients Commonly Applied as Foliar Sprays
| Nutrient | Why foliar application is useful | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Nitrogen (as urea, low biuret) | Quick green-up during stress periods | Only supplements soil nitrogen; cannot supply full crop need |
| Zinc | Corrects deficiency fast, especially on high-pH soils | One of the most common and effective foliar micronutrient treatments |
| Iron | Greens up chlorotic new growth quickly | Effect can be temporary if underlying pH/drainage issue is not addressed |
| Manganese | Availability strongly limited by high pH; foliar often more reliable than soil application | Apply at recommended dilution to avoid leaf scorch |
| Boron | Supports flowering and fruit/seed set at critical stages | Very narrow safe range; measure carefully |
| Calcium | Can reduce blossom-end rot and related disorders in some fruiting crops | Effectiveness depends on consistent, repeated application during fruit development, since calcium moves very little once absorbed |
| Magnesium | Corrects visible interveinal yellowing relatively quickly | Often applied as a dilute magnesium sulfate (Epsom salt) solution |
| Potassium | Can support fruit/grain quality during filling stage | Generally a supplement to, not replacement for, soil-applied potassium |

## When Foliar Feeding Is Most Effective
- **Early morning or late afternoon/evening**, when temperatures are cooler and humidity is higher, slowing evaporation and giving the spray more time to be absorbed before it dries on the leaf.
- **Avoid spraying during the heat of the day**, particularly in full sun, since rapid drying reduces absorption and can increase the risk of leaf scorch, especially with nitrogen or salt-based nutrient solutions.
- **Avoid spraying immediately before rain**, since rain can wash the solution off before absorption occurs; check the weather forecast where possible.
- **Apply when plants are not under severe drought or heat stress**, since stressed leaves absorb foliar nutrients less effectively and are more prone to spray damage.
- **Target the underside of leaves as well as the top where practical**, since many crops have a higher concentration of stomata on the lower leaf surface, improving absorption.

## Application Method and Equipment
- Use a well-calibrated backpack or knapsack sprayer with a fine mist nozzle; fine droplets give better leaf coverage and absorption than a coarse stream.
- Dissolve the fertiliser product fully in water before spraying, following the label's recommended dilution rate; concentrations that are too strong risk leaf scorch, while overly dilute solutions may not deliver an effective nutrient dose.
- Spray to the point of even coverage without heavy runoff; excessive runoff wastes product and moves nutrient into the soil where it behaves as a normal (much smaller) soil application rather than a foliar one.
- Clean sprayer equipment thoroughly between uses, especially if it was previously used for herbicides, since residues can damage the crop being fed.
- Repeat applications are often needed to correct a deficiency fully, since each spray delivers a relatively small dose; follow product-specific guidance on repeat intervals (commonly every 7-14 days during active correction, though this varies by product and severity).

## Compatibility and Mixing
- Not all nutrient products and pesticides can be safely tank-mixed together; combining incompatible products can cause chemical reactions, reduced effectiveness, or crop damage.
- When mixing multiple products, do a small-scale compatibility test (mixing small quantities in a clear container and checking for clumping, separation, or heat) before mixing a full tank load.
- Always check product labels for compatibility guidance, and avoid mixing foliar fertiliser with strong acidic or alkaline pesticide products unless specifically confirmed as safe.
- Rhizobium and other live biofertiliser inoculants (see `fertilisers/biofertilisers_and_biochar.md`) should generally not be tank-mixed with foliar fertiliser or pesticide sprays, since chemical exposure can kill the living organisms.

## Crop Considerations
- **Leafy vegetables (kale, cabbage, spinach, lettuce)**: respond well to foliar nitrogen and micronutrient correction, given the large, actively absorbing leaf surface; take care with pre-harvest intervals for any product used close to harvest.
- **Fruiting vegetables (tomato, pepper, eggplant)**: foliar calcium and boron are commonly used to support fruit set and reduce blossom-end rot and related disorders, especially during dry or inconsistent watering periods.
- **Cereals (maize, rice, wheat)**: foliar zinc and, in some cases, foliar nitrogen or micronutrient mixes are used around key growth stages (e.g., before flowering) to support grain fill.
- **Root and tuber crops (cassava, potato, sweet potato)**: foliar micronutrient correction can help, but bulk potassium needs for tuber development are generally better met through soil application given the scale of demand.
- **Perennial fruit trees (mango, avocado, citrus)**: foliar micronutrient sprays (zinc, boron, manganese) are commonly used around flowering to support fruit set, particularly on soils where these nutrients are known to be poorly available.
- **Legumes**: foliar molybdenum or general micronutrient correction can support nodulation and pod fill, especially where soil conditions restrict root-level micronutrient uptake.

## Foliar Feeding as Part of a Broader Nutrition Plan
- Foliar feeding should follow, not replace, an assessment of soil fertility; use it to correct specific, identified gaps rather than as a routine blanket treatment.
- Combine foliar micronutrient correction with attention to the underlying soil cause where possible (e.g., pH correction for iron/zinc/manganese availability) for a more lasting solution.
- Keep simple records of what was applied, when, and the crop's response, to build a picture of which foliar treatments are actually improving yield or appearance on a given farm over time.

## Common Mistakes
- Using foliar feeding as the primary strategy to supply nitrogen, phosphorus, or potassium, when soil application is far more efficient for the bulk of macronutrient needs.
- Spraying during the hottest part of the day or in direct strong sunlight, leading to rapid drying, poor absorption, and increased risk of leaf scorch.
- Mixing foliar fertiliser with incompatible pesticides or biofertiliser inoculants without checking compatibility first.
- Applying overly concentrated solutions in an attempt to correct a deficiency faster, causing leaf burn instead.
- Relying on repeated foliar correction season after season without addressing the underlying soil condition (pH, drainage, organic matter) causing the recurring deficiency.

## When to Consult an Agricultural Extension Officer
- To confirm which nutrient deficiency is actually present before choosing a foliar product, ideally supported by a soil or tissue test.
- For guidance on locally available foliar fertiliser products, correct dilution rates, and compatible tank-mixing partners.
- If a deficiency recurs despite repeated foliar correction, to investigate and address the underlying soil cause.
- Before combining foliar fertiliser application with pesticide spraying, to confirm compatibility and appropriate timing relative to harvest.
