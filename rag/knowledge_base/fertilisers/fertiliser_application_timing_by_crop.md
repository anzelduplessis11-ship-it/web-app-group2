# Fertiliser Application Timing by Crop

## Overview
When fertiliser is applied often matters as much as how much is applied. Splitting total fertiliser needs across basal, topdress, and (where appropriate) foliar applications, timed to match a crop's changing nutrient demand through its growth cycle, improves nutrient-use efficiency, reduces losses, and generally increases yield compared with a single lump-sum application. This document sets out the general timing principles behind basal, topdress, and foliar application, and how these principles apply across major crop groups. It builds on `fertilisers/fertiliser_basics.md` and should be read alongside `fertilisers/npk_ratios_explained.md` for guidance on matching ratio to stage, and individual crop files under `crops/` for crop-specific detail.

## The Three Core Application Types

### Basal Application
- Applied at or shortly before planting, incorporated into the soil where developing roots will reach it.
- Typically supplies most or all of a crop's phosphorus (since phosphorus moves very little through soil and must be positioned near early roots), plus a portion of its nitrogen and potassium.
- Common basal fertilisers include DAP, single/triple superphosphate, and phosphorus-rich NPK compounds (e.g., 10-20-10, 8-24-24).
- Placement matters: banding fertiliser near but not touching the seed reduces germination damage while keeping nutrients accessible to roots as they grow.

### Topdressing
- Applied to the soil surface near the growing crop during the season, most commonly to supply nitrogen (and sometimes potassium) at the growth stages when the crop needs them most.
- Because nitrogen is mobile and prone to leaching and volatilisation losses, splitting it into 2-3 topdress applications timed to growth stages is generally more efficient than a single large basal dose.
- Timing is usually tied to observable growth stages (e.g., number of leaves, knee-high stage, start of flowering) rather than a fixed number of days, since growth rate varies with weather and management.

### Foliar Application
- A targeted, fast-acting spray application used mainly to correct diagnosed micronutrient deficiencies or to supplement nutrition during root stress or critical reproductive stages.
- Not a substitute for adequate basal and topdress soil fertility; see `fertilisers/foliar_feeding_guide.md` for full detail on method, timing, and compatibility.

## General Split-Application Principles
- **Phosphorus**: apply essentially all of it as basal fertiliser at or before planting; topdressing phosphorus later in the season is generally ineffective since it does not move to where new roots are growing.
- **Nitrogen**: split across the season, generally with a smaller portion (or none) at planting and larger portions during active vegetative growth and, for some crops, again approaching flowering; avoid applying the full seasonal nitrogen requirement at planting.
- **Potassium**: often split between basal and one or more topdress applications, with a general trend toward increasing potassium's relative share as the crop moves into flowering, fruiting, or tuber/grain-filling stages.
- **Micronutrients**: applied based on diagnosed need, either incorporated at basal application (for known field-level deficiencies) or as a targeted foliar spray once symptoms are observed during the season.

## Timing by Crop Group

### Cereals (Maize, Rice, Wheat, Sorghum, Millet)
- **Basal (at planting)**: full phosphorus requirement plus roughly a third to a half of total nitrogen and potassium, banded near the seed row.
- **First topdress (early vegetative stage, commonly 3-5 weeks after emergence depending on crop and climate)**: a substantial nitrogen application timed to support rapid leaf and stem growth.
- **Second topdress (pre-flowering/pre-heading stage, e.g., before tasseling in maize or before panicle initiation in rice)**: a further nitrogen application (and potassium, where soil levels are moderate to low) to support grain fill; this stage is often the most yield-critical timing for nitrogen in cereals.
- **Foliar (optional)**: zinc or other micronutrient correction around early-to-mid vegetative stage if deficiency is identified.
- Note: rice under flooded/paddy conditions has additional considerations around nitrogen timing and loss (denitrification, ammonia volatilisation from standing water); local extension guidance should be sought for paddy-specific timing.

### Legumes (Beans, Groundnut, Soybean, Cowpea, Chickpea, Green Gram)
- **Basal (at planting)**: phosphorus and potassium are the priority; nitrogen is usually applied only in a small "starter" amount, if at all, since well-nodulated legumes can fix a substantial share of their own nitrogen (see `fertilisers/biofertilisers_and_biochar.md` on rhizobium inoculation).
- **Topdress**: generally minimal for nitrogen; a small potassium topdress may be used on potassium-poor soils during flowering/pod-fill.
- **Foliar (optional)**: molybdenum or general micronutrient correction can support nodulation and pod fill, particularly on acidic soils where molybdenum availability is naturally lower.
- Over-applying nitrogen fertiliser to legumes can reduce the plant's own nitrogen fixation activity, so restraint on nitrogen timing/amount is a specific consideration for this crop group.

### Root and Tuber Crops (Cassava, Potato, Sweet Potato, Taro, Yam)
- **Basal (at planting)**: phosphorus and a portion of potassium, since strong early root/tuber initiation depends on phosphorus availability.
- **First topdress (early vegetative growth, before canopy closure)**: nitrogen to support leaf and vine development, which in turn drives the photosynthesis needed for tuber bulking later.
- **Second application (tuber initiation/bulking stage)**: potassium becomes the priority nutrient at this stage, since it strongly influences tuber size, starch/sugar accumulation, and storage quality; many recommendations shift the majority of potassium application to this stage rather than basal.
- Avoid heavy nitrogen application late in the season for tuber crops, since it can promote excessive leafy growth at the expense of tuber bulking and delay maturity.

### Leafy Vegetables (Kale, Cabbage, Spinach, Lettuce)
- **Basal (at planting/transplanting)**: a balanced or phosphorus-forward fertiliser to establish roots, since these crops are often transplanted as seedlings and need quick establishment.
- **Topdress (repeated through the growing period)**: nitrogen is the dominant need throughout, since the harvested product is vegetative leaf growth; light, frequent nitrogen topdressing (every 2-3 weeks depending on crop and cycle length) generally works better than one or two large doses.
- **Foliar (optional)**: nitrogen or micronutrient correction can give a rapid boost, particularly for continuously harvested crops (e.g., kale, spinach picked leaf-by-leaf over an extended period).
- Care should be taken not to apply nitrogen too close to harvest, both to avoid excess nitrate accumulation in leafy tissue and to respect any pre-harvest interval on the specific product used.

### Fruiting Vegetables (Tomato, Pepper, Eggplant, Cucumber, Okra, Pumpkin)
- **Basal (at planting/transplanting)**: balanced or phosphorus-forward fertiliser to establish strong roots before the plant moves into vegetative growth.
- **First topdress (vegetative growth, before flowering)**: nitrogen-focused to build a strong plant framework capable of supporting fruit load.
- **Second topdress/application (flowering through fruit development)**: shift toward a more balanced or potassium-heavier ratio; potassium supports fruit quality, while excess nitrogen at this stage can encourage continued leafy growth at the expense of flowering and fruit set.
- **Foliar (optional)**: calcium and boron sprays are commonly used during flowering and early fruit development to support fruit set and reduce disorders such as blossom-end rot.
- Repeated harvest crops (e.g., tomato, okra picked over many weeks) often benefit from continued smaller potassium/balanced topdressing through the harvest period to sustain fruit quality on later flushes.

### Perennial Tree and Plantation Crops (Mango, Avocado, Citrus, Coffee, Cocoa, Tea, Banana)
- **Young, non-bearing trees**: balanced fertiliser applications focused on establishing strong root and canopy growth, typically split into 2-3 applications across the growing season rather than a single annual dose.
- **Mature, bearing trees — pre-flowering**: an application (often balanced or phosphorus-supported) to support flower initiation and set.
- **Mature, bearing trees — fruit development**: a shift toward potassium-heavier fertiliser to support fruit sizing, sugar/oil accumulation, and quality; this is often the largest single nutrient demand period for bearing fruit trees.
- **Post-harvest**: a further application, particularly of nitrogen and potassium, is often used to help the tree recover reserves and prepare for the next flowering cycle.
- Exact number and timing of splits vary considerably by species, local climate (rainfall pattern, distinct dry/wet seasons), and orchard management system; the general principle — align higher nitrogen periods with vegetative flushes and higher potassium periods with fruit development — applies broadly.

## Summary Timing Table
| Crop group | Basal priority | Early-season topdress priority | Later-season priority | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Cereals | Phosphorus + partial N/K | Nitrogen (vegetative) | Nitrogen/potassium (pre-flowering/grain fill) | Two-split nitrogen topdressing common |
| Legumes | Phosphorus + potassium | Minimal nitrogen | Potassium (pod fill); micronutrients as needed | Avoid excess nitrogen; rely on nodulation |
| Root/tuber crops | Phosphorus + partial potassium | Nitrogen (vegetative) | Potassium (tuber bulking) | Avoid late heavy nitrogen |
| Leafy vegetables | Balanced/phosphorus-forward | Nitrogen (repeated, light doses) | Continue nitrogen; stop before harvest | Frequent, small doses suit continuous harvest |
| Fruiting vegetables | Balanced/phosphorus-forward | Nitrogen (vegetative) | Potassium/balanced (flowering, fruiting) | Foliar calcium/boron often used at flowering |
| Perennial fruit trees | Balanced (young trees) | Balanced/phosphorus (pre-flowering) | Potassium (fruit development); N/K post-harvest | Timing tied to flowering/fruiting cycle, not calendar months alone |

## Adjusting Timing to Local Conditions
- **Rainfall pattern**: in high-rainfall or irrigated systems, nitrogen losses through leaching are greater, favouring more, smaller split applications rather than fewer large ones.
- **Soil type**: sandy soils lose nutrients (especially nitrogen and potassium) faster than clay soils and generally benefit from more frequent, smaller applications.
- **Observed crop growth stage over calendar days**: growth stage (number of leaves, onset of flowering, canopy closure) is a more reliable timing cue than a fixed day count, since growth rate varies with temperature, moisture, and variety.
- **Soil test and residual fertility**: fields with known adequate residual phosphorus or potassium can reduce or skip basal applications of that nutrient, redirecting resources to nutrients still shown to be limiting.

## Common Mistakes
- Applying the entire season's fertiliser requirement in a single basal dose, leading to nitrogen losses and reduced efficiency, and potentially fertiliser burn risk near the seed.
- Applying phosphorus-rich fertiliser as a late-season topdressing, where it is largely wasted since it cannot move to actively growing roots.
- Continuing heavy nitrogen application into flowering and fruiting stages for fruiting vegetables and tree crops, promoting leafy growth at the expense of yield and quality.
- Ignoring the shift in nutrient priority toward potassium during flowering, fruiting, and tuber-bulking stages across nearly all crop groups.
- Using a fixed calendar schedule regardless of actual crop growth stage, missing the biologically relevant timing window for each application.

## When to Consult an Agricultural Extension Officer
- To fine-tune split-application timing and rates for a specific crop, variety, and local climate, especially where irrigation or unusual rainfall patterns affect nutrient loss risk.
- To interpret soil test results into a season-long application plan rather than a single blanket rate.
- If crop growth stages are difficult to identify precisely for timing decisions, particularly for less familiar or newly introduced crop varieties.
- To confirm currently available fertiliser products suited to each stage of the recommended split-application plan.
