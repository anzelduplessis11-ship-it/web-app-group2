# Soil Problem Troubleshooting

## Overview
This guide helps narrow down the likely cause of common soil problems by working through observable patterns in the field. It is a diagnostic starting point, not a substitute for a laboratory soil test or the judgement of an agricultural extension officer. Many soil problems (poor structure, low fertility, salinity) develop over multiple seasons and are corrected gradually rather than in a single intervention, so treat sudden or severe symptoms with more urgency than slow, long-standing patterns.

## Poor Drainage and Waterlogging

| Pattern Observed | Likely Cause(s) | Next Step |
|---|---|---|
| Water stands on the surface for hours or days after rain or irrigation | Compacted subsoil, high water table, or heavy clay content limiting infiltration | Dig a small inspection pit to check for a compacted layer or waterlogged subsoil; see `irrigation_system_troubleshooting.md` if irrigation is a contributing factor |
| Water pools in specific low-lying spots only, rest of field drains normally | Field topography directing runoff to that area | Consider drainage channels or minor land shaping directing water away from the low spot |
| Grey, blue-grey, or mottled soil colour when dug below the surface | Prolonged waterlogging causing anaerobic (oxygen-poor) conditions | Improve drainage before the next planting; avoid working the soil while saturated, as this worsens compaction |
| Roots of affected plants are dark, soft, or have a rotten smell | Root rot from prolonged waterlogged conditions | Improve drainage; avoid replanting the same area until the underlying drainage issue is addressed |
| Water infiltrates quickly in some parts of a field but pools in others of similar slope | Inconsistent soil texture or compaction from past traffic patterns (e.g., machinery paths) | Investigate compaction (see below) in the slow-draining areas specifically |

## Surface Crusting

| Pattern Observed | Likely Cause(s) | Next Step |
|---|---|---|
| Hard, thin crust on the soil surface after rain or irrigation, especially on sandy loam or silty soils | Surface sealing from raindrop impact on bare, low-organic-matter soil | Increase surface organic matter (mulch, compost, residue retention); avoid leaving soil bare between crops |
| Crust forms repeatedly despite mulching, seedlings struggle to emerge through it | Fine-textured surface soil with low aggregate stability | Consider light surface tillage timed just before or after crusting to assist emergence; build organic matter over successive seasons |
| Crusting concentrated where irrigation water lands directly (e.g., under sprinkler heads) | Physical impact of water droplets breaking down surface aggregates | Consider irrigation methods or emitters that reduce droplet impact force; see `irrigation_system_troubleshooting.md` |
| White or greyish crust with a powdery texture, not a hard crust | Possible salt accumulation at the surface rather than a physical crust | See salinity section below |

## Stunted or Patchy Growth Patterns

| Pattern Observed | Likely Cause(s) | Next Step |
|---|---|---|
| Uniform stunting across the whole field despite normal water and visible pest/disease absence | Widespread nutrient deficiency or generally poor soil fertility | Arrange a soil test if available; see `fertilisers/fertiliser_basics.md` |
| Stunting concentrated in a specific patch or strip, sharply bordered from healthy growth | Localised compaction, a buried obstruction, past chemical spill/residue, or a soil texture change | Dig an inspection pit at the boundary between healthy and stunted areas to compare soil structure and colour |
| Stunting following the pattern of old field boundaries, animal pens, or footpaths | Historic compaction or altered fertility from past land use | Consider deep ripping or subsoiling for compaction; build organic matter over time |
| Stunting in a low-lying area that is also slow to drain | Waterlogging limiting root oxygen and nutrient uptake | Address drainage first (see above); reassess growth response afterward |
| Stunting on a slope or eroded hillside, visible loss of topsoil | Soil erosion having removed fertile topsoil | Introduce erosion control measures (contour planting, terracing, cover crops); consult extension officer |
| Circular or expanding patch of stunted or dead plants, unrelated to visible pest damage | Possible soil-borne disease or nematode buildup in that spot | Consult extension officer for soil pest or disease diagnosis before replanting the same crop there |

## Soil Erosion

| Pattern Observed | Likely Cause(s) | Next Step |
|---|---|---|
| Visible rills or small channels cut into the soil surface after rain | Surface water runoff exceeding infiltration, especially on slopes with bare soil | Introduce contour ploughing, grass strips, or terracing; maintain ground cover |
| Exposed roots or a visible step where topsoil has thinned compared to surrounding undisturbed ground | Long-term topsoil loss from repeated erosion | Prioritise this area for cover cropping, mulching, and reduced tillage |
| Sediment deposits at the base of a slope or in field drainage lines | Erosion occurring upslope, being carried downhill | Address the upslope cause; consider sediment traps or vegetative buffer strips |
| Wind-blown soil visible on fences, roads, or downwind structures, especially in dry season on light soils | Wind erosion of exposed, dry, low-organic-matter soil | Establish windbreaks; maintain surface residue or cover crops, particularly before the dry/windy season |

## Salinity and Alkalinity

| Pattern Observed | Likely Cause(s) | Next Step |
|---|---|---|
| White, greyish, or crusty powdery deposits on the soil surface, especially after the soil dries | Salt accumulation at the surface, often from evaporation of saline irrigation water or a high saline water table | Consider a soil or water salinity test; review irrigation water source quality; improve drainage to help leach salts where feasible |
| Leaf tip and margin burning (scorch) on plants otherwise adequately watered | Possible salt stress affecting root water uptake | Cross-check with `crop_troubleshooting_guide.md` wilting/yellowing sections; consider salinity testing |
| Stunted, patchy growth concentrated in low-lying areas where water accumulates and evaporates | Salt concentration in areas of poor drainage | Improve drainage in the affected area; avoid over-irrigating with marginal-quality water |
| Soil feels slippery or soapy when wet, poor water infiltration despite reasonable texture | Possible sodic soil condition (high exchangeable sodium) | Consult extension officer or soil laboratory for confirmation and appropriate amendment options |

## Compaction

| Pattern Observed | Likely Cause(s) | Next Step |
|---|---|---|
| Difficulty pushing a rod, spade, or wire into the soil at a consistent depth (a hard layer) | Compacted layer, often from repeated machinery or livestock traffic at the same depth | Consider deep ripping or subsoiling at the depth of the compacted layer; avoid working the soil when wet |
| Water ponds and drains very slowly even on a gentle slope | Subsurface compaction restricting downward water movement | Investigate compaction depth with an inspection pit; plan mechanical or biological (deep-rooted cover crop) remediation |
| Roots observed to grow sideways or in a flattened mat rather than downward | Roots blocked by a compacted layer | Confirm compaction depth; consider subsoiling before the next planting |
| Compaction concentrated along regular lines across the field | Machinery wheel tracks | Consider controlled traffic patterns in future to limit compaction to fixed lanes |

## Low Organic Matter and Poor Soil Structure

| Pattern Observed | Likely Cause(s) | Next Step |
|---|---|---|
| Soil appears pale, loose, and crumbles into fine powder with little visible organic material | Long-term low organic matter input (residue removal or burning, minimal manure/compost use) | Increase organic matter inputs: crop residue retention, compost, manure, cover cropping |
| Soil forms large hard clods when dry and sticky mud when wet, difficult to work in either state | Poor soil aggregate structure, often linked to low organic matter and/or compaction | Build organic matter over successive seasons; avoid tillage at unsuitable moisture levels |
| Rapid drying of topsoil after rain, frequent irrigation needed | Low water-holding capacity, often linked to low organic matter or sandy texture | Increase organic matter and mulching to improve water retention |
| General decline in yields over several seasons on the same field despite adequate fertiliser use | Possible depletion of soil organic matter and biological activity | Consider a soil test; introduce crop rotation, cover crops, and organic matter inputs; consult extension officer |

## General Troubleshooting Principles
- Dig before deciding: many soil problems are only diagnosed correctly by examining a small inspection pit (30–60 cm deep) to observe colour, structure, compaction, and moisture at different depths, rather than surface appearance alone.
- Compare a suspected problem area directly against a healthy area of the same field to isolate what is actually different (colour, texture, compaction, drainage speed).
- Distinguish a widespread, uniform problem (often fertility- or management-related) from a sharply bounded patch (often localised compaction, drainage, an old structure, or a soil-borne pest/disease).
- Soil problems often interact: poor drainage worsens compaction risk, low organic matter worsens both crusting and drainage, and erosion removes the most fertile topsoil first. Addressing one factor in isolation may give limited improvement.
- Favour gradual, structural improvements (organic matter, cover cropping, reduced tillage, erosion control) over one-time fixes, since most soil physical and biological problems built up over multiple seasons.

## When to Consult an Agricultural Extension Officer or Soil Laboratory
- Before investing in significant remediation (deep ripping, drainage installation, chemical amendments) based on visual observation alone; a soil test helps confirm the diagnosis.
- When salinity, sodicity, or alkalinity is suspected, since these require accurate testing to select an appropriate and safe correction method.
- When a stunted patch appears with no clear physical explanation (drainage, compaction, erosion) and a soil-borne pest or disease is suspected.
- When planning major land-shaping, terracing, or drainage works that affect neighbouring fields or shared water flow.
- When soil problems persist across multiple seasons despite reasonable management changes.
