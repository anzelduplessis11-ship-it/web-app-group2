# Crop Troubleshooting Guide

## Overview
This guide helps narrow down the likely cause of common crop symptoms by working through observable patterns. It is a diagnostic starting point, not a definitive diagnosis; confirm with the relevant `diseases/` or `pests/` document, or an agricultural extension officer, before taking significant action.

## Yellowing Leaves

| Pattern Observed | Likely Cause(s) | Next Step |
|---|---|---|
| Uniform yellowing, starting from older/lower leaves | Nitrogen deficiency | Consider soil test and nitrogen topdress; see `fertilisers/fertiliser_basics.md` |
| Yellowing between veins, veins remain green, on older leaves | Magnesium deficiency | Consider soil test; correct with magnesium-containing amendment if confirmed |
| Yellowing in patches or mosaic/mottled pattern | Possible viral disease | Check relevant crop disease document (e.g., `diseases/cassava_mosaic_disease.md`, `diseases/maize_streak_virus.md`) and inspect for insect vectors |
| Yellowing with wilting, especially in waterlogged conditions | Root rot or waterlogging stress | Improve drainage; inspect roots for rot symptoms |
| Yellowing with visible small insects on leaf undersides | Aphid or whitefly feeding damage | See `pests/aphids.md` |

## Stunted Growth

| Pattern Observed | Likely Cause(s) | Next Step |
|---|---|---|
| Uniform stunting across the field | Poor soil fertility, compacted soil, or widespread water stress | Check soil test history; assess soil compaction and moisture |
| Patchy stunting in specific areas of a cereal field, plants otherwise appear stressed without clear pest/disease sign | Possible Striga (parasitic weed) infestation | See `weeds/striga.md`; check for emerging Striga plants |
| Stunting with visible root damage or unusual root galls | Possible nematode or soil pest damage | Consult extension officer for soil pest diagnosis |
| Stunting following transplanting | Transplant shock or root damage during transplanting | Ensure gentle handling and adequate watering after transplanting |

## Wilting

| Pattern Observed | Likely Cause(s) | Next Step |
|---|---|---|
| Wilting during hot part of day, recovering at night, soil dry | Normal drought stress response | Assess irrigation/watering needs |
| Wilting that does not recover even with adequate soil moisture | Possible vascular disease (e.g., bacterial or fungal wilt) or root damage | Inspect stem base and roots for discolouration; consult extension officer |
| Sudden wilting of individual plants scattered in the field | Possible stem borer or root pest damage | Inspect stem base for entry holes or larvae |
| Wilting spreading rapidly from a point source | Possible soil-borne disease spreading through the field | Isolate area if possible; consult extension officer promptly |

## Poor Flowering, Fruit Set, or Grain Fill

| Pattern Observed | Likely Cause(s) | Next Step |
|---|---|---|
| Poor pollination/fruit set during a period of high temperature | Heat stress during flowering | Consider planting date adjustment in future seasons; not correctable mid-season |
| Poor fruit set with excessive vegetative growth | Excess nitrogen fertiliser relative to phosphorus/potassium | Adjust fertiliser balance in future applications |
| Poor grain fill with visible leaf disease present | Disease reducing photosynthetic capacity during grain fill | Identify and manage the specific disease; see `diseases/` |
| Poor fruit set with visible pollinator scarcity | Possible pollinator decline (e.g., from broad-spectrum insecticide use nearby) | Review recent insecticide application timing and consider pollinator-friendly practices |

## Leaf Spots and Lesions

| Pattern Observed | Likely Cause(s) | Next Step |
|---|---|---|
| Water-soaked lesions, white fuzzy growth on leaf undersides, cool/humid weather | Possible late blight (in tomato/potato) | See `diseases/tomato_late_blight.md`; act promptly given rapid spread potential |
| Angular, brown lesions bounded by leaf veins | Possible bacterial leaf spot/blight | Avoid working in wet fields; consult `diseases/` documents or extension officer |
| Circular lesions with concentric rings ("target spot" pattern) | Possible early blight or similar fungal leaf spot disease | Consider crop rotation and resistant varieties for future seasons |
| Streak or stripe patterns parallel to leaf veins | Possible viral disease (e.g., maize streak virus) | See `diseases/maize_streak_virus.md`; check for leafhopper vectors |

## Storage Problems

| Pattern Observed | Likely Cause(s) | Next Step |
|---|---|---|
| Musty odour, visible mould on stored grain | Grain stored above safe moisture content | Remove affected grain; review drying practices; see `storage/grain_storage.md` |
| Small holes in grain, presence of small beetles/weevils | Storage insect infestation | Consider hermetic storage; review pre-storage drying |
| Rapid spoilage of stored fresh produce | Inadequate temperature/humidity management, or produce stored with damaged items | Review storage conditions; see `storage/perishable_storage.md` |

## General Troubleshooting Principles
- Consider multiple possible causes before acting, since several problems (nutrient deficiency, disease, water stress) can produce similar visible symptoms.
- Examine the pattern of the problem across the field (uniform vs. patchy, spreading vs. static) as an important diagnostic clue.
- Cross-reference suspected causes with the relevant detailed document in `diseases/`, `pests/`, `weeds/`, `fertilisers/`, or `soil/` before taking significant management action.
- When uncertain, or when a problem is spreading rapidly or affecting a large area, consult an agricultural extension officer or plant health specialist rather than guessing at a chemical or other intervention.

## When to Consult an Agricultural Extension Officer or Plant Health Specialist
- When symptoms do not clearly match a single likely cause after working through this guide.
- When a problem is spreading rapidly or affecting a significant portion of a field.
- Before applying any chemical treatment based on an uncertain diagnosis.
- When a suspected cause (e.g., viral disease, parasitic weed) has no direct curative treatment and requires a longer-term management strategy.
