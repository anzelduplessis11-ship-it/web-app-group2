# Seedling and Nursery Troubleshooting

## Overview
This guide helps narrow down the likely cause of common problems in seed beds, nursery trays, and young transplants by working through observable patterns. It is a diagnostic starting point, not a definitive diagnosis. Seedlings are especially vulnerable to small management errors (a day too wet, a slightly too-deep sowing), so review recent watering, sowing, and handling practices alongside the symptom before concluding a cause. Confirm with the relevant `diseases/` or `pests/` document, or an extension officer, before applying any chemical treatment in a nursery.

## Poor or Uneven Germination

| Pattern Observed | Likely Cause(s) | Next Step |
|---|---|---|
| Very few or no seeds germinate across the whole tray/bed | Old or poor-quality seed, seed stored improperly, or incorrect sowing depth | Check seed source and expiry/viability; conduct a simple germination test on a sample before resowing |
| Germination patchy, some cells/rows emerge well and others not at all | Uneven sowing depth, uneven watering, or uneven seed-to-soil contact | Review sowing technique for consistency; ensure even, gentle watering across the whole tray/bed |
| Seeds swell but fail to emerge, appear rotten when dug up | Soil kept too wet, poor drainage in the growing medium, or fungal seed rot | Reduce watering frequency; improve growing medium drainage; consider a treated or fresh seed batch |
| Germination very slow compared to expected timeframe for the species | Low temperature, poor seed-to-soil contact, or seed dormancy not broken (for species requiring pre-treatment) | Check ambient/soil temperature against the crop's requirements; check whether the species needs soaking, scarification, or other pre-treatment |
| Seeds visibly missing or disturbed, empty depressions in the seedbed | Birds, rodents, or ants removing seed | Consider covering the seedbed (netting, mulch) until emergence; consult extension officer on non-chemical deterrents first |
| Seedlings emerge but immediately collapse at soil level | Possible damping-off; see next section | See "Damping-Off" below |

## Damping-Off

| Pattern Observed | Likely Cause(s) | Next Step |
|---|---|---|
| Seedlings collapse suddenly at the soil line, stem appears water-soaked, thin, or pinched | Damping-off, a fungal disease favoured by overly wet, poorly drained, and poorly ventilated nursery conditions | Remove and discard affected seedlings immediately; reduce watering frequency; improve air circulation and drainage in the seed tray/bed |
| Damping-off spreading outward from an initial cluster of affected seedlings | Fungal spread through wet growing medium or standing water between cells/rows | Isolate or discard the affected section of the tray/bed if practical; avoid overhead watering that splashes between cells |
| Recurring damping-off across successive nursery batches in the same tray/bed/soil | Contaminated growing medium, unclean trays, or reused soil without treatment | Use fresh, well-draining growing medium; clean and, where appropriate, disinfect trays and tools between batches |
| White or greyish fuzzy growth visible at the base of collapsed seedlings | Visible fungal growth confirming a damping-off-type disease | Remove affected seedlings and surrounding growing medium; consult extension officer if losses are extensive |

## Leggy or Weak Seedlings

| Pattern Observed | Likely Cause(s) | Next Step |
|---|---|---|
| Seedlings tall, thin, and pale, stems weak and prone to flopping over | Insufficient light, causing seedlings to stretch toward available light | Increase light exposure; if under cover, move trays closer to light or thin overcrowded seedlings |
| Seedlings overcrowded in the tray/bed, competing visibly for space | Sowing density too high | Thin seedlings to the recommended spacing; sow at wider spacing in future batches |
| Seedlings weak with thin stems despite adequate light | Excess warmth without corresponding light increase, or excess nitrogen relative to light availability | Review nursery temperature and ventilation; avoid heavy nitrogen feeding at the seedling stage |
| Seedlings lean consistently in one direction | Light source only from one side (e.g., a single window or shade edge) | Rotate trays regularly or improve even light distribution |

## Yellowing, Purpling, or Discoloured Seedlings

| Pattern Observed | Likely Cause(s) | Next Step |
|---|---|---|
| Uniform pale yellow-green colour across most seedlings | Nitrogen deficiency in the growing medium, or growing medium exhausted of nutrients | Consider a dilute, seedling-appropriate feed; review growing medium fertility |
| Purplish tinge on leaves and stems, seedlings otherwise slow-growing | Possible phosphorus deficiency, often linked to cold growing medium temperature limiting nutrient uptake | Check nursery temperature; review growing medium nutrient content |
| Yellowing between leaf veins, veins remaining green | Possible micronutrient deficiency (e.g., iron or magnesium) or growing medium pH imbalance | Check growing medium pH if testing is available; consider a balanced seedling feed |
| Yellowing and wilting together, growing medium visibly waterlogged | Overwatering leading to root oxygen stress or root rot | Reduce watering frequency; improve tray/bed drainage; check for root rot when leggy |
| Sudden overall yellowing after a feed application | Possible fertiliser burn from an overly strong or poorly diluted feed | Flush the growing medium with plain water; dilute feed further next time |

## Wilting or Collapse Before Transplanting

| Pattern Observed | Likely Cause(s) | Next Step |
|---|---|---|
| Wilting during the heat of the day, recovering by evening | Normal moisture stress response, possibly needing more frequent watering | Increase watering frequency slightly; check tray/bed is not in excessive direct heat |
| Wilting that does not recover even after watering | Root damage, root rot from overwatering, or root-bound seedlings in an undersized container | Check roots for rot (soft, dark, smelly) or root-binding (roots circling tightly); adjust watering or container size accordingly |
| Sudden wilting of isolated seedlings scattered through the tray/bed | Possible soil-dwelling pest (e.g., cutworm) or localised damping-off | Inspect the base of affected seedlings and surrounding soil for pests or fungal signs |
| Wilting following handling or pricking out (transferring from tray to individual pots) | Root disturbance during transplanting | Handle roots gently in future; ensure adequate shade and watering for a few days after pricking out |

## Transplant Shock After Moving to the Field

| Pattern Observed | Likely Cause(s) | Next Step |
|---|---|---|
| Seedlings droop and appear stressed for several days after transplanting, then recover | Normal transplant shock from root disturbance and change in environment | Provide shade and consistent watering for the first several days; avoid transplanting during the hottest part of the day |
| Seedlings fail to recover and eventually die within days of transplanting | Severe root damage during transplanting, transplanting into unsuitably hot/dry/waterlogged conditions, or insufficient hardening-off beforehand | Review hardening-off procedure (gradual exposure to outdoor conditions before transplanting) for future batches |
| Transplanted seedlings scorched or bleached on leaves after moving from shaded nursery to full sun | Insufficient hardening-off to direct sunlight | Gradually increase light exposure over several days before transplanting in future |
| Poor establishment specifically in seedlings that were root-bound in their nursery container | Roots circling tightly in an undersized pot, restricting outward growth after transplanting | Gently loosen or tease out circling roots at transplanting; use appropriately sized containers and timely transplanting in future |
| Transplanted seedlings toppled over or damaged shortly after moving to the field | Wind exposure, inadequate firming of soil around the base, or pest damage | Firm soil gently around the base after transplanting; consider temporary windbreaks in exposed sites |

## Pest Damage in the Nursery

| Pattern Observed | Likely Cause(s) | Next Step |
|---|---|---|
| Seedlings cut off at or just below soil level overnight | Cutworm damage | Inspect soil around the base of affected seedlings for curled larvae; see relevant `pests/` document |
| Small holes chewed in leaves, tiny insects visible on close inspection | General chewing/sap-sucking pest activity | Identify the pest before treatment; see `pests/` documents for the specific insect |
| Sticky residue on leaves with ants present | Possible aphid or similar sap-sucking pest producing honeydew, attracting ants | See `pests/aphids.md`; ants alone are not the primary cause |
| Seedlings disturbed or uprooted with soil visibly dug or scratched | Birds or small animals foraging in the nursery bed | Consider netting or covering the bed until seedlings are established |

## General Troubleshooting Principles
- Check watering practice first: overwatering (leading to damping-off, root rot, and yellowing) and underwatering (leading to wilting and poor germination) are the two most common root causes of nursery problems and should be reviewed before assuming a disease or pest.
- Ensure good air circulation and avoid overcrowding; dense, still, humid conditions favour damping-off and other fungal problems.
- Use clean, fresh growing medium and clean containers for each new batch where damping-off or other soil-borne problems have occurred previously.
- Harden off seedlings gradually before transplanting: sudden exposure to full sun, wind, or temperature swings is a common and preventable cause of transplant shock.
- Compare affected seedlings to healthy ones in the same tray/bed to help distinguish a localised issue (pest, local overwatering) from a systemic one (feed, growing medium, general watering routine).

## When to Consult an Agricultural Extension Officer
- When germination failure or seedling losses are widespread and the cause is not clear after reviewing watering, sowing depth, and seed quality.
- When damping-off or another disease recurs across successive nursery batches despite improved hygiene and watering practices.
- Before applying any fungicide, pesticide, or chemical treatment in the nursery.
- When a nutrient deficiency is suspected but does not resolve with a standard seedling feed, as growing medium pH or a less common deficiency may be involved.
- When planning nursery practices for a new or unfamiliar crop species, particularly around seed pre-treatment, hardening-off duration, or transplant timing.
