# Storage Problem Troubleshooting

## Overview
This guide helps narrow down the likely cause of common problems in stored grain, legumes, and other dried produce by working through observable patterns. It is a diagnostic starting point, not a definitive diagnosis. Stored product problems can spread quickly through a bulk (heating, mould, and pest infestation all tend to accelerate once started), so act promptly on early signs rather than waiting. Never consume or sell produce showing mould growth, unusual odour, or suspected contamination without proper assessment, and consult an extension officer for anything uncertain.

## Heating in the Grain Bulk

| Pattern Observed | Likely Cause(s) | Next Step |
|---|---|---|
| Grain feels distinctly warm to the touch when hand-checked, especially toward the centre of the bulk | Active respiration from grain stored above safe moisture content, insect activity, or fungal growth generating heat | Check moisture content if a meter is available; turn or aerate the grain to release heat; isolate the affected portion |
| Warm, moist air or condensation noticeable when a storage container/bag is opened | Moisture migration within the bulk, often from temperature differences between day and night | Improve ventilation; avoid sealing grain that has not been adequately dried; consider periodic aeration |
| Localised hot spot in one part of a large bulk while the rest feels normal | Localised pocket of high-moisture grain, pest infestation, or an entry point for water (leak, damaged roof/wall) | Investigate and remove the affected pocket; check the structure for the source of moisture ingress |
| Grain temperature rising over successive days despite normal storage conditions when first stored | Progressive insect or mould activity increasing heat output | Inspect closely for pests and mould (see sections below); consider immediate turning, aeration, or re-drying |
| Caking or clumping of grain in the warm area | Moisture and heat causing grains to stick together, often with early mould development | Break up and inspect clumps; separate and dry affected grain before it affects the wider bulk |

## Pest Infestation

| Pattern Observed | Likely Cause(s) | Next Step |
|---|---|---|
| Small holes bored into individual grains, fine powdery dust (frass) present | Weevils or other internal-feeding grain beetles | Sieve out visibly damaged grain and dust; consider hermetic (airtight) storage to control ongoing infestation |
| Small moths flying up when the storage container is disturbed, webbing or silk strands visible in the grain | Moth infestation (e.g., grain moth larvae) | Inspect for larvae and webbing throughout the bulk; remove and isolate affected grain; improve container sealing |
| Live beetles or larvae visible crawling on the grain surface or container walls | Active insect infestation | Identify the pest if possible; consider appropriate storage pest control measures (e.g., hermetic bags, approved storage protectants) after consulting extension guidance |
| Infestation recurring in the same storage structure across seasons despite cleaning | Residual pests surviving in cracks, old grain residue, or the storage structure itself | Thoroughly clean and, where appropriate, treat the empty structure before restocking; repair cracks and gaps |
| Rodent droppings, gnaw marks on bags or containers, or shredded packaging material | Rodent activity | Address entry points and food/nesting attraction; consider traps or other control measures; avoid storing food-grade produce near evident rodent activity without protective barriers |
| Insects present mainly near the surface or edges of the bulk, less so toward the centre | Infestation entering from outside the storage structure or from previously contaminated bags | Inspect and treat the storage structure and any incoming bags before adding new produce |

## Mould and Musty Smell

| Pattern Observed | Likely Cause(s) | Next Step |
|---|---|---|
| Visible mould growth (fuzzy, coloured patches) on grain or produce surface | Storage at moisture content above the safe threshold for the crop, often combined with poor ventilation | Remove and discard visibly mouldy material; do not mix with unaffected produce; review drying practice before the next storage cycle |
| Musty or sour odour noticeable on opening the storage container, no visible mould yet | Early-stage mould or fungal activity, or moisture buildup within the bulk | Inspect more closely for early mould signs; improve ventilation; consider re-drying if moisture content is in doubt |
| Mould concentrated near the walls, floor, or roof of the storage structure | Condensation or moisture ingress at that specific point (structural leak, poor insulation, ground moisture) | Identify and repair the moisture entry point; keep produce raised off the floor and away from walls where possible |
| Mould appearing shortly after a period of humid weather or rain | Ambient humidity entering the storage structure, or produce not adequately dried beforehand | Improve sealing/ventilation appropriate to storage type; verify produce was dried to a safe moisture level before storage |
| Discoloration or unusual smell that persists after removing visibly affected material | Possible mould contamination has spread through the bulk, including produce not yet visibly affected | Consider the whole batch compromised; consult extension officer before deciding whether any portion remains safe to use or sell, given the health risk of some fungal contaminants |

## Excess Moisture and Condensation

| Pattern Observed | Likely Cause(s) | Next Step |
|---|---|---|
| Droplets of water visible on the inside of storage bags, containers, or structure walls | Condensation from temperature fluctuation between day and night, especially if produce was not fully cooled or dried before sealing | Improve ventilation; avoid sealing produce immediately after harvest without adequate drying and cooling |
| Grain feels noticeably soft, rubbery, or bends rather than snapping/cracking when tested | Moisture content too high for safe storage | Re-dry before storing further; check with a moisture meter if available |
| Produce stored in bags directly on damp ground shows dampness at the base | Moisture wicking up from the ground into the storage bags | Store on raised pallets, platforms, or a moisture barrier rather than directly on the ground |
| General dampness throughout the storage structure, especially during humid seasons | Inadequate structure ventilation or sealing against ambient humidity | Review structure design; consider ventilation improvements or a more suitable storage structure for the local climate |

## Off Odours Not Linked to Mould

| Pattern Observed | Likely Cause(s) | Next Step |
|---|---|---|
| Chemical or unusual odour, produce recently treated with a storage protectant | Normal residual smell of an approved treatment, or possible over-application | Check the treatment was applied at the recommended rate; consult extension officer if the odour seems unusually strong |
| Rancid smell, particularly in oil-rich produce (e.g., groundnuts, some oilseeds) | Oxidation of oils in the produce, often linked to prolonged storage, heat exposure, or old stock | Prioritise use or sale of older stock; store oil-rich produce in cooler, more stable conditions where possible |
| Fermented or sour smell in stored fresh or semi-dried produce | Microbial fermentation from moisture content too high for the intended storage method | Assess moisture content; adjust drying or consider a storage method suited to the actual moisture level |
| Strong, unfamiliar odour with no clear explanation | Possible contamination from nearby chemicals, fuel, or an unrelated stored substance | Investigate anything stored nearby that could taint the produce; separate incompatible stored items in future |

## Structural and Handling-Related Problems

| Pattern Observed | Likely Cause(s) | Next Step |
|---|---|---|
| Bags or containers show signs of physical damage (tears, crushing) with spillage | Poor stacking practice, overloading, or handling damage | Review stacking height and handling procedures; repair or replace damaged containers promptly to avoid pest/moisture entry |
| Produce nearest to a wall, window, or roof consistently more affected by heat, moisture, or pests than produce in the centre | Localised structural weakness (leak, poor insulation, gap allowing pest entry) | Inspect and repair the specific structural point; consider keeping produce a small distance from external walls |
| Uneven drying or spoilage pattern correlating with sun exposure through a window or gap | Direct sunlight or heat affecting one part of the storage area unevenly | Block or shade the exposure point; rotate stored produce if practical |
| Recurrent problems each season despite similar management practices | Underlying structural design issue (poor ventilation, inadequate damp-proofing, insufficient pest-proofing) | Consider a more thorough review or upgrade of the storage structure; consult extension officer on suitable local designs |

## General Troubleshooting Principles
- Confirm produce is dried to a safe moisture level before storage; the majority of heating, mould, and rapid spoilage problems trace back to storage above the safe moisture threshold for that crop.
- Inspect stored produce periodically rather than only at the point of removal; early signs of heating, pests, or moisture are far easier to manage than an advanced problem affecting the whole bulk.
- Keep the storage structure clean, dry, well-ventilated, and free of cracks or gaps that allow pest entry or moisture ingress, including thorough cleaning of the structure between storage cycles.
- Isolate and inspect any newly harvested or newly purchased produce before adding it to existing stock, since contamination or infestation is often introduced this way.
- Never mix affected (mouldy, heavily infested, or excessively damp) produce back into a clean batch; this risks contaminating the entire stock.
- Keep basic records of storage dates and any treatments applied, both for food safety and to help identify recurring seasonal patterns.

## When to Consult an Agricultural Extension Officer
- Whenever mould is found on produce intended for consumption or sale, to assess whether any portion of the batch remains safe to use.
- When an infestation recurs across storage cycles despite cleaning and control measures.
- Before applying any chemical storage protectant, to confirm correct product choice, rate, and safety precautions.
- When heating is observed in a large bulk and the cause is not clear after checking moisture and inspecting for pests, since this can indicate a developing problem that spreads quickly.
- When planning or upgrading storage structures, to select a design suited to the local climate and the specific produce being stored.
