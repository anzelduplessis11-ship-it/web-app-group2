# Irrigation System Troubleshooting

## Overview
This guide helps narrow down the likely cause of common irrigation system problems across drip, sprinkler, and surface/furrow systems by working through observable patterns. It is a diagnostic starting point, not a substitute for a qualified irrigation technician. Water is often a scarce and shared resource, so diagnose the actual fault before making changes, and be cautious with any repair involving electrical pump components or pressurised pipework.

## Clogged Drip Emitters and Lines

| Pattern Observed | Likely Cause(s) | Next Step |
|---|---|---|
| Individual emitters not dripping while nearby emitters work normally | Localised blockage from sediment, mineral scale, or biofilm in that emitter | Remove and clean or replace the individual emitter; flush the lateral line |
| Widespread reduced or no flow at emitters across a whole line, pressure at the mainline appears normal | Blockage building up along the line, often from sand, silt, or algae | Flush the line by opening end caps and running water through until it runs clear; check filter condition |
| Emitters clogging repeatedly and frequently after cleaning | Untreated or poor-quality water source (high sediment, organic matter, or dissolved minerals) | Review filtration adequacy; consider a finer filter mesh or additional pre-filtration; have water tested if scaling is suspected |
| White or orange crusty deposits around emitter outlets | Mineral scaling from hard water (calcium/iron deposits) | Consider periodic acid flushing (seek guidance on safe products and concentrations) or a chemical injection system; consult irrigation specialist |
| Slimy residue inside lines when cut open, greenish tint in water source | Algae or bacterial biofilm growth | Improve filtration; consider periodic chlorination flush; shield open water sources from direct sunlight where possible |

## Uneven Water Distribution Across the Field

| Pattern Observed | Likely Cause(s) | Next Step |
|---|---|---|
| Plants nearest the pump/water source thrive, plants furthest away show water stress | Pressure loss along the line, often from pipe length, undersized pipe diameter, or elevation change | Check system design against manufacturer pressure/flow specifications; consider pressure-compensating emitters or a pressure regulator |
| Patchy wet and dry spots scattered irregularly across a drip or sprinkler system | Mixture of clogged and unclogged emitters, or emitters of inconsistent age/type on the same line | Inspect and clean/replace individual emitters; avoid mixing emitter types with different flow rates on one line |
| One section or block of the field consistently under-watered while others are fine | Partially closed valve, kinked pipe, or a break/leak diverting flow before that section | Trace the line from the valve to the affected section checking for kinks, closed valves, or leaks |
| Sprinkler system leaves dry gaps between coverage circles | Incorrect sprinkler spacing or worn/mismatched nozzles | Review spacing against sprinkler throw radius; replace worn nozzles with matched specifications |
| Higher ground consistently drier than low-lying areas under surface/furrow irrigation | Uneven field levelling or slope | Consider land levelling or adjusting furrow layout; consult extension officer on suitable methods for the field |

## Low Flow, No Flow, or Pump Problems

| Pattern Observed | Likely Cause(s) | Next Step |
|---|---|---|
| Pump runs but little or no water is delivered | Possible air lock, blocked foot valve/strainer, or worn impeller | Check and clear the foot valve/strainer; check for suction line leaks allowing air in; if problem persists, pump may need servicing |
| Pump does not start at all | Possible electrical supply issue, tripped breaker, blown fuse, or motor fault | Check power supply and breaker/fuse first; do not open the motor casing unless qualified to do so; consult a technician |
| Pump starts and stops repeatedly (cycling) | Possible pressure switch fault, leak in the system, or waterlogged/undersized pressure tank | Inspect for leaks along the mainline; consult irrigation technician if the pressure switch or tank is suspected |
| Unusual noise (grinding, knocking) or vibration from the pump | Possible cavitation, worn bearings, or debris in the impeller | Stop the pump and inspect if safe to do so; consult a technician before continuing operation, as continued running can cause further damage |
| Flow gradually declining over weeks or months | Gradual clogging of filters/screens, or declining source water level (well, borehole, reservoir) | Check filter and screen condition; check source water level; consult extension officer if the water source itself appears to be declining |
| Water delivered but pressure much lower than expected | Leak somewhere in the mainline, or filter/screen partially blocked | Walk the mainline checking for wet patches or visible leaks; clean or replace filters |

## Leaks and Pipe Damage

| Pattern Observed | Likely Cause(s) | Next Step |
|---|---|---|
| Wet patch or pooling water along a buried or surface pipe route with no rain | Pipe crack, joint failure, or rodent/animal damage | Excavate or expose the section carefully; repair or replace the damaged section and fitting |
| Sudden drop in system pressure with no other explanation | Significant leak or burst pipe somewhere in the system | Shut off the water supply at the source; inspect the full line systematically from source to end before restarting |
| Fittings or connectors showing cracking, especially in older polyethylene pipe exposed to sun | UV degradation of exposed pipe/fittings | Replace affected sections; where possible, bury or shade pipe runs exposed to direct sunlight |
| Recurrent leaks at the same joint after repair | Incorrect fitting size, poor joint technique, or excess pressure at that point | Recheck fitting compatibility and installation method; consider a pressure regulator if pressure is consistently high |

## Waterlogging or Puddling in the Field

| Pattern Observed | Likely Cause(s) | Next Step |
|---|---|---|
| Standing water after each irrigation, slow to drain | Over-irrigation relative to crop need and soil infiltration rate, or poor field drainage | Reduce irrigation duration/frequency and monitor soil moisture; see `soil_problem_troubleshooting.md` for drainage issues |
| Puddling concentrated near emitters or sprinkler heads only | Emitter/nozzle flow rate too high for the soil's infiltration rate | Consider emitters or nozzles with a lower flow rate suited to the soil type |
| Waterlogging in low-lying parts of the field only | Field topography directing runoff/drainage to that area | Consider drainage channels or land shaping; consult extension officer |
| Persistent wet soil between irrigation events, plants showing waterlogging stress | Possible high water table, compacted subsoil, or leak from a buried line | Check for a nearby leak first; if none found, consider a soil profile assessment for compaction or drainage issues |

## Filter and Water Quality Problems

| Pattern Observed | Likely Cause(s) | Next Step |
|---|---|---|
| Filter needs cleaning very frequently (daily or more) | High sediment or organic load in the water source | Consider adding a pre-filter stage (e.g., settling tank or sand media filter) appropriate to the water source |
| Pressure gauge before and after the filter shows a large pressure drop | Filter is clogged and restricting flow | Clean or replace the filter element/screen immediately |
| Sand or silt visibly present in emitters or at line ends after flushing | Filtration inadequate for the sediment load of the source | Reassess filter mesh size and type against water source characteristics |
| Corrosion or unusual deposits inside metal fittings | Water chemistry (pH, dissolved salts) reacting with system materials | Consider a water quality test if this is recurring; consult irrigation specialist on suitable materials or treatment |

## General Troubleshooting Principles
- Work systematically from the water source toward the field: check the pump and filter first, then the mainline, then the laterals, then individual emitters or sprinkler heads, since an upstream problem affects everything downstream.
- Compare affected areas to unaffected areas in the same system; a fault that is isolated to one section usually points to a local blockage, leak, or valve issue rather than a pump or source problem.
- Keep a basic maintenance routine (regular filter cleaning, periodic line flushing, seasonal inspection) since most chronic problems in drip and sprinkler systems stem from a gradual buildup that routine flushing would have prevented.
- Match any replacement part (emitter, nozzle, fitting) to the original specification; mixing incompatible flow rates or pressure ratings is a common cause of uneven distribution.
- Record system pressure and flow readings when the system is working well, so that a later drop can be recognised and measured rather than guessed at.

## When to Consult an Irrigation Technician or Extension Officer
- When a pump shows electrical faults, unusual noise, or requires internal servicing.
- When a water quality problem (scaling, sediment, corrosion) is recurring despite routine filter maintenance.
- When redesigning system layout, spacing, or component selection for a new or expanding field.
- When waterlogging or drainage problems persist despite adjusting irrigation scheduling.
- When uncertain whether a problem originates from the irrigation system itself or from an underlying soil or field condition.
