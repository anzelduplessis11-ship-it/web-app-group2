# Livestock Troubleshooting Guide

## Overview
This guide helps narrow down the likely cause of common livestock symptoms by working through observable patterns in cattle, goats, sheep, pigs, and poultry. It is a diagnostic starting point, not a definitive diagnosis. Livestock health problems can escalate quickly and some causes are zoonotic (transmissible to humans) or highly contagious to other animals, so confirm with a veterinarian or livestock extension officer before taking significant action, and always do so promptly when a sick animal is isolated, in visible pain, or not improving.

## Off Feed / Reduced Appetite

| Pattern Observed | Likely Cause(s) | Next Step |
|---|---|---|
| Gradual reduction in feed intake across several animals, otherwise appear normal | Feed quality decline, feed change, or reduced palatability | Inspect feed/fodder for mould, spoilage, or contamination; review any recent ration change |
| Sudden refusal to eat in a single animal, appears dull or lethargic | Possible early sign of infection, fever, or internal pain | Take rectal temperature if safe to do so; isolate animal; monitor closely |
| Off feed with grinding teeth, excessive salivation, or reluctance to chew | Possible mouth sores, foreign object, or dental problem | Examine mouth and teeth carefully; check for foot-and-mouth disease signs (see below) |
| Off feed with bloated or distended abdomen | Possible bloat, especially after grazing lush legume pasture | Encourage movement; consult veterinarian urgently if severe distension or distress |
| Off feed across the whole herd/flock at once | Possible water shortage, heat stress, or feed/water contamination | Check water supply and quality first; check feed troughs for contamination |

## Lameness and Foot Problems

| Pattern Observed | Likely Cause(s) | Next Step |
|---|---|---|
| Limping on one leg, swelling or heat in a joint | Possible injury, sprain, or joint infection | Rest animal on soft, clean bedding; consult veterinarian if swelling persists or worsens |
| Lameness with foul odour and softened tissue between the claws (cattle, goats, sheep) | Possible foot rot | Isolate affected animal; keep standing area dry; consult veterinarian for treatment |
| Lameness affecting several animals at once, with blisters or erosions in the mouth and on feet | Possible foot-and-mouth disease | Isolate herd/flock immediately; report to veterinary authority without delay; this is a notifiable disease in most regions |
| Overgrown, cracked, or misshapen hooves | Inadequate hoof wear or trimming | Arrange routine hoof trimming; review flooring/paddock surface |
| Lameness in young, fast-growing animals, bowed or swollen leg joints | Possible nutritional deficiency (e.g., calcium/phosphorus imbalance) or developmental joint problem | Review mineral supplementation; consult extension officer or veterinarian |

## Reduced Milk Yield

| Pattern Observed | Likely Cause(s) | Next Step |
|---|---|---|
| Gradual decline across the whole herd over several weeks | Declining forage quality/quantity, seasonal feed shortage | Review feed ration energy and protein content; consider supplementary feeding |
| Sudden drop in yield in one animal, udder hot, swollen, or painful | Possible mastitis | Check milk for clots, discolouration, or abnormal odour; consult veterinarian promptly; avoid mixing milk from affected animal with the bulk supply |
| Sudden drop across several animals at once, with fever or other illness signs | Possible infectious disease outbreak | Isolate affected animals; consult veterinarian; review recent introductions of new animals |
| Decline following a stressful event (transport, weaning of calf, change of handler) | Stress-related reduction in milk let-down | Maintain calm, consistent milking routine; allow time to recover |
| Yield lower than expected despite good body condition and feed | Possible incomplete milking, poor milking technique, or early stage of lactation decline | Review milking routine and frequency; consult extension officer on lactation curve expectations |

## Diarrhoea (Scours)

| Pattern Observed | Likely Cause(s) | Next Step |
|---|---|---|
| Watery diarrhoea in young animals (calves, kids, lambs, piglets), often with dehydration | Possible scours from bacterial, viral, or parasitic infection, or poor colostrum intake | Prioritise rehydration (oral rehydration solution); isolate affected animal; consult veterinarian, especially in young or multiple animals |
| Diarrhoea with visible blood or mucus | Possible coccidiosis or severe bacterial/parasitic infection | Consult veterinarian promptly; do not delay in young animals as dehydration can be rapid |
| Soft, dark diarrhoea following a sudden change to lush or grain-heavy feed | Dietary upset from abrupt feed change | Reintroduce feed changes gradually in future; monitor and provide clean water |
| Recurrent diarrhoea in grazing animals, poor body condition, rough coat | Possible internal parasite (worm) burden | Consider faecal egg count if available; consult extension officer on deworming programme |
| Diarrhoea affecting many animals across the herd/flock simultaneously | Possible contaminated water source or widespread infectious outbreak | Check and secure clean water source; consult veterinarian; consider isolating the group |

## Coughing, Nasal Discharge, and Laboured Breathing

| Pattern Observed | Likely Cause(s) | Next Step |
|---|---|---|
| Occasional coughing, clear nasal discharge, animal otherwise active | Dust, mild irritation, or early mild respiratory infection | Monitor; improve ventilation and reduce dust in housing |
| Persistent cough, thick or discoloured nasal discharge, reduced appetite | Possible respiratory infection (bacterial or viral pneumonia) | Isolate animal; keep warm and dry; consult veterinarian |
| Rapid or laboured breathing, animal reluctant to move, especially in hot weather | Possible heat stress | Provide shade, water, and ventilation immediately; this can become an emergency |
| Coughing and poor growth in young animals kept in crowded, poorly ventilated housing | Possible respiratory disease linked to housing conditions | Improve stocking density and ventilation; consult veterinarian for affected animals |
| Sudden onset of severe respiratory distress across multiple animals | Possible serious infectious respiratory disease outbreak | Isolate affected group; consult veterinarian urgently; restrict movement of animals on and off the farm |

## Skin, Coat, and External Parasite Problems

| Pattern Observed | Likely Cause(s) | Next Step |
|---|---|---|
| Rough, dull coat, poor body condition | Possible nutritional deficiency or internal parasite burden | Review ration; consider deworming assessment |
| Visible ticks, lice, or mites, with scratching or rubbing behaviour | External parasite infestation | Consult extension officer on appropriate parasite control measures for the species |
| Patchy hair loss with scaly or crusty skin | Possible mange, ringworm (fungal), or other skin infection | Isolate affected animal where practical, as some causes are contagious to other animals and humans; consult veterinarian |
| Swellings or lumps under the skin, especially around the neck or shoulders in cattle | Possible abscess or, in some regions, notifiable disease (e.g., lumpy skin disease if accompanied by fever and nodules) | Consult veterinarian promptly; do not attempt to lance or treat unknown lumps without guidance |
| Sunburn or skin irritation on light-skinned or hairless areas | Photosensitisation or sun exposure | Provide shade; consult veterinarian if skin damage is severe or animal shows other illness signs |

## Reproductive Problems

| Pattern Observed | Likely Cause(s) | Next Step |
|---|---|---|
| Repeated return to service / failure to conceive | Possible nutritional deficiency, reproductive disease, or management timing issue | Review body condition and breeding records; consult veterinarian or extension officer |
| Abortion or stillbirth, especially if occurring in more than one animal | Possible infectious reproductive disease | Handle aborted material with care (some causes are zoonotic); isolate the animal; consult veterinarian promptly; keep other pregnant animals away from the area |
| Difficult birthing (dystocia) | Possible malpresentation, oversized foetus, or narrow pelvis | Seek experienced assistance or veterinary help promptly; prolonged difficult labour risks the life of both dam and offspring |
| Retained afterbirth beyond normal expected time | Possible uterine infection risk | Consult veterinarian; monitor for fever or discharge |
| Newborn weak, slow to stand, or fails to suckle | Possible difficult birth, low colostrum intake, or hypothermia | Ensure colostrum intake within the first hours of life; keep newborn warm and dry; consult veterinarian if condition does not improve quickly |

## Sudden Illness or Rapid Decline

| Pattern Observed | Likely Cause(s) | Next Step |
|---|---|---|
| Single animal found suddenly very weak, collapsed, or dead with no prior signs | Possible acute poisoning, severe internal disease, or peracute infectious disease | Do not consume or sell meat/milk from the animal; isolate the carcass from other animals; consult veterinarian and report as needed under local regulations |
| Multiple animals affected within a short period with similar signs | Possible disease outbreak or common source of poisoning (feed, water, plant toxin) | Remove suspected feed/water source; isolate unaffected animals; consult veterinarian urgently |
| High fever, depression, and swelling of the throat/neck | Possible acute infectious disease (e.g., anthrax or similar notifiable disease) | Avoid handling the carcass; contact veterinary authority immediately; these diseases can pose serious human health risk |
| Sudden death after access to a new pasture, weed patch, or chemical storage area | Possible plant or chemical poisoning | Restrict animal access to the suspect area; consult veterinarian; keep other animals away |

## General Troubleshooting Principles
- Observe the whole group before focusing on one animal: a single sick animal calls for isolation and monitoring, while multiple animals showing similar signs at once points toward a shared cause (feed, water, infectious disease) and warrants faster action.
- Record basic observations before acting: which animals are affected, when signs started, body temperature if measured, feed and water changes, and any new animals introduced recently. This history is valuable when consulting a veterinarian.
- Separate a sick or newly affected animal from the rest of the herd/flock where practical, both to reduce disease spread risk and to allow closer monitoring.
- Never assume a single cause explains all symptoms; nutrition, parasites, and infectious disease can produce overlapping signs and sometimes occur together.
- Keep clean water available at all times; water shortage or contamination is a common and often overlooked contributor to many of the symptoms above.
- Do not administer medicines, including dewormers or antibiotics, without proper diagnosis and guidance, as incorrect use can worsen outcomes and contribute to drug resistance.

## When to Consult a Veterinarian or Livestock Extension Officer
- Whenever more than one animal shows similar illness signs at the same time.
- Whenever symptoms include high fever, severe distress, blisters/erosions in the mouth or on the feet, unexplained swellings, or sudden death.
- Before handling or disposing of a carcass from an animal that died suddenly or unexpectedly.
- When a sick or lame animal does not show improvement within a short period of basic supportive care.
- Whenever a reproductive problem (abortion, stillbirth, repeated breeding failure) occurs, particularly if it affects more than one animal.
- Whenever there is any suspicion of a notifiable or zoonotic disease; these situations require prompt professional and, in many cases, regulatory involvement rather than farm-level treatment.
