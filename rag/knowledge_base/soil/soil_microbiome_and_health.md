# Soil Microbiome and Soil Health Indicators

## Overview
Soil is a living system, not simply a physical medium that holds roots and nutrients. A single teaspoon of healthy topsoil can contain billions of microorganisms alongside a wide range of larger soil fauna. This biological community drives many of the processes that make soil fertile: breaking down organic matter into plant-available nutrients, building stable soil structure, suppressing some plant diseases, and improving water infiltration and retention. Soil health refers to the overall condition of soil as a living system, going beyond chemical nutrient levels alone. This document explains the main components of soil biology, how to recognise indicators of healthy versus degraded soil, and practices that build or damage soil health over time. See `soil/soil_testing_and_fertility.md` for chemical fertility testing, and `soil/soil_compaction_and_tillage.md` for how tillage affects the physical environment in which soil organisms live.

## Why Soil Health Matters Beyond Chemical Fertility
- A soil can show adequate nutrient levels on a chemical test yet still perform poorly if its biological and physical condition is degraded, for example through compaction, low organic matter, or a depleted microbial community.
- Healthy soil biology improves the efficiency of applied fertiliser, since organic matter decomposition and microbial activity help make nutrients available in forms roots can use, and helps retain nutrients that would otherwise leach away.
- Soil organisms build and maintain the physical structure (aggregation) that allows water to infiltrate, air to reach roots, and roots to penetrate deeply, which is closely tied to reducing erosion risk (see `soil/soil_conservation_and_erosion.md`).
- A biologically active soil is generally more resilient to stress such as drought, since good structure and organic matter improve water-holding capacity.

## Key Components of Soil Biology

### Bacteria
- The most numerous soil organisms by count, responsible for a large share of organic matter decomposition and nutrient cycling.
- Certain bacteria, notably rhizobia associated with legume roots, fix atmospheric nitrogen into forms plants can use, an important natural source of soil nitrogen.
- Other bacteria are involved in converting nitrogen between forms (ammonium to nitrate) and in nutrient solubilisation.

### Fungi
- Break down tougher organic materials, including woody residue, that bacteria cannot easily decompose.
- **Mycorrhizal fungi** form associations with plant roots, extending far beyond the root's own reach to access water and nutrients (particularly phosphorus) and transferring these to the host plant in exchange for carbon compounds from the plant.
- Fungal networks (hyphae) also help bind soil particles together into stable aggregates, improving soil structure.

### Earthworms and Macrofauna
- Earthworms consume organic matter and soil, and their castings (excreted waste) are typically rich in available nutrients relative to the surrounding soil.
- Earthworm burrowing creates channels that improve water infiltration and aeration and allow deeper root penetration.
- Termites and ants also play significant structural roles in many African soils, redistributing organic matter and creating channels, though some termite species can also damage crops and structures.
- The presence of a healthy population of earthworms and other visible soil fauna is generally a positive sign of soil biological activity.

### Organic Matter as the Foundation
- Soil organic matter consists of decomposing plant and animal residues, and it is the primary food source for the soil biological community.
- Organic matter also directly improves soil water-holding capacity, nutrient retention (cation exchange capacity), and structure, independent of its role in feeding soil organisms.
- Soils with chronically low organic matter, common where crop residue is regularly removed or burned and where synthetic inputs are used without organic amendments, tend to have reduced biological activity and are more vulnerable to structural decline.

## Indicators of Soil Health
Soil health can be assessed through a combination of simple field observations and, where available, laboratory measures.

| Indicator | What to Look For | What It Suggests |
|---|---|---|
| Soil colour | Dark brown to black topsoil | Generally higher organic matter content |
| Soil structure | Soil breaks into small, stable crumbs rather than large hard clods or dust | Good aggregation, supporting root growth and water movement |
| Earthworm presence | Number of earthworms visible when digging a spade-depth hole | Higher counts generally indicate more favourable biological conditions |
| Infiltration | How quickly water soaks into the soil surface after rain or irrigation | Slow infiltration or pooling suggests compaction or structural decline |
| Root development | Depth and branching of roots when a plant is carefully dug up | Restricted, shallow, or twisted roots suggest compaction or poor structure |
| Residue breakdown | Speed at which crop residue or added organic matter decomposes | Slow breakdown may indicate low biological activity |
| Surface crusting | Hard crust forming on bare soil after rain | Suggests low organic matter and structural instability |
| Smell | Fresh, earthy smell versus sour or stagnant smell | An earthy smell is generally associated with healthy, aerobic biological activity |

## Practices That Support Soil Biology
- **Minimising soil disturbance**: Reduced or no-till approaches preserve fungal networks and soil structure that intensive tillage disrupts; see `soil/soil_compaction_and_tillage.md` for detail on conservation tillage.
- **Maintaining ground cover**: Cover crops, mulch, and retained crop residue protect soil organisms from temperature extremes and moisture loss at the surface, while also feeding the soil food web as they decompose (see `soil/cover_cropping_for_soil_health.md`).
- **Diversifying crop rotations**: Different crops support different microbial communities and root architectures; rotating crops, particularly including legumes, helps maintain a more diverse and resilient soil biology and can reduce the build-up of soil-borne pests and diseases associated with continuous monocropping.
- **Adding organic matter regularly**: Compost, well-rotted manure, and crop residue return organic material to the soil, feeding biological activity and building organic matter over time (see `fertilisers/organic_fertilisers.md`).
- **Judicious use of agrochemicals**: Following label rates and integrated pest management approaches (rather than routine, excessive pesticide use) helps limit harm to beneficial soil organisms, including earthworms and mycorrhizal fungi.
- **Avoiding unnecessary compaction**: Controlled traffic, avoiding fieldwork on wet soil, and managing livestock grazing pressure help protect the pore spaces that soil organisms and roots depend on.

## Practices That Harm Soil Biology
- Excessive or repeated deep tillage that breaks down soil structure and disrupts fungal networks and earthworm habitat.
- Leaving soil bare for extended periods, exposing the surface to temperature extremes, moisture loss, and erosion, all of which stress soil organisms.
- Burning crop residue rather than returning it to the soil, which removes organic matter and can also directly harm surface-dwelling organisms.
- Continuous monocropping without rotation, which can deplete specific nutrients, reduce microbial diversity, and allow soil-borne pests and pathogens to build up.
- Overuse or misapplication of pesticides and some fertilisers, which can reduce populations of beneficial organisms alongside target pests.
- Soil compaction from heavy machinery or livestock trampling, which collapses pore spaces needed for air, water, and organism movement.

## Building Soil Health Over Time
- Soil health improvement is a gradual process, generally unfolding over multiple seasons rather than within a single cropping cycle; consistent practice over time yields the most reliable results.
- Combining several supportive practices (reduced tillage, cover cropping, organic matter addition, and rotation) tends to be more effective than relying on any single practice alone.
- Regularly observing the simple field indicators described above, alongside periodic formal soil testing, helps track whether management is moving soil health in the right direction.
- Where a field has a long history of degraded structure or very low organic matter, initial improvement may be slow; patience and consistent management are needed rather than expecting rapid change.

## Common Mistakes
- Focusing exclusively on chemical fertiliser levels while ignoring soil biological and physical condition.
- Burning or removing all crop residue, leaving no organic material to sustain soil organisms.
- Tilling intensively every season out of habit, even where reduced tillage would be feasible and beneficial.
- Applying pesticides at excessive rates or frequency without considering effects on beneficial soil organisms.
- Expecting soil health practices to produce dramatic results within a single season.

## When to Consult an Agricultural Extension Officer
- To access soil health assessment tools or laboratory tests that go beyond standard chemical fertility testing, where available.
- To identify locally suited cover crops, rotation sequences, and organic matter sources for building soil health.
- If persistent poor crop performance appears linked to compaction, poor structure, or low biological activity rather than nutrient deficiency alone.
- To get guidance on integrated pest management approaches that limit harm to beneficial soil organisms while still managing pest pressure.
