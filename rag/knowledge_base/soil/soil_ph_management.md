# Soil pH Management for Crops and Soil Types

## Overview
Soil pH is a measure of how acidic or alkaline the soil is, and it is one of the single most influential soil properties affecting crop performance. pH governs how available nutrients are to plant roots, how well beneficial soil organisms function, and how much certain elements (including some that are toxic at high concentrations) dissolve into the soil solution. Many cases of poor crop growth that appear to be nutrient deficiencies are actually pH problems: the nutrients are present in the soil but are chemically locked up and unavailable to the plant. Understanding and managing soil pH is therefore a foundation of good soil fertility management. See `soil/soil_testing_and_fertility.md` for how pH is measured as part of a standard soil test, and `soil/soil_types_africa.md` for how pH tendencies vary by soil type and region.

## Understanding the pH Scale
- Soil pH is measured on a scale of 0 to 14, with 7 being neutral, below 7 acidic, and above 7 alkaline (also called basic).
- The scale is logarithmic, meaning a soil with pH 5 is ten times more acidic than a soil with pH 6, and one hundred times more acidic than a soil with pH 7. Small differences in pH readings represent large differences in actual acidity.
- Most agricultural soils fall somewhere between pH 4.0 (strongly acidic) and pH 9.0 (strongly alkaline), though extremes outside this range do occur.

## Why pH Matters
- **Nutrient availability**: Each plant nutrient has a pH range where it is most soluble and available for root uptake. Outside that range, the nutrient may bind tightly to soil particles or form insoluble compounds, even if it is present in the soil.
- **Micronutrient toxicity**: In strongly acidic soils, aluminium and manganese can dissolve into the soil solution at concentrations that are directly toxic to plant roots, restricting root growth and water and nutrient uptake.
- **Micronutrient deficiency**: In alkaline soils, iron, zinc, manganese, and copper become less available, often causing visible deficiency symptoms such as yellowing between leaf veins even when these nutrients are physically present in the soil.
- **Phosphorus availability**: Phosphorus is most available in the slightly acidic to neutral range (roughly pH 6.0-7.0). In strongly acidic soils it binds with iron and aluminium; in strongly alkaline soils it binds with calcium, in both cases becoming largely unavailable to plants.
- **Soil biology**: Most beneficial soil bacteria, including nitrogen-fixing rhizobia associated with legumes, function best in a near-neutral pH range. Strongly acidic conditions suppress this activity and can substantially reduce the effectiveness of nitrogen fixation.

## General Nutrient Availability by pH Range
| Soil pH Range | General Effect on Nutrient Availability |
|---|---|
| Below 5.0 (strongly acidic) | Phosphorus, calcium, magnesium, and molybdenum poorly available; aluminium and manganese may reach toxic levels; nitrogen fixation impaired. |
| 5.5-6.5 (slightly acidic) | Most nutrients reasonably available; suitable range for many crops, particularly on naturally acidic tropical soils. |
| 6.0-7.0 (near neutral) | Widest general availability of major nutrients; considered optimal for most annual crops. |
| 7.5-8.5 (moderately alkaline) | Iron, zinc, manganese, copper, and phosphorus availability reduced; boron may become more available, occasionally to excess. |
| Above 8.5 (strongly alkaline, often sodic) | Severe micronutrient deficiencies likely; soil structure often poor; see `soil/soil_salinity_management.md` for sodic soil conditions. |

## Soil pH Preferences of Common Crops
Crop tolerance to pH varies. The following are general guidelines; local soil test recommendations should take precedence where available.

| Crop Group / Example Crops | Preferred pH Range |
|---|---|
| Most cereals (maize, wheat, sorghum) | 5.5-7.0 |
| Rice (paddy conditions) | 5.5-6.5 (flooded conditions alter chemistry) |
| Legumes (beans, groundnut, soybean, cowpea) | 6.0-7.0 (nitrogen fixation favours near-neutral pH) |
| Root and tuber crops (cassava, sweet potato) | 5.0-6.5, generally tolerant of more acidic soils |
| Irish potato | 5.0-6.0, and can tolerate more acidic soils than many crops |
| Most vegetables (tomato, cabbage, onion) | 6.0-7.0 |
| Tea, coffee (arabica) | 4.5-6.0, naturally suited to acidic soils |
| Cotton | 5.5-8.0, relatively wide tolerance |
| Alfalfa/lucerne and some clovers | 6.5-7.5, sensitive to acidity |

## Diagnosing Acidic Soils
- Common in areas of high rainfall, where calcium and magnesium are leached from the soil profile over time, and in soils formed from certain parent rock types.
- Often found on older, highly weathered tropical soils such as Ferralsols (see `soil/soil_types_africa.md`).
- Continuous use of certain nitrogen fertilisers (particularly ammonium-based forms) without balancing lime application can accelerate soil acidification over time.
- Visual clues include stunted, poorly developed root systems, poor nodulation on legumes, and consistently poor response to fertiliser despite adequate application.

## Diagnosing Alkaline and Sodic Soils
- Common in low-rainfall, high-evaporation regions where salts and carbonates accumulate near the surface rather than being leached away.
- Often associated with soils derived from limestone parent material, or with irrigation using water containing dissolved calcium carbonate or bicarbonate.
- Alkaline soils are not always sodic (high in exchangeable sodium), but the two conditions often occur together and require different remediation; see `soil/soil_salinity_management.md` for the distinction and management of sodic and saline soils specifically.
- Visual clues include pale surface crusting, poor water infiltration, and micronutrient deficiency symptoms (yellowing between leaf veins) even where fertiliser has been applied.

## Managing Acidic Soils
- **Liming** is the standard correction for acidic soil: agricultural lime (ground limestone), dolomitic lime (which also supplies magnesium), or other locally available liming materials neutralise soil acidity over time.
- Lime should be incorporated into the topsoil (rather than left on the surface) for it to react effectively with the soil, ideally well before planting to allow time for the reaction to occur.
- The amount of lime required depends on the target pH, the current pH, and the soil's buffering capacity (closely related to clay and organic matter content); a soil test with a lime recommendation is the most reliable way to determine application rates, since applying too little has limited effect and applying too much can push pH too high.
- Liming effects typically last multiple seasons before reapplication is needed, but this should be confirmed through periodic soil testing rather than assumed.
- Where lime is not locally available or affordable, wood ash (in modest quantities) can provide some liming effect along with potassium, though it is far less concentrated and consistent than agricultural lime.
- Building soil organic matter through compost, manure, and cover cropping also improves the soil's buffering capacity and can moderate the effects of acidity over time (see `soil/soil_microbiome_and_health.md`).
- Choosing more acid-tolerant crops or varieties is a practical option where correcting pH fully is not feasible in the short term.

## Managing Alkaline Soils
- Correcting alkaline pH is generally more difficult and slower than correcting acidity, since alkaline conditions are often tied to underlying parent material or irrigation water chemistry that continues to influence the soil.
- **Elemental sulfur** is commonly used to gradually lower pH; soil bacteria convert it to sulfuric acid over weeks to months, so it acts slowly and works best when incorporated well ahead of the cropping season.
- **Acidifying nitrogen fertilisers** (such as ammonium sulfate) can have a modest acidifying effect over repeated applications, though this is a secondary consideration to their role as a nutrient source.
- Regular addition of organic matter (compost, manure, crop residue) helps buffer alkalinity and improves the availability of micronutrients through organic complexes, even without fully changing the pH reading.
- Where alkalinity is accompanied by high exchangeable sodium (sodic soil), gypsum application and improved drainage are typically required rather than sulfur alone; see `soil/soil_salinity_management.md`.
- Selecting alkaline-tolerant crops and micronutrient foliar sprays (particularly for iron and zinc) can help manage productivity while longer-term soil correction is underway.

## Measuring and Monitoring pH
- A formal soil test through a soil testing laboratory provides the most reliable pH measurement along with a specific lime or amendment recommendation; see `soil/soil_testing_and_fertility.md` and `soil/soil_sampling_procedures.md` for correct sample collection.
- Simple field pH test kits and pH meters can provide a useful indicative reading between formal lab tests, though they are generally less precise.
- pH can vary across a field and with depth, so testing multiple zones and depths relevant to the crop's root zone gives a more accurate picture than a single surface reading.
- Because liming and acidifying amendments take time to react in the soil, pH should be rechecked periodically (for example every 2-3 seasons) rather than assumed to remain constant after treatment.

## Common Mistakes
- Applying lime without knowing the current pH or the recommended target, leading to under- or over-correction.
- Assuming a nutrient deficiency symptom is always a fertility problem, when it may actually be a pH-driven availability problem.
- Broadcasting lime on the surface without incorporation, which slows its reaction with the soil substantially.
- Treating pH correction as a one-time task rather than an ongoing management practice, particularly in high-rainfall areas where acidification continues over time.
- Ignoring the difference between alkaline and sodic soil conditions, which require different remediation approaches.

## When to Consult an Agricultural Extension Officer
- To obtain a soil test with a specific lime or amendment recommendation calibrated to local soil and crop conditions.
- Before applying lime or sulfur at scale, to confirm the correct type, rate, and timing for the target crop and soil.
- If crops show persistent deficiency symptoms despite adequate fertiliser use, to help distinguish a pH problem from another underlying cause.
- When establishing a new crop with narrow pH tolerance (such as tea, coffee, or lucerne) on land of uncertain pH history.
