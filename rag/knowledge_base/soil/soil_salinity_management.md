# Soil Salinity and Sodicity Management

## Overview
Salt-affected soils are a growing concern in many irrigated farming areas across Africa, particularly in arid and semi-arid regions where evaporation rates are high and drainage is often poor. Excess soluble salts, or excess exchangeable sodium, can severely restrict crop growth even when other soil fertility indicators appear adequate. Salinity problems are frequently linked to irrigation practices, making them partly a management issue rather than purely a natural soil condition, which means they can often be prevented or mitigated with appropriate practices. This document explains the difference between saline and sodic soils, how to recognise and test for them, and the main approaches to managing and preventing salt buildup. See `soil/soil_testing_and_fertility.md` for general soil testing procedures and `irrigation/irrigation_scheduling.md` for irrigation management practices relevant to preventing salinity.

## Understanding Salinity and Sodicity
These are related but distinct conditions, and correct diagnosis matters because the management approach differs.

- **Saline soils** contain an excess of soluble salts (such as sodium chloride, sodium sulfate, and others) in the soil solution. High salt concentration makes it harder for plant roots to absorb water, even when the soil appears moist, because water moves toward the higher salt concentration in the soil rather than into the root, an effect similar to physiological drought.
- **Sodic soils** have a high proportion of exchangeable sodium attached to soil clay particles (relative to calcium and magnesium), even if total soluble salt content is not particularly high. Excess sodium causes clay particles to disperse rather than bind into stable aggregates, destroying soil structure, severely reducing water infiltration, and often resulting in a hard, crusted surface when dry.
- **Saline-sodic soils** exhibit both high soluble salts and high exchangeable sodium simultaneously.
- Saline soils are generally identified by electrical conductivity (EC) measurement of the soil (often expressed as ECe, the electrical conductivity of a saturated soil extract), while sodicity is assessed through exchangeable sodium percentage (ESP) or sodium adsorption ratio (SAR) of the soil or irrigation water.

## Causes of Salt Accumulation

### Irrigation-Related Causes
- Irrigation water itself always contains some dissolved salts; in arid and semi-arid climates, high evaporation concentrates these salts in the topsoil over time if they are not periodically flushed below the root zone.
- Over-irrigation combined with poor drainage raises the water table, and as shallow groundwater evaporates near the surface it leaves salts behind, a process known as capillary rise.
- Using irrigation water that is inherently high in salts (common with some groundwater sources) accelerates the problem, particularly without adequate leaching or drainage.

### Natural and Land-Use Causes
- Naturally occurring salts in parent material or in shallow saline groundwater can accumulate at the surface in low-lying or poorly drained areas, especially in arid zones.
- Coastal areas can experience salinity from seawater intrusion into groundwater, particularly where groundwater is over-extracted.
- Clearing deep-rooted natural vegetation can raise water tables in some landscapes, bringing naturally occurring salts closer to the surface over time.
- Poor drainage in low-lying parts of irrigation schemes allows salts to accumulate where water collects and evaporates.

## Recognising Salt-Affected Soils
- A white or greyish crust of salt crystals visible on the soil surface, especially after the soil dries following irrigation or rainfall, is a strong visual indicator of salinity.
- Patchy, uneven crop stands with stunted plants in some areas of a field and normal growth in others often indicate localised salt accumulation, frequently in low-lying spots.
- Poor or delayed seed germination, since germinating seeds are especially sensitive to salt stress.
- Leaf symptoms resembling drought stress (wilting, leaf tip and margin burn) even when soil moisture appears adequate.
- In sodic soils specifically, a hard, dense, poorly structured surface crust, very slow water infiltration, and standing water after irrigation or rainfall despite otherwise workable soil texture.

## Relative Crop Tolerance to Salinity
Crop sensitivity to salinity varies considerably. This is a general guide; local variety trials and extension advice should guide specific decisions.

| Tolerance Level | Example Crops |
|---|---|
| Sensitive | Common beans, most fruit trees, onion, carrot |
| Moderately sensitive | Maize, groundnut, most vegetables |
| Moderately tolerant | Sorghum, wheat, soybean, tomato |
| Tolerant | Cotton, barley, sugar beet |
| Highly tolerant | Certain forage grasses and halophytic (salt-adapted) species |

## Testing for Salinity and Sodicity
- Electrical conductivity (EC) of a soil sample, either through a soil testing laboratory or a field EC meter, is the standard measure of soil salinity; higher EC values indicate greater salt concentration.
- Sodicity is typically assessed through exchangeable sodium percentage (ESP) from a laboratory analysis, or estimated from the sodium adsorption ratio (SAR) of the soil or irrigation water.
- Irrigation water itself should also be tested for salinity and sodium content periodically, since water quality is often the underlying driver of soil salinity problems in irrigated systems.
- Testing should be done for both the general field and any visibly problematic patches, since salinity is often unevenly distributed. See `soil/soil_sampling_procedures.md` for sample collection guidance, noting that saline soil sampling should also record the location of visible salt crusts or patchy growth.

## Managing Saline Soils
- **Leaching**: Applying enough water beyond the crop's needs so that excess water moves down through and below the root zone, carrying dissolved salts with it, is the primary method of reducing soil salinity. Leaching requires adequate drainage; without it, leached salts and water simply raise the water table and can worsen the problem.
- **Improving drainage**: Installing or maintaining surface and subsurface drainage allows leached salts to be carried away from the root zone rather than accumulating in a perched water table.
- **Irrigation water quality management**: Where possible, blending or selecting lower-salinity water sources reduces the ongoing salt load added to the soil with each irrigation.
- **Salt-tolerant crop selection**: Where full reclamation is not immediately feasible, selecting more salt-tolerant crops or varieties allows continued production while soil conditions are improved over time.
- **Mulching**: Surface mulch reduces evaporation from the soil surface, which in turn reduces the capillary rise of saline groundwater and surface salt accumulation.

## Managing Sodic Soils
- **Gypsum application** is the standard treatment for sodic soils: gypsum supplies calcium, which displaces sodium from clay particle exchange sites, allowing the sodium to then be leached away with adequate water application and drainage.
- Gypsum must be followed by sufficient leaching water for the displaced sodium to actually move out of the root zone; applying gypsum without adequate follow-up irrigation and drainage limits its effectiveness.
- Improving organic matter content helps stabilise soil structure and can partially offset the structural damage caused by sodium, though it does not replace the need to address the underlying sodium levels directly.
- Because sodic soils have poor infiltration, initial leaching may need to proceed slowly and be repeated over time rather than attempted in a single large application, to avoid surface ponding and runoff rather than actual infiltration.
- Saline-sodic soils require attention to both problems together: leaching to remove excess salts while ensuring gypsum or another calcium source is available to prevent the soil from becoming purely sodic (with poorer structure) as the soluble salts that were previously helping to bind clay particles together are removed.

## Preventing Salinity in Irrigated Systems
- Avoid over-irrigation beyond crop water requirements, since excess water is the primary driver of rising water tables and associated salt accumulation; see `irrigation/irrigation_scheduling.md` for scheduling guidance based on actual crop needs.
- Ensure irrigation systems include adequate drainage provision from the outset, rather than treating drainage as an afterthought.
- Periodically monitor irrigation water quality, particularly from groundwater sources, since water salinity can change over time, especially with heavy groundwater extraction.
- Avoid irrigating low-lying areas prone to waterlogging more than necessary, since these areas are especially prone to salt accumulation through evaporation.
- Rotate to more salt-tolerant or deep-rooted crops periodically in irrigation schemes with a known salinity risk, to help manage water table depth and reduce salt buildup near the surface.

## Common Mistakes
- Continuing to irrigate normally without adjustment once salt symptoms appear, allowing the problem to worsen.
- Applying gypsum to a soil that is saline but not sodic, where it provides little benefit compared to leaching and drainage improvement.
- Leaching without adequate drainage, which can raise the water table and worsen the problem rather than resolve it.
- Ignoring irrigation water quality as a contributing cause and focusing only on the soil itself.
- Assuming visible salt crusts always indicate the same severity of problem; testing is needed to confirm the extent and type of salinity present.

## When to Consult an Agricultural Extension Officer
- To arrange soil and irrigation water testing for electrical conductivity, sodium content, and related salinity indicators.
- Before applying gypsum or undertaking large-scale leaching, to confirm the correct diagnosis (saline versus sodic) and appropriate treatment.
- To access technical support for designing or improving field drainage in irrigation schemes.
- If salt-affected patches are expanding within a field or scheme, to investigate underlying causes such as water table depth or irrigation water quality.
