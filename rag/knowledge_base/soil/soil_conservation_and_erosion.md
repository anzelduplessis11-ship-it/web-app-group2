# Soil Conservation and Erosion Control

## Overview
Soil erosion is the loss of the fertile upper layer of soil to water and wind. Because topsoil holds most of a soil's organic matter, nutrients, and biological life, erosion directly reduces fertility and productivity, often permanently. Erosion is a major threat on African smallholder farms, particularly on sloped land, sandy or degraded soils, and where ground cover is limited. Conserving soil protects both current yields and the long-term productive capacity of the land. See `soil/soil_types_africa.md` for how different soils vary in erosion risk, and `sustainability/sustainable_farming_practices.md` for the wider sustainability context.

## Causes and Types of Erosion

### Water Erosion
- **Sheet erosion**: a thin, fairly uniform layer of topsoil is removed by rainfall and surface runoff. It is easy to overlook because it happens gradually, but it steadily strips the most fertile soil.
- **Rill erosion**: runoff concentrates into small channels (rills) that can be smoothed over by tillage but signal active soil loss.
- **Gully erosion**: rills deepen into large channels that are difficult and costly to reclaim and can make land unusable.
- Water erosion is worsened by intense rainfall, steep or long slopes, bare soil, compacted surfaces, and removal of vegetation.

### Wind Erosion
- Loose, dry, bare soil (especially sandy soils in drier regions) is carried away by wind.
- Worsened by lack of ground cover, over-cultivation, and removal of windbreaks or vegetation.

## Contour Bunds and Physical Structures
- **Contour bunds** are ridges of soil or stone built along the natural contour of a slope to slow and capture runoff, allowing water to soak in rather than carry soil away.
- Stone lines and earth bunds are widely used low-cost options where materials are available.
- Structures must follow the contour accurately; poorly aligned bunds can concentrate water and worsen erosion.
- Physical structures work best combined with vegetative measures and are most effective when maintained each season.

## Terracing
- Terraces convert a slope into a series of level or gently sloping steps, reducing slope length and runoff speed.
- Effective on steeper land but require significant labour to build and ongoing maintenance to remain stable.
- Bench terraces, fanya juu terraces (where soil is thrown uphill to form a bund and a terrace develops over time), and similar approaches are used across African highlands.

## Cover Crops and Ground Cover
- Keeping the soil covered with living cover crops or crop residue protects the surface from raindrop impact and wind, and slows runoff.
- Cover crops also add organic matter, improve infiltration, and can suppress weeds; legume cover crops additionally contribute nitrogen.
- Residue retention must be balanced against competing uses such as livestock feed and fuel, which are common demands in smallholder systems.

## Mulching
- Applying a layer of organic material (crop residue, leaves, grass) over the soil surface protects against raindrop impact, reduces runoff and evaporation, moderates soil temperature, and adds organic matter as it breaks down.
- Particularly valuable on vegetable plots and around establishing crops.

## Agroforestry
- Integrating trees and shrubs with crops helps bind soil with roots, reduce runoff and wind speed, and add organic matter through leaf litter.
- Contour hedgerows of trees or shrubs on sloped land can act as living barriers that trap soil and slow water.
- Nitrogen-fixing species can improve fertility alongside erosion control. Tree-based measures require longer-term planning, as benefits build over several years.

## Conservation Tillage
- Reducing soil disturbance (minimum tillage, or planting directly into residue) helps preserve soil structure, organic matter, and surface cover that protect against erosion.
- Maintaining crop residue on the surface is a key part of the erosion-control benefit.
- Weed management often needs adjustment when tillage is reduced, since ploughing also serves a weed-control function.

## Combining Practices for Effective Protection
- Erosion control is most effective when several complementary measures are combined: for example, contour bunds plus cover crops plus reduced tillage on a sloped field.
- The right combination depends on slope, soil type, rainfall, labour availability, and the farming system; there is no single universal solution.
- Priority should generally go to keeping soil covered and slowing water movement before erosion channels form, since prevention is far cheaper than reclaiming gullied land.

## Common Mistakes
- Leaving soil bare during heavy-rainfall or windy periods, when it is most vulnerable.
- Building contour bunds or terraces off-contour, which concentrates water and can worsen erosion.
- Removing all crop residue for other uses, leaving no surface protection.
- Ignoring early sheet or rill erosion until it develops into gullies that are costly to repair.
- Cultivating up and down a slope rather than along the contour.

## When to Consult an Agricultural Extension Officer
- Before constructing terraces, contour bunds, or waterways, which require technical layout and design for the specific slope.
- If gully erosion has begun, to plan safe and effective reclamation.
- To identify suitable cover crops, agroforestry species, and conservation practices for the local soil, slope, and climate.
- To access soil and water conservation support programmes available in the local area.
