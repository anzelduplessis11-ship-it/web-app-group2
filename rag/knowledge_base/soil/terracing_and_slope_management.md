# Terracing and Contour Farming for Sloped Land

## Overview
Sloped land is common across many African farming regions, particularly in highland areas, and presents a heightened risk of soil erosion because gravity accelerates the speed and erosive power of surface runoff. Farming on slopes without appropriate structural and management measures can lead to rapid loss of fertile topsoil, declining yields, and eventually gully formation that can make land unusable. Terracing and contour farming are long-established techniques that reshape or manage sloped land to slow water movement, reduce erosion, and improve water infiltration for crops. This document describes how to assess slope, lay out contours, and construct and maintain terraces. See `soil/soil_conservation_and_erosion.md` for the broader context of erosion causes and complementary conservation practices, and `soil/soil_types_africa.md` for how soil type interacts with slope-related risk.

## Why Managing Slope Matters
- The steeper and longer a slope, the faster runoff moves and the more erosive energy it carries, increasing both the volume and speed of soil loss during rainfall events.
- Cultivating up and down a slope (rather than along the contour) creates channels that concentrate and accelerate runoff, greatly increasing erosion risk.
- Left unmanaged, sheet and rill erosion on slopes can progress to gully erosion, which is costly and difficult to reverse and can permanently remove land from productive use.
- Beyond erosion control, slope management structures also improve water infiltration, which can benefit crop water availability, particularly important in areas with unreliable rainfall.

## Assessing Slope Before Choosing a Technique
| Slope Category (approximate) | General Management Implication |
|---|---|
| Gentle (roughly 0-5%) | Contour ploughing and cropping alone, combined with ground cover, is often sufficient. |
| Moderate (roughly 5-15%) | Contour bunds, grass strips, or simple terraces are typically warranted. |
| Steep (roughly 15-30%) | Bench terraces or fanya juu/fanya chini terraces are generally needed for sustained annual cropping. |
| Very steep (above roughly 30%) | Often better suited to permanent tree crops, agroforestry, or protected vegetation rather than intensive annual cropping, even with terracing. |

- Slope should be assessed across the whole field, since it often varies within a single plot; different sections of a field may need different treatments.
- Simple slope estimation can be done with basic tools such as a line level, clinometer, or an A-frame level (described below); more precise surveying may be warranted for larger or more complex terracing projects.

## Identifying the Contour
- The "contour" is an imaginary line across a slope along which every point is at the same elevation; water moving along the true contour does not flow downhill.
- An **A-frame level** is a simple, widely used low-cost tool for marking contour lines in the field: it consists of two legs joined at the top in an A-shape with a crossbar, and a plumb line (a weight on a string) hanging from the apex. When the frame is placed on level ground, the plumb line lines up with a marked centre point on the crossbar; moving one leg up or down slope until the plumb line lines up again identifies points along the contour.
- Points identified this way are marked (with stakes or a furrow) and connected to form the contour line along which structures, planting rows, or terraces will follow.
- Because true contour lines can be irregular in shape, a modest deviation may be smoothed for practicality, but structures that stray far from the true contour risk directing water into concentrated flows rather than spreading and slowing it.

## Contour Farming
- Contour farming means ploughing, planting, and cultivating along the contour rather than up and down the slope.
- Each row and furrow acts as a small barrier that slows runoff and encourages infiltration, reducing erosion even without additional structures.
- Contour farming is most effective on gentler slopes or as a complement to physical structures like bunds or terraces on steeper land; on its own it is generally insufficient for steep slopes.

## Types of Terraces and Slope Structures

### Bench Terraces
- Bench terraces convert a slope into a series of near-level steps separated by retaining risers (banks or walls), substantially reducing the effective slope of the cultivated surface.
- Typically constructed by cutting soil from the upper part of each step and moving it to build up the lower part, gradually levelling the terrace surface, sometimes over several seasons.
- Effective for intensive, sustained cultivation on steep land but require significant labour to construct and ongoing maintenance of the risers to prevent collapse.

### Fanya Juu and Fanya Chini Terraces
- Widely used across East African highlands. "Fanya juu" means "make it upward" in Swahili: soil is dug from a trench and thrown uphill to form an embankment, creating a bund that traps runoff and sediment.
- Over time, as sediment accumulates behind the bund and soil is redistributed with cultivation, the field gradually develops a terrace-like, more level profile.
- "Fanya chini" (soil thrown downhill) is used in some contexts, particularly on very gentle slopes or where a slower terrace formation is intended, but is less common than fanya juu for erosion control.
- These techniques are attractive because they can be built with hand tools and modest labour, and the terrace develops progressively rather than requiring all earth-moving to be completed at once.

### Contour Bunds and Stone Lines
- Contour bunds are lower, simpler earth or stone ridges built along the contour that slow and spread runoff without necessarily converting the field into stepped terraces.
- Stone lines are especially useful where stone is locally available and soil for earth bunds is limited; they allow some water through while still slowing its speed and trapping sediment.
- These structures are less labour-intensive than full terracing and are often a practical first step on moderate slopes, or a complement to more substantial terraces elsewhere in a field.

### Grass Strips and Contour Hedgerows
- Strips of dense grass or hedgerow shrubs and trees planted along the contour act as living barriers that slow runoff, trap sediment, and can be harvested for fodder, mulch, or fuel.
- Contour hedgerows using nitrogen-fixing species provide an additional fertility benefit through leaf litter and pruning residue.
- These vegetative measures typically take longer to become fully effective than earth or stone structures but require less initial labour and continue to improve with time as roots and vegetation establish.

## Constructing Terraces or Contour Structures
1. Assess the slope and select an appropriate technique based on steepness, available labour, and materials (see slope category guidance above).
2. Mark the contour lines using an A-frame level or similar tool, working from the top or bottom of the slope as appropriate for the chosen structure.
3. Determine the vertical interval (spacing) between structures: steeper slopes and more erosive soils generally require closer spacing between bunds or terrace risers, while gentler slopes can have wider spacing.
4. Construct the structure along the marked line, ensuring it follows the contour accurately; even small deviations can concentrate water at low points and cause the structure to fail.
5. Include spillways or slightly reinforced sections at low points where excess water can safely overflow during unusually heavy rainfall, rather than allowing the structure to be breached unpredictably.
6. Stabilise bunds, risers, or banks with grass or other vegetation as soon as possible after construction to reduce their own vulnerability to erosion.
7. Plan structures across the whole field or slope as a connected system, rather than in isolation, so that water directed off one structure is safely managed by the next.

## Maintaining Terraces and Slope Structures
- Structures should be inspected after major rainfall events for breaches, slumping, or water concentration points, with repairs made promptly.
- Vegetation on risers and bunds should be managed to remain dense enough to stabilise the structure without excessively shading or competing with the adjacent crop.
- Sediment that accumulates behind bunds over time is fertile soil and can be periodically redistributed onto the cultivated area rather than left to build up indefinitely.
- Terraces and bunds require consistent seasonal maintenance; neglect over even one or two seasons can allow a previously effective structure to deteriorate and fail during heavy rain.

## Combining Slope Management with Other Practices
- Terracing and contour structures work best combined with ground cover measures such as mulching or cover cropping, and with reduced tillage, to further slow runoff and build soil structure (see `soil/soil_conservation_and_erosion.md` and `soil/cover_cropping_for_soil_health.md`).
- On very steep or fragile land, converting to agroforestry, permanent tree crops, or protected vegetation may be a more sustainable long-term option than continued annual cropping, even with terracing in place.

## Common Mistakes
- Constructing bunds or terrace risers off the true contour, which concentrates rather than spreads water and can worsen erosion.
- Building structures without spillways, leading to uncontrolled breaches during heavy rainfall.
- Neglecting maintenance after construction, allowing structures to degrade over time.
- Choosing a technique that does not match the slope steepness, such as relying on contour farming alone on very steep land.
- Cultivating straight through terrace risers or bunds, weakening the structure.

## When to Consult an Agricultural Extension Officer
- Before undertaking significant terracing or earth-moving work, to get technical guidance on layout, spacing, and design suited to the specific slope and soil.
- To access A-frame levels, surveying support, or training in contour marking techniques.
- If existing terraces or bunds are failing or gully erosion has begun on sloped land, to plan safe and effective repair or reclamation.
- To identify suitable grass, hedgerow, or agroforestry species for stabilising terraces and bunds in the local area.
