# Soil Testing and Fertility Management

## Overview
Soil testing measures the physical and chemical properties of soil, providing the most reliable basis for fertiliser, lime, and soil management decisions. Understanding what a soil test measures and how to interpret it helps farmers avoid both under- and over-application of inputs.

## Why Soil Testing Matters
- Nutrient needs vary significantly between fields, even within the same farm, due to differences in prior cropping history, erosion, and management.
- Applying a fixed fertiliser recommendation without knowledge of existing soil fertility often results in either wasted expenditure (over-application) or limited yield (under-application).
- Soil pH strongly affects nutrient availability; correcting pH imbalance can sometimes resolve apparent nutrient deficiencies without adding more fertiliser.

## Key Soil Test Parameters

### Soil pH
- Measures soil acidity or alkalinity on a scale of 0-14, with 7 being neutral.
- Most crops perform best in slightly acidic to neutral soil (pH 6.0-7.0); values below 5.5 or above 7.5 begin to restrict nutrient availability for many crops.
- Acidic soils (common in high-rainfall areas due to nutrient leaching) may require lime application to raise pH.
- Alkaline soils (common in low-rainfall, high-evaporation areas) can restrict availability of iron, zinc, and manganese.

### Organic Matter Content
- Indicates the soil's capacity to retain nutrients and water, and supports microbial activity.
- Low organic matter (common in heavily cropped or sandy soils) suggests a need for organic amendments alongside inorganic fertiliser.

### Nitrogen, Phosphorus, and Potassium (N-P-K) Levels
- Provide a basis for calculating appropriate fertiliser application rates for the target crop and yield goal.
- Nitrogen levels in soil tests reflect only a snapshot in time, since nitrogen is highly mobile and changes rapidly; phosphorus and potassium levels are more stable indicators.

### Micronutrient Levels
- Some soil tests include micronutrients such as zinc, boron, and sulfur, particularly relevant for soils known to be deficient in the region or for sensitive crops.

### Soil Texture
- Describes the relative proportions of sand, silt, and clay, which affects water-holding capacity, drainage, and how quickly fertiliser nutrients may leach or need reapplication.

## How to Collect a Soil Sample
1. Divide the field into uniform management zones based on visible differences in soil colour, slope, drainage, or past crop performance.
2. Within each zone, collect 10-15 small soil cores from a zig-zag pattern across the area, at a depth of about 15-20 cm for most annual crops.
3. Mix the cores together in a clean container to form a single composite sample per zone; avoid contamination from fertiliser bags, rusty tools, or standing water.
4. Air-dry the sample in the shade (not direct sun) and pack according to the testing laboratory's instructions.
5. Label each sample clearly with field location and zone, and record the crop history and current crop plans for the field.

## Interpreting Results and Applying Recommendations
- Soil test reports typically classify nutrient levels as low, medium, or high (or provide numeric values against locally calibrated thresholds) and may include a fertiliser or lime recommendation.
- Where a soil testing laboratory provides a specific fertiliser recommendation, this is generally more reliable than generic crop guidelines, since it reflects the actual field conditions.
- Recommendations should be revisited periodically (every 2-3 seasons or after major management changes) since soil fertility changes over time with cropping and input use.

## Building Long-Term Soil Fertility
- Combine soil test-based fertiliser application with organic matter management (see `fertilisers/organic_fertilisers.md`) to build fertility over time rather than relying solely on annual input correction.
- Practice crop rotation, particularly including legumes, to improve soil nitrogen and structure.
- Minimize soil erosion through appropriate tillage practices, contour farming, and ground cover, since topsoil loss removes the most fertile part of the soil profile.
- Avoid continuous monocropping of nutrient-demanding crops without a fertility-building rotation phase.

## Common Mistakes
- Collecting a soil sample from only one spot in a field and assuming it represents the whole field.
- Sampling immediately after fertiliser or lime application, which can distort nutrient readings.
- Applying fertiliser recommendations from a different region or soil type without local calibration.
- Treating soil testing as a one-time exercise rather than a periodic practice.

## When to Consult an Agricultural Extension Officer
- To identify and access soil testing laboratories or services available in the local area.
- To interpret soil test results in the context of specific crop plans and locally available fertiliser products.
- If soil test results indicate unusual conditions (e.g., very high salinity, extreme pH) requiring specialized remediation advice.
