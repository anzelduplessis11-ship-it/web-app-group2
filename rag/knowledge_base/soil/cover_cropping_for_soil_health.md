# Cover Cropping and Green Manures for Soil Health

## Overview
Cover crops are plants grown primarily to protect and improve the soil rather than for direct harvest and sale, though many also provide secondary benefits such as fodder. Green manures are a specific type of cover crop grown and then incorporated into the soil (or left as surface residue) specifically to add organic matter and nutrients ahead of a following cash crop. Both practices are long-standing tools for building soil fertility, protecting soil from erosion, and improving soil structure between or alongside main cropping cycles. This document covers the benefits, common species, and practical establishment and management of cover crops in smallholder farming systems. See `soil/soil_conservation_and_erosion.md` for erosion control context, `soil/soil_microbiome_and_health.md` for the biological benefits of organic matter, and `fertilisers/organic_fertilisers.md` for related organic fertility practices.

## Benefits of Cover Crops and Green Manures
- **Erosion control**: Living ground cover intercepts rainfall before it strikes bare soil, reducing surface sealing and runoff, and root systems help bind soil in place.
- **Organic matter addition**: Above- and below-ground plant material adds organic matter to the soil as it decomposes, feeding soil biological activity and improving structure over time.
- **Nitrogen fixation**: Leguminous cover crops host nitrogen-fixing bacteria in their root nodules, adding nitrogen to the soil that becomes available to the following crop as the cover crop residue breaks down.
- **Weed suppression**: A dense cover crop canopy competes with weeds for light, water, and nutrients, and some species release compounds that further suppress weed germination and growth.
- **Moisture management**: Cover crop residue left on the surface acts as mulch, reducing evaporation and moderating soil temperature; living cover crops can also improve infiltration during rainfall.
- **Breaking pest and disease cycles**: Including a different plant family in rotation, such as a cover crop between susceptible cash crops, can reduce the buildup of soil-borne pests and diseases associated with continuous cropping of the same species.
- **Improved soil structure**: Root channels, particularly from deep-rooted species, help break up compacted layers and improve aeration and water movement (see `soil/soil_compaction_and_tillage.md`).
- **Grazing and fodder value**: Many cover crop species can also be grazed or cut for livestock feed, providing an additional practical benefit in mixed crop-livestock systems, though this needs to be balanced against the soil benefit of leaving residue in place.

## Types of Cover Crops

### Legume Cover Crops
Valued primarily for nitrogen fixation in addition to general soil protection benefits.
- **Mucuna (velvet bean)**: Vigorous, smothering growth habit, widely used in humid and sub-humid tropical regions for soil fertility improvement and weed suppression.
- **Lablab (hyacinth bean)**: Drought-tolerant legume used both as a cover crop and dual-purpose fodder across many African regions.
- **Cowpea**: Fast-growing, widely adapted legume commonly used as a short-cycle cover crop or intercrop, with dual grain and soil benefit.
- **Sunn hemp**: Fast-growing, tall legume valued for rapid biomass production and nematode-suppressive properties in some contexts.
- **Pigeon pea**: Deep-rooted, longer-duration legume that also provides fodder, fuelwood, and food value alongside soil benefits.
- **Vetch and clover species**: Used in cooler highland areas, valued for nitrogen fixation and quality fodder.

### Grass and Cereal Cover Crops
Valued for rapid biomass production, weed suppression, and building soil organic matter, though they do not fix nitrogen.
- **Sorghum-sudangrass and forage sorghum**: Fast-growing, deep-rooted options that produce substantial biomass and can help break up compacted soil layers.
- **Oats and other small grains**: Used particularly in cooler or highland conditions as a fast-establishing cover between main cropping seasons.

### Brassica Cover Crops
- Species such as forage radish or mustard develop deep taproots that can penetrate compacted soil layers, sometimes described as "biological subsoiling," and decompose relatively quickly, releasing nutrients for a following crop.

### Cover Crop Mixes
- Combining legume and non-legume species (for example a legume with a grass or brassica) can capture complementary benefits: nitrogen fixation alongside rapid biomass production or compaction relief, while also providing more diverse habitat for soil organisms.
- Mixes should be chosen so that species are compatible in growth habit and timing, avoiding one species from excessively suppressing another before it establishes.

## Choosing a Cover Crop
Selection depends on the specific objective, climate, and system:

| Primary Objective | Suitable Cover Crop Characteristics |
|---|---|
| Nitrogen addition | Legume species with strong nodulation, incorporated or left as residue before the following crop |
| Erosion control on bare ground | Fast-establishing, dense-canopy species, potentially combined with a grass or legume mix |
| Weed suppression | Vigorous, smothering species such as mucuna, or dense grass cover |
| Breaking compaction | Deep-rooted species such as forage radish, sorghum-sudangrass, or pigeon pea |
| Dry-season/short window | Fast-maturing, drought-tolerant species such as cowpea or lablab |
| Dual fodder and soil benefit | Lablab, cowpea, pigeon pea, or forage sorghum suited to grazing or cutting |

- Local rainfall pattern and length of the available growing window (particularly between main crop seasons) strongly influence which species will establish and provide meaningful benefit before the next planting.
- Species already well adapted to the local climate and known to the area generally establish more reliably than unfamiliar introductions.

## Establishing Cover Crops
Cover crops can be integrated into a cropping system in several ways:

- **Rotation phase**: Growing a cover crop as a dedicated phase in the rotation, typically during a period when the field would otherwise lie fallow or bare between main crops.
- **Relay planting**: Sowing the cover crop into a standing main crop shortly before or at harvest, allowing it to establish quickly using existing soil moisture once the main crop is removed.
- **Intercropping**: Growing a low-growing or compatible cover crop alongside a main crop simultaneously, provided the cover crop does not compete excessively with the main crop for light, water, or nutrients.
- **Post-harvest planting**: Sowing a cover crop immediately after harvesting the main crop to use residual soil moisture and occupy the field before weeds establish.

Practical establishment steps:
1. Select a species or mix suited to the season length, rainfall, and management objective.
2. Prepare a light seedbed if needed; many cover crops establish well with minimal soil disturbance, particularly when broadcast or relay-planted into existing residue.
3. Sow at an appropriate seeding rate and depth for the species to achieve a dense, even stand, since a thin stand provides substantially less benefit for erosion control and weed suppression.
4. Manage the establishment period to avoid competition from existing weeds until the cover crop is established enough to suppress them itself.

## Terminating and Managing Cover Crops
- **Mowing or slashing**: Cutting the cover crop at or before flowering (for many species) halts its growth and leaves residue on the surface as mulch, without incorporating it into the soil.
- **Incorporation (ploughing under)**: Turning the cover crop into the soil accelerates decomposition and nutrient release, which can be timed to coincide with the nutrient needs of the following crop, though it also increases soil disturbance (see `soil/soil_compaction_and_tillage.md` regarding tillage trade-offs).
- **Rolling or crimping**: Flattening the cover crop to create a mat of residue on the surface, common in reduced-tillage systems, allowing the following crop to be planted directly into the residue.
- **Grazing**: Livestock can graze cover crops directly, though enough residue and root mass should generally be left to retain the soil benefit rather than removing all above-ground biomass.
- Timing of termination matters: terminating too early sacrifices biomass and nitrogen fixation benefit, while terminating too late (especially with grasses) can allow the cover crop to compete with or delay planting of the following cash crop, and legumes left to fully mature and set seed can become a self-sown weed in later seasons.
- Allow adequate time between termination and planting the following crop for decomposition to begin, particularly with high-biomass cover crops, to avoid interference with seed placement or germination.

## Integrating Cover Crops into a Rotation
- Cover crops are most effective as part of a planned rotation sequence rather than an occasional, ad hoc addition; planning the cover crop phase alongside main crop planning helps ensure it fits the available season length and labour capacity.
- Alternating legume and non-legume cover crops, or using mixes, across seasons helps maintain diverse benefits and avoid building up pests or diseases associated with any single cover crop species grown repeatedly.
- In mixed crop-livestock systems, balancing the fodder value of grazing a cover crop against the soil benefit of retaining residue is an important practical decision that depends on the relative priority of livestock feed versus soil building in a given season.

## Common Mistakes
- Sowing cover crops too thinly, resulting in poor ground cover and limited weed suppression or erosion control benefit.
- Allowing a cover crop to fully mature and set seed, creating a weed problem in subsequent seasons.
- Terminating a cover crop too close to planting the following crop, causing residue or decomposition to interfere with establishment.
- Choosing a species poorly matched to the available season length or rainfall, resulting in poor establishment.
- Removing all cover crop biomass through grazing or harvest, leaving little material to benefit the soil.

## When to Consult an Agricultural Extension Officer
- To identify cover crop species well adapted to the local climate, season length, and cropping system.
- To obtain quality cover crop seed, particularly for less common species such as mucuna, sunn hemp, or forage radish.
- To plan a rotation sequence that integrates cover cropping with existing cash crop and, where relevant, livestock management.
- If cover crop residue management is causing difficulty with establishing the following crop, to get guidance on timing and termination methods.
