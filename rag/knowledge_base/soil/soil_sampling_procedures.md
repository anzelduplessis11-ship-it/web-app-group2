# Soil Sampling Procedures for Testing

## Overview
A soil test is only as reliable as the sample submitted for analysis. Poor sampling technique is one of the most common reasons that soil test results fail to reflect real field conditions, leading to fertiliser or lime recommendations that do not match actual need. This document provides detailed, step-by-step guidance on how to correctly collect, handle, and submit soil samples for laboratory testing, and how to think about interpreting the results that come back. See `soil/soil_testing_and_fertility.md` for what a soil test measures and how results translate into fertility recommendations, and `soil/soil_salinity_management.md` and `soil/soil_ph_management.md` for guidance on situations that call for additional or adapted sampling.

## Why Correct Sampling Technique Matters
- Soil properties, particularly nutrient levels, can vary considerably even within a few metres, due to previous fertiliser placement, crop residue distribution, drainage differences, and past management history.
- A single sample point, or a sample collected carelessly, may not represent the field as a whole, leading to a recommendation that is wrong for most of the field even if accurate for the exact spot sampled.
- Because fertiliser, lime, and other inputs represent real cost and effort, an unreliable sample can lead to wasted expenditure on unnecessary inputs or, conversely, continued yield loss from an uncorrected deficiency.

## Equipment Needed
- A soil auger, soil probe, or a clean spade and trowel if an auger is not available.
- A clean plastic bucket or container for mixing soil cores from the same zone (avoid galvanised metal buckets, which can contaminate readings for certain micronutrients).
- Clean sample bags or containers as specified by the testing laboratory, along with waterproof labels or a permanent marker.
- A notebook or form for recording field, zone, and management history information alongside each sample.
- A measuring stick or marked auger/probe to ensure consistent sampling depth.

## When to Sample
- Sample when the soil is at a workable moisture level, neither waterlogged nor extremely dry and hard, to allow even coring and mixing.
- Avoid sampling immediately after fertiliser, lime, or manure application, since this can distort readings and does not reflect the soil's baseline fertility; allow a gap of at least several weeks, and ideally sample before the next application is planned.
- For most annual cropping systems, sampling a few weeks to a couple of months before land preparation or planting allows enough time to receive results and apply any recommended lime or fertiliser adjustments before the season begins.
- Sampling at a consistent time of year across seasons (for example, always shortly after harvest or always before land preparation) makes results more comparable over time.
- For perennial crops such as tree crops, sampling can be done at a similar time each year, outside of periods immediately following fertiliser application.

## Dividing the Field into Sampling Zones
- Do not treat an entire farm, or even an entire large field, as a single uniform unit unless it is genuinely uniform in soil type, slope, drainage, and management history.
- Divide the area into zones based on visible differences such as:
  - Soil colour or texture changes.
  - Slope position (upper, middle, lower slope often differ in fertility and drainage).
  - Drainage differences, including low-lying areas prone to waterlogging.
  - Past management differences, such as areas that received different crops, fertiliser history, or manure application in previous seasons.
  - Visibly different crop performance in previous seasons.
- Each zone should be sampled and submitted as a separate composite sample, since combining distinctly different areas into one sample averages out differences that matter for management decisions.
- Zones do not need to be large or perfectly regular; the goal is that each composite sample represents a reasonably uniform area.

## Sampling Pattern and Depth
1. Within each zone, walk a zig-zag or "W" pattern across the area to select sampling points, avoiding unusual spots such as old manure piles, field edges, near trees, close to paths, or areas with unusual drainage, unless that specific microzone is being deliberately assessed.
2. At each point, collect a core using an auger, probe, or spade to a consistent depth appropriate to the purpose of the test.

| Sampling Purpose | Typical Depth Guidance |
|---|---|
| General fertility testing for annual crops | Approximately 15-20 cm (the main root zone and tillage depth for most annuals) |
| Perennial or tree crops | Sampling at more than one depth (for example a shallow and a deeper sample) can be useful since roots draw from a deeper profile |
| Assessing compaction or subsoil issues | A deeper sample below the normal tillage layer, alongside the standard topsoil sample, to compare layers (see `soil/soil_compaction_and_tillage.md`) |
| Investigating suspected salinity | Multiple depths within and below the main root zone, since salts can concentrate at different depths depending on water movement |

3. Collect at least 10-15 individual cores per zone; more cores generally produce a more representative composite sample, particularly in larger or more variable zones.
4. If using a spade rather than an auger, dig a small pit and cut an even slice of soil from the correct depth, avoiding taking more from the surface than from depth (which biases the sample toward surface conditions).

## Compositing the Sample
- Place all cores from a single zone into a clean bucket and mix them thoroughly by hand, breaking up clods, to form one uniform composite sample.
- Remove any obvious stones, roots, crop residue, or other debris that could interfere with the analysis or clog laboratory equipment.
- From the mixed composite, take a subsample of the amount specified by the laboratory (typically a few hundred grams to around half a kilogram, though the exact requirement will be listed in the laboratory's instructions) for submission.
- Do not combine composites from different zones; each zone's composite sample should remain separate through to the final subsample submitted.

## Handling, Drying, and Packaging
- Spread the composite subsample in a clean, shaded area to air-dry rather than placing it in direct sunlight, which can affect certain readings and simply slows the drying process due to crusting.
- Avoid oven-drying or drying near a heat source, which can significantly distort some chemical test results, particularly nitrogen-related measures.
- Once reasonably dry, place the sample into the labelled bag or container specified by the testing laboratory.
- Avoid contact with fertiliser residue, rusty tools, galvanised containers, or standing water at any stage of collection, mixing, or storage, since these can contaminate specific nutrient readings (particularly nitrogen, zinc, or iron).

## Labelling and Record-Keeping
Label each sample clearly and keep a matching written record including:
- Farm or field name and the specific zone identifier.
- Date of sampling.
- Current and recent crop history for the zone, including any legumes grown recently.
- Fertiliser, lime, or manure application history and approximate timing of the most recent application.
- Any specific concern prompting the test (for example, poor crop performance in that zone, visible salt crusting, or a suspected pH problem).
- The purpose of the test (routine monitoring, investigating a specific problem, or establishing a baseline for new land).

This information helps the laboratory or extension officer interpret results in context, since the same nutrient reading can call for a different recommendation depending on the crop history and intended use of the field.

## Submitting the Sample
- Deliver or send samples to the laboratory as promptly as practical after drying and packaging, following the specific submission instructions, forms, and any request details required by that laboratory.
- Where multiple zones are being tested, submit them together with clear identification so results can be matched back to the correct zone once returned.

## Interpreting the Results
- Soil test reports typically present nutrient levels as numeric values, often alongside a categorical rating (low, medium, high) against locally calibrated thresholds, and may include a specific fertiliser or lime recommendation.
- Results should be interpreted in the context of the intended crop and target yield, since nutrient requirements differ between crops; see `soil/soil_testing_and_fertility.md` for detail on interpreting specific parameters such as pH, organic matter, and N-P-K levels.
- Compare results across zones within the same field to understand variability, rather than looking only at a single field-wide figure, and adjust management zone by zone where practical.
- Retain past soil test results to track trends over time; a single result is a snapshot, while a series of results across seasons shows whether soil fertility is improving, declining, or stable under current management.

## Sampling Frequency
- For actively cropped fields under standard management, resampling every 2-3 seasons is generally sufficient to track changing fertility and adjust recommendations.
- More frequent sampling may be warranted when investigating a specific suspected problem, following a major management change (such as a new lime or gypsum application), or when establishing baseline data for newly acquired or newly cleared land.
- Avoid sampling so infrequently that fertiliser and lime decisions are based on outdated information, particularly in fields under intensive cropping where nutrient status can change relatively quickly.

## Common Mistakes
- Collecting only one or two cores and treating them as representative of an entire field.
- Sampling from unusual spots (old manure piles, field edges, near trees) and including them in a general composite sample.
- Sampling shortly after fertiliser, lime, or manure application, distorting the results.
- Mixing samples from clearly different zones into a single composite, masking important variability.
- Using contaminated containers or tools (galvanised buckets, fertiliser sacks) that can bias specific nutrient readings.
- Failing to record crop and management history, limiting how useful the laboratory or extension officer can be in interpreting the result.

## When to Consult an Agricultural Extension Officer
- To identify accessible soil testing laboratories and understand their specific sample submission requirements.
- For assistance dividing a field into appropriate sampling zones, particularly on larger or more variable farms.
- To interpret returned soil test results in the context of specific crop plans and locally available fertiliser and lime products.
- When a suspected specific problem (salinity, severe compaction, extreme pH) calls for a modified or additional sampling approach beyond routine fertility testing.
