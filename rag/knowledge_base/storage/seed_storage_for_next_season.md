# Seed Storage for Next Season (Farmer-Saved Seed)

## Overview
Farmers who save their own seed for the next planting season need storage practices that go beyond general grain storage, since the goal is preserving germination viability rather than food or feed quality alone. Seed that is stored incorrectly may look normal but fail to germinate well when planted, resulting in poor crop establishment and yield loss. This document covers how to store farmer-saved seed to maintain viability until the next planting season. For general dried-produce storage principles, see `storage/grain_storage.md`; for hermetic storage technology also applicable to seed, see `storage/hermetic_storage_pics_bags.md`; for planting timing, see the relevant `planting_calendars/` document for the region; for crop-specific detail, see the relevant `crops/` document.

## Why Seed Storage Differs from Grain Storage
- Seed must remain biologically alive (capable of germinating) throughout storage, whereas grain intended for food or feed only needs to remain safe and nutritious.
- Seed viability naturally declines over time even under good storage conditions, and the rate of decline accelerates sharply under poor conditions (high moisture, high temperature, or pest damage).
- Some seed-damaging factors (for example, minor insect feeding or moderate mechanical damage) may not significantly affect food or feed value but can substantially reduce or destroy germination capacity.
- Because of this, seed intended for planting is often best stored separately from grain intended for consumption or sale, and handled with additional care.

## Selecting Seed for Storage
- Save seed only from healthy, mature, true-to-type plants, since seed from diseased, stressed, or off-type plants is more likely to carry disease, have lower vigour, or fail to reproduce desired crop characteristics.
- Harvest seed at full physiological maturity, since seed harvested too early is typically lower in viability and storage life than fully mature seed.
- Avoid using seed that shows visible insect damage, mould, discolouration, or mechanical injury, since these reduce germination potential even where the seed appears otherwise usable.

## Drying Seed for Storage
- Seed generally needs to be dried to a lower moisture content than grain intended only for consumption, since excess moisture is one of the most significant causes of viability loss and mould development during seed storage.
- Dry seed gradually and evenly in thin layers, in shade or filtered light where possible, since very rapid or very intense direct-sun drying can damage some seed types, particularly larger-seeded legumes.
- Avoid drying seed on hot, dark surfaces (such as bare metal or dark tarpaulin in direct sun) that can raise seed temperature excessively, since high temperature during drying can injure the embryo and reduce germination.
- Where a moisture meter is unavailable, use simple field checks (such as the salt test described in `storage/grain_storage.md`) and, if in doubt, favour additional drying time, since underdried seed is a common cause of storage failure.

## Handling Seed to Preserve Viability
- Handle seed gently during threshing, shelling, and cleaning, since mechanical damage (cracked seed coats, bruising) increases vulnerability to pests, mould, and moisture uptake, and can directly injure the embryo.
- Clean seed thoroughly, removing debris, damaged seed, and other crop or weed seed, since clean seed stores better and is easier to monitor for developing problems.
- Avoid unnecessary handling or transfer between containers once seed is dried and stored, since repeated handling increases the risk of mechanical damage and moisture exposure.

## Storage Conditions for Seed Viability

### Moisture and Humidity
- Keep stored seed as dry as possible and protect it from humidity re-entry throughout the storage period, since even brief exposure to damp conditions can significantly shorten seed life.
- Store seed containers in the driest available location, away from sources of dampness such as earthen floors without a moisture barrier, leaking roofs, or humid corners of a building.

### Temperature
- Cooler storage temperatures generally help preserve seed viability for longer, since seed deterioration processes accelerate at higher temperatures, particularly when combined with higher moisture.
- Avoid storing seed in locations that become very hot, such as under direct metal roofing without insulation or shade, since sustained high temperature accelerates viability loss even in otherwise dry seed.

### Containers
- Airtight (hermetic) containers, including hermetic bags and sealed jars, tins, or drums, help protect dried seed from humidity re-entry and insect infestation, in the same way they protect grain (see `storage/hermetic_storage_pics_bags.md`).
- Ensure containers are clean and free of residue from previous contents, and that seed is fully dry before sealing, since hermetic sealing of damp seed traps moisture and accelerates spoilage rather than preventing it.
- Where formal hermetic containers are not available, clean, dry, tightly sealed traditional containers (dry gourds, tins with tight lids) can offer some protection, though generally less reliable than proper hermetic bags or containers.

## Protecting Stored Seed from Pests
- Insect pests, particularly bruchids (weevils) in legume seed, can severely damage seed viability even when moisture and temperature conditions are otherwise well managed; see `pests/bruchids.md` for detail on this pest group.
- Hermetic storage is effective at controlling insect infestation in seed as in grain, without needing chemical treatment.
- Where chemical seed treatments are used, confirm currently approved and locally registered products with an agricultural extension officer, and note that seed treated with certain chemicals should be clearly labelled and never used for food or feed.
- Protect stored seed from rodents using the same physical barriers and sanitation practices described in `storage/grain_storage.md`.

## Labelling and Record-Keeping
- Clearly label seed containers with the crop, variety name, harvest date, and source, since seed lots can otherwise become mixed or misidentified, undermining variety integrity and future planting decisions.
- Keep simple records of seed quantity saved, intended planting area, and any quality observations, to support planning for the next planting season and to track how well specific batches performed.
- Store seed for planting completely separately from grain intended for consumption or sale, both physically and by clear labelling, to avoid accidental consumption of treated seed or accidental use of food-grade grain as seed without appropriate testing.

## Germination Testing Before Planting
- Before the next planting season, test a small sample of stored seed for germination rate (for example, by placing a counted number of seeds between moist paper or cloth, kept warm, and counting how many germinate over a period appropriate to the crop) to estimate expected field performance.
- A germination test helps identify whether seed viability has declined enough to require a higher seeding rate, sourcing of fresh seed, or replacement of the saved seed lot.
- Repeat germination testing each season before planting, since viability can change from year to year depending on how the seed was stored and its age.

## Typical Storage Life Considerations
- Different crops and seed types naturally retain viability for different lengths of time even under good storage conditions; some seed types remain viable across multiple seasons when stored well, while others decline more quickly and are best used within one season of saving.
- Confirm expected viability duration for a specific crop with an agricultural extension officer or the relevant `crops/` document, rather than assuming all seed types behave the same way.

## Common Mistakes
- Saving seed from damaged, diseased, or off-type plants without adequate selection.
- Drying seed too quickly or at excessive temperature, injuring the embryo.
- Storing seed at a higher moisture content than recommended because it is intended for planting rather than immediate sale, when in fact seed storage requires equal or greater attention to dryness.
- Mixing seed lots of different ages, varieties, or quality without labelling, leading to confusion at planting time.
- Storing seed for planting together with, or in the same containers as, chemically treated grain or seed intended for other uses.
- Failing to test germination before planting, only discovering poor viability after a poor crop stand emerges in the field.

## When to Consult an Agricultural Extension Officer
- To confirm appropriate drying and storage moisture targets for a specific crop's seed.
- If germination testing shows a low or declining germination rate, to decide whether to adjust seeding rate or source replacement seed.
- Before using any chemical seed treatment, to confirm currently approved products and safe handling and labelling practices.
- To learn about locally recommended seed-saving practices for maintaining variety purity and quality across seasons.
