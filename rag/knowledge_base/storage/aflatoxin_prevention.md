# Aflatoxin Prevention

## Overview
Aflatoxins are toxic compounds produced by certain moulds, particularly *Aspergillus* species, that can grow on crops in the field and in storage under warm, humid conditions or where produce is inadequately dried. They are a serious food-safety hazard in many parts of Africa, especially on maize and groundnut. Because contaminated produce often looks and tastes normal, prevention through good harvest and storage practice is far more reliable than trying to detect or remove aflatoxins afterward. This document explains the risk and how to reduce it. For general grain storage, see `storage/grain_storage.md`; for the affected crops, see `crops/maize.md` and `crops/groundnut.md`; for urgent situations, see `emergency_guides/crop_emergency_guide.md`.

## What Aflatoxin Is
- Aflatoxins are natural toxins produced by moulds (mainly *Aspergillus flavus* and related species) as they grow on crops.
- The moulds thrive in warm, humid conditions and on crops that are stressed, damaged, insect-attacked, or not dried quickly and thoroughly.
- Contamination can begin in the field before harvest and worsen after harvest during drying and storage, especially if produce stays damp.
- Aflatoxins are largely invisible and are not destroyed by ordinary cooking, so they cannot be assumed gone simply because grain looks clean or has been cooked.

## Food-Safety and Health Risk
- Aflatoxins are harmful to both human and animal health. High exposure can cause acute, severe illness, and repeated lower-level exposure over time is associated with serious long-term health effects, including harm to the liver.
- Children and people with poor nutrition or existing illness may be especially vulnerable.
- Contaminated grain and feed can also affect livestock and pass into animal products.
- Because of these risks, many formal and export markets test for aflatoxin and reject produce that exceeds safety limits, so contamination causes both health harm and economic loss.
- This is a food-safety matter; visibly mouldy or suspect produce should not be eaten or sold, and severe or uncertain situations warrant consultation with authorities (see `ai_guidelines/ai_safety_guidelines.md`).

## Crops Most at Risk
- **Maize** and **groundnut** are the crops most commonly associated with aflatoxin in African smallholder systems.
- Other crops that can be affected include some other nuts, oilseeds, and dried products stored under warm, humid conditions.
- Risk is highest where crops experience drought or heat stress in the field, insect or physical damage, delayed harvest, slow drying, or damp storage.

## Pre-Harvest Prevention
- Grow adapted, healthy crops and manage stress: good soil fertility, appropriate spacing, weed control, and drought management reduce the crop stress that predisposes plants to mould.
- Control insect pests in the field, since insect damage creates entry points for mould.
- Where available and locally recommended, use varieties or biological control approaches shown to reduce aflatoxin risk; confirm suitability with an agricultural extension officer.
- Harvest at the right time and avoid leaving mature crops in the field, where they are exposed to moisture, pests, and mould before harvest.

## Post-Harvest Prevention

### Timely Harvest and Handling
- Harvest promptly once the crop is mature; delay in the field or in heaps before drying allows mould to develop.
- Handle produce carefully to minimise physical damage, and keep it off bare ground to reduce contamination.

### Rapid Drying to Safe Moisture
- Dry produce quickly and thoroughly to a safe moisture level as soon as possible after harvest; slow or interrupted drying is a major cause of aflatoxin development.
- Dry in thin layers on a clean, raised surface or tarpaulin, turning regularly, and protect from rain, dew, and direct ground contact.
- For grains, dry to the recommended safe storage moisture content before storing (see the drying table in `storage/grain_storage.md`). Where a moisture meter is unavailable, use approximate field checks such as the salt test, and err on the side of drying longer.
- For groundnut, dry pods promptly and thoroughly and avoid conditions where they remain damp.

### Sorting
- Sort out and discard mouldy, discoloured, shrivelled, damaged, or insect-attacked kernels and pods before storage and again before use or sale, since these carry the highest contamination risk.
- Keep clearly mouldy or suspect produce separate from clean produce immediately, and do not consume or sell it.
- Sorting reduces but does not guarantee removal of aflatoxin, since contamination can be present without visible signs; it is a complement to good drying, not a substitute.

### Hermetic and Proper Storage
- Store only well-dried produce; storing damp produce, even in good containers, allows mould to grow.
- Hermetic (airtight) storage, such as sealed bags or containers, helps control insects and limit moisture re-entry, reducing conditions that favour mould (see `storage/grain_storage.md`).
- Keep storage dry, cool, well-ventilated where appropriate, and rodent- and insect-proof, and protect stored produce from re-wetting.
- Inspect stored produce regularly, especially during humid periods, and remove any produce showing mould promptly.

## Summary of Key Prevention Steps
| Stage | Key actions to reduce aflatoxin |
|---|---|
| In the field | Manage stress and fertility; control insects; harvest on time |
| At harvest | Harvest promptly; handle carefully; keep off bare ground |
| Drying | Dry rapidly and thoroughly to safe moisture; protect from rain and dew |
| Sorting | Remove mouldy, damaged, discoloured, and insect-attacked produce |
| Storage | Store only dry produce; keep dry and pest-proof; hermetic storage helps; inspect regularly |

## When to Consult Authorities and Extension Officers
- If aflatoxin contamination is suspected, produce shows widespread mould, or people or animals show signs of illness after consuming stored produce, consult an agricultural extension officer, plant health or food-safety authority, or health services promptly.
- For guidance on safe disposal or handling of contaminated produce, rather than consuming or selling it.
- To learn about locally recommended aflatoxin-resistant varieties, biological control options, testing services, and safe storage practices.
- Because aflatoxin is a food-safety hazard that is hard to detect without testing, err on the side of caution and seek qualified advice when in doubt.

## Common Mistakes
- Assuming grain is safe because it looks and smells normal, when aflatoxin can be present without visible signs.
- Storing produce before it is properly dried, due to time pressure or lack of drying facilities.
- Delaying harvest or leaving harvested produce in damp heaps before drying.
- Consuming or selling visibly mouldy produce, or mixing it with clean produce.
- Relying on cooking to remove the toxin, which it does not.
