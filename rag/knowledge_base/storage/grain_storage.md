# Grain Storage

## Overview
Proper grain storage protects harvested cereals and legumes from moisture, insects, rodents, and mould, preserving both food value and market quality. Poor storage is a major source of post-harvest loss across Africa, in many cases exceeding losses from field pests and diseases.

## Pre-Storage Drying Requirements
| Grain Type | Recommended Storage Moisture Content |
|---|---|
| Maize | 12-13% |
| Rice (paddy) | 12-14% |
| Beans and other legumes | 12-14% |
| Sorghum/Millet | 12-13% |

- Grain above these moisture levels is at significantly higher risk of mould growth, including moulds that produce aflatoxins and other mycotoxins harmful to human and animal health.
- Moisture content can be measured directly with a moisture meter where available, or estimated using traditional methods (e.g., the "salt test," where grain is mixed with dry salt in a sealed container and checked for salt clumping, which indicates excess moisture) as an approximate field check.
- Dry grain in thin layers on a clean, raised surface or tarpaulin, turning regularly, and protect from rain and direct ground contact during drying.

## Storage Structure Options

### Hermetic (Airtight) Storage
- Includes hermetic bags (e.g., triple-layer plastic bags) and metal or plastic silos designed to be sealed airtight.
- Controls insect infestation by depleting oxygen inside the sealed container, suffocating insects without chemical treatment.
- Highly effective for both grain and legume storage when properly dried grain is used and the seal is not compromised.
- Requires an initial investment in appropriate bags or containers but reduces ongoing need for chemical grain protectants.

### Traditional Granaries and Cribs
- Elevated structures made from local materials (mud, wood, thatch), commonly used for maize cob storage in many regions.
- Elevation and ventilation help reduce moisture buildup and rodent access when well-constructed and maintained.
- Effectiveness varies significantly with construction quality; gaps or poor sealing allow pest and rodent entry.

### Metal Silos
- Sealed metal containers providing strong protection against insects, rodents, and moisture when properly used.
- Higher upfront cost than bags but durable and reusable over many seasons.
- Must be filled with adequately dried grain, since sealed metal storage can trap moisture and promote mould if grain is not properly dried first.

## Insect Pest Management in Storage
- Key storage pests: maize weevil, larger grain borer, and various bean/cowpea weevils (bruchids).
- Ensure grain is thoroughly dried before storage, since most storage insects are less able to reproduce successfully in properly dried grain.
- Hermetic storage is an effective non-chemical control method for existing infestations by suffocation.
- Where chemical grain protectants are used, confirm currently approved and locally registered products with a national agricultural regulatory authority or extension officer, and strictly follow label instructions and safety waiting periods before consumption.

## Rodent Management
- Store grain in rodent-proof containers or elevate structures with rodent guards (smooth metal or plastic barriers) to prevent access.
- Keep storage areas clean of spilled grain, which attracts rodents.
- Combine physical barriers with appropriate, safe rodent control measures rather than relying on a single method.

## Monitoring Stored Grain
- Inspect stored grain at least monthly for signs of moisture (condensation, clumping), insect activity (live insects, holes in grain, webbing), mould odour, or rodent activity (droppings, gnaw marks).
- Check grain temperature where possible; a noticeable increase in temperature within a storage mass can indicate active insect infestation or mould growth generating heat.
- Address any signs of moisture or infestation promptly, since problems compound quickly in stored grain.

## Aflatoxin and Mycotoxin Risk
- Aflatoxins are toxic compounds produced by certain moulds (particularly *Aspergillus* species) that can develop on grain, especially maize and groundnuts, under warm, humid storage conditions or from inadequate drying.
- Aflatoxin contamination poses serious health risks and can also result in rejection of grain for sale, particularly in formal/export markets with quality standards.
- Prevention relies on: timely harvest, thorough drying to recommended moisture levels before storage, protection from moisture re-entry during storage, and prompt removal of any visibly mouldy grain from storage.
- Visibly mouldy or discoloured grain should not be consumed and should be separated from clean grain immediately.

## Common Mistakes
- Storing grain before it has reached safe moisture content, due to time pressure or lack of drying facilities.
- Reusing storage bags or structures without cleaning them of residue from a previous, possibly infested, batch.
- Ignoring early signs of insect or moisture problems until the entire stored batch is affected.
- Storing different quality or moisture-level batches of grain together, allowing problems to spread.

## When to Consult an Agricultural Extension Officer
- If unsure whether grain has reached safe storage moisture content and no moisture meter is available.
- If signs of significant mould or aflatoxin risk are observed, to get guidance on safe disposal or use of affected grain.
- Before investing in storage infrastructure (silos, hermetic bags) for a larger operation, to select the most cost-effective option for local conditions and grain volumes.
