# Hermetic Storage: PICS Bags and Metal Silos

## Overview
Hermetic (airtight) storage is a non-chemical method of protecting stored grain and legumes from insect damage by sealing produce inside a container that prevents air exchange with the outside environment. As insects and organisms already inside the sealed container respire, they deplete the available oxygen and raise carbon dioxide levels, creating conditions in which most storage insects cannot survive or reproduce. This document explains how hermetic storage works and describes the two most common smallholder-scale hermetic technologies: triple-layer hermetic bags (commonly referred to by the term PICS bags, after the Purdue Improved Crop Storage project that helped popularize the design) and metal or plastic silos. For general grain storage guidance, see `storage/grain_storage.md`; for aflatoxin risk related to storage moisture, see `storage/aflatoxin_prevention.md`; for detail on specific storage insect pests, see `pests/grain_weevils_and_borers.md`, `pests/bruchids.md`, and `pests/larger_grain_borer.md`.

## How Hermetic Storage Works
- A truly airtight seal prevents outside air from entering the storage container, so any insects or mould already present inside continue to consume the limited oxygen trapped within, without it being replenished.
- As oxygen falls and carbon dioxide rises inside the sealed container, insect metabolism, feeding, and reproduction are suppressed, and most insect life stages (eggs, larvae, adults) are eventually killed.
- This process controls existing infestations without adding any chemical grain protectant, and also prevents new insects from entering, since the seal blocks their access.
- Hermetic storage also limits moisture exchange with the outside air, helping maintain a stable moisture level inside the container as long as the grain was properly dried before sealing.
- Hermetic storage does not dry wet grain and does not reduce moisture content; grain must already be at a safe storage moisture level before sealing, or trapped moisture will promote mould growth inside the sealed container.

## PICS and Other Triple-Layer Hermetic Bags

### Construction
- These bags typically consist of two inner liners made of high-density polyethylene (or similar plastic film) placed inside an outer woven polypropylene bag, which provides physical protection and structural strength.
- The inner liners are sealed (commonly by tying tightly, or by twisting and folding, depending on the specific product design) to exclude air, while the outer bag protects the liners from tearing, sunlight, and rough handling.

### Correct Use
- Fill with grain or legumes that have already been dried to the recommended safe moisture content for that crop (see the drying table in `storage/grain_storage.md`); hermetic bags cannot compensate for inadequate drying.
- Expel as much air as practical while sealing the inner liners, since trapped air pockets contain oxygen that can allow some insect survival before the oxygen is depleted.
- Seal the inner liners carefully and completely; a poorly tied, twisted, or damaged seal allows air exchange and defeats the purpose of hermetic storage.
- Avoid puncturing the bags with sharp tools, rough handling, or storage on uneven or sharp surfaces, since a torn liner breaks the airtight seal.
- Store filled bags off the ground on pallets or a raised platform, in a dry, shaded location, away from potential rodent damage, since rodents can chew through the bags.

### Advantages and Limitations
- Lower upfront cost than metal silos, and no need for electricity or ongoing chemical inputs.
- Portable and suitable for smallholder quantities; multiple bags can be used to store larger volumes in modular fashion.
- Bags have a limited number of reuses before the liners degrade, tear, or lose their airtight integrity; inspect liners before reuse and discard any with visible damage.
- Effectiveness depends entirely on achieving and maintaining a complete seal; farmers unfamiliar with the correct sealing technique may not get the full benefit until properly trained.

## Metal and Plastic Silos

### Construction and Use
- Rigid metal (or, less commonly, plastic) silos are manufactured with sealable lids or openings designed to create an airtight enclosure once closed.
- As with bags, grain must be dried to a safe moisture content before filling, since sealed rigid containers can trap and concentrate moisture problems if wet grain is stored.
- Silos are typically filled and sealed once, then opened only when grain is removed for use or sale, minimizing the frequency of air exposure.

### Advantages and Limitations
- Provide strong, durable protection against insects and rodents (rodents generally cannot gnaw through metal) once properly sealed.
- Reusable over many seasons, which can make the higher upfront cost more economical over time compared to repeatedly purchasing bags.
- Larger capacity per unit than typical hermetic bags, useful for households or groups storing larger volumes.
- Higher upfront cost is a barrier for many individual smallholders; some programmes and cooperatives support shared or group ownership of metal silos to spread the cost.
- Metal silos exposed to direct sun can heat up considerably, which may affect grain quality over time even if the seal itself remains intact; site silos in shaded or well-ventilated locations where possible.
- Physical damage (dents, rust holes, damaged seals) can compromise the airtight seal over the silo's lifespan and should be checked regularly.

## Comparing Hermetic Options

| Feature | PICS / Hermetic Bags | Metal Silos |
|---|---|---|
| Upfront cost | Lower | Higher |
| Typical lifespan | Limited number of reuses | Many seasons with proper care |
| Portability | High | Low (fixed or semi-fixed) |
| Rodent resistance | Moderate (liners can be chewed) | High (when intact) |
| Capacity per unit | Smaller, modular | Larger |
| Skill needed for effective sealing | Moderate (technique-dependent) | Lower (mechanical lid/seal) |
| Best suited to | Individual/household, smaller volumes | Household, group, or cooperative, larger volumes |

## Suitable Crops for Hermetic Storage
- Hermetic storage is effective for a wide range of dried cereals (maize, rice, sorghum, millet) and legumes (beans, cowpea, groundnut) once properly dried.
- It is particularly valuable for legumes prone to bruchid (weevil) infestation, since hermetic conditions suffocate bruchids at all life stages without chemical treatment.
- Confirm any crop-specific handling notes with an agricultural extension officer, since some produce may have particular sensitivities not covered by general hermetic storage guidance.

## Maintenance and Reuse
- Inspect hermetic bags and silos at each use for holes, tears, rust, or seal damage, and repair or replace components before the next filling cycle.
- Clean containers thoroughly between uses to remove residue, dust, or pest debris from a previous batch, which could otherwise carry infestation into newly stored produce.
- Keep a record of how many times a bag has been reused, since repeated folding, tying, and handling gradually weakens the liner material even without visible damage.

## Common Mistakes
- Sealing grain or legumes that have not been adequately dried, leading to mould growth inside the airtight container.
- Failing to fully exclude air when sealing bag liners, reducing the oxygen-depletion effect that controls insects.
- Reusing bags with visibly torn, brittle, or degraded liners, which no longer hold an airtight seal.
- Puncturing bags through rough handling, storage on sharp surfaces, or stacking heavy objects on top.
- Assuming hermetic storage can substitute for drying, rather than complementing properly dried produce.
- Leaving metal silos or bags exposed to prolonged direct sun without any shade, increasing internal temperature.

## When to Consult an Agricultural Extension Officer
- Before selecting between hermetic bags and metal silos for a specific household or cooperative's volume and budget.
- If unsure whether grain has reached a safe moisture content for hermetic sealing and no moisture meter is available.
- If a sealed hermetic bag or silo is opened and shows signs of mould, unusual odour, or insect activity despite correct use, to investigate the cause.
- Before investing in shared or group ownership of metal silos, to plan usage scheduling and maintenance responsibilities among users.
