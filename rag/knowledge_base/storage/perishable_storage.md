# Perishable Crop Storage (Fruits, Vegetables, and Root Crops)

## Overview
Fresh fruits, vegetables, and root/tuber crops continue biological processes (respiration, ripening, water loss) after harvest, making them far more perishable than dried grains and requiring different storage strategies focused on temperature, humidity, and handling care.

## Key Principles of Perishable Storage

### Temperature Management
- Lower storage temperature generally slows respiration rate and extends shelf life, but many tropical and subtropical crops are sensitive to chilling injury below certain thresholds.
- Chilling-sensitive crops (e.g., tomato, most tropical fruits) should not be stored below approximately 10-13°C, since lower temperatures cause chilling injury (pitting, discolouration, accelerated decay upon removal from cold storage).
- Crops without chilling sensitivity (e.g., temperate vegetables like cabbage, carrots in cooler regions) can tolerate lower storage temperatures, closer to 0-4°C.

### Humidity Management
- Most fresh produce requires high relative humidity (85-95%) during storage to minimize water loss and shrivelling.
- Root and tuber crops in particular benefit from humid conditions, but excess moisture combined with poor ventilation increases decay risk; adequate airflow is needed alongside humidity.

### Ethylene Sensitivity
- Some produce (e.g., ripe tomatoes, ripening fruits) release ethylene gas, which accelerates ripening and senescence in nearby ethylene-sensitive produce.
- Store ethylene-producing and ethylene-sensitive crops separately where possible to avoid accelerating spoilage of sensitive items.

## Low-Cost Storage Methods for Smallholder Farmers

### Evaporative Cooling Structures
- Simple structures (e.g., a charcoal cooler or zero-energy brick cooler) use evaporation of water from wet sand or bricks to lower internal temperature and raise humidity around stored produce.
- Effective for extending shelf life of vegetables and fruits by several days to a couple of weeks without requiring electricity.
- Most effective in hot, dry climates where evaporation readily occurs; less effective in already humid climates.

### Shade and Ventilation
- Simple shaded, well-ventilated storage areas reduce direct heat exposure and slow deterioration compared to storage in direct sun or poorly ventilated enclosed spaces.
- Raised platforms or slatted shelving improve airflow around stored produce compared to piling directly on the ground.

### Sand or Soil Clamping (for Root Crops)
- Root crops such as cassava can be stored for a limited period by burying in moist sand or soil, which maintains humidity and moderates temperature fluctuation.
- Effectiveness varies by crop and climate; monitor regularly for signs of rot.

### Curing (for Certain Root and Bulb Crops)
- Some root and bulb crops benefit from a curing period (controlled drying of the outer skin under warm, well-ventilated conditions) before storage, which helps heal minor harvest wounds and reduce decay entry points.

## Handling to Reduce Spoilage
- Handle produce gently at every stage (harvest, sorting, transport) since bruising and mechanical damage are major entry points for decay-causing organisms.
- Remove damaged, diseased, or overripe items promptly from a batch, since they can accelerate spoilage of surrounding healthy produce.
- Avoid washing produce before storage unless required for market preparation, since added moisture can promote decay unless produce is then properly dried before storage.

## Processing as an Alternative to Storage
- For crops with very short storage life (e.g., tomato, cassava), processing into more stable forms (drying, pastes, flour, chips) shortly after harvest can be more practical than attempting extended fresh storage.
- Processing decisions should weigh available equipment, market demand for processed vs. fresh product, and expected storage duration needed.

## Common Mistakes
- Storing chilling-sensitive tropical produce at very low refrigeration temperatures, causing chilling injury.
- Mixing ethylene-producing and ethylene-sensitive produce in the same storage space, accelerating spoilage.
- Delaying cooling or shading of produce after harvest, allowing field heat to accelerate deterioration.
- Storing produce with visible damage or decay together with healthy produce.

## When to Consult an Agricultural Extension Officer
- To assess which low-cost storage structure (e.g., evaporative cooler) is best suited to local climate and available materials.
- If experiencing unusually high post-harvest losses despite following general storage guidance, since this may indicate a specific handling, pest, or disease issue.
- Before investing in cold storage or processing equipment for a larger commercial operation.
