# Common Broadleaf Weeds in African Cropland

## Overview
Broadleaf weeds (botanically, dicotyledonous weeds) make up the largest and most diverse group of weeds affecting African cropland, spanning many plant families and growth forms. They range from small, fast-cycling annuals that flush after every rain to persistent perennials that regrow from underground rootstocks. Because of this diversity, no single control method works for all broadleaf weeds; effective management depends on correctly identifying the general group involved and matching cultural, mechanical, and, where appropriate, chemical control to its biology. For general weed control principles, see `weeds/general_weed_management.md`; for the grass and sedge equivalents of this guide, see `weeds/grassy_weeds_general.md` and `weeds/nutsedge_and_couch_grass.md`.

## General Identification Features
- Leaves have net-like (reticulate) venation, in contrast to the parallel venation of grasses.
- Seedlings typically emerge with two seed leaves (cotyledons), unlike grasses, which emerge with a single narrow leaf.
- Flowers are generally more conspicuous than those of grasses, ranging from tiny greenish clusters to showy, colourful blooms, and are often useful for identification once the plant matures.
- Growth forms vary widely: erect herbs, prostrate mat-forming plants, climbing vines, and woody-based perennials are all represented among common cropland broadleaf weeds.

## Common Groups of Broadleaf Weeds

| Weed (common name) | Genus / Family | Key Identification Features | Life Cycle |
|---|---|---|---|
| Pigweed | *Amaranthus* spp. (Amaranthaceae) | Erect annual, alternate simple leaves, small green flower clusters, thrives in fertile soils, prolific seed producer | Annual |
| Purslane | *Portulaca oleracea* (Portulacaceae) | Prostrate, fleshy, reddish stems and succulent leaves; tolerates heat and drought; fragments re-root readily | Annual |
| Blackjack | *Bidens pilosa* (Asteraceae) | Erect annual with serrated compound leaflets, small white-and-yellow flower heads, barbed seeds that cling to clothing and animal fur | Annual |
| Gallant soldier | *Galinsoga parviflora* (Asteraceae) | Low, branching annual, small white flowers with yellow centres, very short life cycle, common in vegetable plots | Annual |
| Fat hen / lambsquarters | *Chenopodium album* (Amaranthaceae) | Erect annual with mealy, grey-green leaves, inconspicuous green flower clusters | Annual |
| Morning glory / bindweed | *Ipomoea* spp. (Convolvulaceae) | Twining vines with funnel-shaped flowers, can climb and smother crops; some species perennial via tuberous roots | Annual or perennial (species-dependent) |
| Wild marigold / khaki weed | *Tagetes minuta* (Asteraceae) | Strongly aromatic erect annual with finely divided leaves; some allelopathic effects reported | Annual |
| Thorn apple / jimsonweed | *Datura stramonium* (Solanaceae) | Coarse erect annual, large lobed leaves, white or purple trumpet flowers, spiny seed capsules; **toxic to people and livestock** | Annual |
| Cocklebur | *Xanthium strumarium* (Asteraceae) | Coarse annual with rough heart-shaped leaves and hooked spiny burs; seedlings **toxic to livestock** | Annual |
| Tropical spiderwort / wandering jew | *Commelina benghalensis* (Commelinaceae) | Creeping, fleshy stems that root at nodes, blue three-petalled flowers, shade-tolerant | Perennial |

**Note on Commelina benghalensis**: although it looks and behaves like a broadleaf weed and is generally grouped with them in field guides, it is botanically a monocot (Commelinaceae), like grasses and sedges. This matters practically: many herbicides that reliably control true broadleaf (dicot) weeds are far less effective on Commelina, which is one reason it persists in fields where other broadleaf weeds have been successfully managed. Its creeping stems also root at each node, giving it a perennial, cutting-resistant habit similar to true perennial weeds; see `weeds/nutsedge_and_couch_grass.md` for related discussion of node-rooting perennial spread.

## Life Cycle Categories
- **Annual broadleaf weeds** (e.g., pigweed, blackjack, gallant soldier, fat hen) complete their life cycle within a single season, relying entirely on seed production for persistence and spread. They are generally the easier group to manage, provided seed set is prevented consistently over time.
- **Perennial broadleaf weeds** (e.g., tropical spiderwort, some *Ipomoea* species with tuberous roots) persist across seasons via rootstocks, creeping stems, or tuberous roots, and require repeated, sustained control similar to the perennial grasses and sedges described elsewhere in this knowledge base.

## Crops Affected
- Virtually every cropping system is affected by one or more broadleaf weed groups, but competition is most damaging in vegetables, grain legumes, and other row crops during the early establishment period.
- Fast-cycling annuals such as gallant soldier and blackjack are especially problematic in vegetable production because of their short generation time, which allows several weed generations within a single cropping cycle if left uncontrolled.
- Climbing species such as morning glory can physically smother young crops and interfere with harvesting in crops like beans, groundnut, and some cereals.
- Toxic species such as thorn apple and cocklebur are a particular concern where crop residues or contaminated hay may be fed to livestock.

## Damage and Competition Caused
- Direct competition for light, water, nutrients, and space, which is most damaging during a crop's critical weed-free period (typically the first three to six weeks after emergence for most annual crops).
- Some broadleaf weeds host pests and diseases that also affect crops; for example, several common broadleaf weeds can serve as alternate hosts for whiteflies, aphids, and certain plant viruses and nematodes, allowing pest and disease populations to persist between cropping seasons.
- Seed contamination of harvested grain, hay, and planting seed reduces market quality and can spread weed seed to new fields.
- Certain species (notably thorn apple and cocklebur) contain toxic compounds that pose a direct poisoning risk to livestock and, in the case of thorn apple, to people if plant material is mistakenly ingested; care should be taken to identify and remove these species from field margins, pastures, and any area where livestock graze or where crop residues will be fed as fodder.

## Management

### Cultural Control
- Rotate crops with different growth habits and planting times to disrupt the life cycles of weeds adapted to a specific cropping pattern.
- Establish dense, uniform crop stands with good early canopy closure to shade out emerging broadleaf weed seedlings.
- Use mulching to suppress germination and early growth, particularly effective against small-seeded annuals such as gallant soldier and fat hen.
- Use clean, certified crop seed to avoid introducing new weed seed into a field.

### Mechanical and Manual Control
- Hand weeding and hoeing remain the most widely used control method for broadleaf weeds on smallholder farms, and are most effective when timed to the crop's critical weed-free period rather than performed on a fixed schedule.
- Remove weeds before flowering and seed set wherever possible; species such as blackjack produce burs that mature and disperse even after the plant has been cut, so cut or pulled plants carrying seed heads should be removed from the field rather than left on the surface.
- For creeping perennial species such as tropical spiderwort, avoid tillage practices that chop stems into fragments capable of re-rooting; remove uprooted material from the field.

### Chemical Control
- Broadleaf-selective herbicides can effectively control true dicot weeds in cereal and other crops where the crop itself is not a broadleaf plant, but selectivity and crop safety depend entirely on matching the correct product and rate to the crop being grown.
- As noted above, common broadleaf-selective herbicide classes are generally less effective against Commelina benghalensis because of its monocot physiology; a different approach, often combining mechanical control with a suitable graminicide or integrated strategy, may be needed where it dominates.
- Always confirm currently approved, locally registered herbicide active ingredients, rates, and crop-specific approvals with an agricultural extension officer or national regulatory authority, and follow label instructions precisely. See `regulations/pesticide_safety_and_handling.md` for safe handling guidance.

## Livestock and Human Safety Notes
- Learn to identify thorn apple and cocklebur specifically, since both are toxic and relatively common in disturbed field margins and pastures; remove them before livestock have access, and do not include them in hay, silage, or crop residue fed to animals.
- Wash hands after handling any unfamiliar weed species, and supervise children around field margins where toxic broadleaf weeds may be present.

## When to Consult an Agricultural Extension Officer
- To confirm the identity of an unfamiliar broadleaf weed, particularly where a toxic species such as thorn apple or cocklebur is suspected.
- Before selecting a broadleaf-selective herbicide, to confirm it is registered, effective, and safe for the specific crop and weed species present.
- If a persistent or spreading perennial broadleaf weed such as tropical spiderwort does not respond to standard broadleaf herbicide programmes, since a different control strategy may be required.
- To help distinguish troublesome broadleaf weeds from grasses, sedges, and parasitic weeds covered elsewhere in this knowledge base, such as `weeds/grassy_weeds_general.md`, `weeds/nutsedge_and_couch_grass.md`, and `weeds/striga.md`.
