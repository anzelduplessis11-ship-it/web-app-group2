# Striga (Witchweed)

## Overview
Striga is a parasitic weed that attaches to the roots of cereal crops and legumes, causing severe yield loss across much of sub-Saharan Africa. Unlike ordinary weeds that compete only for light, water, and nutrients, Striga directly parasitizes the host plant's roots, making it particularly damaging and difficult to control.

## Identification
- Species of concern: *Striga hermonthica* (affecting mainly sorghum, maize, millet, and rice) and *Striga asiatica* and *Striga gesnerioides* (affecting cowpea and other legumes in some regions).
- Small plant, typically 20-50 cm tall, with narrow leaves and attractive pink, red, purple, or white tubular flowers depending on species.
- Most visible damage occurs before the weed emerges above ground: host plants show stunting and wilting from underground parasitism weeks before Striga plants are seen.

## Crops Affected
- Cereals: sorghum, maize, millet, and upland rice.
- Legumes: cowpea and some other legume crops, depending on Striga species present.
- Striga does not typically parasitize non-host crops such as most legumes (for cereal-specific Striga species) or cotton, which is the basis for some rotation-based control strategies.

## Conditions Favouring Infestation
- Low soil fertility, particularly low nitrogen, strongly favours Striga establishment and severity.
- Continuous cereal monocropping without rotation allows the Striga seed bank in the soil to build up over successive seasons.
- Striga seeds can remain viable in the soil for many years, germinating only in response to chemical signals released by the roots of a suitable host crop.

## Damage Caused
- Severe stunting, wilting, and yellowing of the host crop, often mistaken for drought stress or nutrient deficiency.
- Yield losses can range from 20% to complete crop failure in heavily infested fields, even before Striga plants become visible above ground.
- Each Striga plant produces tens of thousands of tiny seeds, rapidly building up the soil seed bank if allowed to flower and set seed.

## Management and Prevention
- **Soil fertility management**: improving soil nitrogen through fertiliser application and organic matter strongly reduces Striga germination and severity, since Striga thrives in nutrient-poor conditions.
- **Crop rotation and intercropping**: rotate with or intercrop cereals with non-host legumes (particularly certain legumes shown to stimulate "suicidal germination" of Striga seeds without successful parasitism) to reduce the soil seed bank over time.
- **Resistant/tolerant varieties**: use cereal varieties bred for Striga resistance or tolerance where available and suited to local conditions.
- **Hand pulling**: remove and destroy Striga plants before they flower and set seed, to prevent further buildup of the seed bank; this must be done consistently over several seasons to have a meaningful impact.
- **Trap crops and push-pull systems**: certain companion plants used in push-pull cropping systems have been shown in regional research to reduce Striga infestation while also managing stem borer pests; consult local extension guidance for suitability.
- **Avoid moving contaminated soil, manure, or crop residue** from infested fields to clean fields, as Striga seeds can be transported this way.

## Monitoring Recommendations
- Inspect cereal crops for unexplained stunting and wilting from 4-6 weeks after emergence, even before Striga plants are visible above ground.
- Scout field margins and previously infested areas for emerging Striga plants, particularly during the flowering period, and remove them before seed set.
- Keep records of field infestation history to guide rotation planning over multiple seasons, since Striga management is a long-term, multi-season effort rather than a single-season fix.

## When Farmers Should Consult an Agricultural Extension Officer
- If unexplained stunting and yellowing appear across a cereal field with no clear nutrient or water cause, to help distinguish Striga parasitism from other stress factors.
- Before selecting a Striga-resistant variety, to confirm which varieties have documented performance against the Striga species present locally.
- To access information on trap crops, rotation sequences, or push-pull systems suited to the local farming system, since these strategies require field-specific planning.
