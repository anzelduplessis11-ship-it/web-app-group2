# Common Grassy Weeds in African Cropland

## Overview
Grassy weeds (botanically, weeds in the grass family, Poaceae) are among the most competitive and difficult-to-control weeds in African farming systems, largely because so many of the region's staple crops — maize, sorghum, millet, rice, wheat, and sugarcane — are themselves grasses. This close botanical relationship narrows the range of herbicides that can selectively remove a grass weed without also harming a grass crop, making cultural and mechanical control especially important for this group. For general weed control principles, see `weeds/general_weed_management.md`; for related perennial grasses and sedges with underground spread, see `weeds/nutsedge_and_couch_grass.md` and `weeds/bermuda_grass_management.md`.

## General Identification Features
- Leaves have parallel venation and are typically narrow, with a sheath that wraps around the stem and a blade that extends outward.
- Stems (culms) are usually rounded or slightly flattened, jointed at nodes, and often hollow between nodes.
- Seedlings emerge with a single, rolled or folded first leaf (unlike the two seed leaves of broadleaf weeds).
- Flowers are small and inconspicuous, wind-pollinated, and arranged in spikelets that form the seed heads characteristic of each species (spikes, panicles, or finger-like racemes).
- Because grass weed seedlings can closely resemble young cereal crop seedlings, correct identification often requires observing the seed head or waiting for the plant to mature.

## Common Groups of Grassy Weeds

| Weed (common name) | Genus | Key Identification Features | Life Cycle |
|---|---|---|---|
| Crabgrass / finger grass | *Digitaria* spp. | Spreading, prostrate to semi-erect habit, finger-like seed heads radiating from a single point | Annual |
| Goosegrass / wiregrass | *Eleusine indica* | Tough, flattened, whitish stem base; finger-like seed head similar to crabgrass but coarser and more wiry | Annual |
| Barnyard grass / jungle rice | *Echinochloa* spp. | Thrives in wet or flooded conditions; seedlings closely resemble young rice, making early identification difficult; a major weed of rice and other wetland crops | Annual |
| Itchgrass | *Rottboellia cochinchinensis* | Tall, vigorous annual with hairy leaf sheaths that cause skin irritation on contact; prolific seed producer; major weed of cereals and sugarcane | Annual |
| Foxtail grasses | *Setaria* spp. | Bristly, cylindrical, brush-like seed heads | Annual |
| Wild sorghum / Johnson grass | *Sorghum halepense* and related wild *Sorghum* species | Tall, cane-like grass spreading by thick rhizomes as well as seed; can cross-pollinate with cultivated sorghum; young or stressed growth can accumulate compounds toxic to livestock | Perennial |
| Bermuda grass / star grass | *Cynodon dactylon* | Low, creeping perennial spreading by both stolons and rhizomes; finger-like seed head; see `weeds/bermuda_grass_management.md` for detailed coverage | Perennial |
| Cogongrass / spear grass | *Imperata cylindrica* | Aggressive rhizomatous perennial with sharp-edged leaves and an off-centre white midrib; colonises degraded and fallow land; allelopathic and fire-tolerant | Perennial |

**Note on sedges**: nutsedges (*Cyperus* spp.) are frequently mistaken for grasses because of their similar narrow leaves, but they are botanically sedges, not true grasses, and are readily distinguished by their triangular (three-sided) stems, compared with the rounded or flattened stems of true grasses. See `weeds/nutsedge_and_couch_grass.md` for full identification and control details.

## Life Cycle Considerations
- **Annual grassy weeds** (e.g., crabgrass, goosegrass, barnyard grass, itchgrass, foxtail grasses) reproduce entirely by seed within a single season but can produce very large numbers of seeds quickly, so preventing seed set is the priority for long-term control.
- **Perennial grassy weeds** (e.g., Johnson grass, Bermuda grass, cogongrass) spread through rhizomes or stolons in addition to seed, allowing them to persist and regrow after cutting, grazing, or shallow cultivation; these require the same sustained, multi-season approach described for other rhizomatous perennials in this knowledge base.

## Crops Affected
- Cereal crops (maize, sorghum, millet, rice, wheat) are particularly vulnerable because the close botanical relationship between crop and weed limits the number of herbicides that can be used selectively without harming the crop.
- Sugarcane is heavily affected by itchgrass, Johnson grass, and Bermuda grass, all of which compete aggressively in the wide row spacing typical of cane production.
- Rice fields are especially susceptible to barnyard grass, which thrives under the same flooded or wet conditions rice requires and closely mimics rice seedlings early in the season.
- Pastures and rangeland can also become dominated by aggressive perennial grasses such as cogongrass, reducing forage quality and grazing capacity over time.

## Damage and Competition Caused
- Grass weeds are strong competitors for soil nitrogen in particular, often outcompeting cereal crops for this critical nutrient during early growth stages.
- Because seedlings of species such as barnyard grass closely resemble the crop itself (especially rice), farmers may fail to remove them in time, allowing substantial yield loss before the weed is correctly identified.
- Several grassy weeds serve as alternate hosts for cereal pests, including stem borers, allowing pest populations to persist in field margins and volunteer growth between cropping seasons.
- Itchgrass causes direct physical discomfort to farm workers, since its hairy leaf sheaths can cause skin irritation during weeding, harvesting, or other field operations.
- Johnson grass and other wild sorghum relatives can accumulate cyanogenic compounds (associated with prussic acid poisoning) in young regrowth or in plants stressed by drought, and in cooler highland areas, by cold or frost damage; this poses a poisoning risk if such growth is grazed or fed to livestock.
- Cogongrass releases allelopathic compounds that can suppress the growth of neighbouring plants, and its dense, dry biomass increases fire risk in fallow land and rangeland.
- Cross-pollination between Johnson grass or other wild *Sorghum* relatives and cultivated sorghum can affect the genetic purity and performance of sorghum seed saved from affected fields.

## Management

### Cultural Control
- Rotate cereal and other grass crops with broadleaf crops (legumes, oilseeds, or vegetables) to break the life cycle of grass weeds adapted to grass-crop cropping patterns, and to open the option of using grass-selective herbicides during the broadleaf phase of the rotation.
- Use the stale seedbed technique: prepare land early, allow a flush of grass weed seedlings to emerge, then destroy them mechanically before planting the crop, reducing the weed seed load the crop will have to compete against.
- Use clean, certified crop seed, since several grassy weeds, including itchgrass and wild sorghum relatives, can be introduced or spread through contaminated seed lots.
- Establish dense, early-canopy-closing crop stands to shade out germinating grass weed seedlings.

### Mechanical and Manual Control
- Hand weeding and hoeing before seed set are critical, since many annual grassy weeds produce large seed quantities rapidly once flowering begins.
- Take particular care to distinguish weed seedlings from crop seedlings before removal, especially barnyard grass in rice, to avoid either damaging the crop or leaving the weed in place too long.
- For rhizomatous perennials such as Johnson grass, Bermuda grass, and cogongrass, avoid tillage that fragments and spreads rhizomes without a firm follow-up plan; see `weeds/nutsedge_and_couch_grass.md` and `weeds/bermuda_grass_management.md` for detailed guidance on managing these species.
- Wear protective clothing when working in fields infested with itchgrass to reduce skin irritation from its hairy leaf sheaths.

### Chemical Control
- Grass-selective herbicides (graminicides) can be used to control grass weeds within broadleaf crops, where the crop itself is not harmed by a grass-targeting product.
- Controlling grass weeds within a grass crop is inherently more difficult; pre-emergence residual herbicides and rotation-based herbicide programmes (using different modes of action across the rotation) are generally required rather than relying on in-season post-emergence options alone.
- Perennial rhizomatous grasses generally require systemic, translocated herbicides that move into underground rhizomes to be effective, similar to the couch grass control principles described in `weeds/nutsedge_and_couch_grass.md`.
- Always confirm currently approved, locally registered herbicide active ingredients, rates, and crop-specific approvals with an agricultural extension officer or national regulatory authority before use. See `regulations/pesticide_safety_and_handling.md` for safe handling guidance.

## When to Consult an Agricultural Extension Officer
- To confirm identification of a grass weed seedling, especially when it closely resembles the crop itself, as with barnyard grass in rice.
- To confirm which graminicides or grass-selective herbicide options are registered and safe for a specific crop and rotation.
- If Johnson grass, Bermuda grass, cogongrass, or another persistent perennial grass is spreading despite repeated control, to design a multi-season integrated management plan.
- For guidance on protective measures and safe field practices where itchgrass or other irritant species are present.
