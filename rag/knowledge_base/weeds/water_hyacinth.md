# Water Hyacinth (Eichhornia crassipes)

## Overview
Water hyacinth is a free-floating aquatic weed, native to the Amazon basin in South America, that has become one of the most damaging invasive plants in freshwater bodies across Africa. It forms dense floating mats on lakes, rivers, dams, and irrigation canals, and is widely regarded as one of the world's worst aquatic weeds because of how quickly it multiplies and how broadly it disrupts irrigation, fisheries, transport, and water quality. Infestations such as those historically seen on Lake Victoria and various river systems and irrigation schemes across the continent illustrate how rapidly this weed can transform a water body once established. Because it affects shared water resources rather than a single field, its management usually requires coordination beyond an individual farm.

## Identification
- Family: Pontederiaceae.
- A free-floating perennial aquatic plant (occasionally rooting in mud in very shallow water), typically forming dense interlocking mats on the water surface.
- Leaves are rounded to oval, glossy dark green, and attached to inflated, spongy petioles (leaf stalks) that trap air and give the plant its buoyancy.
- Roots are thick, feathery, and purplish-black, hanging freely in the water beneath each plant and providing habitat for small aquatic organisms.
- Produces attractive lavender to purple flowers, each with a distinctive yellow-and-blue marking at the base of the uppermost petal, arranged on an upright spike above the leaves.
- Individual plants connect via short runners (stolons) to form dense, continuous mats that can cover entire bays, canals, or sections of river.

## Biology and Spread
- Water hyacinth reproduces both vegetatively and by seed, and its vegetative reproduction is exceptionally fast: under warm, nutrient-rich conditions, plant numbers and mat coverage can double in a matter of one to two weeks.
- It also produces large numbers of seeds that sink and settle in bottom sediment, where they can remain viable for many years, meaning that even a water body cleared of floating mats can re-infest itself from the seed bank in the sediment.
- It spreads between water bodies via water currents and flooding, wind-driven drift of mats, attachment to boats, fishing gear, and other equipment, and, in some cases, deliberate or accidental introduction through the ornamental pond and aquarium plant trade.
- Growth is especially explosive in nutrient-rich (eutrophic) water, particularly water bodies receiving agricultural fertiliser runoff, animal waste, or untreated sewage, which supply the nitrogen and phosphorus the plant needs for rapid growth.

## Water Bodies and Irrigation Systems Affected
- Irrigation canals, main and secondary channels, and offtake structures, where mats physically obstruct water flow.
- Dams, reservoirs, and water storage structures used for irrigation, domestic water supply, or hydropower.
- Rivers, lakes, and wetlands, including those supporting fisheries and water transport.
- Fish ponds and small water storage structures on individual farms.

## Damage Caused
- **Blocked irrigation infrastructure**: dense mats clog canals, intake screens, and pumps, reducing or completely stopping water delivery to fields and raising the cost and effort of maintaining irrigation systems.
- **Reduced water flow and increased water loss**: mats slow water movement in channels and increase water loss through evapotranspiration compared with open water, reducing the water actually available for irrigation.
- **Impeded transport and fishing**: dense mats can make boat access difficult or impossible, disrupting fishing operations, ferrying, and other water-based transport and trade.
- **Water quality decline**: decomposing plant material consumes dissolved oxygen, harming fish and other aquatic life, while thick mats block sunlight and gas exchange at the water surface, further degrading conditions beneath the mat.
- **Disease vector habitat**: the dense root mats and still water beneath infestations can provide breeding habitat for mosquitoes and disease-carrying snails, raising local public health concerns in some contexts.
- **Hydropower and infrastructure impacts**: mats can obstruct water intake structures at hydroelectric facilities, and can damage or wear pumps and other equipment drawing water through infested areas.
- **Economic losses**: cumulative impacts on irrigation, fisheries, transport, and, in some areas, tourism can be substantial for communities dependent on affected water bodies.

## Monitoring Recommendations
- Inspect irrigation canals, reservoirs, and other water sources regularly for early signs of floating plants, since small new infestations are far easier and cheaper to control than established mats.
- Pay particular attention to intake points, canal junctions, and slow-moving or still sections of water, where mats tend to accumulate first.
- Monitor for signs of nutrient enrichment (unusually rapid algal or aquatic plant growth generally) in water bodies receiving farm runoff, since this indicates conditions favourable to water hyacinth establishment.
- Coordinate monitoring with neighbouring water users and the relevant water management authority, since this weed spreads along shared water systems regardless of individual field or farm boundaries.

## Control

### Manual and Mechanical Control
- Hand removal is practical for very small or new infestations, particularly in confined channels, but must be repeated as regrowth from remaining fragments and germinating seed occurs.
- Mechanical harvesters or booms can remove larger quantities of biomass from canals, dams, and reservoirs where equipment is available, and are often used at high-priority sites such as major irrigation intakes.
- Physical barriers or booms placed across canals and channel entrances can intercept and concentrate floating mats before they reach intake structures, making removal more efficient and protecting critical infrastructure.
- All mechanical control requires ongoing maintenance, since the weed's rapid vegetative growth means that partial control efforts can be quickly reversed if not sustained.

### Biological Control
- Several insect biological control agents have been introduced into African water bodies, most notably weevils (*Neochetina eichhorniae* and *Neochetina bruchi*), whose larvae and adults feed on the plant and, over time, can substantially suppress mat growth and vigour.
- A moth species (*Niphograpta albiguttalis*) and various fungal pathogens have also been investigated as additional biological control options in some regions, with variable success.
- Biological control is slow-acting, typically taking multiple years to establish and show substantial impact, but it is self-sustaining once established and can provide long-term suppression at a landscape scale that mechanical control alone cannot match.
- Biological control works best as part of an integrated strategy, combined with mechanical removal at critical infrastructure points while biological agents suppress growth more broadly across the water body.

### Chemical Control
- Herbicides registered specifically for aquatic use can be effective against actively growing water hyacinth, but chemical control in open water carries significant risk to fish, other aquatic life, and downstream water users, including those relying on the water for drinking or domestic use.
- Chemical control in shared water bodies should only be carried out using products specifically registered for aquatic application, by trained personnel, and in coordination with the relevant water resource or catchment management authority and environmental regulations.
- Because of these risks, chemical control is generally reserved for situations where mechanical and biological methods are insufficient, and is not recommended as a routine or first-line option for individual farmers to apply independently in canals or water bodies shared with others.

### Addressing the Root Cause: Nutrient Management
- Because explosive water hyacinth growth is strongly linked to nutrient-rich water, reducing the nutrient load entering water bodies is an important long-term control strategy.
- Practices such as maintaining vegetative buffer strips along waterways, applying fertilisers accurately and avoiding excess application near water sources, and properly managing animal waste and sewage all help reduce the nutrient runoff that fuels infestations.
- Nutrient management will not eliminate an existing infestation on its own but reduces the rate at which the weed can regrow after control efforts and helps prevent new infestations in previously clean water bodies.

## Prevention
- Never discard aquarium or ornamental pond plants, including water hyacinth, into rivers, dams, canals, or other natural water bodies.
- Clean boats, nets, pumps, and other equipment thoroughly before moving them between water bodies to avoid transporting plant fragments or seed.
- Support community-based early detection and rapid response systems, since coordinated action across a catchment is far more effective than isolated efforts by individual farmers.
- Where locally regulated, avoid trading or planting water hyacinth as an ornamental species in areas at risk of infestation.

## Safety and Environmental Considerations
- Because water hyacinth affects shared water resources, coordinate control efforts with the relevant water resource, irrigation scheme, or catchment management authority rather than acting in isolation.
- Exercise particular caution with chemical control near sources of drinking water, fish ponds, and active fisheries, given the potential for harm to aquatic life and water users downstream.
- Removed plant material can be composted away from waterways, or in some cases put to secondary uses such as mulch, animal bedding, or craft material after adequate drying or composting; avoid stockpiling removed biomass where it, or its seeds, can wash back into the water.
- Wear appropriate protective clothing when handling large volumes of decomposing plant material, and be aware that infested water bodies may carry an elevated risk of disease vectors such as mosquitoes and snails.

## When Farmers Should Consult an Agricultural Extension Officer or Water Authority
- To report a new infestation promptly, since early intervention while the infestation is small is far more effective and less costly than controlling an established mat.
- Before undertaking any chemical control in a shared canal, dam, or water body, to confirm registered aquatic-use products and coordinate with other water users.
- To find out whether biological control agents are already established locally and how their impact is being monitored.
- For guidance on reducing nutrient runoff from farm operations into nearby water bodies as part of long-term prevention.
