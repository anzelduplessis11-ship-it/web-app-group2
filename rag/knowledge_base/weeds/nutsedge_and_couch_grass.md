# Nutsedge and Couch Grass

## Overview
Nutsedge (also called nutgrass) and couch grass (also called Bermuda grass, quack grass, or star grass depending on region and species) are among the most troublesome perennial weeds on African smallholder farms. Unlike annual weeds that spread only by seed, these weeds also spread through underground structures, allowing them to regrow persistently even after the above-ground parts are removed. This makes them difficult to eradicate with a single control measure and requires a sustained, integrated approach. For general weed control principles, see `weeds/general_weed_management.md`.

## Identification

### Nutsedge (Cyperus species)
- Grass-like plant but not a true grass; stems are distinctly triangular (three-sided) in cross-section, unlike the round or flattened stems of true grasses.
- Leaves are shiny, V-shaped in cross-section, and arranged in groups of three from the stem base; colour ranges from yellowish-green (yellow nutsedge) to darker green (purple nutsedge).
- Produces small underground tubers ("nuts") along the roots, which are the main means of spread and survival.
- Often forms dense patches and grows rapidly in warm, moist conditions.

### Couch Grass (Cynodon and related species)
- A true grass with creeping habit, spreading by above-ground runners (stolons) and underground stems (rhizomes) that root at the nodes.
- Forms a dense mat that can quickly cover bare soil and crop rows.
- Narrow leaves and wiry stems; seed heads often have several finger-like spikes radiating from a single point.

## Why They Are Hard to Control
- **Underground storage organs**: nutsedge tubers and couch grass rhizomes store energy and buds below the soil surface, allowing regrowth after the above-ground plant is cut, pulled, or killed.
- **Fragmentation spreads them**: tillage that chops rhizomes into pieces can multiply couch grass rather than control it, since each fragment with a node can grow into a new plant. Nutsedge tubers similarly survive cultivation and can be spread on tools and in soil.
- **Persistent seed and tuber banks**: tubers and rhizomes can remain viable in the soil for extended periods, so infestations rebuild over several seasons if not managed consistently.
- **Rapid regrowth**: both weeds regrow quickly in warm, moist conditions, competing with crops early in the season when crops are most vulnerable.

## Crops Affected
- Widely damaging in vegetables, maize, sugarcane, cassava, and other row and field crops, particularly where soils are moist and fertile.
- Competition is most damaging during the crop's early establishment stage, when these weeds compete for light, water, and nutrients and can significantly reduce yield.
- Dense infestations also interfere with land preparation, weeding labour, and harvesting.

## Control Methods

### Cultural Control
- Establish a vigorous, competitive crop stand (appropriate spacing, healthy seedlings, adequate fertility) so the crop shades the soil and suppresses weed growth.
- Use crop rotation and, where practical, a fallow or cover-crop phase with a dense, competitive cover crop to smother the weeds and deplete their underground reserves over time.
- Maintain clean field margins, as these weeds often spread inward from uncultivated edges.

### Mechanical and Physical Control
- Repeated, timely removal of above-ground growth exhausts the tubers and rhizomes over time, since the plant must draw on stored reserves to regrow. Consistency over multiple cycles matters more than any single weeding.
- Where feasible, careful hand-digging to remove tubers and rhizomes intact (rather than chopping them) reduces regrowth; remove the dug material from the field so fragments do not re-root.
- Avoid tillage practices that fragment and spread rhizomes; if cultivating, follow up promptly before fragments re-establish.
- Mulching with a thick organic layer or, where available, opaque sheeting can suppress emergence by blocking light, though vigorous nutsedge can sometimes pierce thin mulch.

### Chemical Control
- Chemical control alone rarely eradicates these perennials and works best as part of an integrated programme combined with cultural and mechanical methods.
- Selective and non-selective herbicide options exist, but effectiveness, crop safety, and legality vary widely. Refer to herbicides by their locally registered active-ingredient class rather than any brand name.
- Systemic (translocated) herbicides that move down into the tubers and rhizomes are generally more effective on perennials than contact herbicides, which only kill exposed foliage.
- Always confirm currently approved and locally registered products, correct crop-specific use, and safety intervals with an agricultural extension officer or the national regulatory authority, and strictly follow label instructions. See `regulations/pesticide_safety_and_handling.md` for safe handling guidance.

## Prevention
- Clean tools, implements, and machinery before moving between fields to avoid transporting tubers and rhizome fragments.
- Avoid moving contaminated soil, manure, or crop residue from infested fields to clean fields.
- Inspect new planting material and incoming soil or manure for contamination.
- Address small, new infestations promptly and persistently, since these weeds are far easier to manage before they establish extensive underground networks.

## Common Mistakes
- Relying on a single weeding or a single herbicide application and expecting eradication of a perennial weed.
- Ploughing through couch grass in a way that chops and spreads rhizomes, worsening the infestation.
- Allowing weeds to regrow fully between weedings, letting them replenish their underground reserves.
- Neglecting field margins and then wondering why infestations keep returning.

## When to Consult an Agricultural Extension Officer
- If an infestation is severe or spreading despite repeated control efforts, to design a multi-season integrated management plan.
- To confirm which herbicide active-ingredient classes are locally registered, appropriate for the crop, and safe to use.
- For advice on suitable competitive cover crops or rotations for the local farming system.
- For distinguishing these perennial weeds from other problem weeds; for the parasitic weed Striga, see `weeds/striga.md`.
