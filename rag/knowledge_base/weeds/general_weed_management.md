# General Weed Management Principles

## Overview
Weeds compete with crops for light, water, nutrients, and space, and can host pests and diseases. Effective weed management combines prevention, cultural practices, mechanical control, and, where appropriate, chemical control, applied at the correct timing for each crop.

## Why Weed Timing Matters
- Every crop has a "critical weed-free period" — a window early in growth during which weed competition causes the greatest yield loss.
- For most cereals and legumes, this critical period falls within the first 3-6 weeks after emergence.
- Weeding after this critical period provides less yield benefit, since much of the competitive damage has already occurred, though it remains important to prevent weeds from setting seed and building up the soil weed seed bank.

## Categories of Weeds
| Category | Description | Examples of Management Considerations |
|---|---|---|
| Annual broadleaf weeds | Complete lifecycle in one season, reproduce by seed | Generally controlled by timely hand weeding, hoeing, or post-emergence herbicides |
| Annual grasses | Complete lifecycle in one season, compete strongly for nutrients | Similar control to broadleaf weeds; some require grass-specific herbicides |
| Perennial weeds | Persist multiple seasons via rhizomes, tubers, or rootstocks | Require repeated control over time; hand weeding alone often insufficient |
| Parasitic weeds | Attach to host crop roots (e.g., Striga) | Require long-term, multi-season strategies including soil fertility management and resistant varieties; see `weeds/striga.md` |

## Cultural Weed Control Practices
- **Crop rotation**: disrupts weed life cycles adapted to a specific cropping pattern.
- **Dense, uniform planting**: crops with good early canopy closure suppress weeds by shading them out.
- **Mulching**: organic or plastic mulch suppresses weed germination and growth by blocking light.
- **Clean seed and equipment**: using certified, weed-seed-free crop seed and cleaning equipment between fields reduces the introduction of new weed species.
- **Timely land preparation**: ploughing and harrowing before planting can expose and desiccate weed seedlings and buried weed seeds.

## Mechanical and Manual Weed Control
- Hand weeding and hoeing remain the most widely used weed control method for smallholder farmers across Africa.
- Effective timing is more important than frequency: two well-timed weedings often outperform three poorly timed ones.
- Inter-row cultivation with hand or animal-drawn implements can efficiently manage weeds between crop rows in wider-spaced crops.

## Chemical Weed Control (Herbicides)
- Pre-emergence herbicides are applied to the soil before crop and weed emergence, forming a barrier that prevents weed seedling establishment.
- Post-emergence herbicides are applied after weeds have emerged and target actively growing weeds; selectivity (crop safety) depends on matching the correct herbicide to the crop.
- Always confirm currently approved and locally registered herbicide active ingredients with a national agricultural regulatory authority or extension officer, since registered products, rates, and crop-specific approvals vary by country.
- Follow label instructions precisely regarding application rate, timing, and crop stage, since herbicide misuse can damage crops or leave harmful residues.

## Integrated Weed Management
- No single method controls all weeds effectively; combining cultural, mechanical, and (where appropriate) chemical methods gives the most reliable and sustainable results.
- Rotate control methods and, where herbicides are used, rotate modes of action across seasons to reduce the risk of herbicide-resistant weed populations developing.
- Monitor fields regularly to identify shifts in the dominant weed species present, since weed management practices over time can favour certain weed types over others.

## Safety Considerations for Herbicide Use
- Wear appropriate personal protective equipment (gloves, mask, protective clothing) when handling and applying herbicides.
- Avoid spray drift onto neighbouring crops, especially broadleaf crops near grass-selective or broadleaf-selective herbicide applications.
- Observe the pre-harvest interval specified on the product label before harvesting treated crops.
- Store herbicides securely, separate from food and water sources, and away from children.

## When to Consult an Agricultural Extension Officer
- If a weed problem persists or worsens despite consistent management, which may indicate a shift toward resistant weed populations or an unidentified parasitic weed.
- Before using an unfamiliar herbicide product, to confirm it is approved for the specific crop and correctly timed for the growth stage.
- If unusual or unfamiliar weed species appear in a field, to assist with identification and appropriate management strategy.
