# Alectra (Yellow Witchweed)

## Overview
Alectra vogelii, commonly known as yellow witchweed or cowpea witchweed, is a parasitic weed closely related to Striga but distinguished by its strong preference for legume hosts rather than cereals. It is a major constraint on cowpea, groundnut, and Bambara groundnut production in parts of southern, eastern, and western Africa, causing damage that is often underestimated because, like Striga, most of the harm occurs underground before the weed is ever seen above the soil surface. For related parasitic weed information, see `weeds/striga.md`; for general weed control principles, see `weeds/general_weed_management.md`.

## Identification
- Family: Orobanchaceae — the same family as Striga.
- A small, hairy herb, generally shorter and less showy than Striga, with stems and leaves covered in short bristly hairs.
- Flowers are typically bright yellow and tubular to star-shaped, a key distinguishing feature from Striga, whose flowers are usually pink, red, purple, or white depending on species.
- As with Striga, the majority of the plant's damage occurs underground during a parasitic phase that precedes emergence; visible yellow-flowered shoots above ground indicate that substantial damage to the host has likely already occurred.
- Below ground, Alectra attaches to host roots using specialised structures called haustoria, through which it withdraws water, nutrients, and photosynthate from the host plant.

## Striga vs Alectra: Key Differences

| Feature | Striga | Alectra |
|---|---|---|
| Primary hosts | Cereals (sorghum, maize, millet, upland rice); some species affect cowpea | Legumes (cowpea, groundnut, Bambara groundnut) |
| Flower colour | Pink, red, purple, or white (species-dependent) | Yellow |
| Family | Orobanchaceae | Orobanchaceae |
| Underground parasitic phase before emergence | Yes | Yes |
| Germination trigger | Chemical signals (strigolactones) from host roots | Chemical signals (strigolactones) from host roots |
| Seed bank persistence | Many years | Many years |

Because both weeds rely on the same underground germination-and-attachment strategy and both are favoured by low soil fertility, they are often discussed together, but correct identification matters: a field rotation designed to reduce Striga (rotating cereals with legumes) can, if the wrong legume is chosen, do little to reduce — or in some cases help maintain — an Alectra problem, and vice versa. Farmers dealing with both parasitic weeds in the same farming system need a rotation plan that accounts for both.

## Biology and Life Cycle
- Alectra produces extremely small, dust-like seeds in very large numbers — comparable to Striga — allowing rapid buildup of a soil seed bank wherever a susceptible host legume is grown repeatedly.
- Seeds can remain dormant but viable in the soil for many years, germinating only when they detect chemical signals (strigolactones) released by the roots of a nearby host plant.
- After germination, the young Alectra seedling must attach to a suitable host root within a short window or it will die; successful attachment initiates the underground parasitic phase, during which the weed draws water and nutrients from the host for several weeks before any shoot emerges above ground.
- Only after this underground phase does Alectra send up the visible yellow-flowered shoot, by which point significant damage to the host crop has typically already occurred.

## Crops and Situations Affected
- Cowpea is the most significantly affected crop, with heavy losses reported in susceptible varieties grown under continuous cropping.
- Groundnut and Bambara groundnut are also affected, along with some other legume species depending on the local Alectra population.
- The problem is most severe in areas with a long history of continuous legume cropping without rotation, and on soils with low fertility, particularly low nitrogen and phosphorus.

## Conditions Favouring Infestation
- Low soil fertility, especially low nitrogen and phosphorus, strongly favours both the germination success and the severity of Alectra parasitism, similar to the pattern seen with Striga.
- Continuous cropping of susceptible legumes without rotation allows the soil seed bank to build up steadily over successive seasons.
- Long-term seed viability in the soil means that fields can carry a hidden Alectra problem for years before symptoms become obvious, particularly if susceptible legumes have been grown repeatedly in the past.

## Damage Caused
- Stunting, yellowing (chlorosis), and wilting of the host legume, frequently mistaken for nutrient deficiency, drought stress, or disease, especially before any Alectra shoots are visible above ground.
- Reduced flowering and pod formation, leading directly to lower grain yield.
- Yield losses can be severe in heavily infested fields with susceptible varieties, particularly where poor soil fertility compounds the direct effects of parasitism.
- As with Striga, each mature Alectra plant that is allowed to flower and set seed contributes tens of thousands of seeds to the soil seed bank, worsening the problem in future seasons.

## Management and Prevention
- **Resistant or tolerant varieties**: several cowpea varieties have been screened and bred for resistance or tolerance to Alectra; consult local extension services or agricultural research institutions to identify varieties with documented performance against the Alectra populations present locally.
- **Soil fertility management**: improving nitrogen and phosphorus fertility through appropriate fertiliser use and organic matter inputs reduces both germination stimulant activity and the severity of parasitism once attachment occurs.
- **Crop rotation**: rotate susceptible legumes with non-host cereals or other non-host crops to reduce the Alectra seed bank over multiple seasons; where both Striga and Alectra are present, plan rotations carefully so that the sequence addresses both parasites rather than inadvertently favouring one while suppressing the other.
- **Hand pulling (roguing)**: remove and destroy Alectra shoots before they flower and set seed; this must be repeated consistently across several seasons to meaningfully reduce the soil seed bank, since a single season of roguing will not eliminate an established infestation.
- **Avoid spreading contaminated soil, manure, or crop residue** from infested fields to clean fields, since Alectra seed can be transported this way, just as with Striga.
- **Trap and catch cropping**: certain non-host or poorly-suited host plants can stimulate Alectra seed germination without successful parasitism ("suicidal germination"), depleting the seed bank over time; consult local extension guidance for options validated for the local farming system, since suitable trap crops depend on the specific Alectra population and local cropping conditions.

## Monitoring Recommendations
- Inspect legume fields for unexplained stunting, yellowing, or wilting from several weeks after emergence, well before any Alectra shoots may be visible above ground.
- Scout field margins and areas with a history of continuous legume cropping for emerging yellow-flowered shoots, particularly during the flowering period, and remove them before seed set.
- Keep field-by-field infestation records over multiple seasons to guide rotation planning, since, as with Striga, effective Alectra management is a long-term, multi-season commitment rather than a single-season intervention.

## When Farmers Should Consult an Agricultural Extension Officer
- If unexplained stunting and yellowing appear in a legume crop with no clear nutrient or water-related cause, to help distinguish Alectra parasitism from other stress factors.
- To confirm which cowpea or other legume varieties have documented resistance or tolerance to the Alectra populations present locally.
- To design a rotation sequence that addresses both Alectra and Striga where a farm faces both parasitic weeds, since a rotation optimised for one alone may not adequately address the other.
- For guidance on trap crops or other stimulant-based control approaches suited to the local farming system.
