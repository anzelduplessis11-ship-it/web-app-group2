# Weather Impacts on Farming

## Overview
Weather is one of the most significant factors determining crop performance, pest and disease pressure, and farm operation timing. Understanding how different weather conditions affect crops helps farmers plan planting, input application, and risk management decisions.

## Rainfall

### Rainfall Amount and Distribution
- Total seasonal rainfall matters less than its distribution; a season with adequate total rainfall but a long dry spell during a crop's critical water-sensitive stage (e.g., flowering) can still cause severe yield loss.
- Erratic onset of rains complicates planting date decisions; planting too early into a false start can result in seedling death from subsequent dry spells, while planting too late shortens the effective growing season.

### Drought
- Reduces yield most severely when it coincides with critical growth stages (flowering, grain/fruit filling) rather than during vegetative growth.
- Drought-tolerant crop choices (e.g., sorghum, millet, cassava, cowpea) and varieties can reduce risk in drought-prone areas.
- Water conservation practices (mulching, reduced tillage, water harvesting) help buffer against short dry spells.

### Excess Rainfall and Flooding
- Waterlogging reduces root oxygen availability, causing stunted growth, yellowing, and increased susceptibility to root diseases.
- Heavy rainfall events increase soil erosion risk, particularly on sloped or bare land, and can physically damage crops (lodging, flower/fruit drop).
- Flooding of low-lying fields can cause complete crop loss; drainage planning and crop choice (e.g., rice, which tolerates flooding, versus flood-sensitive crops) should account for local flood risk.

## Temperature

### Heat Stress
- High temperatures during flowering commonly cause poor pollination and fruit/grain set across many crops (maize, beans, tomato), even when soil moisture is adequate.
- Extended heat stress accelerates crop development, sometimes shortening the grain/fruit filling period and reducing final yield.

### Cold Stress and Frost
- Frost causes direct tissue damage and can kill sensitive crops (e.g., maize, beans, tomato) outright; frost risk is a key consideration for planting date and altitude/region selection.
- Cool temperatures, even without frost, can slow germination, extend the growing period, and increase susceptibility to certain fungal diseases in some crops.

## Humidity and Leaf Wetness
- Extended periods of high humidity or leaf wetness (from rain, dew, or overhead irrigation) strongly favour many fungal and oomycete diseases (e.g., late blight, various leaf spot diseases).
- Dry conditions generally reduce fungal disease pressure but can favour certain pests (e.g., aphids, spider mites, some leafhopper vectors) that thrive in warm, dry weather.

## Wind
- Strong winds can cause physical crop damage (lodging in cereals, leaf shredding, fruit drop).
- Wind also increases evapotranspiration, raising crop water demand, and can spread certain wind-dispersed pathogen spores (e.g., late blight) and pests over greater distances.
- Windbreaks (tree lines, hedges) can reduce wind speed at crop level and moderate these effects on adjacent fields.

## Seasonal and Climate Variability Considerations
- Many African farming regions experience significant year-to-year rainfall variability, making historical average planting calendars a useful starting guide rather than a guaranteed schedule (see `planting_calendars/` documents).
- Climate change is associated with increasing variability and shifts in the timing and reliability of seasonal rainfall in many regions, reinforcing the value of flexible planting decisions based on observed conditions rather than fixed calendar dates alone.
- Diversifying crops and varieties (including drought-tolerant and short-duration options) helps manage weather-related production risk.

## Using Weather Information for Farm Decisions
- Monitor short-term forecasts to time planting, fertiliser application (avoiding immediate heavy rain that can wash away surface-applied fertiliser), and pesticide/herbicide application (avoiding application immediately before rain, which reduces effectiveness).
- Track seasonal rainfall onset patterns locally where possible, since even within a broad region, local onset timing can vary from the general regional pattern.
- Use weather-informed risk management (e.g., staggered planting dates, drought-tolerant variety selection) rather than relying on a single fixed strategy each season.

## Common Mistakes
- Planting immediately after the first rain of the season without confirming it represents genuine season onset rather than an isolated early shower.
- Applying fertiliser or pesticide immediately before heavy rain, reducing effectiveness and increasing runoff/environmental loss.
- Ignoring local microclimate differences (e.g., low-lying frost pockets, wind-exposed ridges) when planning field-specific crop choices.

## When to Consult an Agricultural Extension Officer or Meteorological Service
- To access localized seasonal forecast information for planting date planning.
- If experiencing recurring weather-related crop losses, to discuss risk management strategies (e.g., crop insurance where available, variety diversification, water harvesting structures) suited to the specific farm.
- For guidance on responding to unusual or severe weather events (e.g., unseasonal frost, extreme flooding) affecting standing crops.
