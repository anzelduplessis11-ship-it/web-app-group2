# Heatwave Management for Crops and Livestock

## Overview
A heatwave is a prolonged period of unusually high temperatures for a given location and season, lasting from several days to weeks. Extreme heat stresses both crops and livestock, reducing yields, productivity, and, in severe cases, causing outright losses or mortality. Heat stress often compounds other weather risks, since heatwaves frequently coincide with or follow dry spells and increase overall water demand at the same time that water may be scarce. This document covers how heat stress affects crops and livestock, practical measures to reduce its impact, and priorities during and after a heatwave. For general temperature effects, see `weather/weather_impacts_on_farming.md`; for drought-specific water management, see `weather/drought_and_flood_management.md` and `irrigation/irrigation_scheduling.md`.

## How Heat Stress Affects Crops

### Flowering and Pollination
- Many crops are most vulnerable to heat stress during flowering and pollination; high temperatures at this stage commonly cause poor pollen viability, flower or pod abortion, and reduced fruit or grain set in crops such as maize, beans, and tomato, even when soil moisture is otherwise adequate.
- Because this sensitive window is often brief, a short but intense heat event timed at flowering can cause disproportionate yield loss compared to the same heat occurring during vegetative growth.

### Water Demand and Growth
- Higher temperatures increase evapotranspiration (water loss from soil and plant surfaces), raising a crop's water requirement at exactly the time when heat and any accompanying dry conditions make water less available.
- Sustained heat stress accelerates the crop's developmental clock, which can shorten the grain- or fruit-filling period and reduce final yield even where the plant otherwise appears to tolerate the heat.
- Heat stress can also reduce photosynthetic efficiency and increase respiration losses, further reducing the carbohydrate available for yield formation.

### Secondary Effects: Pests and Sunscald
- Hot, dry conditions favour some pests, such as spider mites and certain aphid species, which can reproduce rapidly in warm weather and add further stress to already heat-affected crops.
- Direct sun and heat exposure can cause sunscald (localized tissue damage) on exposed fruit and leaves, particularly where a plant has recently lost canopy cover from pruning, defoliation, or pest damage.
- Heat-stressed plants are generally more vulnerable to secondary stresses such as moisture deficit and pest pressure than plants growing under moderate conditions, so combined stresses during a heatwave often cause more damage than any single stress alone.

## Managing Heat Stress in Crops

### Water Management
- Where irrigation is available, timing watering for the early morning or evening reduces immediate evaporative loss and helps cool the root zone and surrounding microclimate compared to midday watering.
- Maintaining consistent soil moisture through a heatwave, rather than allowing the crop to experience combined heat and drought stress, meaningfully reduces the risk of flower and fruit abortion (see `irrigation/irrigation_scheduling.md`).
- Mulching reduces soil surface temperature and evaporation, helping conserve soil moisture and moderate root-zone conditions during extreme heat (see `soil/soil_conservation_and_erosion.md`).

### Shade and Cover
- Shade netting or temporary shading over high-value crops and nursery beds can reduce direct heat and sun exposure during the most extreme part of a heatwave, particularly useful for seedlings and transplants, which are especially vulnerable.
- Maintaining adequate plant canopy cover (avoiding unnecessary defoliation or excessive pruning ahead of expected heat) helps protect fruit and lower stems from direct sun exposure and sunscald.

### Variety Choice and Planting Timing
- Where heat-tolerant varieties are available and proven for local conditions, they can reduce the risk of flowering-stage heat damage; confirm suitability with an agricultural extension officer rather than assuming a variety marketed as heat-tolerant performs well in every location.
- Where feasible, adjusting planting dates so that the flowering stage of heat-sensitive crops avoids the historically hottest part of the season can reduce exposure, balanced against rainfall-driven planting timing constraints.
- Diversifying crops and varieties with different heat sensitivities and flowering windows spreads the risk that a single heatwave affects the entire farm's production at once (see `weather/climate_smart_agriculture.md`).

## How Heat Stress Affects Livestock
Livestock, like crops, have a thermal comfort range outside of which they must expend energy to regulate body temperature; sustained heat above this range reduces feed intake, growth, milk production, and fertility, and in severe or prolonged cases can cause mortality, particularly in young, old, sick, or high-producing animals.

### Recognising Heat Stress
Common general signs of heat stress across livestock species include increased respiration rate and open-mouth panting, reduced feed intake, increased water intake, seeking shade or wallowing, reduced activity and lying down more, and in severe cases, weakness, staggering, or collapse.

| Species | Additional heat stress signs | Notes on vulnerability |
|---|---|---|
| Poultry | Wing-spreading away from the body, panting, reduced egg production, thinner or smaller eggshells, huddling near water sources | Among the most heat-vulnerable livestock due to limited sweating ability and high metabolic rate; can suffer rapid mortality in severe, poorly ventilated conditions |
| Dairy cattle (especially high-producing exotic/crossbred breeds) | Reduced milk yield, reduced rumination, crowding around water or shade | High-producing dairy breeds generally tolerate heat less well than indigenous or adapted breeds due to higher metabolic heat production |
| Beef cattle, goats, sheep | Reduced grazing activity (often shifting to early morning/evening), seeking shade | Indigenous and well-adapted breeds generally show greater heat tolerance than exotic breeds, but all classes are affected under extreme or prolonged heat |
| Pigs | Reduced feed intake, wallowing or seeking water/mud, laboured breathing | Pigs have very limited ability to sweat and rely heavily on external cooling behaviours (wallowing) or provided cooling |

## Managing Heat Stress in Livestock

### Shade, Shelter, and Ventilation
- Ensure animals have reliable access to shade throughout the hottest part of the day, whether from trees, constructed shelters, or shade cloth, sized adequately for the number of animals to avoid crowding, which itself generates additional heat.
- Improve ventilation in housing and shelters (adequate openings, appropriate roof design, avoiding overcrowded pens or coops), since poor airflow allows heat and humidity to build up around confined animals.
- Roofing materials and orientation that reduce direct solar heat gain (for example, appropriate roof insulation or reflective roofing, and shelter orientation that reduces direct afternoon sun exposure) help moderate indoor temperatures in housed livestock.

### Water Access
- Increase the availability of clean, cool drinking water during heat events, since water intake typically rises significantly as animals attempt to cool themselves and replace fluid lost through increased respiration and, in some species, sweating.
- Ensure sufficient watering points and trough capacity so that no animals are prevented from accessing water by crowding or dominance behaviour, particularly in mixed or larger herds and flocks.
- Check water sources are not directly exposed to full sun where avoidable, since warm drinking water is less effective at helping animals cool down.

### Feeding Adjustments
- Where practical, shift the main feeding times to the cooler parts of the day (early morning and evening) rather than around midday, since digestion itself generates internal body heat (particularly in ruminants), which adds to overall heat load if concentrated during the hottest hours.
- Avoid unnecessary handling, transport, or other stressful activity during the hottest part of the day, since additional physical exertion or stress compounds heat load.
- Ensure feed and forage quality is maintained, since reduced intake during heat stress can already limit nutrient intake without additional feed quality problems compounding the effect.

### Species-Specific Considerations (Poultry, Dairy, Pigs, Small Ruminants)
- **Poultry**: Reduce stocking density in housing during extreme heat where possible, maximise ventilation, ensure constant access to cool water, and avoid midday handling or transport, since poultry are highly susceptible to rapid heat-related mortality. See `livestock/poultry_keeping.md`.
- **Dairy cattle**: Prioritise shade and water access at all times, and be aware that milk yield often drops during and shortly after a heatwave even with good management, recovering gradually once heat stress ends.
- **Pigs**: Provide wallows, sprinklers, or other means of active cooling where feasible, since pigs cannot cool themselves effectively through sweating alone.
- **Goats and sheep**: Generally more heat-tolerant than exotic cattle or pig breeds, but still require shade and water access, particularly for young, pregnant, or lactating animals, which are more heat-sensitive than healthy adults.
- For general biosecurity and health monitoring relevant during periods of heat-related stress and increased disease susceptibility, see `livestock/livestock_health_and_biosecurity.md`.

## During an Active Heatwave: Priorities
- Prioritise water access for both crops and livestock, since water shortage combined with heat causes more severe and more rapid damage than either stress alone.
- Shift labour-intensive or animal-handling activities to the coolest parts of the day.
- Monitor livestock, especially young, old, sick, pregnant, or high-producing animals, closely for early signs of heat stress, since intervention before collapse or severe distress is far more effective than after.
- Delay non-essential fertiliser or pesticide applications and other crop stress-inducing activities until the heat event passes, where feasible, since crops already under heat stress are more vulnerable to additional chemical or physical stress.

## Recovery After a Heatwave
- Assess crop flowering, fruit set, and grain fill outcomes once the heat event has passed, since losses at these stages are often not visible until later in crop development.
- Monitor livestock body condition, milk production, and fertility in the weeks following a heatwave, since recovery of production levels can lag behind the return of comfortable temperatures.
- Watch for secondary pest outbreaks (such as spider mites) that may have built up during hot, dry conditions and can continue to affect crops after the heat itself subsides.
- Resume normal feeding, fertiliser, and management schedules gradually as conditions return to normal, rather than abruptly reintroducing full activity while animals or crops are still recovering.

## Common Mistakes
- Watering crops or providing feed to livestock only during the hottest part of the day, when both are more effective and cause less additional stress in the cooler morning or evening.
- Failing to provide adequate shade or water capacity for the full number of animals, resulting in crowding and unequal access.
- Assuming indigenous or previously heat-tolerant livestock breeds do not need heat stress management during an extreme or unusually prolonged heat event.
- Handling, transporting, or otherwise stressing livestock during the hottest part of the day.
- Overlooking the increased pest risk that often follows hot, dry conditions and failing to monitor crops after the heatwave itself has passed.

## When to Consult an Agricultural Extension Officer or Veterinary Service
- To identify heat-tolerant crop varieties and livestock breeds proven for the local climate.
- To design appropriate shade, shelter, or ventilation improvements for livestock housing in a heat-prone area.
- If livestock show signs of severe heat stress (collapse, staggering, marked distress) requiring urgent veterinary attention.
- To plan water infrastructure (troughs, storage, access points) adequate for herd or flock size during extreme heat events.
