# El Niño / La Niña Effects on African Agriculture

## Overview
El Niño and La Niña are opposite phases of the El Niño–Southern Oscillation (ENSO), a natural, recurring pattern of warming and cooling in the tropical Pacific Ocean that shifts atmospheric circulation patterns worldwide. ENSO is the single most influential known driver of year-to-year seasonal climate variability across much of Africa, altering the odds of wetter or drier seasons in different regions in fairly well-documented, though not perfectly consistent, ways. Understanding the general regional patterns associated with El Niño and La Niña helps farmers interpret seasonal forecasts and anticipate broad risks such as drought or flooding. This document explains the ENSO cycle and summarises its typical, region-specific effects on African rainfall and agriculture. For how to interpret and use forecast probabilities, see `weather/seasonal_forecasting_basics.md`; for drought and flood response, see `weather/drought_and_flood_management.md`.

## Understanding the ENSO Cycle
- ENSO has three phases: **El Niño** (unusually warm sea-surface temperatures in the central and eastern tropical Pacific), **La Niña** (unusually cool sea-surface temperatures in the same region), and **ENSO-neutral** (near-average conditions, neither phase active).
- These ocean temperature patterns alter tropical atmospheric circulation, rainfall, and pressure patterns far beyond the Pacific itself, through what are known as teleconnections — indirect links between climate conditions in different, sometimes very distant, parts of the world.
- An ENSO event typically develops over several months, often peaks around the northern hemisphere winter, and can last from several months to more than a year; some events are followed directly by the opposite phase, while others are followed by a neutral period.
- ENSO phases are monitored using sea-surface temperature indices in specific tropical Pacific regions; meteorological agencies issue regular ENSO status updates and outlooks indicating the current phase and the likelihood of it strengthening, weakening, or changing over the coming months.
- Not every El Niño or La Niña event has identical strength or identical regional effects; the strength of the event and its interaction with other ocean basins (Indian Ocean, Atlantic Ocean) both influence how strongly and consistently the typical regional pattern actually appears in a given year.

## Regional Effects Across Africa
ENSO's influence on rainfall varies by region, by season within the year, and by the specific rainfall system affecting that region (for example, a single long rainy season versus separate "long rains" and "short rains"). The table below summarises the general, most commonly documented tendencies; local and seasonal exceptions occur, and any single season's actual outcome should be confirmed against the current seasonal forecast rather than assumed from ENSO phase alone.

| Region | Typical El Niño tendency | Typical La Niña tendency |
|---|---|---|
| Southern Africa (e.g., South Africa, Zimbabwe, Zambia, Mozambique, Malawi, parts of Botswana and Namibia) | Increased likelihood of below-normal rainfall and drought risk during the main October–April rainy season | Increased likelihood of near- to above-normal rainfall, with associated flood risk in some areas |
| Horn of Africa / East Africa short rains (October–December, e.g., Kenya, Somalia, southern Ethiopia) | Increased likelihood of enhanced (above-normal) short-rains rainfall | Increased likelihood of below-normal short-rains rainfall, raising drought risk |
| East Africa long rains (March–May) | Relationship less consistent than for the short rains; effects vary by sub-region and event | Some tendency toward below-normal long-rains rainfall in parts of the region, but less consistent than the short-rains relationship |
| Sahel and much of West Africa | Relationship is generally weaker and less consistent; monsoon rainfall is more strongly influenced by Atlantic Ocean conditions than by the Pacific alone | Relationship is generally weaker and less consistent than in southern or eastern Africa |
| Central Africa | Teleconnections are generally weaker and less consistent than in southern or eastern Africa | Teleconnections are generally weaker and less consistent than in southern or eastern Africa |
| North Africa (Mediterranean-influenced areas) | Some association with drier winter conditions in parts of the region | Some association with wetter winter conditions in parts of the region |

Because the strength and consistency of these relationships differ by region, southern Africa and the Horn of Africa's short rains are the areas where ENSO phase provides the clearest and most actionable seasonal signal, while West Africa's Sahel and Central Africa show a much weaker and less reliable relationship with ENSO alone.

## How ENSO-Driven Rainfall Shifts Affect Farming

### Drought Risk
- In regions and seasons where El Niño tends to suppress rainfall (notably southern Africa's main rainy season), a strong El Niño year raises the risk of a poor rainy season, shortened growing period, and reduced yields for rain-fed crops, and can also reduce water availability for irrigation, livestock, and household use.
- In the Horn of Africa, La Niña years raise the risk of poor short rains; consecutive La Niña years occurring back-to-back have historically been associated with prolonged, multi-season drought conditions in parts of the Horn of Africa, since the region does not have time to recover between poor seasons.
- Drought preparedness measures — drought-tolerant varieties, water harvesting, soil moisture conservation, diversification — are especially relevant to strengthen ahead of a season when ENSO monitoring and seasonal forecasts indicate a heightened drought risk for a given region (see `weather/climate_smart_agriculture.md` and `weather/drought_and_flood_management.md`).

### Flood Risk
- In regions where La Niña tends to enhance rainfall (notably southern Africa), an active La Niña raises the risk of heavy rainfall events, flooding, and waterlogging, particularly in low-lying and poorly drained areas.
- Flood preparedness measures — drainage planning, raised beds, erosion control, safe and elevated produce storage — become more important ahead of a season with an active La Niña forecast for a flood-prone region.

### Effects on Pests, Diseases, and Livestock
- Shifts in rainfall and temperature associated with ENSO phases can influence pest and disease pressure; for example, unusually wet conditions favour some fungal diseases and pests that thrive in humid environments, while unusually dry, warm conditions can favour others (see `weather/weather_impacts_on_farming.md`).
- Historically, favourable breeding conditions linked to unusual rainfall patterns in certain regions have been associated with increased risk of some migratory pest outbreaks; monitoring regional pest advisories alongside ENSO and seasonal forecast updates is good practice during years of unusual rainfall.
- Livestock are affected indirectly through pasture and water availability: drought conditions linked to an unfavourable ENSO phase reduce grazing and water access and increase disease and mortality risk, while excess rainfall can increase parasite and disease pressure and damage grazing land through waterlogging (see `livestock/livestock_health_and_biosecurity.md`).

## Using ENSO Information for Farm Planning
- ENSO phase and strength are useful background context for interpreting the current seasonal forecast for a specific country or region, but the official national or regional seasonal outlook (see `weather/seasonal_forecasting_basics.md`) should be the primary reference for a specific location, since it already incorporates the ENSO signal together with other relevant climate drivers and local historical relationships.
- Farmers and advisors in regions with a strong, well-documented ENSO relationship (southern Africa, the Horn of Africa's short rains) can reasonably give more weight to ENSO status when assessing risk than farmers in regions with weaker relationships (Sahel, Central Africa), where other factors are typically more influential.
- ENSO updates are issued regularly through the season by meteorological agencies and regional climate centres, and a phase can strengthen, weaken, or shift; checking for updates through the season, rather than relying only on an outlook issued months in advance, improves planning accuracy.
- Because ENSO influences the odds rather than guaranteeing a specific outcome, farm-level risk management (diversified crops and varieties, water harvesting, flexible planting dates) remains valuable in every ENSO phase, not only in years identified as high risk.

## Monitoring ENSO Status
- National meteorological services and regional climate centres routinely report the current ENSO phase, its strength, and the outlook for whether it is expected to persist, weaken, or transition to a different phase.
- International bodies that coordinate global climate monitoring issue regular ENSO advisories and outlooks that inform the regional and national seasonal forecasts farmers ultimately receive.
- Agricultural extension services in ENSO-sensitive regions often incorporate ENSO status directly into their seasonal planting advisories, translating the technical ENSO outlook into practical guidance for local crops and conditions.

## Common Mistakes
- Assuming that El Niño or La Niña produces the same effect in every African region; the relationship is strong and consistent in some regions (southern Africa, the Horn of Africa's short rains) and weak or inconsistent in others (Sahel, Central Africa).
- Treating an ENSO phase alone as a substitute for the official seasonal forecast, rather than as background context that the forecast already incorporates.
- Assuming a given ENSO phase guarantees a specific outcome (drought or flood) for every location within an affected region, when in reality it shifts the odds and does not eliminate variability at the local level.
- Ignoring within-season and event-strength differences — a weak ENSO event generally has a less reliable and less pronounced regional effect than a strong one.
- Failing to prepare for both drought and flood risk management, since the region-specific ENSO relationship can favour either outcome depending on the region and the active phase.

## When to Consult a Meteorological Service or Agricultural Extension Officer
- To obtain the current official ENSO status and its relationship to the seasonal outlook for a specific country or region.
- To understand how strongly ENSO typically affects rainfall in the specific local area, since the strength of the relationship varies significantly by region.
- To translate ENSO and seasonal forecast information into practical crop, variety, and water management decisions for the specific farm.
- To access early warning and drought or flood preparedness support where an ENSO-linked high-risk season has been identified for the region.
