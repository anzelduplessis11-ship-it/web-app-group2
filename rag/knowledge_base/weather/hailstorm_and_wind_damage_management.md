# Hailstorm and Wind Damage Management

## Overview
Hailstorms and damaging winds are short-duration, often highly localized weather extremes that can cause severe crop losses within minutes, in contrast to the slower-developing risks of drought or excess rainfall. Because they are difficult to predict precisely and act quickly, managing their risk relies heavily on general preparedness, resilient farm design, and a structured response after damage occurs, rather than on responding to the event itself. This document covers how hail and damaging winds form, the types of damage they cause, ways to reduce risk beforehand, and how to assess and recover from damage afterward. For general weather effects on crops, see `weather/weather_impacts_on_farming.md`; for broader climate risk-reduction strategies, see `weather/climate_smart_agriculture.md`.

## How Hailstorms and Damaging Winds Form
- Hail forms inside strong convective thunderstorms, where powerful updrafts carry raindrops far up into the cold upper atmosphere, where they freeze into ice pellets; repeated cycling within the storm can add successive layers of ice before the stones become too heavy for the updraft to support and they fall to the ground.
- The size of hailstones depends on the strength of the storm's updraft and how long the ice pellets circulate within the freezing zone before falling; the same general storm conditions that produce heavy rain and lightning can also produce hail when the atmosphere is sufficiently unstable and cold air is present at altitude.
- Damaging winds are commonly associated with the same convective thunderstorms that produce hail, particularly from downdrafts and gust fronts, where a sudden rush of cold, dense air descends from the storm and spreads out rapidly at ground level, sometimes producing sudden, brief but intense wind gusts well ahead of or alongside the main rain and hail.
- Some regions and seasons are more prone to hail-bearing storms than others — generally areas with strong seasonal convective activity, including many highland and plateau regions, are more susceptible than areas with predominantly gentle, stratiform (widespread, steady) rainfall systems. Local meteorological services can advise on whether a given area has an established hail season or hail-prone period.

## Types of Damage

### Hail Damage
- Direct impact bruising and puncturing of leaves, stems, and fruit, which reduces marketable quality even where the plant itself survives.
- Defoliation (stripping of leaves), which reduces the plant's capacity for photosynthesis and can set back or halt development, particularly damaging if it occurs during a critical growth stage such as flowering or grain/fruit filling.
- Stem and stalk bruising or breakage, which can kill the growing point outright in young plants or create entry points for disease and pests in more mature plants.
- Complete destruction of exposed fruit, vegetables, or grain heads in severe storms, sometimes with little or no salvageable yield from the affected area.

### Wind Damage
- Lodging (stems bending or breaking near the base) in cereals and other tall crops, which can reduce yield, complicate harvest, and increase grain quality loss from ground contact and pest access.
- Windthrow (uprooting or toppling) in trees, banana plants, and other tall or top-heavy crops, especially where root systems are shallow or soils are saturated at the time of the wind event.
- Leaf shredding, flower and fruit drop, and physical bruising from branches or debris striking plants.
- Increased evapotranspiration and moisture stress in the period following a wind event, and dispersal of certain wind-borne pests and disease spores over greater distances (see `weather/weather_impacts_on_farming.md`).
- Physical damage to structures such as shade netting, greenhouses, fencing, and farm buildings, which can have knock-on effects on crop and livestock protection.

## Recognising Warning Signs
- Official severe weather or thunderstorm warnings issued by the national meteorological service, where available.
- Rapidly building, tall, dark cumulonimbus clouds with a notably dark or greenish tinge to the sky, often visible well before the storm arrives.
- A sudden, noticeable drop in temperature and a sudden increase in wind ahead of an approaching storm.
- Distant thunder or lightning, which indicates an active convective storm system that may be approaching.
- Local knowledge of typical seasonal hail or severe-storm patterns and times of day (many convective storms in tropical and subtropical regions tend to develop during the afternoon and evening).

## Reducing Risk Before a Storm

### Site, Structure, and Crop Choices
- Where hail is a recurring, well-established local risk and the crop value justifies the cost, hail netting over high-value crops (such as fruit orchards or vegetable production) is a recognised protective structure, though it represents a significant investment and is generally only practical for higher-value or protected cropping systems.
- Windbreaks (rows of trees, shrubs, or hedges) planted upwind of fields reduce wind speed at crop level over the sheltered distance behind them, lowering the risk of lodging, windthrow, and physical wind damage; see `weather/weather_impacts_on_farming.md` for further discussion of windbreak benefits.
- Choosing crop varieties with sturdier stalks or stems, where such options are available and otherwise suitable, can reduce lodging risk in windy areas.
- Avoiding excessive plant density and excessive nitrogen fertilisation, both of which can produce taller, weaker, more lodging-prone growth in cereals.

### Physical Protection
- Staking, trellising, or tying tall or top-heavy crops (such as tomatoes, some legumes, and banana plants) provides direct physical support against wind, and propping heavily fruiting banana plants is a widely used practical measure in wind-prone areas.
- Securing loose structures — shade netting, greenhouse covers, temporary shelters, fencing — ahead of a forecast severe storm reduces both direct damage to the structure and secondary damage from debris.
- Harvesting crops that are already at or near maturity ahead of a forecast severe storm, where feasible, can avoid loss of an otherwise-complete crop.

### Timing and Diversification
- Where a local hail or severe-storm season is well established, adjusting planting dates so that the most vulnerable growth stages (flowering, fruit set, near-maturity grain heads) do not coincide with the peak of that season can reduce exposure, where this is compatible with rainfall-driven planting timing.
- Diversifying crops, varieties, and planting dates across a farm reduces the chance that a single localized hailstorm or wind event destroys the entire season's production at once.
- Spreading plantings across more than one field or location, where feasible, reduces the risk that one localized, highly damaging storm affects all of a farmer's production, since hail and severe wind damage are often very localized rather than affecting a wide area evenly.

### Financial Risk Management
- Where index-based or crop insurance products covering hail or severe weather damage are available, these can provide a financial buffer against a loss that field-level protective measures cannot fully prevent; farmers should review the specific coverage, terms, and claims process of any such product carefully with the provider.
- Keeping basic records of planting dates, crop stages, and field conditions through the season supports any subsequent insurance claim or extension request for recovery assistance.

## Assessing Damage After a Storm
- Wait until it is safe to enter fields, and avoid walking on waterlogged or storm-damaged soil unnecessarily, since this can cause additional compaction on top of existing crop damage.
- Assess the percentage of plant stand lost, the extent of defoliation, and the proportion of fruit, grain heads, or marketable produce damaged, ideally by sampling several representative points across the field rather than only the worst-affected area.
- Distinguish between plants that are killed outright (growing point destroyed, stem snapped below the point of possible recovery) and plants with damaged but intact tissue that may still recover, since the appropriate response differs for each.
- Photograph and document damage promptly, including the date and extent, particularly if an insurance claim or disaster assistance request may follow.

## Recovery Actions

### Immediate Steps
- For lodged but not broken cereal crops, some recovery in stem angle can occur naturally over the following days, particularly at earlier growth stages; assess before assuming a total loss.
- Re-stake or re-tie plants that have been blown over but are not badly broken, where doing so promptly can support recovery.
- Clear and safely dispose of severely broken or destroyed plant material, particularly where it may harbour pests or disease if left in the field.

### Disease Prevention After Damage
- Wounded plant tissue from hail or wind damage is a direct entry point for fungal and bacterial pathogens; monitor damaged crops closely over the following one to two weeks for signs of infection at wound sites.
- Where fungal or bacterial disease risk following physical damage is a recognised concern for the specific crop and local conditions, a protective fungicide application after a damaging storm is a measure used in some cropping systems; any product used should be selected and applied according to its label instructions and appropriate local guidance.
- Avoid working in or handling wet, damaged crops more than necessary, since this can spread disease-causing organisms between plants.

### Replanting Decisions
- Base the decision to replant on the realistic recovery potential of the damaged stand and the amount of growing season remaining, rather than reacting immediately before assessing regrowth over the following one to two weeks.
- Where stand loss is severe and beyond recovery, and sufficient season remains, replanting with a short-duration variety may be the best option to still obtain some yield.
- Where the season is too advanced for a full replanting to reach maturity, consider a cover crop, green manure, or fallow with erosion protection for the remainder of the season rather than leaving the damaged field bare (see `soil/soil_conservation_and_erosion.md`).

## Common Mistakes
- Assuming a total loss and abandoning or replanting a field immediately, without allowing time to observe whether damaged but not destroyed plants recover.
- Delaying assessment and documentation of damage, making later insurance or disaster-assistance claims more difficult to support.
- Ignoring the increased disease risk in physically damaged crops and failing to monitor wound sites in the following days and weeks.
- Concentrating all plantings, varieties, and fields in a single area or timing window, increasing the chance that one localized storm destroys the entire season's production.
- Leaving storm-damaged fields bare for the rest of the season instead of protecting the soil with a cover crop or residue.

## When to Consult an Agricultural Extension Officer
- To assess storm damage and get guidance on realistic recovery potential versus replanting for a specific field and crop stage.
- To identify sturdier varieties, appropriate windbreak designs, or other locally suited protective measures for a farm in a hail- or wind-prone area.
- For guidance on appropriate disease-prevention measures following physical crop damage.
- To access crop insurance, disaster relief, or recovery support programmes available in the local area after a significant hail or wind event.
