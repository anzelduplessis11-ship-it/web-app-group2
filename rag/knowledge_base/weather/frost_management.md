# Frost Management

## Overview
Frost occurs when air or plant surface temperatures fall to or below freezing (0°C), and it can kill or severely damage sensitive crop tissue within a single cold night. While much of Africa is not at high frost risk, highland and high-altitude regions — including parts of the East African highlands (Kenya, Ethiopia, Rwanda, Burundi, Uganda), the Lesotho and South African highlands and Drakensberg region, highland Zimbabwe, and other elevated plateau areas — experience frost during cool, dry-season months and it is a significant risk for susceptible crops in those areas. This document explains what causes frost, which conditions and locations are most at risk, how to recognise frost warning signs, and how to protect crops before and manage them after a frost event. For broader temperature-related crop effects, see `weather/weather_impacts_on_farming.md`.

## What Causes Frost
Frost forms when the temperature of a plant surface (which can be colder than the surrounding air temperature) drops to freezing point, causing ice crystals to form on or within plant tissue. There are two main types, and they call for different protection strategies.

### Radiation Frost
- The most common type of agricultural frost, occurring on clear, calm nights when the ground and plant surfaces lose heat rapidly to the sky through radiative cooling, with no cloud cover to trap the heat.
- Calm wind is important to this process: still air allows a shallow layer of very cold air to settle and remain near the ground and crop canopy, rather than being mixed with warmer air above.
- Radiation frost is often worst in low-lying areas, valley bottoms, and hollows, because cold, dense air drains downhill overnight and pools in these locations — commonly called "frost pockets."
- Radiation frost typically develops in the hours before dawn, when accumulated overnight heat loss reaches its peak, and dissipates once the sun warms the surface after sunrise.

### Advection Frost
- Occurs when a mass of already-cold air moves into a region (for example, with a cold front or a broader cold spell), regardless of cloud cover or wind calmness.
- Often accompanied by wind, unlike radiation frost, and can affect a wider area more uniformly, including areas not normally considered frost pockets.
- Advection frost is generally harder to protect against with methods that rely on trapping a local layer of warm air (such as wind machines or smoke), since the wind mixes the air and prevents such layers from forming.

## Conditions That Increase Frost Risk
- Clear night skies with little or no cloud cover, allowing heat to escape freely into the atmosphere.
- Calm or very light wind, allowing cold air to settle near the ground rather than mix with warmer air aloft.
- Low humidity and dry air, which cools more quickly than moist air overnight.
- Recently cooler daytime temperatures or the passage of a cold air mass, which lowers the starting point for overnight cooling.
- Low-lying topography (valleys, depressions, the base of slopes) where cold air naturally drains and accumulates.
- Bare or dry soil, which absorbs and stores less daytime heat than moist, bare soil, and therefore releases less heat back to the air overnight (see soil moisture guidance below).

## Frost-Prone Areas and Susceptible Crops
Frost risk in Africa is concentrated in highland, high-altitude, and higher-latitude areas during the cool, dry months of the year; farmers in these zones should treat frost as a recurring seasonal risk to plan around, particularly for susceptible crops planted late in the season or at higher elevations within their local area.

| Crop sensitivity | Examples | Typical frost response |
|---|---|---|
| Highly frost-sensitive | Maize (especially young seedlings and tasseling stage), beans, tomato, banana, papaya, cucurbits (pumpkin, squash, cucumber) | Foliage blackens and collapses; young plants and seedlings can be killed outright by a single hard frost |
| Moderately frost-sensitive | Potato (foliage), sweet potato, coffee, many vegetable crops | Foliage damage, growth setback; established root systems may survive even where above-ground growth is killed back |
| More frost-tolerant | Wheat, most temperate small grains, many brassica vegetables (cabbage, kale), some highland pasture grasses | Can generally tolerate light to moderate frost with limited damage, though damage still increases with harder or repeated frosts |

Frost tolerance also depends on the crop's growth stage: young seedlings and actively flowering or fruiting tissue are typically far more vulnerable than dormant or mature, hardened tissue of the same species.

## Warning Signs of Frost Risk
- Official frost warnings or advisories issued by the national meteorological service, typically based on forecast overnight minimum temperatures.
- A clear sky, calm wind, and a noticeably rapid drop in temperature in the hours after sunset.
- Low relative humidity in the evening air, since dry air cools faster overnight than humid air.
- Recent passage of a cold front or an unusually cool day, especially in already frost-prone highland areas during the cool season.
- Visible dew or frost forming on grass, vehicle windscreens, or exposed surfaces in the early morning in the surrounding area, which indicates conditions were at or near freezing locally.

## Protecting Crops Before a Frost Event

### Site and Microclimate Management
- Avoid planting the most frost-sensitive crops in known frost pockets (valley bottoms, low-lying hollows) where cold air is known to settle; reserve these areas for more frost-tolerant crops or non-frost-season use.
- Where windbreaks are used near frost-prone fields, ensure they do not block the natural downhill drainage of cold air on sloped land, since a solid barrier across a slope can trap cold air on the uphill side and worsen frost severity there.
- Where possible, time planting so that the most frost-sensitive growth stages (young seedlings, flowering) do not coincide with the period of highest local frost risk.

### Soil Moisture and Irrigation
- Moist, bare, firm soil absorbs more heat during the day and releases it back to the air more effectively overnight than dry or loosely mulched soil, which helps keep the air layer near the crop slightly warmer on a frost-risk night.
- Because of this, a light irrigation shortly before an expected frost night can help raise the heat-holding capacity of the soil and moderate the overnight temperature drop near the crop canopy, in areas where irrigation is available.
- Note that thick mulch, while beneficial for moisture conservation in warm conditions, insulates the soil surface from releasing stored heat and can therefore increase frost risk at the plant surface immediately above it; consider temporarily pulling back mulch from the most frost-sensitive plants ahead of a forecast frost night.
- Sprinkler irrigation run continuously through a frost event is a recognised active protection method in some higher-value production systems, since the heat released as water freezes on the plant surface can keep the plant tissue itself at or near 0°C rather than allowing it to fall further; this requires reliable water supply and careful management and is generally impractical for extensive field crops.

### Covering and Physical Protection
- Covering high-value or small-scale plantings (nursery beds, vegetable seedlings, young transplants) with cloth, plastic sheeting, straw, or purpose-made row covers overnight can trap ground heat and reduce frost damage; covers should be removed after temperatures rise the following morning to avoid overheating and to allow normal growth.
- Cloches, low tunnels, or simple frames covered with plastic or fabric offer season-long frost buffering for high-value seedbeds and transplant nurseries in frost-prone areas.
- For tree crops and perennials, wrapping or protecting the lower trunk and young growth can reduce damage to the most vulnerable tissue even where full canopy protection is impractical.

### Traditional and Active Protection Methods
- Lighting smoky fires around field margins on a still, clear, forecast-frost night is a long-used traditional method intended to create a haze layer that reduces radiative heat loss and, in calm conditions, can also generate a small amount of convective air movement; it has meaningful practical limitations, including reduced effectiveness in any wind, significant labour and fuel requirements, and air-quality and safety concerns, and should be weighed against these drawbacks.
- Wind machines and fans, used in some commercial orchard operations, work by mechanically mixing the shallow layer of very cold surface air with the relatively warmer air typically found a short distance above the ground during a radiation frost (a "temperature inversion"); this method is ineffective during windy advection frost events, when no such warm layer aloft is present to mix in.
- Combining more than one method (for example, ensuring moist soil plus covering the most sensitive plantings) generally gives more reliable protection than relying on a single method alone.

## Responding After a Frost Event
- Wait until the plant tissue has fully thawed naturally in daylight before assessing the extent of damage, since frozen tissue can appear misleadingly intact until it thaws and visibly blackens or collapses.
- Delay pruning or removing visibly damaged tissue until the risk of further frost in that period has passed, since the damaged tissue can offer some residual insulation to the healthy tissue beneath it during any subsequent cold night.
- For crops with surviving root systems or growing points (many perennials, and some crops where only the foliage was killed back), assess for regrowth potential before deciding to replant, since some plants recover from below-ground or protected growing points.
- For badly damaged annual crops with no realistic recovery, evaluate whether there is still enough remaining season length to replant a short-duration crop or variety.
- Monitor frost-damaged crops closely afterward, since damaged tissue is more vulnerable to secondary fungal and bacterial infection entering through injured plant surfaces.

## Common Mistakes
- Planting the most frost-sensitive crops or varieties in known low-lying frost pockets without adjusting for the added risk.
- Applying a thick mulch layer to frost-sensitive plants immediately before a forecast frost night, which can increase rather than reduce frost damage at the plant surface.
- Removing or pruning frost-damaged tissue too soon, before confirming the frost-risk period has fully passed.
- Placing a solid windbreak directly across a slope in a way that traps cold air on the uphill side rather than allowing it to continue draining away.
- Relying solely on smoke or fires for frost protection in conditions with even light wind, where this method is much less effective.

## When to Consult an Agricultural Extension Officer or Meteorological Service
- To obtain localized frost risk information and typical frost-season timing for the specific altitude and location of the farm.
- To identify frost-tolerant crop and variety options suited to a known frost-prone area.
- To plan appropriate protection measures (covering, irrigation timing, site selection) for high-value nursery beds or susceptible plantings ahead of the frost season.
- After a significant frost event, to assess damage and discuss recovery, replanting, or salvage options for affected fields.
