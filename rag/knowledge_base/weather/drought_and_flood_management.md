# Drought and Flood Management

## Overview
Drought and flooding are two of the most damaging weather extremes for African smallholder farmers, and both are becoming more frequent and severe with climate change. Managing them involves preparing before they occur, responding effectively while they are happening, and protecting crops, soil, and stored produce throughout. This document covers both drought (too little water) and floods or waterlogging (too much water). For urgent, acute situations, see `emergency_guides/crop_emergency_guide.md`; for day-to-day watering decisions, see `irrigation/irrigation_scheduling.md`.

## Preparing for Drought
- Choose drought-tolerant and short-duration crop varieties suited to the local area where available, so crops can produce even with limited or shortened rains.
- Build soil organic matter and keep the soil covered (mulch, residue, cover crops) to improve water retention and reduce evaporation.
- Establish water harvesting and storage (contour bunds, planting basins, tanks, ponds) to capture rain for use during dry spells.
- Diversify crops and income sources so a dry season does not cause total loss.
- Plan planting around the reliable onset of rains rather than a fixed date, and consider staggered planting to spread risk.

## Responding During Drought
- Prioritise scarce water for the most water-sensitive crop stages, typically flowering and grain or fruit filling, rather than spreading limited water thinly across all stages (see `irrigation/irrigation_scheduling.md`).
- Reduce evaporation losses by mulching and by watering during cooler parts of the day.
- Control weeds promptly, since they compete with crops for scarce soil moisture.
- Where water is severely limited, focus resources on the area or crop most likely to yield, rather than trying to save everything.
- Protect livestock water and fodder needs as part of overall drought planning where livestock are kept.

## Preparing for Floods and Waterlogging
- Improve field drainage where flooding is a known risk, for example through drainage channels and raised beds or ridges that lift roots above standing water.
- Where practical, favour better-drained fields for flood-sensitive crops and reserve low-lying, flood-prone areas for more tolerant uses.
- Maintain soil organic matter and structure, which improve infiltration and reduce surface ponding.
- Protect against erosion on slopes above and within fields, since heavy rain drives both flooding and soil loss (see `soil/soil_conservation_and_erosion.md`).
- Plan for safe, dry storage of harvested produce ahead of the wet season.

## Responding During Floods and Waterlogging
- Clear blocked drainage channels to remove standing water as quickly as safely possible, since prolonged waterlogging starves roots of oxygen and promotes root rot.
- Avoid working or walking on saturated soil where avoidable, as this causes compaction that worsens drainage.
- After water recedes, inspect crops for root damage and disease; waterlogged and flood-damaged crops are more vulnerable to disease and may need close monitoring.
- Be cautious about food safety: produce contaminated by floodwater may carry health risks and should be assessed before consumption or sale.
- Delay replanting until fields have drained and dried sufficiently to avoid repeating losses.

## Protecting Soil
- In drought, keeping soil covered reduces moisture loss and protects the surface from wind erosion.
- In floods, cover crops, residue, contour measures, and terraces slow runoff and reduce soil loss.
- Avoid leaving soil bare before an expected extreme, since bare soil is most vulnerable to both wind (in drought) and water (in floods) erosion.

## Protecting Stored Produce
- Ensure grain and other produce are dried to safe moisture levels before storage, as damp conditions during and after floods raise the risk of mould, including aflatoxin-producing moulds (see `storage/aflatoxin_prevention.md` and `storage/grain_storage.md`).
- Store produce in dry, elevated, well-ventilated, and rodent-proof conditions; raise storage above potential flood levels in flood-prone areas.
- Inspect stored produce more frequently during and after wet or humid periods, and separate any mouldy or damaged produce from clean stock immediately.
- Protect stored produce from re-wetting; sealed or hermetic storage helps guard against moisture re-entry.

## Common Mistakes
- Spreading scarce drought water evenly across all growth stages instead of protecting the most sensitive stages.
- Leaving soil bare before an expected drought or heavy-rain period.
- Failing to clear or maintain drainage before the wet season, then losing crops to prolonged waterlogging.
- Storing produce that is not properly dried, then losing it to mould after humid or flood conditions.
- Replanting into still-saturated soil and repeating losses.

## When to Consult an Agricultural Extension Officer
- To plan drought preparation (varieties, water harvesting) or flood mitigation (drainage, raised beds) for the specific farm.
- When crops are severely affected by drought or flooding, for guidance on salvage, replanting, and recovery.
- If flood-affected produce may pose a food-safety risk, for guidance on safe use or disposal.
- To access disaster preparedness, relief, or recovery support available in the local area.
