# Seasonal Forecasting Basics

## Overview
A seasonal forecast estimates how rainfall and temperature over the coming weeks to months are likely to differ from the long-term average for a region. Unlike a daily weather forecast, it does not predict specific rain events on specific days; instead, it gives a probability-based outlook — for example, an increased chance of below-normal, normal, or above-normal rainfall over an entire season. For African smallholder farmers, seasonal forecasts are a valuable planning tool for planting dates, variety choice, and input decisions, but they must be understood and used correctly to be helpful rather than misleading. This document explains how seasonal forecasts are produced, how to interpret them, and how to apply them to farm decisions. For general weather effects on crops, see `weather/weather_impacts_on_farming.md`; for typical regional rainfall timing, see the relevant document in `planting_calendars/`.

## What a Seasonal Forecast Is (and Is Not)
- A seasonal forecast is a probability statement about the coming season as a whole (for example, the next three months), not a day-by-day prediction of when it will rain.
- It cannot reliably tell a farmer the exact date rains will start, when a specific dry spell will occur, or whether a particular week will bring a storm.
- It is built from an understanding of large-scale climate drivers (see below) that influence the odds of a wetter, drier, warmer, or cooler season than usual — it describes shifted probabilities, not certainties.
- A seasonal forecast is most useful for shaping overall risk management and planning decisions, while short-range (daily to two-week) forecasts remain necessary for day-to-day operational decisions such as the actual planting date, fertiliser timing, and spraying.

### Seasonal vs Short-Term Forecasts
| Forecast type | Typical lead time | What it tells you | Best used for |
|---|---|---|---|
| Nowcast / daily forecast | Hours to a few days | Whether it will rain tomorrow, temperature, wind | Day-to-day field operations, spraying, harvest timing |
| Short-range / extended forecast | Up to about two weeks | General trend in coming days | Fine-tuning planting date once rains appear to be starting |
| Seasonal forecast | One to six months | Likelihood of above-, near-, or below-normal rainfall/temperature for the season | Overall season planning: crop choice, variety, land preparation, input budgeting |

## How Seasonal Forecasts Are Produced

### Ocean-Atmosphere Drivers
Seasonal forecasts rely heavily on the state of slow-changing parts of the climate system, especially ocean temperatures, because these change slowly enough (over months) to influence the odds of a season's rainfall pattern before the season begins.
- The El Niño–Southern Oscillation (ENSO), driven by sea-surface temperature patterns in the tropical Pacific Ocean, is the single most influential driver of seasonal climate variability worldwide and has well-documented, though not uniform, effects on rainfall across different African regions (see `weather/el_nino_la_nina_effects_on_african_agriculture.md`).
- Sea-surface temperatures in the Indian Ocean and Atlantic Ocean also influence rainfall patterns in parts of Africa, sometimes reinforcing and sometimes counteracting the effect of the Pacific Ocean state.
- Soil moisture, snow cover, and other slow-changing land and atmospheric conditions can also be incorporated into forecast models.

### Climate Models and Regional Climate Outlook Forums
- National meteorological services and regional climate centres run numerical climate models that simulate how these ocean and atmospheric conditions are likely to evolve and how they typically relate to rainfall and temperature outcomes in specific regions.
- Regional Climate Outlook Forums bring together national meteorological services, regional climate centres, and international climate scientists to review model outputs and historical relationships, then issue a consensus seasonal outlook for a broad region ahead of each rainy season.
- These consensus outlooks are typically downscaled or interpreted at the national level by individual meteorological services, who may adjust the regional signal based on local knowledge and additional local data.
- Because forecasts rely on historical statistical relationships as well as physical modelling, their reliability varies by region, season, and by how strong the influencing ocean signal is in a given year; a strong El Niño or La Niña year generally produces a more confident forecast than a neutral year.

## Understanding Probabilistic Forecasts

### Tercile Categories
Seasonal rainfall forecasts are commonly presented using three categories, based on how the historical rainfall record for that specific location and season is divided into thirds:
- **Below-normal**: rainfall in the driest third of years on record for that location and season.
- **Near-normal**: rainfall in the middle third of years on record.
- **Above-normal**: rainfall in the wettest third of years on record.

If there were no useful climate signal at all, each category would have an equal one-in-three (roughly 33%) chance in any given year. A seasonal forecast shifts these odds based on the current climate drivers — for example, stating a 45% chance of below-normal rainfall, a 30% chance of near-normal, and a 25% chance of above-normal rainfall for a given season. This is a meaningfully increased risk of a dry season compared to the baseline, but it is far from a certainty, and above-normal or near-normal rainfall remains possible.

### Interpreting Probability Language
- A forecast favouring "below-normal" rainfall does not mean a drought is guaranteed; it means the odds are tilted toward a drier-than-usual season compared to the historical baseline for that specific place.
- Small shifts in probability (for example, 38% versus the baseline 33%) represent weak signals and warrant more cautious, flexible responses than large shifts (for example, 55% or more).
- Seasonal forecasts describe overall season totals and tendencies; they generally cannot predict the distribution of rainfall within the season (such as whether a mid-season dry spell will occur), which is a separate and important source of risk.
- Temperature outlooks are produced in the same tercile format (below-normal, near-normal, above-normal) and are relevant for heat stress risk (see `weather/heatwave_management_for_crops_and_livestock.md`) and frost risk (see `weather/frost_management.md`) planning.

## Where African Farmers Can Access Seasonal Forecasts
- National meteorological or hydrometeorological services are the primary source of authoritative seasonal outlooks for a country, often issued ahead of each main rainy season and updated during the season.
- Regional climate centres produce broader regional outlooks that national services draw on and downscale; these are typically intended for use by national services and agricultural advisories rather than for direct interpretation by individual farmers without local context.
- Agricultural extension services often translate meteorological seasonal outlooks into practical farming advisories (for example, suggested variety or planting-date adjustments) suited to local crops and conditions.
- Radio broadcasts, government agricultural bulletins, mobile phone SMS or app-based advisory services, and community meetings organised by extension officers are common channels through which seasonal forecast information reaches farmers, particularly where internet access is limited.
- Farmer groups and cooperatives can be a useful channel for accessing and discussing forecast information collectively, and for pooling interpretation with local and traditional knowledge.

## Using Seasonal Forecasts for Planting Decisions

### Land Preparation and Input Planning
- A forecast favouring below-normal rainfall supports prioritising drought-tolerant crops and varieties, moisture-conserving land preparation (see `soil/soil_conservation_and_erosion.md`), and cautious input investment.
- A forecast favouring above-normal rainfall supports attention to drainage planning, erosion control on sloped land, and readiness for potential waterlogging (see `weather/drought_and_flood_management.md`).
- Input purchase and credit decisions (such as how much fertiliser or seed to buy) can be informed by the seasonal outlook, balanced against the farmer's own risk tolerance, since the forecast is probabilistic rather than certain.

### Variety and Crop Choice
- Under a forecast favouring below-normal rainfall, shorter-duration and drought-tolerant varieties reduce the risk of the crop failing to complete its cycle before the rains end.
- Under a forecast favouring above-normal or normal rainfall, longer-duration, higher-yield-potential varieties may be a reasonable choice where they are otherwise well suited to the area.
- Diversifying across crops and varieties with different water requirements remains good practice regardless of the outlook, since seasonal forecasts carry real uncertainty (see `weather/climate_smart_agriculture.md`).

### Planting Date Decisions
- Seasonal forecasts inform expectations about the season as a whole, but the specific planting date should still be triggered by the actual, locally observed onset of reliable rains rather than the seasonal outlook alone or a fixed calendar date.
- Where a forecast favours a later or less reliable rainy season onset, farmers can plan to delay major planting and monitor short-range forecasts and local rainfall more closely before committing seed.
- Staggered planting across multiple dates remains a sound risk-spreading strategy even when a forecast is available, since within-season timing of individual rainfall events is not something seasonal forecasts reliably predict.

### Water Harvesting and Irrigation Planning
- A forecast favouring below-normal rainfall increases the value of water harvesting structures and careful irrigation scheduling established ahead of the season (see `irrigation/irrigation_scheduling.md`).
- Where irrigation water sources depend on rainfall-fed rivers, dams, or groundwater recharge, a below-normal seasonal outlook may also signal reduced water availability later in the season, supporting more conservative water-use planning.

### Financial and Marketing Decisions
- Farmers and cooperatives sometimes use seasonal outlooks to inform decisions about the scale of planting, labour and credit arrangements, and anticipated marketing timing, while recognising that market prices depend on many additional factors beyond the local harvest outcome.
- Where index-based weather insurance products are available, seasonal forecast information can help farmers assess and communicate their risk exposure, though enrolment and payout terms are specific to each product and provider.

## Combining Forecasts with Local Knowledge and Observation
- Official seasonal forecasts are most effective when combined with close monitoring of local conditions as the season approaches and unfolds, since local microclimate and terrain can cause real deviations from the broader regional or national outlook.
- Traditional and indigenous forecasting indicators (such as the behaviour of certain plants, animals, or insects, or observed wind and cloud patterns) are used by many farming communities alongside meteorological forecasts; treating the two as complementary sources of information, discussed together within the community, is a widely recommended approach rather than relying on either exclusively.
- Seasonal forecasts issued at the start of a season are commonly updated with revised outlooks partway through; checking for updates rather than relying solely on the initial pre-season forecast improves decision quality as the season progresses.

## Limitations and Uncertainty
- Forecast skill (how reliably the forecast matches what actually happens) varies substantially by region and by season; some areas and seasons have historically shown a stronger, more predictable relationship with drivers like ENSO than others.
- Even a well-founded seasonal outlook is a shift in probability, not a guarantee; a season forecast to be "likely below-normal" can still turn out normal or above-normal, and farmers who commit entirely to a single strategy based on the forecast bear the risk if the season does not unfold as expected.
- Seasonal forecasts generally cannot predict specific extreme events (an individual severe storm, a specific short dry spell, or the exact date of the last frost) — these require shorter-range forecasts and local monitoring, discussed in `weather/weather_impacts_on_farming.md` and the other documents in this `weather/` folder.
- Forecasts are typically issued for fairly broad areas; local terrain, altitude, and microclimate can cause a specific farm's actual conditions to differ from the general outlook for its wider region.

## Common Mistakes
- Treating a seasonal forecast as a guaranteed prediction rather than a probability-based outlook.
- Committing an entire farm to a single strategy (for example, only very short-duration varieties) based solely on a seasonal outlook, leaving no buffer if the season turns out differently than forecast.
- Ignoring mid-season forecast updates and continuing to plan around the original pre-season outlook alone.
- Using a seasonal forecast in place of, rather than alongside, short-range forecasts and direct observation when deciding the actual planting date.
- Applying a broad regional or national forecast directly to a specific farm without accounting for known local microclimate differences.

## When to Consult a Meteorological Service or Agricultural Extension Officer
- To obtain the current official seasonal outlook for the specific country or region, and to understand how confident that particular forecast is.
- To get help translating a general seasonal outlook into specific, practical recommendations for local crops, varieties, and planting windows.
- When deciding how much to adjust input purchases, credit plans, or crop area based on a forecast favouring an unusually dry or wet season.
- To access mid-season forecast updates and short-range forecasts that should guide the specific planting date once the season approaches.
