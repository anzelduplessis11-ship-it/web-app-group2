# Climate-Smart Agriculture

## Overview
Climate-smart agriculture describes farming practices that help smallholders cope with increasingly variable and extreme weather while maintaining or improving productivity and protecting the land. Across Africa, farmers face later or more erratic rains, longer dry spells, heavier downpours, and higher temperatures. Climate-smart practices aim to reduce vulnerability to these shifts, use water and soil more efficiently, and spread risk. Many overlap with sustainable farming practices; see `sustainability/sustainable_farming_practices.md`. For how specific weather events affect crops, see `weather/weather_impacts_on_farming.md`.

## Drought-Tolerant and Adapted Varieties
- Drought-tolerant and short-duration (early-maturing) varieties can produce a harvest even when the rainy season is shorter or drier than usual, since they need less total water or finish before late-season dry spells.
- Growing a mix of varieties with different maturity lengths spreads the risk of a poorly timed rainfall onset or an early end to the rains.
- Variety performance is highly location-specific; confirm which drought-tolerant or short-season varieties are proven and available locally with an agricultural extension officer, and avoid assuming a variety that performs well in one area will do so everywhere.

## Water Harvesting and Storage
- Capturing and storing rainfall and runoff helps carry crops and households through dry spells.
- Common approaches include contour bunds, planting basins or pits that concentrate water around plants, small check dams, and tanks or ponds to store water for supplementary irrigation.
- Water harvesting reduces runoff and erosion while making more water available to crops; structures should be planned for the local slope and rainfall.

## Adjusting Planting to Shifting Rains
- Where rains are starting later or becoming less predictable, adjusting planting timing to the actual onset of reliable rains, rather than a fixed calendar date, reduces the risk of failed germination from a false start.
- Staggered planting across several dates or plots spreads the risk that any single planting is caught by a dry spell or poorly timed rain.
- Local seasonal forecasts and traditional indicators, used together and with appropriate caution, can help inform planting decisions; treat forecasts as guidance carrying uncertainty rather than certainty. See regional planting calendars in `planting_calendars/` for typical seasonal patterns.

## Soil Moisture Conservation
- Practices that keep water in the soil buffer crops against dry spells: mulching to reduce evaporation, cover crops and residue retention to protect and shade the soil, and reduced tillage to preserve structure and organic matter.
- Building soil organic matter over time improves both water retention during dry spells and drainage during heavy rain.
- Contour measures slow runoff and give water more time to soak into the root zone. See `soil/soil_conservation_and_erosion.md`.

## Diversification
- Growing several crops and varieties, rather than depending on one, reduces the risk that a single weather, pest, or price shock causes total loss.
- Diversification can include mixing crops with different water needs and maturity lengths, and integrating livestock or trees where appropriate.
- Diversified systems also spread labour and income across the season.

## Agroforestry
- Integrating trees and shrubs with crops can moderate the microclimate (shade and reduced wind), improve soil fertility (especially with nitrogen-fixing species), reduce erosion, and provide additional products such as fodder, fruit, and fuelwood.
- Trees can help buffer crops against heat and moisture extremes, though benefits build over several years and require longer-term planning.

## Combining Practices to Build Resilience
- Resilience comes from combining complementary practices rather than relying on any single measure: for example, drought-tolerant varieties plus water harvesting plus soil moisture conservation plus diversification.
- The most suitable combination depends on the local climate, soil, water supply, labour, and household needs; practices should be matched to the specific farm situation.
- Gradual adoption, starting with measures offering the clearest near-term benefit alongside long-term resilience, is often more practical than attempting comprehensive change in one season.

## Common Mistakes
- Planting on a fixed calendar date despite clear evidence that the rains have shifted.
- Adopting a "drought-tolerant" variety without confirming it is adapted and available for local conditions.
- Relying on a single crop, leaving the whole farm exposed to one weather shock.
- Treating a seasonal forecast as a certainty rather than guidance with real uncertainty.
- Leaving soil bare, losing moisture to evaporation and soil to erosion during extreme weather.

## When to Consult an Agricultural Extension Officer
- To identify drought-tolerant, short-duration, or otherwise climate-adapted varieties proven for the local area.
- To plan water harvesting or soil and water conservation structures suited to the local slope and rainfall.
- To interpret seasonal forecasts and adjust planting decisions for the specific farming system.
- To access climate-smart agriculture training and support programmes available locally.
