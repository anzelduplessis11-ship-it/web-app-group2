# Reducing Post-Harvest Losses

## Overview
Post-harvest loss (PHL) refers to the measurable reduction in the quantity or quality of a crop that occurs at any point between harvest and final consumption or sale. Losses can be quantitative (a physical reduction in weight or volume, such as spillage or consumption by pests) or qualitative (a reduction in nutritional value, market value, or edibility, even where the physical volume remains largely intact). Across African smallholder value chains, post-harvest losses represent one of the largest preventable threats to food security and farm income, and a large share of these losses can be reduced through existing, low-cost practices rather than expensive new technology.

## Where Post-Harvest Losses Occur
Losses can occur at every stage of the value chain, and the causes and appropriate interventions differ by stage.

| Stage | Typical Loss Causes |
|---|---|
| Harvesting | Wrong maturity timing, rough handling, harvesting during poor weather |
| Field handling and collection | Delayed collection, exposure to sun/rain, poor container choice |
| Transport | Rough handling, overloading, compression damage, long delays |
| Drying (grains) | Uneven drying, contamination, re-wetting by rain |
| Storage | Pest infestation, mould, poor ventilation, high moisture content |
| Processing | Inefficient processing, contamination, delays before processing |
| Marketing/distribution | Extended time to sale, poor display/storage conditions at market |

## Causes of Post-Harvest Loss by Crop Category

### Cereal and Grain Crops
- Harvesting before or well after optimal maturity, increasing shattering losses or exposing grain to weather and pests.
- Storing grain at moisture levels above the safe threshold for the crop, encouraging mould growth and, in susceptible crops such as maize and groundnut, aflatoxin contamination (see `grain_moisture_testing.md` for safe moisture thresholds).
- Insect pest infestation in storage (weevils, grain borers, and similar pests), often worsened by poor storage structures that do not exclude pests.
- Rodent damage in storage facilities without adequate pest-proofing.
- Slow or interrupted drying, allowing mould to establish before the grain reaches a safe moisture level.

### Legumes (Dry Beans and Pulses)
- Pod shattering when harvest is delayed past the point where pods are fully dry.
- Infestation by bruchids (seed weevils) both in the field before harvest and in storage afterward.
- Storage at excessive moisture content, leading to mould and reduced seed viability.

### Root and Tuber Crops
- Mechanical damage during harvest (cuts, bruises, skin breaks), which provides entry points for rot-causing organisms and accelerates moisture loss.
- Rapid physiological deterioration after harvest in crops such as cassava, which begins to spoil within days if not processed or properly handled.
- Sprouting during storage, particularly in potato and similar crops, which reduces both weight and quality.
- Rotting due to the naturally high moisture content of root and tuber tissue combined with poor storage ventilation.

### Fruits
- Bruising and physical damage from rough handling, dropping, or overfilled containers.
- Over-ripening or decay resulting from delayed transport or marketing after harvest.
- Poor temperature and handling conditions accelerating ripening and spoilage, particularly in climates with high ambient temperatures.
- Fungal and bacterial decay entering through skin wounds caused during harvest or handling.

### Vegetables
- Wilting and moisture loss, particularly in leafy vegetables, when produce is left in the sun or not cooled promptly after harvest.
- Physical damage from rough handling or crushing under the weight of produce stacked too high in containers.
- Decay resulting from poor temperature and humidity conditions during storage or transport.
- Loss of market value from poor sorting, mixing damaged produce with sound produce, which can accelerate spoilage of the whole batch.

## Practical Loss-Reduction Strategies

### At Harvest
- Harvest at the correct maturity stage for the intended use and market, since both premature and delayed harvest increase loss risk (see `harvesting_best_practices.md`).
- Use clean, sharp tools and careful technique to minimise cuts, bruises, and tears.
- Avoid harvesting during or immediately after rain wherever possible, since wet produce is more vulnerable to disease and handling damage.
- Sort out visibly damaged, diseased, or pest-affected produce at the point of harvest, rather than mixing it with sound produce.

### During Handling and Transport
- Minimise the number of times produce is handled between the field and its destination; each additional handling step introduces further risk of damage.
- Use appropriately sized, ventilated containers rather than large sacks for produce prone to compression damage, and avoid overfilling containers.
- Move produce out of direct sun promptly after harvest to reduce field heat, particularly for perishable fruits and vegetables.
- Transport produce as soon as practical after harvest, and protect it from rain, dust, and excessive jolting during transport.

### During Drying (Grains and Pulses)
- Dry grain promptly after harvest to reach a safe storage moisture level, since delayed drying allows mould to establish even before moisture testing shows a problem.
- Dry on raised platforms, tarpaulins, or mats rather than directly on bare ground, to reduce contamination and prevent moisture re-uptake from damp soil.
- Spread grain in a thin, even layer and turn it regularly to promote uniform drying.
- Cover or move grain quickly if rain threatens during the drying process.

### During Storage
- Ensure grain and pulses are fully dried to the appropriate safe moisture level before placing them into storage.
- Use storage structures and containers designed to exclude pests and rodents, and consider low-cost technologies such as hermetically sealed storage bags, which limit oxygen available to insect pests and can reduce the need for chemical pest control.
- Sort and grade produce before storage, keeping damaged or substandard material separate to prevent it from accelerating spoilage of the rest of the batch.
- Ensure adequate ventilation in storage structures for produce prone to heat and moisture buildup, while still protecting against pests and rain.
- Monitor stored produce periodically for signs of moisture problems, insect activity, or mould, and act promptly if problems are detected.

### During Processing and Value Addition
- Where appropriate, processing highly perishable produce soon after harvest (for example, drying, fermenting, or otherwise converting it into a more stable form) can substantially reduce losses that would otherwise occur while awaiting sale or storage.
- Ensure processing equipment and surfaces are clean to avoid introducing contamination that shortens the shelf life of the processed product.

## Low-Cost Technologies and Practices
- **Hermetic (airtight) storage bags**: Sealed multi-layer storage bags that limit the exchange of air between the grain and the outside environment, reducing oxygen levels inside the bag over time and suppressing the activity of storage insect pests without necessarily requiring chemical treatment.
- **Improved storage structures**: Granaries and storage containers built or modified with pest- and rodent-exclusion features (raised platforms, sealed openings, screened vents) reduce losses compared to open or poorly protected storage.
- **Raised drying platforms and drying tarpaulins**: Simple, low-cost tools that keep grain off bare ground during drying, reducing contamination and re-wetting risk.
- **Basic sorting and grading**: Separating produce by quality and maturity at harvest and before storage is one of the lowest-cost and most effective loss-reduction practices available, since it prevents a small amount of damaged or diseased material from compromising a larger batch.
- **Ventilated crates for fresh produce**: Rigid, ventilated containers reduce compression damage compared to sacks, particularly for fruits and vegetables.

## Economic and Nutritional Impact
Post-harvest losses reduce the income smallholder farmers earn from crops they have already invested labour, land, and inputs into producing, and they reduce the amount of food available for household consumption and local markets. Because much post-harvest loss occurs through practices that can be modified at low cost — improved timing, gentler handling, better drying, and appropriate storage — targeted attention to these practices can meaningfully improve both farm income and food availability without requiring large capital investment.

## Common Mistakes
- Focusing loss-reduction efforts only on storage, while overlooking losses that occur earlier, at harvest and during transport.
- Mixing produce of different maturity, quality, or damage levels in a single batch, allowing spoilage to spread from a few damaged items to the whole lot.
- Delaying drying of grain after harvest, assuming it can be dealt with later once other tasks are complete.
- Storing produce in structures that are not adequately protected against pests, rodents, or moisture.
- Underestimating the cumulative effect of small losses at each stage of the value chain, which together can amount to a substantial overall loss by the time produce reaches the final consumer.

## When to Consult an Agricultural Extension Officer
- To assess which stage of the value chain is contributing most to losses on a particular farm, since the appropriate intervention differs by stage and crop.
- To learn about locally available low-cost storage and handling technologies, such as hermetic bags or improved storage structure designs.
- When experiencing recurring quality problems (mould, insect infestation, rapid spoilage) despite following general good practice, since this may indicate an underlying issue requiring specific diagnosis.
- Before investing in new storage infrastructure or processing equipment, to confirm it is well suited to the crops and volumes involved.
