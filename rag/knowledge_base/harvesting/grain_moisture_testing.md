# Grain Moisture Testing

## Overview
Grain moisture content is one of the single most important factors determining whether harvested grain can be stored safely, for how long, and at what quality. Grain that is too wet at the time of storage is highly vulnerable to mould growth, insect infestation, and heating, while grain dried well below the safe threshold, though safer, can represent unnecessary drying effort and, for some uses, a loss of weight sold. Testing moisture accurately, at the right points in the process, is therefore a core skill for anyone harvesting, storing, or trading grain.

## Why Moisture Content Matters
- **Mould and mycotoxin risk**: Grain stored above its safe moisture threshold is highly susceptible to mould growth. In crops such as maize and groundnut, certain moulds can produce aflatoxins, harmful compounds that pose serious health risks and can also cause grain to be rejected by buyers.
- **Insect pest activity**: Storage insect pests generally reproduce and cause damage more rapidly in grain with higher moisture content, compounding losses from mould with losses from pest feeding.
- **Heating and physical spoilage**: Grain continues to respire after harvest. In bulk storage, grain with excess moisture generates heat through this respiration, which can cause caking, further moisture migration within the bulk, and localized spoilage.
- **Market grading and acceptance**: Many buyers, millers, and grain marketing bodies specify maximum moisture content for accepting grain, since it affects storability, processing quality, and shelf life of the resulting products.
- **Seed viability**: For grain retained for planting, correct moisture content at storage is important for maintaining germination viability over time.

## Methods for Testing Grain Moisture

### Electronic Moisture Meters
- Electronic moisture meters give a direct, numerical moisture reading and are the most accurate and reliable method where available.
- Take readings from multiple points and depths within a bag or bulk pile, since moisture content is rarely uniform throughout a stored quantity of grain, particularly if drying was uneven or if any part of the grain has been exposed to damp conditions.
- Follow the manufacturer's calibration and sample preparation instructions, since accuracy can be affected by grain temperature and how the sample is presented to the meter.
- Where a meter is shared within a community or cooperative, regular checking against a known reference or calibration standard helps maintain reliable readings over time.

### Traditional and Field Methods (Where Meters Are Unavailable)
These approximate methods are useful when an electronic meter is not accessible, but they are less precise than a properly calibrated meter and should be treated as a guide rather than a definitive measurement.
- **Bite/chew test**: A dry, safely storable grain kernel typically cracks or shatters cleanly when bitten, rather than denting or crushing, which indicates the kernel is still too moist.
- **Shake and listen test**: Dry grain still in the pod, husk, or ear tends to produce a distinct rattling sound when shaken, since the kernels have separated slightly from the surrounding tissue as they dried.
- **Salt bottle test**: Place a sample of dry table salt together with a sample of the grain in a clean, dry, sealable glass bottle or jar, close it, and shake well. If the salt sticks to the sides of the bottle or forms visible clumps, the grain is still too moist for safe storage; if the salt remains dry and free-flowing, the grain is more likely to be within a safe range. This is a simple, low-cost check that can be repeated periodically during drying.
- **Sun/heat exposure test**: Grain that curls, cracks, or splits readily when briefly exposed to strong sun or heat is generally closer to a safe storage moisture level than grain that remains pliable.
- **Visual and tactile assessment**: Hardness of the kernel, ease of removing the husk or hull, and general firmness can offer a rough indication of dryness, though this comes with experience and is the least precise of the field methods.
- Field methods are most useful as a first check to decide whether grain is close to ready; wherever possible, confirm with an electronic meter before committing large volumes to long-term storage.

## Sampling for Accurate Testing
- Take multiple samples from different points and depths within a bag, sack, or bulk storage pile, since moisture content can vary considerably across a stored quantity, especially near the surface, edges, or bottom of a pile where conditions differ.
- Test at multiple points in time during the drying process, not only once, to track progress and confirm the grain has reached a stable, safe moisture level before it is placed into long-term storage.
- Avoid testing grain immediately after removing it from strong, direct sun, since surface heating can produce a temporarily misleading reading; allow the sample to reach a more typical temperature before testing where possible.
- When buying or selling grain, take samples from several locations within the batch rather than relying on a single spot, to obtain a more representative moisture estimate.

## Safe Storage Moisture Thresholds by Grain Type
The moisture level considered safe for extended storage varies by crop. The figures below are commonly cited general reference ranges; always confirm with local extension guidance or buyer specifications where precision matters for a specific transaction.

| Grain/Crop | Commonly Cited Safe Storage Moisture (Approximate) | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Maize | Around 12-13% | Higher moisture significantly increases aflatoxin risk in this crop |
| Sorghum/millet | Around 12-13% | Similar mould risk profile to maize |
| Rice (paddy) | Around 13-14% | Excess moisture affects both storability and milling quality |
| Wheat | Around 12-13% | Standard cereal storage threshold |
| Dry beans and other pulses | Around 13-14% | Higher moisture increases mould and weevil activity |
| Soybean | Around 11-12% | Prone to rapid quality decline if stored too moist |
| Groundnut (in-shell or shelled) | Generally lower, often cited around 7-9% | Particularly sensitive to aflatoxin-producing mould; extra care with drying is warranted |

## Drying to Reach Safe Moisture
- Sun-dry grain on raised platforms, mats, or tarpaulins rather than directly on bare ground, to reduce contamination from soil and to prevent the grain from re-absorbing ground moisture.
- Spread grain in a thin, even layer to promote uniform drying, and turn it regularly so that all parts are exposed evenly to sun and air.
- Where humid climate conditions or large harvest volumes make sun drying slow or unreliable, mechanical or artificial drying methods can help reach a safe moisture level more quickly and consistently.
- Cover grain promptly, or move it under shelter, if rain threatens during drying, since re-wetting partially dried grain undoes previous drying progress and increases mould risk.

## Monitoring Moisture During Storage
- Re-test stored grain periodically, particularly soon after it is placed into storage and again during humid seasons or after periods of heavy rain, since stored grain can re-absorb moisture from a humid storage environment even after correct initial drying.
- Watch for physical warning signs of a moisture problem in storage, including caking or clumping of the grain mass, a noticeable rise in temperature within the storage container, a musty or off odour, visible mould, or increased insect activity.
- If a moisture problem is detected, remove and re-dry the affected grain promptly, and inspect surrounding grain for signs of spreading spoilage.

## Common Mistakes
- Judging grain as "dry enough" for market or long-term storage based only on visual appearance or touch, without any form of testing.
- Testing only the surface or a single point of a stored bulk, missing pockets of higher moisture at different depths or locations.
- Assuming that grain correctly dried at harvest will remain safe indefinitely, without accounting for the risk of re-wetting from a humid storage environment or seasonal rains.
- Storing different batches of grain with different moisture levels together, allowing moisture to migrate from wetter to drier grain and creating localized spoilage.
- Relying solely on approximate field methods (such as the bite or shake test) when an electronic meter is available and precision matters, for example for high-value sales or long-term seed storage.

## When to Consult an Agricultural Extension Officer
- To confirm safe storage moisture thresholds for a specific grain variety or local climate, since some local guidance may differ slightly from general reference ranges.
- To access or learn to properly use an electronic moisture meter where one is not already available on the farm.
- If recurring mould, off-odours, or aflatoxin concerns arise despite apparently correct moisture testing and storage practice, since this may indicate a deeper issue requiring specific diagnosis or testing.
- When preparing grain for sale to buyers with specific moisture and quality requirements, to ensure the crop will meet those standards before delivery.
