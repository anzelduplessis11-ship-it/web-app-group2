# Vegetable Harvesting Best Practices

## Overview
Vegetables are generally more perishable than grains and many fruits, and the correct harvest timing and technique varies considerably depending on which part of the plant is harvested (leaves, fruit, roots, or bulbs). Getting this right is essential, since vegetables harvested too early or too late lose eating quality, storage life, and market value, and rough handling at harvest is one of the leading causes of downstream spoilage.

## Harvesting by Vegetable Category

### Leafy Vegetables (Cabbage, Spinach, Lettuce, Amaranth, Kale/Sukuma Wiki, and Similar Crops)
- Harvest during the coolest part of the day, ideally early morning, to reduce field heat in the leaves and slow subsequent wilting.
- Pick or cut leaves or heads at an appropriate size before the plant begins to bolt (flower) or before older leaves become tough, bitter, or yellowed.
- Use a clean, sharp knife to cut cleanly rather than tearing leaves or stems, since torn tissue decays faster and is a more common entry point for disease.
- For heading crops such as cabbage, judge readiness by the firmness and compactness of the head; a head that still feels loose or gives noticeably when pressed is generally not yet mature.

### Fruiting Vegetables (Tomato, Pepper/Chili, Eggplant, Cucumber, Okra, Pumpkin/Squash)
- **Tomato**: Harvest stage depends on market destination — a firmer, less-coloured stage ("colour break" through partial colour) for produce that must travel further, and fuller colour for immediate local sale.
- **Pepper and chili**: Harvest either at the mature green stage or after full colour development, depending on the variety and market requirement; colour change from green to the variety's ripe colour (red, yellow, or orange) indicates advancing maturity.
- **Cucumber**: Harvest based on an appropriate size for the variety and market, while the skin is still glossy and before seeds inside begin to enlarge and the fruit starts to yellow, since overmature cucumbers become bitter and seedy.
- **Okra**: Harvest pods while still young and tender, since pods mature and become fibrous within a few days of flowering; frequent harvesting, often every one to two days during the peak growing period, is necessary to keep pace with rapid pod development and to encourage continued production.
- **Eggplant**: Harvest when the skin is glossy and the fruit has reached an appropriate size for the variety, before the skin dulls and seeds inside begin to harden, which reduces eating quality.

### Root Vegetables (Carrot, Beetroot, Radish, and Similar Crops)
- Harvest based on an appropriate size for the variety and market, since oversized roots are more prone to woodiness, cracking, and hollow centres.
- Lift roots carefully, loosening the surrounding soil first where the soil is compacted, to avoid snapping or bruising the root during removal.
- Where possible, harvest when the soil is moderately moist rather than very dry or waterlogged, since very dry, hard soil increases the risk of breakage, while waterlogged soil makes roots more prone to skin damage and clinging debris.

### Bulb Vegetables (Onion, Garlic)
- Maturity is generally indicated by the foliage beginning to yellow, soften at the neck, and fall over, which signals that the bulb has stopped actively growing and is transitioning toward dormancy.
- After harvest, bulb crops require a field or shaded drying (curing) period to dry down the neck tissue and outer skins into a papery layer that protects the bulb during storage; this process is similar in purpose to, though distinct in method from, the curing of root and tuber crops (see `curing_of_root_and_tuber_crops.md`).

### Legume Vegetables (Green Beans, Green Peas, Cowpea Leaves)
- Harvest pods while still young and tender, before the seeds inside fully mature and the pod wall becomes fibrous or tough.
- As with okra, frequent picking cycles are usually necessary, since pods can move from tender to overmature within a short period, and leaving mature pods on the plant can also reduce the plant's continued production of new pods.

### Cucurbits Harvested for Storage (Butternut, Storage Pumpkin, and Similar Crops)
- Unlike cucumber and courgette, which are harvested young, cucurbits intended for storage are left on the plant until the rind hardens fully and resists denting with a fingernail, and the stem attaching the fruit to the vine has begun to dry and shrink.
- Handle carefully at harvest, leaving a short section of stem attached where possible, since damage to the stem attachment point can allow decay organisms to enter during storage.

## General Maturity Indicators Summary

| Vegetable | Key Maturity Indicator | Harvest Frequency Note |
|---|---|---|
| Cabbage | Head firmness/compactness | Single harvest per head |
| Leafy greens | Appropriate leaf size, before bolting | May allow repeat cutting of outer leaves |
| Tomato | Colour development stage matched to market | Repeated harvest as fruit ripens |
| Okra | Pod still tender, before fibrous | Very frequent (every 1-2 days) |
| Green beans/peas | Pod tender, seeds not fully developed | Frequent, repeated picking |
| Cucumber | Size and gloss, before yellowing | Frequent, repeated picking |
| Carrot/beetroot | Appropriate root size for variety | Single harvest per root |
| Onion/garlic | Foliage yellowing and falling over | Single harvest, followed by curing |
| Storage pumpkin/butternut | Rind hardness, stem drying | Single harvest per fruit |

## Harvesting Technique
- Use clean, sharp tools appropriate to the crop (knives, shears, or by-hand picking) to avoid tearing plant tissue, which invites disease and accelerates spoilage.
- Place harvested vegetables promptly into shaded, well-ventilated containers rather than leaving them exposed to direct sun in the field.
- Minimise the number of times produce is handled and the height from which it is dropped into containers, since even minor impact damage can develop into visible bruising or decay later.
- Where feasible, begin cooling perishable vegetables (moving to shade, using evaporative cooling methods, or otherwise reducing field heat) as soon as possible after harvest to extend usable shelf life.

## Timing Considerations
- Harvest during the early morning where possible, avoiding the heat of midday, both to protect worker wellbeing and to reduce field heat absorbed by the produce.
- For crops that produce continuously over an extended period (okra, green beans, cucumber, and similar crops), maintain a consistent harvesting frequency, since allowing pods or fruit to overmature on the plant not only reduces the quality of that individual item but can also reduce the plant's total future yield.
- Avoid harvesting immediately after irrigation or rainfall where possible, since wet foliage and produce are more prone to fungal disease spread during handling, and wet conditions generally make careful handling more difficult.

## Sorting and Grading at Harvest
- Separate produce by size and quality as early as possible, ideally at the point of harvest, rather than waiting until after transport or storage.
- Remove diseased, pest-damaged, or otherwise substandard produce immediately, rather than allowing it to remain mixed with sound produce, since damaged material can accelerate spoilage of an entire batch.

## Common Mistakes
- Harvesting an entire planting of a continuously producing crop at once, rather than picking selectively and repeatedly as individual fruits or pods reach the correct stage.
- Allowing fruiting vegetables such as okra, beans, or cucumber to overmature on the plant, which both lowers the eating quality of that harvest and can reduce the plant's ongoing productivity.
- Handling produce roughly, tossing it into containers, or overfilling containers so that produce near the bottom is crushed.
- Leaving harvested vegetables exposed to direct sun in the field for an extended period before moving them to shade or onward transport.
- Harvesting root vegetables from very dry, hard soil without first loosening it, resulting in broken or damaged roots.

## When to Consult an Agricultural Extension Officer
- To confirm maturity indicators and expected harvest windows for specific vegetable varieties, since these can differ between varieties of the same crop.
- When planning harvest frequency for continuously producing crops, to balance yield, quality, and labour availability.
- If experiencing unusually rapid spoilage, wilting, or decay after harvest despite following standard timing and handling guidance, since this may indicate a variety, disease, or post-harvest handling issue requiring specific diagnosis.
