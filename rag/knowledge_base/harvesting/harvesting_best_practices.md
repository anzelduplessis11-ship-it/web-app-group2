# Harvesting Best Practices

## Overview
Correct harvest timing and technique directly determine crop quality, storage life, and market value. Harvesting too early or too late, or using poor technique, can cause losses that no amount of post-harvest care can fully recover.

## Determining Harvest Timing
- Every crop has specific physical indicators of maturity (see individual crop documents in `crops/` for crop-specific harvest indicators).
- General categories of maturity indicators include:
  - **Colour change** (e.g., husk browning in maize, fruit colour change in tomato).
  - **Physical texture change** (e.g., pod dryness and rattle in beans, firmness in root crops).
  - **Moisture content** (particularly important for grains, measured directly with a moisture meter where available, or estimated through physical tests such as biting a kernel or the "shake and listen" method for dry grain).
- Harvesting at the correct maturity stage balances maximum yield/quality against the risk of field losses (weathering, pests, shattering) from delayed harvest.

## General Harvesting Techniques by Crop Category

### Cereals and Grains
- Harvest when grain moisture reaches the appropriate level for the intended use (fresh consumption vs. storage; see specific crop documents).
- Cut, thresh, and dry promptly to minimize field exposure to weather and pests.
- Avoid harvesting during or immediately after rain, since wet grain is far more prone to mould and quality loss.

### Legumes
- Harvest dry beans and similar legumes when pods are dry and rattle, but before pods begin to shatter and drop seed in the field.
- Harvest fresh/green legumes (green beans, green peas) while pods are still tender, before seeds fully mature and toughen.

### Root and Tuber Crops
- Harvest carefully to avoid bruising, cutting, or breaking the storage organ, since damaged tissue spoils rapidly and provides entry points for disease organisms.
- Where possible, harvest during drier soil conditions to reduce soil adherence and make cleaning easier.

### Fruits and Vegetables
- Harvest at a maturity stage appropriate for the intended market: full ripeness for immediate local sale, or a slightly less ripe stage if extended transport time is required.
- Handle gently to minimize bruising, and use appropriate containers (ventilated crates rather than sacks) for produce prone to compression damage.

## Timing Considerations
- Harvest during the coolest part of the day (early morning) where possible for perishable crops, to reduce field heat and improve subsequent storage life.
- Avoid harvesting during rain or immediately after heavy rain for both grain crops (moisture/mould risk) and fresh produce (increased disease susceptibility and handling difficulty).
- Coordinate harvest timing with available labour, equipment, and onward transport/processing/storage capacity to avoid delays between harvest and proper handling.

## Common Harvesting Losses and Their Causes
| Loss Type | Common Cause |
|---|---|
| Shattering losses (grain/pod drop in the field) | Delayed harvest past optimal maturity |
| Mechanical/bruising damage | Rough handling during harvest and transport |
| Mould/spoilage | Harvesting during wet conditions or delayed drying |
| Pest damage (birds, rodents) | Delayed harvest allowing extended pest access |
| Quality downgrading | Mixing immature, mature, and overripe produce together |

## Tools and Equipment Considerations
- Keep harvesting tools (knives, sickles, pruning shears) clean and sharp to make clean cuts and reduce plant damage and disease entry points.
- Clean tools between fields, especially where disease pressure is a known concern, to avoid spreading pathogens.
- Use appropriately sized containers for the crop to minimize compression damage during collection and transport.

## Post-Harvest Handling Priorities
- Move harvested produce out of direct sun promptly to reduce field heat, particularly for perishable crops.
- Sort and grade produce as early as possible to separate high-quality produce from damaged or substandard material, preventing spoilage spread.
- See `storage/grain_storage.md` and `storage/perishable_storage.md` for detailed guidance on storage following harvest.

## Common Mistakes
- Harvesting based on the calendar alone rather than actual physical maturity indicators, which vary with weather and management each season.
- Delaying harvest due to labour or market timing considerations, resulting in avoidable field losses.
- Mixing harvest from different maturity stages or quality levels in the same storage container or batch.
- Harvesting and immediately bagging produce without allowing field heat to dissipate, accelerating spoilage in storage.

## When to Consult an Agricultural Extension Officer
- To confirm crop-specific maturity indicators for varieties not well documented locally.
- When investing in harvesting equipment for a larger operation, to select tools appropriate to the crop and scale.
- If experiencing unusually high harvest losses despite following standard timing and technique guidance, since this may indicate an underlying pest, disease, or handling issue requiring specific diagnosis.
