# On-Farm Water Conservation Practices

## Overview
Water conservation on smallholder farms extends well beyond irrigation efficiency to include practices that reduce water loss, improve infiltration and storage, and lower crop water demand across the whole farming system. Given increasingly variable rainfall patterns across much of Africa, and the fact that many smallholders farm without access to reliable irrigation, on-farm water conservation practices such as mulching, contouring, water harvesting, and drought-tolerant variety selection are often the most accessible and impactful tools for improving resilience to dry spells and maximizing the productive use of available rainfall. For irrigation-specific guidance, see `irrigation/irrigation_methods.md`.

## Mulching
Mulching covers the soil surface with organic or, less commonly, synthetic material to reduce water loss and moderate soil conditions.
- A layer of organic mulch (crop residue, straw, dried grass, or leaf litter) reduces direct evaporation from the soil surface by shielding it from sun and wind exposure.
- Mulch moderates soil temperature, reducing heat stress on roots during hot periods and buffering against rapid temperature swings.
- Organic mulch suppresses weed germination and growth, reducing competition for the limited available soil moisture.
- As organic mulch decomposes, it contributes organic matter to the soil, gradually improving the soil's own water-holding capacity over time.
- Mulch should be applied at a sufficient depth to meaningfully suppress evaporation and weeds, while avoiding direct contact with plant stems, which can encourage rot or pest harbourage in some crops.
- Where crop residue is also needed for livestock feed or other household uses, farmers should plan a balance between the amount retained for mulch and the amount allocated to competing uses.
- Living mulches (low-growing companion plants covering the soil between main crop rows) can provide similar surface protection benefits while also contributing to biodiversity and, if a legume, soil nitrogen.

## Contouring and Land Shaping
Contour-based practices slow the movement of water across a field, giving it more time to infiltrate into the soil rather than running off the surface.
- **Contour farming**: Planting rows, ridges, or furrows along the natural contour of sloped land, rather than up and down the slope, slows runoff velocity and reduces soil erosion while improving water infiltration.
- **Contour bunds and stone lines**: Small earthen or stone barriers built along the contour intercept and slow runoff, allowing more water to soak into the soil before it can leave the field; they also trap sediment, which can gradually build fertile terraces over time.
- **Terracing**: On steeper land, constructed terraces create level or gently sloped growing surfaces that substantially reduce runoff velocity and erosion, though they require significant upfront labour or investment to establish.
- **Tied ridging and basin planting**: Forming small cross-ties within furrows, or planting into shallow basins, creates individual water-catching pockets that hold rainfall in place near the plant rather than allowing it to run off, particularly valuable in low and erratic rainfall areas.
- Contour and land-shaping structures are most effective when combined with vegetative cover (grass strips, planted hedges) along the contour line, which further slows water and stabilizes the structure over time.
- These practices are especially important on sloped or erosion-prone land, where uncontrolled runoff both wastes rainfall that could otherwise be used by crops and actively degrades the soil resource.

## Water Harvesting Structures
- Small-scale water harvesting structures, such as micro-catchments, half-moon (semi-circular) bunds, or small check dams across drainage lines, capture and concentrate rainfall runoff for productive use rather than allowing it to be lost from the farm entirely.
- Harvested water can be directed to infiltrate near crop root zones, recharge shallow groundwater used by wells, or be stored in small ponds or tanks for later supplemental use.
- Structures should be sized and placed based on the local slope, soil type, and typical rainfall intensity to avoid structural failure during heavy rainfall events; oversized or poorly sited structures can concentrate water destructively rather than beneficially.
- Community-level or watershed-scale water harvesting, coordinated across multiple farms, can achieve greater cumulative benefit than isolated individual structures, particularly for groundwater recharge.

## Reducing Crop Water Demand Through Variety and Timing Choices
- **Drought-tolerant and short-duration varieties**: Selecting crop varieties bred or locally adapted for lower water requirements or shorter growing periods reduces the total water needed to reach maturity and lowers the risk of a crop failing to complete its cycle during a shortened or erratic rainy season.
- **Planting date timing**: Aligning planting dates with the reliable onset of rains, based on local seasonal forecasts and historical patterns, reduces the risk of early-season crop stress from a false start followed by a dry spell.
- **Staggered planting**: Spreading planting dates across a farm or community reduces the risk that a single poorly timed dry spell affects the entire crop at its most vulnerable growth stage.
- **Appropriate plant spacing and population density**: Adjusting spacing to avoid excessive competition for available soil moisture among plants, particularly in drier areas or seasons, can improve individual plant water status and yield reliability.
- **Crop and variety diversification**: Growing a mix of crops and varieties with different water requirements and drought sensitivities spreads risk across a farm, so that a dry spell affecting one crop does not necessarily affect all.

## Soil Management for Water Retention
- Building soil organic matter through composting, manure application, cover cropping, and residue retention improves the soil's inherent capacity to absorb and hold water, complementing physical water conservation structures.
- Reducing tillage helps preserve soil structure and the natural channels that support infiltration, rather than repeatedly disrupting them (see `conservation_agriculture.md` for detail on minimum tillage as part of a broader conservation system).
- Avoiding soil compaction, for example from uncontrolled livestock movement or heavy machinery on wet soil, preserves the pore structure needed for water to infiltrate rather than pool and run off the surface.
- Healthy soil organic matter simultaneously improves drought buffering (greater water-holding capacity) and drainage (better structure allowing excess water to move through rather than waterlog the root zone), providing resilience at both ends of the rainfall variability spectrum.

## Reducing Non-Productive Water Loss
- Weed control reduces the amount of soil moisture consumed by non-crop plants competing with the main crop for limited water.
- Where livestock and cropping share water sources, planning watering points and schedules helps avoid unnecessary depletion of stored or harvested water intended for crop use.
- Maintaining and repairing any water storage or conveyance structures (ponds, small tanks, channels) reduces losses to leakage or seepage over time.

## Integrating Practices Into a Farm Water Conservation Plan
- Combining multiple practices, such as contour bunds plus mulching plus drought-tolerant varieties, generally provides greater resilience than relying on a single measure alone.
- Practices should be matched to the farm's specific slope, soil type, and rainfall pattern; a technique effective on steep highland terrain may not be the right priority for a flatter, lower-rainfall area, and vice versa.
- Water conservation investments involving labour or construction (terraces, bunds, water harvesting structures) benefit from planning during the dry season or between cropping cycles, ahead of the rains they are intended to manage.
- Farmer groups and community structures can support shared learning, labour-pooling for structure construction, and coordinated watershed-level water management that benefits all participating farms.

## Common Mistakes
- Applying mulch too thinly to meaningfully reduce evaporation or suppress weeds, reducing the practical benefit of the practice.
- Constructing contour bunds or water harvesting structures without accounting for local rainfall intensity and slope, risking structural failure or water damage during heavy storms.
- Selecting drought-tolerant varieties based on marketing claims alone without confirming their performance and availability in the specific local agroecological zone.
- Removing all crop residue for competing uses, leaving no material available for mulching or soil cover.
- Treating water conservation as relevant only in very dry areas, overlooking that improved infiltration and moisture retention also reduce waterlogging and erosion risk during heavy rainfall periods.

## When to Consult an Agricultural Extension Officer
- Before constructing contour bunds, terraces, or water harvesting structures, to ensure correct siting, sizing, and design for the local slope, soil, and rainfall conditions.
- To identify drought-tolerant or short-duration crop varieties validated for the specific district or agroecological zone.
- To access community or watershed-level water conservation planning and coordination support where available.
- If recurring waterlogging or erosion suggests that current water management practices are not adequately matched to the farm's conditions.
