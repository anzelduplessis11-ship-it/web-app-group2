# Irrigation Methods and Water Management

## Overview
Irrigation supplements or replaces rainfall to meet crop water needs, and is increasingly important across Africa given rainfall variability and climate change. Choosing the right irrigation method depends on water availability, crop type, terrain, and available investment capital.

## Major Irrigation Methods

### Surface (Flood/Furrow) Irrigation
- Water is directed across the field surface, either flooding the whole plot (basin irrigation) or channeled along furrows between crop rows.
- Low initial equipment cost but high water use efficiency loss (often only 40-60% of applied water reaches the crop root zone due to runoff and deep percolation).
- Requires relatively level land or careful grading; well suited to crops like rice grown in flooded conditions.
- Risk of uneven water distribution, waterlogging in low spots, and increased soil erosion on sloped land if not managed carefully.

### Drip (Trickle) Irrigation
- Water is delivered slowly and directly to the plant root zone through a network of tubes and emitters.
- High water use efficiency (often 80-95%), since water losses to evaporation and runoff are minimized.
- Reduces weed growth between rows (since only the crop root zone is wetted) and reduces foliar disease risk by keeping leaves dry.
- Higher initial equipment cost and requires reasonably clean water (or filtration) to prevent emitter clogging.
- Well suited to high-value crops such as tomato, vegetables, and orchard crops.

### Sprinkler Irrigation
- Water is sprayed over the crop through pressurized pipes and nozzles, mimicking rainfall.
- Moderate water use efficiency (typically 70-85%), better than surface irrigation but less efficient than drip.
- Suitable for a wide range of crops and can work on uneven terrain better than surface irrigation.
- Requires investment in pumping and pipe infrastructure; wind can distort spray pattern and reduce efficiency.
- Overhead sprinkler use late in the day can increase foliar disease risk for disease-susceptible crops (e.g., tomato, potato) by prolonging leaf wetness.

### Manual/Bucket Irrigation
- Water carried and applied by hand, often used for small home gardens or nursery beds.
- Very low equipment cost but labour-intensive and difficult to scale to larger areas.
- Useful for establishing seedlings or watering high-value small plots where other methods are not feasible.

## Choosing an Irrigation Method
| Factor | Favours Surface Irrigation | Favours Drip Irrigation | Favours Sprinkler Irrigation |
|---|---|---|---|
| Water availability | Higher water requirement | Best for limited water supply | Moderate water requirement |
| Terrain | Flat or gently sloped, graded land | Any terrain, including uneven | Relatively even terrain preferred |
| Crop value | Lower-value field crops (e.g., rice, some cereals) | High-value crops (vegetables, fruit) | Wide range of field and horticultural crops |
| Budget | Lower initial investment | Higher initial investment | Moderate to high initial investment |
| Disease sensitivity | Less relevant | Reduces foliar disease risk | Can increase foliar disease risk if used late in the day |

## Water Scheduling Principles
- Irrigate based on crop growth stage water sensitivity (see individual crop documents in `crops/` for critical water-sensitive stages).
- Avoid both under-watering (yield loss from water stress) and over-watering (waterlogging, nutrient leaching, increased disease risk, and wasted water).
- Where possible, monitor soil moisture directly (e.g., by feel/appearance at root depth) rather than irrigating on a fixed schedule regardless of actual soil conditions.
- Irrigate during cooler parts of the day (early morning or evening) to reduce evaporation losses, except where evening irrigation would prolong leaf wetness and increase disease risk for susceptible crops.

## Water Conservation Practices
- Mulching reduces evaporation from the soil surface and can significantly reduce irrigation frequency needs.
- Alternate wetting and drying (used particularly in irrigated rice) can reduce water use substantially with minimal yield impact.
- Deficit irrigation (deliberately applying somewhat less than full crop water requirement during less sensitive growth stages) can conserve water in water-scarce areas, but requires care to avoid yield loss during critical growth stages.
- Maintaining healthy soil organic matter improves water-holding capacity, reducing irrigation frequency requirements over time.

## Common Mistakes
- Irrigating on a fixed schedule regardless of rainfall, leading to waterlogging after unexpected rain.
- Using overhead irrigation late in the day on disease-susceptible crops, increasing fungal disease risk.
- Under-investing in water source protection, leading to contamination or depletion of irrigation water sources over time.
- Applying water unevenly across a field due to poor land levelling or clogged emitters, causing patchy crop performance.

## When to Consult an Agricultural Extension Officer or Irrigation Specialist
- Before investing in irrigation infrastructure, to assess water source reliability, appropriate system choice, and cost-effectiveness for the specific farm.
- If experiencing recurring waterlogging or drainage problems despite irrigation scheduling adjustments.
- To access water quality testing if water source suitability for drip irrigation (risk of emitter clogging) or crop suitability (salinity) is uncertain.
