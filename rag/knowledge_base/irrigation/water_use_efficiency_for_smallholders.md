# Water Use Efficiency for Smallholders

## Overview
Water use efficiency means getting the greatest crop benefit from every unit of water applied, minimizing losses to evaporation, runoff, deep percolation, and uneven distribution. For smallholders, improving water use efficiency stretches limited water supplies further, reduces pumping and labour costs, and builds resilience against dry spells. This document brings together practical, low-cost techniques for improving irrigation efficiency, drawing on and cross-referencing the other documents in this folder: `irrigation/irrigation_methods.md`, `irrigation/irrigation_scheduling.md`, `irrigation/drip_irrigation_deep_dive.md`, and `irrigation/rainwater_harvesting_techniques.md`.

## Why Water Use Efficiency Matters
- Water is often the most limiting resource for smallholder crop production, particularly in drought-prone and semi-arid regions.
- Inefficient irrigation wastes not only water but also the labour, fuel, or electricity cost of pumping and applying it.
- Excess water application can cause waterlogging, nutrient leaching, and increased disease pressure, reducing yield even where water itself is not scarce.
- Improving efficiency can allow the same water source to irrigate a larger area or support more resilient cropping through dry spells, without requiring a larger water source.

## Understanding Where Water Is Lost
| Loss Pathway | Description | Most Affected By |
|---|---|---|
| Evaporation | Water lost to the atmosphere from the soil surface or from spray before reaching the ground | Open water application (surface, overhead sprinkler), hot/dry/windy conditions, bare soil |
| Runoff | Water flowing off the field surface rather than infiltrating | Application rate exceeding soil infiltration rate, sloped or crusted/compacted soil |
| Deep percolation | Water draining below the root zone, unavailable to the crop | Over-application, especially on sandy soils or with surface irrigation |
| Conveyance loss | Water lost in transit from source to field (seepage from unlined channels, leaking pipes) | Unlined earth channels, damaged or leaking pipes and fittings |
| Non-uniform application | Some areas overwatered while others are underwatered | Poor land levelling, uneven sprinkler coverage, clogged or inconsistent drip emitters |

## Choosing and Improving the Irrigation Method
- Matching the irrigation method to the crop, water supply, and budget is the single largest lever for water use efficiency; see the comparison table in `irrigation/irrigation_methods.md`.
- Drip irrigation typically achieves the highest efficiency (often 80-95%) by delivering water directly to the root zone, making it a strong option for high-value crops where the investment is justified (see `irrigation/drip_irrigation_deep_dive.md`).
- Sprinkler irrigation (typically 70-85% efficiency) improves on surface irrigation and suits a wider range of crops and terrain, but should be scheduled to minimize wind drift and evaporation losses (see `irrigation/sprinkler_irrigation_systems.md`).
- Surface irrigation (typically 40-60% efficiency) can be made more efficient through better land levelling, shorter furrow runs, and careful management of flow rate and timing, even without switching to a different method entirely.
- Where budget allows only incremental upgrades, converting the most water-demanding or highest-value section of a field to drip or micro-sprinkler irrigation first often delivers the greatest efficiency gain per unit of investment.

## Scheduling for Efficiency
- Irrigating based on actual crop and soil needs, rather than a fixed calendar, avoids both under- and over-watering; see the soil-moisture check methods in `irrigation/irrigation_scheduling.md`.
- Irrigating during cooler parts of the day (early morning or evening) reduces evaporation losses compared to midday irrigation, except where evening irrigation would extend leaf wetness and raise disease risk for susceptible crops.
- Applying water to wet the full active root zone and then allowing the soil to draw down before the next irrigation encourages deeper rooting and reduces the frequency of shallow, less efficient applications.
- Adjusting irrigation timing and amount to account for recent and forecast rainfall avoids unnecessary applications that waste water and risk waterlogging.
- Prioritising the crop's most water-sensitive growth stages (commonly flowering and grain/fruit filling) when water is limited protects yield more effectively than spreading a fixed limited water budget evenly across all growth stages.

## Soil and Field Management for Efficiency
### Mulching
- A layer of organic or plastic mulch on the soil surface reduces evaporation directly from the soil, lengthening the interval between irrigations and conserving soil moisture for crop use.
- Organic mulches (crop residue, straw, dried grass) also add organic matter to the soil as they decompose, contributing to the longer-term water-holding capacity described below.

### Building Soil Organic Matter
- Higher soil organic matter improves the soil's capacity to hold water available to plants, reducing the frequency and volume of irrigation needed over time.
- Practices that build organic matter - compost and manure application, cover cropping, reduced tillage, crop residue retention - support water use efficiency indirectly but meaningfully over multiple seasons (see `sustainability/sustainable_farming_practices.md` and `soil/soil_testing_and_fertility.md`).

### Land Levelling and Furrow Design
- Levelling fields used for surface irrigation reduces uneven ponding and runoff, improving distribution uniformity without requiring a change in irrigation method.
- Shorter furrow or basin lengths and controlled inflow rates reduce the risk of water running off the end of the field before infiltrating evenly along its length.

### Reducing Soil Crusting and Compaction
- Crusted or compacted soil surfaces reduce infiltration rates, increasing runoff and reducing the effective amount of applied water that reaches the root zone.
- Practices that maintain soil structure (organic matter addition, appropriate tillage, avoiding traffic on wet soil) support better infiltration and, indirectly, irrigation efficiency.

### Contour Farming and Bunds on Sloped Land
- On sloped fields, contour planting and bunds slow runoff and encourage infiltration, improving the effective use of both rainfall and applied irrigation water (see `irrigation/rainwater_harvesting_techniques.md` and `soil/soil_conservation_and_erosion.md`).

## Deficit and Alternate Irrigation Strategies
- **Deficit irrigation**: deliberately applying somewhat less than the full crop water requirement during less water-sensitive growth stages can conserve water with limited yield impact, provided the most sensitive stages (typically flowering and fruit/grain filling) still receive adequate water.
- **Alternate wetting and drying**: used particularly in irrigated rice systems, this approach allows the field to dry moderately between irrigations rather than maintaining continuous flooding, reducing total water use with limited effect on yield when managed correctly.
- These strategies require closer monitoring than simply applying full water at every opportunity, since misjudging the timing or degree of deficit can cause avoidable yield loss; they are best used deliberately in water-scarce conditions rather than as a general default.

## Reducing Conveyance and Equipment Losses
- Line or cover open water channels where seepage losses are significant, or switch to piped conveyance where feasible, to reduce water lost in transit from the source to the field.
- Repair leaking pipes, fittings, and valves promptly; even small leaks add up to substantial water loss over a season.
- For drip systems, maintain filtration and clear clogged emitters promptly, since clogging causes both uneven application and wasted pumping effort on sections of the field receiving too little water (see `irrigation/drip_irrigation_deep_dive.md`).
- For sprinkler systems, maintain nozzles and check for pressure loss, since worn or clogged nozzles distort the application pattern and reduce uniformity (see `irrigation/sprinkler_irrigation_systems.md`).

## Harvesting and Storing Water to Support Efficient Use
- Capturing rainwater in tanks, ponds, zai pits, or behind bunds provides an additional buffer of water that can be used efficiently through drip or careful surface application rather than relying solely on a single primary source (see `irrigation/rainwater_harvesting_techniques.md`).
- Reducing reliance on a single groundwater source by supplementing with harvested rainwater supports more sustainable long-term use of boreholes and wells (see `irrigation/borehole_and_well_management.md`).
- Covering or shading open storage (ponds, tanks) reduces evaporation losses from stored water before it is even applied to the crop.

## A Practical Efficiency Checklist for Smallholders
- Is the irrigation method reasonably matched to the crop value, water supply, and terrain, or would a different method meaningfully improve efficiency?
- Is irrigation scheduled based on actual soil moisture and crop needs, rather than a fixed routine regardless of rainfall?
- Is water applied during cooler parts of the day where disease risk allows?
- Is the soil surface mulched or otherwise protected from excessive evaporation?
- Are leaks, clogged emitters, or worn nozzles repaired promptly rather than left for a future season?
- Is the field level enough (for surface irrigation) or the sprinkler/drip layout even enough to avoid significant non-uniform application?
- Is rainwater harvesting used where feasible to supplement the primary water source and reduce pressure on it?
- During water-scarce periods, is available water being prioritised toward the crop's most sensitive growth stages?

## Common Mistakes
- Continuing to use a highly inefficient irrigation method for high-value crops when a modest investment in drip or improved sprinkler irrigation would pay back through water and yield savings.
- Irrigating on a fixed schedule regardless of rainfall or actual soil moisture, wasting water and risking waterlogging.
- Leaving leaks, clogged emitters, or damaged channels unrepaired, accepting ongoing water loss as unavoidable.
- Ignoring mulching and organic matter building as "extra" measures rather than core parts of a water efficiency strategy.
- Applying deficit irrigation indiscriminately across all growth stages rather than protecting the most water-sensitive stages.
- Treating water use efficiency purely as an irrigation-system question, overlooking the substantial role of soil health and field management.

## When to Consult an Agricultural Extension Officer or Irrigation Specialist
- To assess which efficiency improvements (system change, scheduling adjustment, soil management) would give the greatest benefit for a specific farm's water constraints and budget.
- Before implementing deficit irrigation or alternate wetting and drying, to plan the approach correctly for the specific crop and avoid yield loss from misjudged timing.
- If water losses seem high but the cause (conveyance, application method, soil infiltration, or something else) is unclear from field observation alone.
- To integrate rainwater harvesting, groundwater use, and irrigation scheduling into a coordinated farm water management plan.
