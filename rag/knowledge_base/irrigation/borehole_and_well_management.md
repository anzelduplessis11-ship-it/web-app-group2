# Borehole and Well Management for Irrigation

## Overview
Boreholes and wells tap groundwater to provide a reliable water source for irrigation, especially where surface water is scarce, seasonal, or unreliable. Groundwater sources require careful siting, construction, protection, and ongoing management to remain safe and productive over the long term. This document covers sourcing (siting and construction basics), maintenance, and sustainable use of boreholes and wells for farm irrigation. For assessing whether groundwater is suitable for irrigation, see `irrigation/irrigation_water_quality.md`; for choosing how to apply the water once pumped, see `irrigation/irrigation_methods.md`.

## Boreholes vs. Dug Wells
| Feature | Borehole | Dug Well |
|---|---|---|
| Construction method | Drilled mechanically to significant depth | Dug by hand or machine, generally shallower |
| Typical depth | Can reach deep aquifers, often tens of metres or more | Usually shallow, tapping the water table near the surface |
| Construction cost | Higher, requires drilling equipment and casing | Lower, can be built with local labour and materials |
| Water yield reliability | Often more reliable across seasons if it taps a deeper aquifer | More vulnerable to seasonal water table fluctuation |
| Contamination risk | Lower if properly sealed and cased near the surface | Higher, since shallow water is more exposed to surface contamination |
| Typical extraction | Requires a pump (submersible, handpump, or motorized) | Can be extracted by bucket, handpump, or motorized pump |

## Siting a Borehole or Well
- Groundwater availability and depth vary significantly by geology; hydrogeological surveys (where affordable) or knowledge of local groundwater conditions greatly improve the chance of siting a productive point.
- Avoid siting new water points close to latrines, animal pens, waste disposal areas, or chemical storage; a safe separation distance reduces the risk of contamination reaching the water source, and specific safe distances should be confirmed with a water or health professional based on local soil and geology.
- Consider the site relative to the fields to be irrigated, balancing pumping distance and elevation against water availability and contamination risk.
- Low-lying areas may have shallower groundwater but are also more prone to contamination from surface runoff; slightly elevated, well-drained sites reduce this risk for wells with exposed openings.
- Where multiple farmers rely on nearby water points, consider cumulative groundwater use in the area to avoid excessive drawdown that could deplete or lower the water table for all users.

## Construction and Protection Basics
- Casing (lining) a borehole, particularly near the surface, prevents collapse and blocks the entry of contaminated shallow water and soil into the well shaft.
- A well head should be sealed and raised slightly above the surrounding ground level with a concrete apron sloped away from the opening, preventing surface runoff and spilled water from flowing back into the well.
- Cover wells and borehole heads securely to prevent debris, animals, and contaminants from entering, and to reduce evaporation and safety hazards.
- Fencing around the water point can help keep livestock and vehicles away from the immediate area, reducing both contamination risk and physical damage to the structure.
- Grouting or sealing the annular space around borehole casing (the gap between the casing and the surrounding earth) helps prevent surface water from seeping down alongside the casing into the aquifer.

## Pumping Systems
### Handpumps
- Manually operated pumps suited to shallow wells and boreholes with modest daily water demand.
- Low operating cost (no fuel or electricity) but limited output, generally more suitable for domestic use or very small-scale irrigation than larger commercial plots.

### Motorized Pumps (Diesel or Petrol)
- Provide higher flow rates suited to irrigating larger areas, but require ongoing fuel supply and mechanical maintenance.
- Should be sized to match the borehole or well's sustainable yield; a pump that withdraws water faster than the aquifer can recharge will draw the water level down excessively (see "Sustainable Yield" below).

### Electric Pumps
- Where grid electricity is available and reliable, electric pumps offer lower running costs than fuel-powered pumps and simpler operation.
- Submersible electric pumps are commonly used in boreholes, positioned below the water level inside the casing.

### Solar-Powered Pumps
- Increasingly used where grid electricity is unavailable or unreliable, drawing power from photovoltaic panels.
- Water output varies with sunlight availability, so systems are often paired with a storage tank to buffer supply for use during low-sunlight periods or at times of peak irrigation demand.
- Have low ongoing fuel costs after installation but require correct sizing of the pump, panels, and any storage to match both water demand and available sunlight.

## Understanding Sustainable Yield
- Every borehole or well has a sustainable yield: the rate at which water can be withdrawn without permanently lowering the water table or drying out the source.
- Pumping faster than the aquifer recharges causes the water level to drop during pumping (drawdown); if drawdown becomes excessive, the pump may run dry, drawing in sediment or air and risking damage.
- Recovery time - how long the water level takes to return to normal after pumping stops - gives a practical indication of whether current pumping rates are sustainable; slow recovery suggests pumping is close to or exceeding sustainable yield.
- Seasonal variation matters: a borehole or well may support heavier pumping during and shortly after the rainy season, when the water table is higher, but require reduced pumping during the dry season as the water table falls.
- Multiple water points drawing from the same aquifer can interact, so sustainable use should consider not just an individual borehole but neighbouring water points drawing from the same groundwater source.

## Monitoring and Record-Keeping
- Track the static water level (level when the pump is off) and pumping water level (level while pumping) periodically to detect long-term declines that may indicate over-abstraction or drought impact.
- Record pump running hours or approximate volumes pumped where possible, to relate water use to crop area and compare against expected sustainable yield.
- Note any changes in water taste, odour, colour, or clarity, which can signal contamination or changes in the aquifer, and warrant water quality testing (see `irrigation/irrigation_water_quality.md`).
- Keep basic maintenance records (pump servicing dates, repairs, filter changes) to plan preventive maintenance and anticipate part replacement.

## Routine Maintenance
- Inspect the well head, casing, and apron regularly for cracks, settling, or damage that could allow contaminated water to enter.
- Clean and clear the area around the water point of debris, spilled water, and vegetation that could attract animals or harbour contamination sources.
- Service pumps according to the manufacturer's recommended intervals (checking seals, bearings, and moving parts on motorized and electric pumps; checking valves and rods on handpumps).
- Check pipes, fittings, and storage tanks connected to the system for leaks, which waste pumped water and can also allow contamination to enter under certain conditions.
- For solar systems, keep panels clean and free of shading to maintain expected output, and check wiring connections periodically.
- Test water quality periodically, especially after any flooding, nearby construction, or changes in surrounding land use that could introduce contamination.

## Signs of Problems
| Sign | Possible Cause | Response |
|---|---|---|
| Declining yield over successive seasons | Aquifer depletion, over-abstraction, or pump wear | Reduce pumping rate, investigate recharge conditions, service or replace pump |
| Sudden drop in water level during pumping | Pumping rate exceeds recharge (drawdown exceeding sustainable yield) | Reduce pump run time, space out irrigation, consult a hydrogeologist if persistent |
| Cloudy, sediment-laden water | Casing or seal damage allowing sediment ingress, or pump drawing from near the bottom | Inspect casing/seal, adjust pump intake depth, consider filtration |
| Unusual taste, odour, or colour | Possible contamination from surface sources or natural mineral content | Test water quality before continued use, especially for food crop irrigation |
| Pump running but little or no water output | Pump wear, blocked intake, or water level below intake | Inspect and service pump, check water level, clear blockages |

## Sustainable Use Practices
- Match crop area and irrigation method to the confirmed sustainable yield of the water source rather than expanding irrigated area based on peak short-term pumping capacity.
- Favour efficient irrigation methods (such as drip irrigation, see `irrigation/drip_irrigation_deep_dive.md`) where groundwater supply is limited, to extract more productive value from each unit of water withdrawn.
- Where multiple households or farms share an aquifer, coordinate on pumping schedules and total withdrawal where possible to avoid depleting the shared resource.
- Combine groundwater use with rainwater harvesting (see `irrigation/rainwater_harvesting_techniques.md`) to reduce reliance on the borehole or well during peak demand periods.
- Avoid pumping during the hottest, driest part of the dry season if the water table is known to be at its lowest, where alternative timing is feasible.

## Common Mistakes
- Siting a new well or borehole too close to latrines, waste pits, or animal housing, creating a lasting contamination risk.
- Installing a pump sized well beyond the source's sustainable yield, leading to drawdown, pump damage, or eventual source failure.
- Neglecting the well head seal and apron, allowing surface water and contaminants to enter directly.
- Failing to monitor water levels or quality over time, so that gradual depletion or contamination goes unnoticed until it becomes a serious problem.
- Sharing a water point among many users without any coordination on total withdrawal, risking collective over-abstraction.
- Delaying pump maintenance until complete failure, rather than following a preventive servicing schedule.

## When to Consult a Hydrogeologist, Driller, or Agricultural Extension Officer
- Before drilling or digging a new borehole or well, to assess likely groundwater availability, appropriate depth, and safe siting relative to contamination sources.
- If yield declines noticeably over time, to assess whether the cause is aquifer-level depletion, drought, or a mechanical/construction problem.
- To size a pump (and, where relevant, solar power system) correctly for the confirmed sustainable yield of the source and the planned irrigation area.
- If water quality changes are observed, to arrange testing and interpret results, especially before continuing to irrigate food crops.
- Where several farms share groundwater resources, to help coordinate sustainable collective use.
