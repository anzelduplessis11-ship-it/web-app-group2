# Rainwater Harvesting Techniques for Farm Water Supply

## Overview
Rainwater harvesting captures and stores rainfall or runoff for later use, buffering farms against dry spells and unreliable rainfall patterns common across much of Africa. Harvested water can supplement irrigation (see `irrigation/irrigation_methods.md` and `irrigation/irrigation_scheduling.md`), provide livestock water, and support domestic use. This document covers common structures - ponds, tanks, zai pits, and bunds - along with their design, construction, and maintenance considerations. For linking harvested water to efficient use on the field, see `irrigation/water_use_efficiency_for_smallholders.md`; for broader climate resilience strategies, see `weather/drought_and_flood_management.md` and `weather/climate_smart_agriculture.md`.

## Why Rainwater Harvesting Matters
- Rainfall in many farming regions is seasonal, variable, and increasingly unpredictable, leading to dry spells even within the rainy season.
- Harvested water can fill the gap during short dry spells without relying solely on groundwater or distant surface water sources.
- Reduces pressure on boreholes and wells, supporting more sustainable use of groundwater (see `irrigation/borehole_and_well_management.md`).
- Can reduce soil erosion and improve groundwater recharge when harvesting structures slow and capture runoff rather than letting it wash away.
- Low-cost, low-technology methods are accessible to smallholders with limited capital, though larger structures require more labour, materials, and planning.

## Types of Rainwater Harvesting Structures

### Roof Catchment and Storage Tanks
- Rainfall is collected from a roof surface (house, barn, greenhouse) via gutters and channeled into a storage tank or drum.
- Water quality is generally better than open pond water since it has less contact with soil, though the first flush after a dry period can carry dust, bird droppings, and debris from the roof and should ideally be diverted away from the storage tank.
- Storage tanks (built from brick, concrete, ferrocement, plastic, or metal) protect stored water from evaporation losses and contamination compared to open storage.
- Tank size should be matched to roof catchment area, expected rainfall, and intended use; larger tanks store more water but cost more to build.
- Well suited to supplying drip irrigation kits, nursery watering, and domestic/livestock use, since the water is relatively clean and enclosed storage limits mosquito breeding if properly sealed.

### Farm Ponds
- Excavated basins that collect and store runoff from a catchment area (fields, hillsides, or channeled rainfall) for irrigation and livestock use.
- Can store much larger volumes than tanks, making them suitable for supplying irrigation over a larger area or a longer dry period.
- Site selection should avoid areas with highly permeable (sandy) soil unless lined, since seepage losses can be severe; clay-rich soils hold water more naturally.
- Lining with clay, plastic sheeting, or concrete reduces seepage losses in permeable soils but adds cost.
- Open ponds lose water to evaporation, especially in hot, dry, windy conditions; shading, wind breaks, or partial covers can reduce these losses.
- Require an inlet to direct runoff into the pond and often a spillway or overflow to safely release excess water during heavy storms, preventing structural damage.
- Open water bodies can become breeding sites for mosquitoes; managing vegetation around the pond edge and considering fish stocking (which can help control mosquito larvae) are common mitigation measures.

### Zai Pits
- Small planting pits (commonly used in dryland and Sahelian farming systems) dug in a grid pattern across a field, each collecting and concentrating rainfall and runoff directly at the planting point.
- Pits are typically filled partially with organic matter (manure, compost, crop residue) before planting, which improves water retention and soil fertility at the same time.
- Particularly effective on degraded, crusted, or compacted soils where water would otherwise run off rather than infiltrate.
- Labour-intensive to dig by hand across a field, but require no purchased materials and directly combine water harvesting with soil fertility improvement.
- Best suited to staple grain crops (such as sorghum, pearl millet, and maize) grown in low-rainfall, drought-prone areas.

### Bunds (Contour Bunds, Stone Bunds, Earth Bunds)
- Low ridges or barriers built along the contour of a slope to slow runoff, encourage infiltration, and reduce soil erosion.
- Earth bunds are built from compacted soil; stone bunds use lines of stones, often where stone is locally abundant and soil is thin.
- Water that would otherwise run off the field is held behind the bund long enough to soak into the soil, recharging soil moisture and, over time, shallow groundwater.
- Effective for field-scale water and soil conservation on gently to moderately sloped land; on steep slopes, bunds must be combined with other erosion control measures to avoid failure during heavy rain.
- Bunds also help trap eroded soil and organic matter moving down-slope, gradually improving soil fertility on the uphill side of each bund.
- Related terracing and trench techniques are covered in `soil/soil_conservation_and_erosion.md`.

### Semi-Circular and Trapezoidal Bunds
- Variants of bund construction shaped to capture and concentrate runoff toward individual trees, shrubs, or planting stations, commonly used for tree establishment and rangeland rehabilitation in dry areas.
- Require less labour than full contour bunding across a field and are often used to rehabilitate degraded rangeland or establish orchards in low-rainfall zones.

## Comparing Rainwater Harvesting Options
| Structure | Typical Scale | Water Quality | Relative Cost/Labour | Best Suited To |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Roof tank | Household/small plot | Relatively clean | Moderate (tank cost) | Drip kits, nursery, domestic/livestock use |
| Farm pond | Field to farm scale | Variable; needs management | Moderate to high (excavation, lining) | Supplemental irrigation, livestock water |
| Zai pits | Individual planting points | Not stored as free water | Low cost, high labour | Dryland grain crops on degraded soil |
| Contour/stone bunds | Field to landscape scale | Not stored as free water | Low to moderate cost, moderate to high labour | Soil moisture retention, erosion control on slopes |

## Planning and Siting Considerations
- Estimate the catchment area (roof, field, or hillside) contributing runoff, since structure size should be matched to realistic available runoff rather than built larger than the catchment can fill.
- Consider local rainfall patterns: total annual rainfall, distribution across the season, and the likelihood and length of dry spells to be bridged.
- Assess soil type and permeability at the site, since sandy or highly permeable soils lose water quickly from unlined ponds or open storage.
- Include a safe overflow or spillway for structures that store larger volumes, so that heavy rainfall events do not cause dam or bund failure and downstream damage.
- Site structures to avoid contamination risk from latrines, animal pens, or chemical storage uphill of the catchment or storage point.
- Where a structure will be shared or affects neighbouring land (e.g., diverting runoff), agree on layout and maintenance responsibilities with neighbouring farmers where relevant.

## Construction and Maintenance Basics
- Compact soil thoroughly when building earth bunds and pond walls to reduce the risk of seepage and structural failure.
- Establish grass or vegetative cover on bund surfaces and pond banks where possible, since vegetation stabilizes soil and reduces erosion of the structure itself.
- Inspect structures before and after the rainy season for cracks, erosion, animal burrows, or blockages in inlets and spillways.
- Clear sediment that accumulates in ponds periodically, since sediment buildup reduces storage capacity over time.
- Repair small cracks or breaches in bunds and pond walls promptly, before heavy rain enlarges them into major failures.
- Keep inlet and overflow channels clear of debris and vegetation blockage so that water can enter and excess water can exit safely.

## Water Quality Considerations for Harvested Water
- Water collected from roofs, ponds, or runoff may carry sediment, organic debris, animal waste, or agrochemical residue from the catchment area; see `irrigation/irrigation_water_quality.md` for assessment guidance.
- Simple settling (allowing sediment to settle before use) and filtration can improve water quality before use in drip systems, which are sensitive to clogging.
- Water intended for direct crop-contact use close to harvest (especially on crops eaten raw) warrants more caution regarding contamination than water used earlier in the season or for field crops with a longer gap before harvest.
- Covering storage tanks and managing pond vegetation reduces both contamination risk and mosquito breeding.

## Common Mistakes
- Building storage structures larger than the catchment area can realistically fill, resulting in a structure that rarely reaches capacity.
- Failing to include an overflow or spillway, risking structural failure during heavy storms.
- Placing ponds or tanks downhill from latrines, animal housing, or chemical storage, risking water contamination.
- Neglecting sediment removal from ponds, gradually reducing usable storage volume.
- Ignoring evaporation losses from open ponds in hot, windy conditions, overestimating how much stored water will actually be available later in the dry season.
- Digging zai pits or bunds without a contour survey on sloped land, reducing their effectiveness or causing uneven water distribution.

## When to Consult an Agricultural Extension Officer or Water Engineer
- Before constructing larger ponds or bunds, to assess site suitability, structural design, and safe overflow capacity for local rainfall intensity.
- If seepage losses from a pond are higher than expected, to evaluate lining options suited to the local soil and budget.
- To plan integration of harvested water with a chosen irrigation method (see `irrigation/irrigation_methods.md`) and scheduling approach.
- If contamination of stored water is suspected, to arrange water quality testing before use on food crops.
- For guidance on combining rainwater harvesting structures with soil conservation measures on sloped or degraded land (see `soil/soil_conservation_and_erosion.md`).
