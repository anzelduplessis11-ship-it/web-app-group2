# Irrigation Scheduling: When and How Much to Water

## Overview
Irrigation scheduling means deciding when to irrigate and how much water to apply. Good scheduling gives crops the water they need at the right times while avoiding waste, waterlogging, and disease. Both under-watering (causing water stress and yield loss) and over-watering (causing waterlogging, nutrient leaching, disease, and wasted water) reduce productivity. Scheduling should respond to actual crop needs and soil conditions rather than following a fixed calendar regardless of rainfall. For choosing an irrigation system, see `irrigation/irrigation_methods.md`; for how weather affects water needs, see `weather/weather_impacts_on_farming.md`.

## What Determines Crop Water Needs
- **Crop type and variety**: different crops and varieties use different amounts of water.
- **Growth stage**: water needs change through the season and some stages are far more sensitive to stress than others.
- **Weather**: hot, dry, windy, and sunny conditions increase water loss from the crop and soil; cool, humid, cloudy conditions reduce it.
- **Soil type**: sandy soils hold less water and need more frequent, smaller applications; clay soils hold more water and are watered less often but risk waterlogging if over-applied.
- **Rainfall**: recent and expected rain must be subtracted from irrigation needs to avoid over-watering.

## Crop Water Needs by Growth Stage
Most annual crops follow a general pattern, though the exact sensitive stages vary by crop (see individual crop documents in `crops/`).

| Growth stage | General water sensitivity | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Establishment / seedling | Moderate to high | Young roots are shallow; keep the surface zone moist but not waterlogged |
| Vegetative growth | Moderate | Steady moisture supports leaf and stem development |
| Flowering / pollination | Usually most sensitive | Water stress here often causes the greatest yield loss |
| Fruit / grain filling | High | Adequate water supports yield and quality |
| Maturity / ripening | Low | Water is often reduced to aid ripening and drying |

- Where water is limited, prioritise irrigation during the most sensitive stages (commonly flowering and grain/fruit filling) over less sensitive stages.

## Deciding When to Irrigate
- The goal is to replenish soil moisture before the crop experiences stress, but not so often that the soil stays saturated.
- Check the soil in the root zone rather than judging from the surface, which dries fastest.
- Take account of recent rainfall and the weather forecast; delay irrigation if meaningful rain is expected.
- Irrigate during cooler parts of the day (early morning or evening) to reduce evaporation losses, except where evening watering would keep leaves wet overnight and raise disease risk for susceptible crops such as tomato and potato.

## Deciding How Much to Apply
- Apply enough to wet the active root zone, then allow the soil to draw down before the next irrigation, rather than applying frequent shallow amounts that only wet the surface and encourage shallow rooting.
- Sandy soils need smaller amounts applied more often; clay soils need larger amounts applied less often.
- Avoid applying more than the soil can absorb, which causes runoff, erosion, and deep losses below the roots.

## Simple Soil-Moisture Checks
- **Feel and appearance method**: take a handful of soil from root depth and squeeze it.
  - Sandy soil that will not hold together and feels dry is likely too dry; if it forms a weak ball it has some moisture.
  - Loam or clay soil that forms a firm ball and feels moist has adequate moisture; if it is dry and crumbly it needs water; if it is wet and sticky and water can be squeezed out, it is likely over-wet.
- **Digging a small hole**: check moisture at several depths in the root zone; the surface can be dry while deeper soil is still moist.
- **Observing the crop early in the day**: temporary midday wilting can be normal in heat, but wilting that persists into the cooler morning suggests genuine water stress.
- Where affordable, simple soil-moisture indicators or meters can support these checks, but the feel method is a reliable low-cost approach.

## Signs of Under-Watering and Over-Watering
| Signs of under-watering | Signs of over-watering |
|---|---|
| Persistent wilting, including in the cool morning | Constantly wet or waterlogged soil |
| Slow, stunted growth | Yellowing lower leaves and poor growth from oxygen-starved roots |
| Leaf curling, scorching, or premature drop | Root rot and increased fungal disease |
| Poor flowering or small fruit/grain | Nutrients leached below the root zone; algae or moss on soil surface |

## Water-Saving Tips
- Mulch the soil surface to reduce evaporation and lengthen the interval between irrigations.
- Match the irrigation method to the crop and water supply; efficient methods such as drip deliver water directly to the root zone with less loss (see `irrigation/irrigation_methods.md`).
- Repair leaks, clear clogged emitters, and level fields so water is applied evenly.
- Harvest and store rainwater where feasible to buffer against dry spells.
- Build soil organic matter over time to improve water-holding capacity and reduce irrigation frequency.
- In water-scarce conditions, consider applying slightly less than full water needs during the least sensitive stages, while protecting the sensitive stages, to stretch limited water.

## Common Mistakes
- Irrigating on a fixed schedule regardless of rainfall, leading to waterlogging after unexpected rain.
- Judging soil moisture from the dry surface rather than checking the root zone.
- Frequent shallow watering that wets only the surface and encourages weak, shallow roots.
- Over-watering clay soils, causing waterlogging and root problems.
- Failing to prioritise the crop's most water-sensitive stages when water is limited.

## When to Consult an Agricultural Extension Officer
- To identify the critical water-sensitive stages and typical water needs for a specific crop and variety.
- If waterlogging or drainage problems persist despite adjusting scheduling.
- To plan efficient irrigation and water-saving measures suited to the local water supply, soil, and climate.
- If water quality (salinity) may be affecting crop performance.
