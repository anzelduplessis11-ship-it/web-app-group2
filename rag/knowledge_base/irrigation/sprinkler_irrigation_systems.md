# Sprinkler Irrigation Systems: Types, Suitability, and Maintenance

## Overview
Sprinkler irrigation applies water over the crop through pressurized pipes and nozzles, spraying it through the air to mimic rainfall. It sits between surface irrigation and drip irrigation in terms of water use efficiency and cost, and can work well on terrain unsuited to surface irrigation. This document expands on the sprinkler overview in `irrigation/irrigation_methods.md`, covering system types, suitability, design basics, and maintenance in more detail. For scheduling sprinkler irrigation, see `irrigation/irrigation_scheduling.md`; for comparing efficiency to other methods, see `irrigation/water_use_efficiency_for_smallholders.md`.

## How Sprinkler Irrigation Works
- Water is pumped under pressure through a network of pipes to sprinkler heads or nozzles, which break the water into droplets and distribute it over the field, similar to natural rainfall.
- Pressure and nozzle design determine droplet size, throw distance, and application pattern; correct matching of pressure to nozzle type is important for even coverage.
- Sprinklers can be set out in fixed positions, moved manually between irrigation sets, or mounted on mechanized moving systems, depending on the scale of operation and available investment.

## Types of Sprinkler Systems

### Fixed (Solid-Set) Sprinkler Systems
- Pipes and sprinkler heads are laid out to cover the whole field (or a large section of it) at once, with sprinklers left in place through the irrigation season.
- Reduces labour compared to moving equipment between sets, since irrigation is simply switched on and off by valve or zone.
- Higher upfront cost due to the larger quantity of pipe and sprinkler heads required to cover the full area at once.
- Well suited to high-value crops or nurseries where minimizing labour and achieving very even coverage justifies the higher investment.

### Portable/Hand-Move Sprinkler Systems
- Pipes and sprinklers are moved manually between different sections of the field after each irrigation set is complete.
- Lower equipment cost than solid-set systems since a smaller quantity of pipe and sprinklers can irrigate a larger total area over time, but requires more labour to move equipment.
- A practical middle-cost option for smallholders wanting sprinkler irrigation without the investment of a solid-set or mechanized system.

### Wheel-Move (Wheel Line) Systems
- A lateral pipe mounted on wheels can be rolled across the field between irrigation sets, reducing the labour of disconnecting and reassembling pipe compared to fully portable systems.
- Suited to relatively level, obstacle-free fields where the wheeled line can be moved in straight lines.
- Represents a middle ground between hand-move and fully mechanized center-pivot or linear-move systems in both cost and labour requirements.

### Center-Pivot and Linear-Move Systems
- Mechanized systems where a long sprinkler lateral rotates around a central pivot point (center-pivot) or moves in a straight line across a field (linear-move), automatically irrigating large areas with minimal labour.
- Suited to larger commercial-scale fields due to the substantial investment required; less common on smallholder plots but relevant where farmers cooperate on larger shared irrigated areas or on larger commercial operations.
- Provide very even water application across large areas once properly calibrated, and can be automated with timers and, in more advanced setups, soil moisture sensors.

### Micro-Sprinklers and Mini-Sprinklers
- Smaller-scale sprinkler heads that apply water over a limited radius, often used under tree crops (orchards) or in nurseries as an alternative to drip irrigation.
- Operate at lower pressure and flow than standard field sprinklers, sitting closer to drip irrigation in water use efficiency while still wetting some soil surface area around the plant, which can help even out root zone moisture for wider-rooted crops like fruit trees.
- Require filtration similar to drip systems, since nozzle openings can be small enough to clog with sediment or debris.

## Suitability of Sprinkler Irrigation
### Terrain
- Handles uneven or gently sloping terrain better than surface irrigation, since it does not depend on gravity flow across graded land.
- Very steep or highly irregular terrain may still cause uneven pressure and coverage; design should account for elevation changes across the field.

### Crops
- Suitable for a wide range of field and horticultural crops, including cereals, legumes, and many vegetables.
- Overhead wetting of foliage makes sprinkler irrigation less suitable for crops highly susceptible to foliar fungal or bacterial diseases (such as tomato and potato) unless irrigation timing and system choice are managed carefully to minimize leaf wetness duration; drip irrigation is often preferred for such crops (see `irrigation/irrigation_methods.md`).
- Micro-sprinklers under tree crops can reduce this disease risk somewhat compared to full-canopy overhead wetting, since the spray is directed at the root zone area rather than over the canopy.

### Wind
- Wind distorts the spray pattern, reducing uniformity and increasing evaporative losses, especially with fine droplet nozzles.
- In windy conditions or locations, sprinkler irrigation is generally scheduled for calmer times of day (commonly early morning or evening) and larger droplet nozzles may be preferred to reduce wind drift.

### Water Source and Investment
- Requires a pump capable of generating and sustaining the pressure needed for the chosen sprinkler and nozzle type; pressure requirements vary by system type and should be confirmed against the pump's specifications.
- Moderate to high initial investment in pipe, sprinklers, and pumping infrastructure compared to surface irrigation, though generally less than a comparably sized, fully developed drip system with fine filtration.

## Comparing Sprinkler System Types
| System Type | Relative Cost | Labour Requirement | Typical Scale | Best Suited To |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Fixed/solid-set | Higher | Low (once installed) | Small to medium plots, high-value crops | Nurseries, high-value crops needing minimal labour |
| Portable/hand-move | Lower to moderate | High (frequent manual moves) | Small to medium plots | Smallholders balancing cost and labour |
| Wheel-move | Moderate | Moderate | Medium plots, level fields | Farms wanting reduced labour vs. hand-move |
| Center-pivot/linear-move | High | Low (mechanized) | Large fields | Larger commercial-scale or cooperative irrigation |
| Micro/mini-sprinkler | Moderate | Low to moderate | Orchards, nurseries | Tree crops, nursery beds |

## Design and Operating Basics
- Match nozzle type, spacing, and operating pressure to achieve even coverage without excessive overlap or gaps; manufacturer specifications for a given nozzle type provide the throw radius and application rate to plan spacing.
- Size the pump and pipe network to deliver the required flow and pressure to the sprinklers operating simultaneously in a given zone; dividing a large field into irrigation zones or sets allows a smaller pump to serve a larger total area over successive irrigation rounds.
- Install filtration appropriate to the water source, particularly for micro-sprinklers with small nozzle openings, to prevent clogging (see `irrigation/irrigation_water_quality.md`).
- Check pressure at multiple points in the system after installation; low pressure at the far end of a lateral line indicates the need for pressure regulation, larger pipe diameter, or smaller zones.
- Schedule irrigation timing to balance evaporation losses (favouring cooler, calmer parts of the day) against disease risk from prolonged leaf wetness (favouring irrigation that allows foliage to dry before nightfall for disease-susceptible crops).

## Maintenance
- Clean or replace clogged nozzles and sprinkler heads regularly; partial clogging distorts the spray pattern and reduces uniformity even when some water still emerges.
- Check and maintain filters at the pump intake and, for micro-sprinkler systems, inline filters protecting individual laterals.
- Inspect pipes, fittings, and risers for leaks, cracks, or damage from field operations (tillage, livestock, vehicles), repairing promptly to avoid water loss and pressure drops elsewhere in the system.
- Periodically check that sprinkler heads remain upright and correctly oriented, since heads knocked out of position by fieldwork or animals will misdirect water.
- For mechanized systems (center-pivot, linear-move), follow the manufacturer's servicing schedule for drive mechanisms, alignment, and electrical/control components.
- Drain and protect pipes and pumps appropriately during any extended off-season period to avoid damage from sediment settling or, in cooler climates, freezing.

## Common Problems and Troubleshooting
| Problem | Likely Cause | Response |
|---|---|---|
| Uneven wetting pattern across the field | Clogged or misaligned nozzles, incorrect spacing, wind drift | Clean/realign nozzles, check spacing design, irrigate during calmer periods |
| Weak spray or low throw distance | Insufficient pressure, undersized pipe, or pump limitations | Check pump output, pipe sizing, and number of sprinklers operating per zone |
| Excessive misting/fine droplets carried by wind | Operating pressure too high for the nozzle type, or windy conditions | Reduce pressure to manufacturer specification, switch to larger droplet nozzles, avoid windy periods |
| Prolonged leaf wetness and rising disease pressure | Evening/night irrigation on disease-susceptible crops | Shift timing to allow foliage to dry before nightfall, or switch susceptible crops to drip irrigation |
| Frequent nozzle clogging | Poor water quality (sediment, algae, mineral content) | Improve filtration, test and manage water quality (see `irrigation/irrigation_water_quality.md`) |
| Pooling or runoff in parts of the field | Application rate exceeds soil infiltration rate | Reduce application rate per set, split into shorter cycles, or improve soil structure/organic matter |

## Common Mistakes
- Applying water at a rate that exceeds soil infiltration capacity, causing runoff and uneven distribution rather than deep, even soil wetting.
- Irrigating overhead late in the day on disease-susceptible crops, extending leaf wetness overnight and raising fungal disease risk.
- Ignoring wind conditions, leading to poor distribution uniformity and unnecessary evaporation losses.
- Under-sizing the pump or pipe network relative to the number of sprinklers operating simultaneously, causing weak pressure and poor coverage.
- Neglecting filter maintenance, leading to progressive nozzle clogging and declining application uniformity over a season.
- Choosing a mechanized system scaled for commercial fields when a simpler portable system would better match the farm's size and budget.

## When to Consult an Agricultural Extension Officer or Irrigation Specialist
- Before selecting a sprinkler system type and scale, to match investment level, labour availability, and field size and terrain to the right option.
- To calculate correct pump, pipe, and nozzle sizing for even pressure and coverage across the field.
- If recurring disease pressure on overhead-irrigated crops persists despite scheduling adjustments, to consider a switch to drip irrigation for the most susceptible crops.
- If water quality issues are causing frequent nozzle clogging, to plan appropriate filtration (see `irrigation/irrigation_water_quality.md`).
- Before investing in mechanized systems (center-pivot, linear-move), to assess whether the scale of operation justifies the cost compared to simpler alternatives.
