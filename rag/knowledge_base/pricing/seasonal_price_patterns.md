# Seasonal Price Patterns

## Overview
Agricultural prices for most crops and many livestock products tend to move in a recurring pattern over the course of a year, rising and falling with the rhythm of planting, growing, and harvesting. This document explains, in general terms, why these seasonal patterns occur and how a farmer can plan around them. It does not state specific prices, price levels, or the exact timing of price movements for any crop, region, or year, since these vary by location, crop, weather, and market conditions, and stating them as fact could mislead farmers relying on outdated or incorrect information (see `ai_guidelines/ai_safety_guidelines.md`). For how markets set prices generally see `marketplace/market_fundamentals.md`; for how buyers' seasonal needs affect demand see `marketplace/buyer_preferences_and_seasonal_demand.md`; and for using timing as part of a broader pricing strategy see `pricing/price_negotiation_and_value_addition.md` and `pricing/price_risk_management.md`.

## Why Agricultural Prices Follow Seasonal Patterns

### Supply Is Concentrated at Harvest
- Most crops are harvested within a defined window rather than continuously throughout the year, meaning a large share of total annual supply reaches the market within a short period.
- When many farmers in a growing region harvest around the same time, the market is flooded with supply, and prices for that crop typically fall to their lowest point of the year during and immediately after the main harvest period.
- As the harvested supply is gradually sold, consumed, or moves into storage, and as time passes since harvest, available supply on the market shrinks, and prices tend to rise through the rest of the season until the next harvest arrives.

### Demand Is Comparatively Stable
- Unlike supply, demand for food staples tends to be relatively stable throughout the year, since people need to eat consistently regardless of the harvest calendar.
- Because supply swings widely while demand does not, the imbalance between the two at different points in the season is what drives the characteristic seasonal price pattern: low prices at peak supply (harvest), rising prices as supply tightens (the lean season), and prices falling again once the next harvest begins.

### Storage and Spoilage Constraints
- Many crops, particularly perishable fruits and vegetables, cannot be stored for long without spoiling, so nearly the entire harvest must be sold within a short window, reinforcing the post-harvest price dip.
- Crops that can be stored well (such as properly dried grains and pulses) allow some of the harvest to be held back and released later in the season, which moderates, but does not eliminate, the seasonal price swing.
- The capacity of farmers, traders, and buyers in a given market to store a crop safely and affordably strongly influences how large the seasonal price swing for that crop tends to be.

### The Lean Season
- The period before the next harvest, when stocks from the previous harvest are running low, is often called the lean season (or hunger season in some staple food contexts). Supply is typically at its tightest and prices at their highest during this period.
- The lean season is also when household food stocks may be depleted, so demand pressure from consumers needing to purchase food (rather than relying on their own stored harvest) can add further upward pressure on prices.

### Weather and Production Risk
- Seasonal patterns are also shaped by the underlying growing season: a delayed start of rains, drought, flooding, or pest outbreaks can shift the timing and size of a harvest, which shifts the timing and depth of the seasonal price pattern for that year.
- Because of this, seasonal price patterns are a general tendency, not a fixed rule that repeats identically every year; the exact timing and magnitude vary from season to season (see `weather/weather_impacts_on_farming.md`).

### Regional and Import/Export Effects
- In regions with more than one growing season per year, or where a crop can be imported from regions with a different harvest calendar, the seasonal price pattern can be less pronounced, since supply from a different source or season can offset a local shortage.
- Where a crop is mostly grown and consumed locally with a single annual harvest and little ability to import from elsewhere, the seasonal price swing tends to be more pronounced.

### Livestock and Seasonal Patterns
- Livestock and livestock products also show seasonal patterns, though driven by somewhat different factors: seasonal feed and pasture availability, breeding cycles, disease risk that varies by season, and demand spikes around cultural, religious, or festival periods.
- For example, feed shortages during a dry season can raise the cost of maintaining livestock and influence the timing of sales, while demand for certain animals or products can rise sharply around specific calendar events.

## How Seasonal Patterns Interact With Other Price Drivers
- Seasonal patterns are one layer on top of the underlying supply and demand fundamentals described in `marketplace/market_fundamentals.md`; a season with an unusually large or small harvest, currency movements (see `pricing/currency_and_inflation_effects_on_farmgate_prices.md`), or a demand shock can make actual prices deviate significantly from the typical seasonal pattern in a given year.
- This means seasonal patterns describe a general tendency that is useful for planning, but should never be treated as a guaranteed or precise prediction of what will happen in any specific season.

## Planning Around Seasonal Price Patterns

### Understand Your Crop's Typical Pattern
- Learn, from local market information services, cooperatives, or extension officers, how pronounced the typical seasonal price swing is for your specific crop and region, since this varies significantly by crop and by how storable, perishable, and locally concentrated its production is.

### Weigh the Trade-Off of Selling at Harvest Versus Storing
- Selling immediately at harvest provides quick cash, avoids storage costs and storage losses, and avoids the risk that prices might not rise as expected, but typically means accepting a price at or near the seasonal low.
- Storing and selling later can capture a higher price if the typical seasonal pattern holds, but requires safe storage facilities, incurs storage costs, carries a risk of storage losses (including pest damage and, for grains, aflatoxin risk; see `storage/aflatoxin_prevention.md` and `storage/grain_storage.md`), and carries the risk that prices do not rise as much as hoped, or even fall, due to factors other than the usual seasonal pattern.
- The right choice depends on the farmer's cash flow needs, access to safe and affordable storage, risk tolerance, and the reliability of current, local market information, not on this knowledge base predicting future prices.

### Stagger Sales Rather Than Selling All at Once
- Where storage allows, selling produce in smaller batches spread across several months, rather than selling the entire harvest at once at the post-harvest low, can average out the price received across the season and reduce the risk of selling everything at the single worst moment.

### Use Collective Storage and Marketing
- Farmer cooperatives can sometimes provide access to storage facilities, aggregation, and collective decision-making about timing that would not be practical for an individual smallholder, improving the ability to benefit from seasonal price patterns (see `pricing/price_negotiation_and_value_addition.md`).

### Plan Cash Flow Around the Pattern
- Because many farmers face their greatest cash needs around planting time, which may not coincide with the best time to sell for price purposes, planning cash flow in advance (through savings, staggered sales, or appropriate credit) can reduce the pressure to sell at the lowest-price point purely out of financial necessity.

### Diversify Timing Across Crops or Enterprises
- Growing or raising more than one product with different harvest and selling calendars can smooth household income across the year and reduce dependence on getting the timing of a single crop's sale right (see `pricing/price_risk_management.md`).

## Common Mistakes
- Assuming the seasonal pattern is identical and precisely repeatable every year, and making major decisions based on that assumption alone without checking current conditions.
- Selling the entire harvest immediately purely out of habit or urgent cash need, without considering whether even partial storage and staggered sale is feasible and worthwhile.
- Holding produce for a hoped-for price rise without accounting for storage costs, storage losses, and the real possibility that prices may not rise as expected.
- Ignoring the difference in seasonal price behaviour between perishable and storable crops, and applying storage-based strategies to crops that cannot be safely stored.
- Relying on this knowledge base, or any static document, for the current stage of the seasonal price cycle; only current local market information can indicate where prices stand right now relative to the pattern.

## When to Consult an Agricultural Extension Officer or Market Information Service
- To learn the typical seasonal price pattern for your specific crop, livestock product, and region, including how pronounced it usually is.
- To obtain current, location-specific price information indicating where prices currently stand in the seasonal cycle.
- For guidance on safe, affordable storage options suited to your crop and resources, if considering storing produce to sell later in the season.
- For support developing a staggered selling plan or exploring collective storage and marketing through a cooperative.
