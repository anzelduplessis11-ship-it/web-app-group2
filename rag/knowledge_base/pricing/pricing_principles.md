# Fair Pricing Principles

## Overview
This document explains the principles behind fair and informed agricultural pricing decisions. It does not provide specific current prices for any crop, region, or market, since prices change constantly and vary by location, season, and buyer. FarmConnect must never invent, estimate, or state specific prices as fact (see `ai_guidelines/ai_safety_guidelines.md`).

## Why Specific Prices Are Not Provided in This Knowledge Base
- Agricultural prices fluctuate daily and vary significantly by country, region, market, and even individual buyer.
- A static reference document cannot reliably reflect current prices, and presenting outdated or incorrect prices as current could cause real financial harm to farmers or buyers relying on the information.
- Farmers and buyers should consult local market information services, agricultural marketing boards, cooperative networks, or trusted local sources for current, location-specific prices.

## Components of a Fair Price

### Cost of Production
- A fair price should, at minimum, cover the direct costs of production: seed/planting material, fertiliser, pest/disease control, labour, land preparation, and harvesting.
- Understanding actual production costs (which vary by farm and management practice) helps a farmer assess whether an offered price is sustainable for continued production.

### Post-Harvest and Transport Costs
- Costs incurred after harvest (drying, storage, transport, grading, packaging) should also be factored into the price a farmer needs to receive to remain viable, particularly when selling through intermediaries.

### Market-Based Price Discovery
- Beyond covering costs, a fair market price reflects genuine supply and demand conditions at the time and place of sale (see `marketplace/market_fundamentals.md`).
- Prices that are artificially suppressed through lack of buyer competition, poor market information access, or exploitative practices can result in farmers receiving less than a fair, competitive market price would provide.

### Quality-Based Price Differentiation
- Fair pricing reflects genuine quality differences (see `marketplace/product_grading_and_quality.md`); higher-grade produce reasonably commands a premium, and lower-grade produce a discount, provided the grading standard applied is transparent and consistently used.

## Principles for Fair Price Negotiation
- Access multiple sources of price information before negotiating, where possible, to understand the reasonable current market range rather than relying on a single buyer's stated price.
- Understand the grading/quality standard being applied by the buyer, to assess whether a price discount for lower grade is reasonable and consistently applied.
- Consider the value of a reliable, ongoing buyer relationship alongside the price of any single transaction, since consistent market access has its own value.
- Where collective bargaining is available (through farmer groups or cooperatives), it can improve negotiating position, particularly for smallholder farmers with otherwise limited individual bargaining power.

## Recognizing Potentially Unfair Pricing Practices
- Prices offered significantly and consistently below multiple independent market information sources, without a clear quality justification, may indicate an unfair pricing practice.
- Lack of transparency about how a price or grade discount was determined can be a signal to seek a second opinion or alternative buyer where feasible.
- Extension officers, agricultural marketing boards, and farmer cooperative networks can often provide guidance on recognizing and responding to unfair pricing practices specific to a region and crop.

## Role of Farmer Cooperatives and Collective Marketing
- Collective marketing through cooperatives can improve access to market information, negotiating power, and sometimes access to buyers not practical to reach individually.
- Cooperative arrangements involve their own terms and potential trade-offs (e.g., delivery requirements, membership fees, shared decision-making) that should be understood before joining.

## When to Consult an Agricultural Extension Officer or Market Information Service
- To obtain current, location-specific price information before selling.
- To understand locally relevant production cost benchmarks for a specific crop.
- If experiencing what appears to be an unfair or exploitative pricing practice, to access guidance or support available through agricultural marketing boards, cooperatives, or relevant authorities.
