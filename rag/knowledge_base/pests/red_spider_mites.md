# Red Spider Mites

## Pest Identification
- Scientific names: several species in the genus *Tetranychus* are important on African crops, including *Tetranychus urticae* (two-spotted spider mite) and *Tetranychus evansi* (tomato red spider mite); despite the common name, these are not insects but mites (Arachnida), related to ticks and spiders.
- Adults are extremely small, less than 1 mm long, oval-bodied, and have four pairs of legs (distinguishing them from insects, which have three pairs); body colour varies from pale green or yellow to orange-red or brown depending on species, host plant, and season, often with darker spots visible on the sides of the body.
- A key diagnostic feature under heavy infestation is fine silk webbing produced on the underside of leaves and between leaves and stems, which gives the pest its "spider" name.
- Because of their tiny size, mites are best observed with a 10x hand lens, or by tapping suspect leaves over a sheet of white paper and watching for tiny specks that begin to move.

## Life Cycle
- Eggs are spherical, translucent to pale, and laid on the underside of leaves, often along the veins where webbing is also concentrated.
- Development passes through an egg, a six-legged larval stage, and two eight-legged nymphal stages (protonymph and deutonymph), each separated by a brief quiescent resting period, before reaching the adult stage.
- Development is extremely fast under hot, dry conditions: egg to adult can be completed in as little as 5-7 days above about 30°C, compared with two to three weeks in cooler conditions.
- A single female can lay 50-100 or more eggs over her lifetime, and because generations overlap and develop so quickly, populations can explode from barely noticeable to severe within one to two weeks.
- Hot, dry, dusty conditions strongly favour outbreaks, and populations often decline sharply once cooler, wetter weather returns or humidity increases.

## Crops Affected
- Red spider mites have an unusually broad host range, including cassava (`crops/cassava.md`), tomato (`crops/tomato.md`), common beans (`crops/common_beans.md`), cotton, maize, papaya, eggplant, cucurbits, and many ornamental plants.
- *Tetranychus evansi* shows a particular preference for solanaceous crops such as tomato and eggplant, while *Tetranychus urticae* is a broad generalist attacking a very wide range of field and horticultural crops.
- Because of this wide host range, mites can persist on weeds and volunteer plants between main crop seasons, providing a continuous source of reinfestation.

## Damage Caused
- Feeding punctures individual plant cells with piercing-sucking mouthparts, removing chlorophyll and cell contents, which produces a fine chlorotic stippling or speckled ("salt and pepper") pattern visible on the upper leaf surface.
- Heavily infested leaves develop a bronzed or yellowed appearance, dry out, curl, and eventually drop prematurely, reducing the plant's photosynthetic capacity.
- Under severe infestation, fine silk webbing covers leaves, shoot tips, and sometimes flowers or young fruit, further reducing plant vigour and marketability of affected produce.
- Damage is most severe and rapid during hot, dry periods, and along dusty field edges or roadsides where mite dispersal by wind and dust is highest.
- Yield losses can be substantial on susceptible crops when infestations are left unmanaged, particularly during prolonged dry spells when host plants are already under moisture stress.

## Monitoring Methods
- Examine the underside of leaves with a hand lens on a regular basis, especially during hot, dry weather when populations can build up quickly, starting scouting near field edges and dusty access roads where mites often appear first.
- Use the tap test: hold a leaf over a sheet of white paper or a clipboard and tap it firmly, then look for tiny specks that begin moving across the paper.
- Look for early stippling symptoms on older, lower leaves and for fine webbing on new growth and shoot tips, which indicates a more advanced infestation.
- Monitor soon after any broad-spectrum insecticide application, since removal of natural enemies can trigger a rapid mite population increase (resurgence) even where mites were not previously a problem.

## Prevention
- Avoid drought stress by maintaining adequate and consistent irrigation, since water-stressed plants are markedly more attractive and susceptible to mite buildup.
- Avoid excessive nitrogen fertilisation, which promotes soft, lush growth that favours rapid mite reproduction.
- Reduce dust in and around fields, for example by damping down dusty access roads during dry periods, since dusty conditions are strongly associated with mite outbreaks.
- Avoid unnecessary broad-spectrum insecticide applications, which kill the natural predators that normally keep mite populations in check and can directly trigger outbreaks (a phenomenon known as pesticide-induced resurgence).
- Remove and destroy heavily infested plant debris and manage weeds that can host mites between cropping cycles.
- Overhead water sprays, used judiciously in some vegetable systems, can physically dislodge mites and disrupt webbing, reducing populations without chemical input.

## Integrated Pest Management
- Because chemical insecticides frequently make mite problems worse by killing natural enemies, IPM for red spider mites places unusually strong emphasis on cultural practices (irrigation management, dust control, sanitation) and conservation of predatory mites and insects.
- Reserve any acaricide (mite-specific pesticide) use for situations where populations clearly exceed a locally recommended threshold, rather than routine or preventive spraying.
- Rotate among different acaricide chemical classes if repeated treatment is required, since mites develop resistance rapidly due to their short generation time and high reproductive rate.
- Encourage a diverse field environment (hedgerows, flowering border plants, minimal insecticide disturbance) that supports populations of predatory mites and insects year-round.

## Biological Control
- Predatory mites, including *Phytoseiulus persimilis* and various *Amblyseius* (*Neoseiulus*) species, are highly effective natural enemies that feed specifically on spider mite eggs, nymphs, and adults, and are used both as naturally occurring biological control agents and, in some systems, as commercially reared releases.
- Predatory insects such as ladybird beetles in the genus *Stethorus*, minute pirate bugs, predatory thrips, and lacewing larvae also feed on spider mites and contribute to natural suppression.
- These natural enemies are highly sensitive to broad-spectrum insecticides; conserving them by minimizing unnecessary spraying is often the single most effective long-term mite management strategy available to smallholder farmers.

## Chemical Control
- Use products specifically registered as acaricides (miticides) rather than general insecticides, since many insecticides have little or no effect on mites and their use can worsen outbreaks by killing natural enemies without controlling the mites themselves.
- Avoid pyrethroid insecticides in particular, as this chemical class is well documented to "flare" (worsen) spider mite populations even when applied to control a different, unrelated pest.
- Mites develop resistance to acaricides very quickly due to their short generation time and large population sizes; rotate between different chemical classes (modes of action) and avoid repeated consecutive use of the same product.
- Confirm currently approved and locally registered acaricide active ingredients with a national agricultural regulatory authority or extension officer, as registered products and resistance management recommendations vary by country and change over time.
- Ensure thorough spray coverage of the leaf underside, where mites and their eggs are concentrated, since poor coverage is a common cause of unsatisfactory control.

## Safety Considerations
- Always follow label instructions for any acaricide or insecticide product, including dosage, application method, and pre-harvest interval.
- Use appropriate personal protective equipment (gloves, mask, long sleeves) when handling and applying any chemical control product.
- Avoid spraying flowering crops during pollinator activity hours to protect bees and other beneficial insects.
- Avoid spraying during windy conditions to prevent chemical drift onto non-target areas, water sources, or neighbouring fields.
- Store all pesticide products securely away from children, food, and water sources, and dispose of empty containers as directed on the label.
- If mite populations persist or worsen despite treatment, consult an agricultural extension officer, since this often indicates resistance or destruction of natural enemies rather than a need for stronger or more frequent chemical applications.
