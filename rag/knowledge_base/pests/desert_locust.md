# Desert Locust

## Overview
The desert locust is one of the most destructive migratory pests in the world and a recurring threat to food security across the Sahel, the Horn of Africa, North Africa, and parts of East Africa. Under favourable conditions it can transform from scattered, harmless solitary grasshoppers into dense, fast-moving swarms containing tens of millions of insects per square kilometre. A single swarm can travel more than 100 kilometres in a day on the wind and can strip a field of every green leaf within hours. Because a small swarm eats roughly the same amount of food in a day as tens of thousands of people, an uncontrolled upsurge can devastate cereal, pasture, and vegetable production over entire regions.

For African smallholders, the desert locust is unusual among crop pests because effective control depends heavily on regional and national early-warning systems rather than on the actions of an individual farmer. Locusts breed in remote desert and semi-arid areas, then migrate long distances into farmland. This document explains how to recognise the pest, understand its behaviour, monitor for it, protect crops, and act responsibly within a coordinated response. The central message is that desert locust management is a collective, government-led effort: individual farmers play a vital role in scouting and reporting, but large-scale control is carried out by national plant protection organisations and regional bodies such as the Desert Locust Control Organization for Eastern Africa (DLCO-EA) and the FAO Desert Locust Information Service.

## Pest Identification
The desert locust is a large grasshopper. Correct identification matters because many harmless grasshoppers and non-swarming locusts look broadly similar, and unnecessary panic or spraying wastes scarce resources. The most important identification feature is not the individual insect but the behaviour and density of the population.

**Adults.** Adults are 4.5 to 6 centimetres long, with females larger than males. They have long hind legs adapted for jumping, two pairs of wings (the hind wings are transparent, the front wings are narrow and speckled), and prominent compound eyes. Colour depends dramatically on phase and maturity: immature solitary adults are pale grey to brown and blend into the landscape; immature gregarious adults are bright pink; mature gregarious adults turn bright yellow. This colour change from pink to yellow is a reliable indicator of gregarious, swarming behaviour and imminent egg-laying.

**Nymphs (hoppers).** The wingless immature stages are called hoppers. Solitary hoppers are green or sandy-coloured and blend into vegetation. Gregarious hoppers are boldly patterned in black and yellow (or black and orange) and gather in dense groups called "hopper bands" that march across the ground together, sometimes covering many hectares.

**Phase change.** The single most important concept is that the desert locust exists in two phases. In the **solitary phase**, insects are cryptic, low in number, and avoid one another. When rainfall produces abundant vegetation and populations become crowded, they shift into the **gregarious phase**: they change colour, become more active, and aggregate. Crowding of hoppers triggers this transformation within a single generation. Recognising the transition from scattered solitary insects to gregarious bands and swarms is the key early-warning signal.

## Scientific Name
*Schistocerca gregaria* (Forskål). It belongs to the order Orthoptera, family Acrididae (short-horned grasshoppers). The species name *gregaria* refers directly to its gregarious, swarm-forming behaviour. It should not be confused with the African migratory locust (*Locusta migratoria*), the red locust (*Nomadacris septemfasciata*), or the many species of non-swarming grasshoppers, though management principles overlap.

## Life Cycle
The desert locust develops through three stages: egg, hopper (nymph), and adult. It does not have a pupal stage (incomplete metamorphosis).

- **Egg stage.** Mature females lay eggs in moist, sandy or soft soil, drilling the tip of the abdomen down 5 to 10 centimetres and depositing an egg pod containing roughly 60 to 100 eggs. A single female may lay several pods over her life. Eggs absorb moisture from the surrounding soil and hatch in about 10 to 14 days in warm, moist conditions, though this can extend to several weeks when it is cooler.
- **Hopper stage.** Newly hatched hoppers pass through five instars (growth stages), moulting between each. This stage lasts approximately 30 to 40 days depending on temperature. Gregarious hoppers form marching bands that can cover long distances on foot before they can fly.
- **Adult stage.** After the final moult, adults are soft and pale ("fledglings") and cannot yet fly well. Over the following one to several weeks they harden and mature. Mature adults form flying swarms. Adults may live for two to five months.

Under ideal conditions of warmth and repeated rainfall, a full generation is completed in about two to three months, and populations can multiply roughly ten- to twentyfold with each successive generation. This explosive reproductive potential is why an early localised outbreak can escalate into a regional upsurge or a continental plague within a few generations if not detected and controlled early.

## Crops Affected
The desert locust is highly polyphagous and will eat almost any green vegetation. It is not restricted to a single crop family. Crops at high risk include:

- Cereals: maize, sorghum, pearl millet, rice, wheat, and barley (see `crops/maize.md`, `crops/sorghum.md`, and `crops/pearl_millet.md`).
- Legumes: cowpea, common beans, groundnut (see `crops/cowpea.md`, `crops/common_beans.md`, and `crops/groundnut.md`).
- Vegetables: tomato, cabbage, onion, okra, and leafy greens (see `crops/tomato.md`, `crops/cabbage.md`).
- Root and tuber crops: cassava and sweet potato foliage.
- Fruit trees, bananas and plantains, sugarcane, and pasture and rangeland grasses on which livestock depend.

Because locusts also destroy grazing land, an outbreak threatens both crop farmers and pastoralists, compounding food insecurity.

## Damage Caused
Damage is caused by direct feeding of both hoppers and adults. Locusts chew leaves, stems, flowers, growing points, developing grain heads, and bark of young shoots. Key features of the damage:

- **Speed and completeness.** Unlike most pests that cause gradual damage, a locust swarm can defoliate a field almost completely in a matter of hours, leaving only bare stems.
- **Whole-plant loss.** Because they eat growing points and reproductive structures, even plants that are not completely stripped may fail to recover or produce grain.
- **Landscape scale.** Damage is not confined to one field; swarms move across whole districts, so neighbouring cooperation and regional response are essential.
- **Secondary losses.** Loss of pasture forces livestock movement and can trigger conflict over remaining resources; loss of seed crops undermines the following season's planting.

A distinguishing sign of locust feeding, compared with caterpillar pests, is the sudden arrival of large numbers of jumping or flying insects, the audible sound of a settling swarm, and dense faecal pellets on the ground beneath feeding sites.

## Monitoring Methods
Monitoring for desert locust is fundamentally about **early detection and rapid reporting**. Individual smallholders are the eyes on the ground that feed national and regional surveillance.

- **Watch the weather and vegetation.** Locusts breed after unusual rains in normally dry areas. Following good rains in semi-arid zones, watch for fresh green flushes of vegetation where locusts may congregate to breed.
- **Look for hoppers and colour change.** Scout uncultivated land, field margins, and recently rained areas for grasshoppers that are grouping together, showing black-and-yellow patterning, or marching in bands. Note any pink or yellow adults, as these colours indicate gregarious insects.
- **Record and report immediately.** Note the date, exact location (a GPS point or clear description), estimated area covered, density (scattered, grouped, or swarming), stage (hoppers or adults), and direction of movement. Report to the local agricultural extension officer or national plant protection organisation without delay. Many countries and the FAO support mobile reporting tools (such as eLocust systems) used by trained scouts.
- **Follow official bulletins.** Regional and national desert locust bulletins forecast swarm movement. Farmers and cooperatives should follow these forecasts to prepare in advance.

There is no meaningful "economic threshold" for an individual farmer as with other pests: any confirmed gregarious hopper band or swarm is a regional emergency that should be reported and managed by the responsible authorities.

## Prevention
True prevention operates at the regional scale through preventive control of breeding populations in remote areas before swarms form. At the farm and community level, preparedness measures include:

- **Community awareness and reporting networks.** Establish and maintain contact with extension services so that sightings are reported quickly and forecasts are received.
- **Preparedness planning.** Know in advance who to contact, and cooperate with neighbouring farmers and cooperatives, because fragmented responses are ineffective against a mobile pest.
- **Crop timing and diversification.** Where forecasts warn of incoming swarms, spreading planting dates and growing a diversity of crops can reduce the risk of total loss, though this offers only partial protection against a large swarm.
- **Protecting seed and stored produce.** Safeguard seed stocks and harvested grain so that even if a standing crop is lost, the household retains planting material and food reserves (see `storage/` guidance).

## Integrated Pest Management
Integrated pest management (IPM) for the desert locust differs from IPM for most farm pests because control at swarm scale is beyond the capacity of any single household and is coordinated by governments and regional organisations. The integrated approach combines:

- **Surveillance and forecasting** as the foundation, so that breeding areas are identified early.
- **Preventive control** of hopper bands and immature populations in breeding grounds, which is far more efficient and uses far less pesticide than fighting mature swarms over farmland.
- **Mechanical and physical measures** at the local level for small, isolated hopper bands, such as digging trenches to trap marching hoppers, beating and crushing, and making noise, smoke, or dust to deter settling swarms from a specific field. These methods are labour-intensive and effective only against limited local infestations, not against large swarms.
- **Targeted control** using biopesticides or, where authorised, conventional insecticides applied by trained teams, prioritising hopper bands before they fledge into flying swarms.
- **Protecting food and livelihoods** by safeguarding seed, stored grain, and pasture.

The guiding principle is that early action against hoppers in breeding areas is dramatically more effective and safer than late action against swarms over cropland.

## Biological Control
Biological control of the desert locust centres on a biopesticide based on the naturally occurring fungus *Metarhizium acridum* (a strain of *Metarhizium anisopliae* selected specifically for locusts and grasshoppers). This entomopathogenic fungus infects and kills locusts and grasshoppers without harming people, livestock, bees, or other beneficial insects, which makes it well suited to use in environmentally sensitive areas, near water, and in organic systems. Its main limitation is that it acts more slowly than chemical insecticides, killing over several days to two weeks, so it is best deployed preventively against hopper bands rather than as an emergency knockdown of an advancing swarm.

Natural enemies also help suppress solitary and low-density populations. Birds (including storks, larks, and various raptors), reptiles, predatory beetles, robber flies, parasitic wasps and flies, and parasitic nematodes all feed on locusts or their eggs. During upsurges, however, locusts reproduce far faster than natural enemies can respond, so these predators cannot prevent a plague on their own. Conserving natural enemies by minimising unnecessary broad-spectrum spraying supports background suppression between outbreaks.

## Chemical Control
Large-scale chemical control of swarms is carried out by government and regional locust-control teams using vehicle-mounted or aerial ultra-low-volume (ULV) sprayers, not by individual farmers using knapsack sprayers. Smallholders should not attempt to treat a swarm on their own; the correct action is to report and to cooperate with official control operations.

Where chemical intervention is authorised, control programmes rely on fast-acting insecticide classes. Historically these have included organophosphates and pyrethroids; insect growth regulators (such as benzoylurea chitin-synthesis inhibitors) are used against hopper bands because they prevent moulting. The specific active ingredients, formulations, and application methods used in any campaign are decided by national plant protection authorities based on efficacy, environmental safety, and current registrations.

Farmers must never source, mix, or apply locust insecticides informally, and must always confirm any locally registered, approved products and correct dosages with a qualified agricultural extension officer or the national plant protection organisation. Chemical control is a coordinated last-resort tool deployed under expert supervision, with buffer zones around water bodies, human settlements, livestock, and beekeeping areas, and with attention to residues on crops and pasture. See `regulations/pesticide_safety_and_handling.md` for general pesticide safety principles.

## Safety Considerations
- Do not attempt to control a swarm with farm pesticides or by spraying products not registered for locust control; this is ineffective, dangerous, and may leave harmful residues on food and pasture.
- If official control teams treat an area near your farm, follow all instructions on withholding periods before harvesting crops or grazing livestock on treated vegetation.
- Do not collect and eat locusts that may have been sprayed with insecticide. While locusts are traditionally eaten in some regions, insects from a treated area may carry harmful residues.
- Keep children and livestock away from areas being treated by control teams during and immediately after spraying.
- Wear appropriate protective equipment if you assist trained teams with local mechanical control, and wash hands and clothing afterwards.
- Report large or unusual infestations promptly to agricultural authorities so that a coordinated regional response can protect the wider community.
- Follow all guidance in `regulations/pesticide_safety_and_handling.md` and defer to national and regional locust authorities for current advice.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: How can I tell a desert locust swarm apart from ordinary grasshoppers in my field?**
A: Ordinary grasshoppers are usually few, scattered, and green or brown, and they stay in one general area. Desert locusts in the dangerous gregarious phase gather in huge numbers, show bold black-and-yellow patterning as hoppers or bright pink to yellow colour as adults, march or fly together, and arrive suddenly in dense groups. If you see large numbers of grouping, brightly coloured hoppers marching in bands, treat it as a possible locust outbreak and report it immediately.

**Q: What should I do the moment I see a hopper band or swarm?**
A: Report it straight away to your local agricultural extension officer or national plant protection organisation. Record the date, exact location, the area covered, whether they are hoppers or flying adults, and the direction they are moving. Early reporting lets control teams act while the infestation is still small.

**Q: Can I just spray them with the insecticide I use for caterpillars or aphids?**
A: No. Individual farmers cannot effectively spray a swarm with a knapsack sprayer, and using unregistered or wrong products wastes money, endangers your health, harms beneficial insects, and may leave dangerous residues on food and grazing land. Large-scale control is done by trained government and regional teams. Confirm all products and any local action with a qualified extension officer.

**Q: Are there safe, non-chemical options?**
A: Yes. A biopesticide based on the fungus *Metarhizium acridum* is effective against locusts and grasshoppers and is safe for people, livestock, and bees. It works best preventively against hopper bands. For small local bands, mechanical methods such as trench trapping, beating, and creating smoke or noise can help protect a specific field, but they do not stop large swarms.

**Q: Is it safe to eat locusts I catch during a swarm?**
A: Locusts are a traditional protein source in some communities, but you must never eat locusts collected from an area that has been treated with insecticide, as they may carry harmful chemical residues. If in doubt, do not eat them.

**Q: Why does the government control locusts far away in the desert instead of only on farms?**
A: Locusts breed in remote semi-arid areas after rains and then migrate into farmland. Killing hopper bands early in the breeding grounds is far cheaper, safer, and more effective than trying to fight enormous flying swarms once they reach crops. This is why regional surveillance and preventive control are the backbone of desert locust management.

**Q: Will my crop recover after a swarm passes?**
A: It depends on the growth stage and how much was eaten. Locusts eat growing points and grain heads, so heavily stripped plants often cannot recover or set grain. Protect your seed and stored food so you can replant, spread your planting dates where forecasts allow, and consult your extension officer about the best recovery options for your crop and season.
