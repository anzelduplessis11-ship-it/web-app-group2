# Fruit Flies

## Pest Identification
- Common name "fruit flies" in African agriculture generally refers to tephritid fruit flies, including the Mediterranean fruit fly (*Ceratitis capitata*), the marula/mango fruit fly (*Ceratitis cosyra*), both native to Africa, and the Oriental fruit fly (*Bactrocera dorsalis*), an invasive species first detected in East and West Africa in the early 2000s that has since become a dominant and highly damaging pest of mango and many other fruits across the continent.
- Adults are 5-8 mm long with patterned wings, and body colouring that varies by species from yellowish-brown to darker tones, often marked with yellow bands or spots; *Bactrocera dorsalis* typically shows a banded abdominal pattern, while *Ceratitis* species often show a dark band across the middle of the wing.
- Females have a pointed, needle-like ovipositor at the tip of the abdomen used to pierce fruit skin and insert eggs beneath the surface.
- Larvae (maggots) are legless, cream-white, and tapered, found feeding inside fruit flesh rather than on the surface, which is why infestation is frequently not visible from the outside until fruit begins to rot.

## Life Cycle
- Females puncture the skin of ripening or nearly ripe fruit and lay clusters of eggs just beneath the surface; eggs hatch within 1-3 days.
- Larvae feed inside the fruit flesh for roughly 6-14 days, causing internal breakdown and rot as they tunnel and feed.
- Mature larvae exit the fruit (often after it has fallen to the ground) and burrow into the soil to pupate, remaining as pupae for approximately 1-3 weeks depending on temperature and species.
- Adults emerge from the soil, feed on plant sugars and microbial films on foliage, and can live for several weeks to a few months; females begin laying eggs within days of emergence and mating.
- A complete generation takes roughly three to five weeks under warm conditions, and because generations overlap continuously, populations can persist and reproduce year-round in favourable climates.

## Crops Affected
- Fruit flies attack a very broad range of hosts, including mango (`crops/mango.md`), citrus (`crops/citrus_orange.md`), guava, papaya (`crops/papaya_pawpaw.md`), avocado (`crops/avocado.md`), and many other fruit species.
- Certain species also attack fruiting vegetables such as chilli pepper (`crops/chilli_pepper.md`) and tomato (`crops/tomato.md`).
- Because of this wide host range, wild and abandoned host trees near orchards and fields can sustain populations even when a cultivated crop is not currently fruiting.

## Damage Caused
- Oviposition punctures create wounds in the fruit skin that serve as entry points for decay-causing fungi and bacteria, accelerating spoilage.
- Internal larval feeding causes breakdown of fruit flesh, premature ripening, and early fruit drop, directly reducing marketable yield.
- Infested fruit is generally rendered inedible and unmarketable, representing a direct and often severe economic loss, particularly on high-value fruit crops.
- Beyond direct crop loss, the presence of quarantine-significant fruit fly species (particularly invasive species like *Bactrocera dorsalis*) triggers import restrictions and quarantine measures in many export markets, which can block or complicate access to lucrative domestic and international markets even where actual field infestation is moderate.
- The economic impact of fruit flies is therefore often magnified well beyond the direct value of damaged fruit due to these market access consequences.

## Monitoring Methods
- Deploy species-specific parapheromone traps combined with a killing agent — for example, traps using methyl eugenol as an attractant for male *Bactrocera dorsalis*, or trimedlure for male *Ceratitis capitata* — hung within the orchard canopy and checked and serviced on a regular schedule (commonly weekly) to track population trends and detect the onset of activity.
- Periodically cut open a representative sample of ripening fruit from the orchard or field to check directly for the presence of larvae, since trap catches alone do not confirm actual fruit infestation levels.
- Monitor fruit drop beneath trees, since an unusually high rate of premature fruit fall can indicate an active infestation.

## Prevention
- Practice thorough field sanitation by collecting all fallen and visibly infested fruit on a daily or near-daily basis throughout the fruiting season, since unmanaged fallen fruit allows larvae to complete development and pupate, sustaining the population.
- Destroy collected fruit by deep burial (at least 50 cm to prevent emerging adults from reaching the surface), sealed-bag solarization, or burning, rather than simply discarding it on the surface nearby.
- Harvest fruit promptly at physiological maturity rather than allowing it to over-ripen on the tree, since over-ripe fruit is especially attractive to egg-laying females.
- Bag individual fruits or fruit clusters with paper, cloth, or fine mesh bags where practical, physically excluding females from reaching the fruit surface to lay eggs.
- Remove or manage wild, abandoned, or unmanaged host trees near the orchard that can serve as a continuous breeding reservoir.

## Integrated Pest Management
- Combine the male annihilation technique (using attractant-and-kill stations to remove male flies and disrupt mating), protein or food-lure bait sprays applied as targeted spot treatments, thorough field sanitation, and fruit bagging as the core components of an integrated programme, since no single tactic reliably controls fruit flies alone.
- Area-wide, coordinated management across neighbouring farms and orchards substantially improves outcomes, since fruit flies are highly mobile and readily move between properties; community-level coordination on trapping and sanitation timing is strongly encouraged.
- Reserve full-coverage insecticide spraying for severe outbreaks, since bait-based and trapping approaches are generally more selective and better integrated with biological control and pollinator conservation.

## Biological Control
- Parasitoid wasps, including *Fopius arisanus* and other braconid species, attack fruit fly eggs and larvae and have been used in both classical and augmentative biological control programmes against tephritid fruit flies.
- Predatory ants and generalist ground-dwelling predators consume fruit fly larvae and pupae in and on the soil beneath infested trees.
- Entomopathogenic fungi can infect adult flies and pupae under conditions of adequate humidity, contributing to natural population suppression.
- Conserve these natural enemies by favouring targeted bait applications over broad-spectrum cover sprays wherever possible.

## Chemical Control
- Bait-based approaches — a protein or food-lure attractant combined with a small amount of a registered insecticide, applied as spot treatments to foliage rather than as a full cover spray — are generally preferred within IPM, since they attract and kill fruit flies while minimizing harm to natural enemies, pollinators, and the wider environment.
- Full-coverage cover sprays should be reserved for severe outbreaks where bait stations and sanitation are insufficient, since broad-spectrum spraying has a greater impact on beneficial insects and pollinators.
- Confirm currently approved and locally registered insecticide active ingredients and bait formulations with a national agricultural regulatory authority or extension officer, as registered products vary by country and change over time.
- Rotate insecticide modes of action (chemical classes) where repeated treatment is necessary, to reduce the risk of resistance development.

## Safety Considerations
- Strict adherence to pre-harvest intervals is critical, since affected fruit crops are typically eaten fresh with minimal processing.
- Use appropriate personal protective equipment (gloves, mask, long sleeves) when preparing and applying any chemical bait or cover spray.
- Avoid spraying during periods of pollinator activity and avoid direct application to flowering parts of the plant.
- Position bait stations and traps out of reach of children and livestock, and store all chemical products securely away from food and water sources.
- Avoid spraying during windy conditions to prevent drift onto neighbouring fields, water sources, or non-target areas.
- Dispose of empty containers and used bait station materials as directed on the product label, and consult an agricultural extension officer if infestation persists despite an integrated control programme, since this may indicate insufficient area-wide coordination or the need for revised sanitation practices.
