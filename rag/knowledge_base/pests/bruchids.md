# Bruchids (Pulse Beetles)

## Overview
Bruchids, also called pulse beetles, cowpea weevils, or bean weevils, are the most important storage pests of grain legumes (pulses) across Africa. They attack cowpea, common beans, pigeon pea, green gram, bambara groundnut, and other pulses, and are capable of destroying a farmer's entire stored harvest within a few months. Because the larvae develop hidden inside individual seeds, infestations that begin almost invisibly at harvest can build into total losses by the time seed is needed for planting or sale in the dry season.

Pulses are a critical source of protein and cash income for African smallholders, and storage losses to bruchids directly threaten household nutrition and seed security. The good news is that bruchids are among the storage pests most effectively controlled by low-cost, non-chemical methods, especially airtight (hermetic) storage, which has been widely and successfully promoted for cowpea in West Africa. This document should be read together with `storage/grain_storage.md` and the crop documents `crops/cowpea.md` and `crops/common_beans.md`.

## Pest Identification
- Adults are small, oval, compact beetles about 2-3.5 mm long, brownish or greyish, often with a mottled or banded pattern on the wing cases.
- In the common cowpea species, the tip of the abdomen (pygidium) is left exposed beyond the wing cases, and there are often two dark spots on the wing cases.
- The antennae are comb-like or saw-toothed rather than clubbed, and the legs are relatively long.
- Larvae are small, white, C-shaped, legless grubs that live entirely inside a single seed and are not normally seen.
- The clearest sign of infestation is round exit holes in the seeds, often with a small circular "window" or flap, together with adult beetles crawling among the pulses and fine powder.
- Heavily infested seed may feel light and hollow, and large numbers of adults can appear seemingly overnight because development is hidden inside the seed.

## Scientific Name
The main pests are *Callosobruchus maculatus* (cowpea seed beetle) and *Callosobruchus chinensis* on cowpea and other pulses, and *Acanthoscelides obtectus* on common beans. They belong to the family Chrysomelidae, subfamily Bruchinae (formerly treated as the family Bruchidae).

## Life Cycle
- Females lay eggs glued to the surface of pods in the field or directly onto seeds in storage; a single female may lay several dozen eggs.
- Each larva bores directly into a seed and completes its entire development, feeding hidden inside that one seed.
- Pupation occurs inside the seed, and the new adult chews a round exit hole to emerge, leaving the characteristic window or flap.
- Under warm conditions (around 30 degrees Celsius) the complete cycle from egg to adult takes only about 3-4 weeks, so populations multiply extremely rapidly with many overlapping generations.
- Adults are short-lived and do not need to feed or drink; their energy comes from reserves built up as larvae, so they immediately mate and lay eggs.
- The cowpea species can produce two forms: a normal, largely non-flying form under low crowding, and an active flying form under crowded conditions, which helps the pest disperse to new stores and fields.

## Crops Affected
- Cowpea is the most seriously affected crop and can be completely destroyed in storage (see `crops/cowpea.md`).
- Common beans are heavily attacked, mainly by the bean bruchid (see `crops/common_beans.md`).
- Other affected pulses include pigeon pea, green gram (mung bean), black gram, bambara groundnut, lentils, chickpea, and soybean to a lesser extent.
- Bruchids do not develop in true groundnuts (which are stored in shell differently), but grain legume stores are often managed alongside groundnut, so see `crops/groundnut.md` for related storage practices.

## Damage Caused
- Larvae hollow out the inside of seeds, producing round exit holes, powder, and lightweight, damaged grain.
- Seed weight and food value fall sharply, and heavily infested pulses become unfit for consumption and unmarketable.
- Germination of seed intended for planting is drastically reduced or destroyed, threatening the next season's crop.
- Because generations are rapid and hidden, an apparently sound harvest can be reduced to a mass of holed, powdery seed within a few months of storage.
- Damaged seed also becomes more prone to mould and to secondary pest attack.

## Monitoring Methods
- Inspect stored pulses regularly, at least every one to two weeks, taking samples from different parts of the store or bag.
- Look for round exit holes, windows or flaps on seeds, live adults, and fine powder; even a few adults indicate that hidden development is under way.
- Because infestation often starts in the field, examine drying pods and freshly harvested seed for eggs and early damage.
- Separate and closely watch any seed showing early signs, and keep records of when and where infestations appear to plan timely storage measures.
- Act at the very first sign, since delay of even a few weeks allows populations to multiply many times over.

## Prevention
- Harvest pulses promptly when mature and dry them thoroughly, because well-dried seed stored quickly is less exposed to field infestation.
- Thresh and clean seed, removing damaged and already-infested grain before storage, and store only clean, sound, well-dried seed.
- Clean storage containers and stores thoroughly before use, removing old, infested residues that carry the pest over.
- Store pulses in airtight (hermetic) containers or triple-layer hermetic bags; airtight cowpea storage is a well-established, highly effective smallholder method that kills bruchids by depriving them of oxygen (see `storage/grain_storage.md`).
- Coating seed with a small amount of edible vegetable oil is a widely used, effective traditional method for small quantities of cowpea, because the oil film prevents eggs from hatching and larvae from entering.
- Solar heating (spreading seed in a thin layer on dark plastic in strong sun, or using a solar heater) can kill eggs and larvae in the seed before airtight storage.

## Integrated Pest Management
- Combine prompt harvest, thorough drying, cleaning, hermetic storage, traditional seed treatments, and regular monitoring, keeping chemical use as a last resort.
- Hermetic storage is the single most reliable tool for smallholders and should be the foundation of any bruchid management plan for pulses (see `storage/grain_storage.md`).
- Oil coating, ash admixture, and botanical materials (such as neem, *Tephrosia*, or aromatic leaves) are useful supplementary measures for small quantities, though their effectiveness varies and they work best combined with airtight storage.
- Inert dusts such as food-grade diatomaceous earth, at labelled rates, can protect stored pulses physically.
- Because pulses are food and seed, prioritise physical and biological methods and strictly limit any chemical use to products approved for stored pulses.

## Biological Control
- Several natural enemies attack bruchids, including parasitic wasps such as *Dinarmus basalis* and *Anisopteromalus calandrae*, which parasitise the larvae inside seeds, and egg parasitoids such as *Uscana* species.
- These natural enemies can reduce populations in traditional stores but rarely give complete control on their own, so they are best regarded as supplementary to good hygiene and airtight storage.
- Conserve natural enemies by avoiding unnecessary broad-spectrum chemical use in and around stores.
- Maintaining clean, dry, sealed storage conditions is the most reliable way to prevent bruchid populations from building up faster than any natural regulation can contain them.

## Chemical Control
- Only insecticides specifically registered and labelled for treatment of stored pulses intended for human consumption may be applied to pulses; field insecticides must never be used on stored food.
- Where a grain protectant is justified for larger quantities, approved admixture dusts registered for stored pulses may be used strictly at labelled rates, and phosphine fumigation of larger stocks should be carried out only by trained, qualified operators.
- Confirm currently approved and locally registered stored-pulse products, permitted uses, and dosages with a qualified agricultural extension officer or national regulatory authority, since registrations vary by country and change over time (see `regulations/pesticide_safety_and_handling.md`).
- Observe the required interval between treatment and consumption, and rotate approved modes of action if repeated treatment is unavoidable, to reduce resistance.
- For most smallholders, hermetic storage and traditional methods make chemical treatment unnecessary for household quantities of pulses.

## Safety Considerations
- Pulses are food and seed: never treat them with any product not specifically registered as safe for stored pulses intended for consumption, and follow label dosage and pre-consumption waiting periods exactly.
- Fumigants are highly toxic and potentially fatal if misused; leave fumigation to trained operators and ensure stores are sealed, marked, and ventilated before re-entry (see `regulations/pesticide_safety_and_handling.md`).
- Wear gloves, a mask, and long sleeves when handling any grain protectant, inert dust, or treated seed, and keep children and animals away from treatment areas.
- Clearly mark and separate any chemically treated seed kept for planting so it is never accidentally eaten or sold as food.
- Store pesticides securely, away from food, water, and children, and dispose of empty containers as directed on the label.
- Consult an agricultural extension officer if infestations persist despite good management, or before any fumigation.

## Frequently Asked Questions
**Q: My cowpea looked clean at harvest but is now full of holes. How did that happen?**
A: Bruchids usually lay eggs on the pods in the field, so the infestation was already present but hidden inside the seeds. The larvae develop unseen, and adults emerge weeks later, producing many holes seemingly at once. Storing clean, dried seed in airtight containers immediately after harvest stops this cycle.

**Q: Does coating seed with cooking oil really protect it?**
A: Yes, for small quantities of cowpea. A light coating of edible vegetable oil (mixed thoroughly so all seeds are covered) prevents bruchid eggs from hatching and larvae from entering. It is a cheap, food-safe method well suited to seed kept for household use or planting.

**Q: What is hermetic storage and why is it so effective for pulses?**
A: Hermetic storage means airtight containers or triple-layer sealed bags. Once sealed, the insects consume the available oxygen and die, and no new ones can enter. For cowpea in particular, airtight storage has been very successful across West Africa and is the most reliable low-cost method (see `storage/grain_storage.md`).

**Q: Can I still plant seed that has bruchid holes?**
A: Heavily holed seed usually will not germinate well because the growing point or food reserves have been eaten. Sort out damaged seed and keep only sound, unholed grain for planting, and store it airtight to protect its germination.

**Q: Can I eat pulses that have some bruchid damage?**
A: Lightly infested pulses can often still be eaten after removing holed seeds, insects, and powder by winnowing and sorting. Heavily damaged, powdery, or mouldy pulses should be discarded, and you should seek advice if you are unsure about quality.

**Q: Will drying alone stop bruchids?**
A: Thorough drying is essential and slows the pest, but drying alone does not stop bruchids, because they thrive in dry seed. Drying must be combined with clean, airtight storage (and, for small lots, oil coating or solar heating) for reliable control.

**Q: Do I need to spray chemicals to protect my pulses?**
A: For household quantities, usually not. Airtight storage, clean sound seed, and traditional treatments such as oil coating control bruchids without chemicals. If a protectant is needed for larger stocks, use only a product registered for stored pulses and confirm it with an extension officer (see `regulations/pesticide_safety_and_handling.md`).
