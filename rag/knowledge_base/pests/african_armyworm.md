# African Armyworm

## Pest Identification
The African armyworm, *Spodoptera exempta*, is a migratory caterpillar pest native to Africa and is a distinct species from the more recently invasive fall armyworm (*Spodoptera frugiperda*); the two should not be confused, as their outbreak patterns, geographic history, and some management details differ. African armyworm is known for sudden, widespread, and highly destructive outbreaks that can appear with little local warning after being carried in on wind systems.

- **Larvae (caterpillars)** show striking variation in colour and behaviour depending on population density, a phenomenon similar in principle to the phase change seen in locusts. At low density ("solitary phase"), larvae are pale green, well camouflaged, feed mainly at night, and hide in soil or leaf litter during the day. At high density ("gregarious phase"), larvae become darker, often nearly black to dark brown or velvety with pale stripes running the length of the body, and feed openly, including in daylight, moving en masse across the ground in large numbers, giving rise to the name "armyworm."
- Mature larvae reach approximately 3.5-4 cm in length.
- **Adults** are medium-sized, mottled greyish-brown moths with a wingspan of roughly 3-4 cm, each forewing typically showing a small, pale, diamond- or dot-shaped mark; they are active at night and are strong fliers capable of long-distance migration on wind systems.
- **Eggs** are laid in clusters on leaves, usually covered with a felted layer of scales from the female's body, giving egg masses a fuzzy appearance.

## Life Cycle
- **Egg stage:** approximately 2-5 days, with a single female capable of laying several hundred eggs in multiple batches.
- **Larval stage:** approximately 14-22 days, passing through six instars; early instars are solitary and cryptic, while later instars in outbreak populations become gregarious, highly mobile, and voracious, consuming the great majority of total food intake in the last two instars before pupation.
- **Pupal stage:** approximately 7-10 days, occurring in the soil.
- **Adult stage:** approximately 7-10 days; adults are strong, long-distance migrants that can be carried hundreds of kilometres by wind systems, particularly along weather fronts associated with the rains, allowing outbreaks to appear suddenly far from the source population.
- A full generation can be completed in roughly three to five weeks under favourable warm, moist conditions, allowing successive generations and waves of outbreaks to move across a region as the rains progress, often tracked moving from one part of eastern or southern Africa to another over a season.

## Crops Affected
- **Primary hosts:** maize, sorghum, and finger and pearl millet.
- **Other cereals:** wheat, rice, and sugarcane.
- **Pasture and rangeland grasses**, which are a major host and the reason armyworm outbreaks also threaten livestock feed supplies, not only cropland.
- Because armyworm is not restricted to a single crop, it can move from grassland and pasture into adjacent cereal fields as local food sources are exhausted.

## Damage Caused
- Outbreak populations of late-instar larvae move and feed as dense, visible "armies," capable of stripping a field of leaves within a single night or over one to two days.
- Young seedlings can be completely destroyed, including the growing point, resulting in total plant loss, while more mature plants may be reduced to bare stems and midribs.
- Damage typically progresses rapidly from a field's edge or from adjacent grassland inward, and neighbouring fields can be affected in quick succession as larvae march or as new moth flights arrive.
- Because larval populations can build up rapidly and largely unnoticed while still small and solitary, farmers are often confronted with severe, visible defoliation only once larvae have reached late, highly destructive instars.
- Pasture and rangeland damage reduces livestock feed availability, compounding the impact of a severe outbreak on rural livelihoods beyond crop loss alone.

## Monitoring Methods
- **Pheromone trapping.** Traps baited with the species-specific sex pheromone are used to catch adult male moths and are the backbone of early warning; several countries in eastern and southern Africa maintain coordinated pheromone trap networks and national armyworm forecasting services that alert farmers and extension officers to rising moth activity.
- **Moth flight monitoring informs scouting timing.** A sharp rise in trap catches signals that egg-laying and subsequent larval hatching are likely in the following one to two weeks, prompting more intensive field scouting.
- **Field scouting for egg masses and young larvae.** Regularly check leaves, particularly of grasses and young cereal plants, for felted egg masses and for small, solitary-phase larvae before they reach damaging, gregarious densities.
- **Watch for frass and early defoliation.** Small, ragged feeding holes and the presence of caterpillar droppings (frass) on leaves or the ground are early indicators of an active infestation.
- **Follow national and regional early-warning bulletins**, where available, which combine trap data and weather information to forecast the likely timing and location of outbreaks.

## Prevention
- **Early and community-synchronised planting** helps crops pass through their most vulnerable early growth stages before typical outbreak periods associated with the rains.
- **Participation in pheromone trap networks**, where available through extension services, gives individual farmers and communities advance notice of rising moth populations.
- **Field sanitation and grassland management** around cropland can reduce the availability of continuous host vegetation that sustains armyworm populations between crop seasons.
- **Ploughing fields after an outbreak** can help destroy pupae in the soil, reducing the number of moths emerging to start the next generation locally.
- **Maintaining crop vigour** through balanced soil fertility supports better recovery in plants that suffer partial defoliation.

## Integrated Pest Management
- Combine pheromone-based early warning, regular field scouting, cultural practices (early planting, sanitation, post-outbreak ploughing), conservation of natural enemies, and threshold-based chemical or biological insecticide use rather than relying on any single tactic.
- Because outbreaks develop and spread rapidly, timing is critical: control action taken while larvae are still small and in early instars is substantially more effective and requires less pesticide than action taken once larvae have reached large, highly mobile late instars.
- Community-level coordination, informed by shared trap data and extension bulletins, allows fields to be scouted and treated in a timely, synchronised way across a wider area, reducing the chance that untreated neighbouring fields simply re-seed the outbreak.
- Conserving natural enemies by avoiding unnecessary broad-spectrum spraying supports background suppression of armyworm populations between major outbreak events.

## Biological Control
- **Parasitic wasps and flies**, including various *Cotesia* species and tachinid flies, attack armyworm larvae and can provide significant natural suppression, particularly of low to moderate density populations.
- **Nucleopolyhedrovirus (NPV).** A naturally occurring, species-specific virus of *Spodoptera exempta* causes disease epidemics in dense larval populations and is formulated in some countries as a biopesticide; it is generally most effective against young larvae and works more slowly than chemical insecticides.
- **Predators**, including various birds, ground beetles, and other generalist predators, feed on armyworm eggs and larvae and contribute to natural population regulation, especially between outbreak years.
- Conserving these natural enemies by minimising unnecessary broad-spectrum insecticide applications supports longer-term suppression and can reduce the frequency or severity of outbreaks over time.

## Chemical Control
- Chemical control is most effective and most economical when applied against young, still-solitary larvae before they reach large, highly mobile, and voracious late instars.
- Historically important active ingredient classes used against African armyworm include pyrethroids and organophosphates, alongside microbial biopesticides based on *Bacillus thuringiensis* (Bt), which are effective against young larvae with a lower impact on natural enemies than broad-spectrum chemical products.
- Because armyworm larvae can move rapidly between fields once populations are dense, prompt, threshold-based action guided by scouting and pheromone trap data is more effective than delayed, reactive spraying of a field already showing heavy defoliation.
- Confirm currently approved and locally registered insecticide active ingredients, application rates, and any relevant resistance-management guidance with a national agricultural regulatory authority or extension officer, since registered products and recommendations vary by country and are updated over time.

## Safety Considerations
- Always follow label instructions for any pesticide product, including dosage, application method, and pre-harvest interval, and use appropriate personal protective equipment (gloves, mask, long sleeves) when handling and applying chemical products.
- Avoid spraying during windy conditions to reduce drift onto non-target crops, water sources, or neighbouring fields, and avoid spraying flowering crops during periods of high pollinator activity where alternatives exist.
- Keep children and animals away from treated fields and pasture for the period specified on the product label, and observe any required withholding period before grazing livestock on treated pasture or grassland.
- Store pesticides securely, away from food, water sources, and children, and dispose of empty containers as directed on the label.
- Report severe, unusual, or rapidly spreading outbreaks to local agricultural extension or plant protection authorities promptly, since early reporting supports regional forecasting and coordinated response, which benefits the wider farming community beyond a single field.
- Consult an agricultural extension officer if control appears ineffective, as this may indicate resistance, misapplication, or misidentification of the pest, and should be investigated before repeated or increased chemical use.
