# African Bollworm

## Overview
The African bollworm, also widely known as the cotton bollworm, tomato fruitworm, maize earworm, or simply "the bollworm", is one of the most damaging and economically important crop pests in Africa and worldwide. It is a caterpillar that attacks the most valuable parts of a plant: the flowers, buds, pods, bolls, cobs, and fruits. Because it feeds directly on the harvestable produce and bores inside it, a small number of larvae can cause disproportionately large yield and quality losses. It attacks an exceptionally wide range of crops, breeds rapidly, migrates over long distances, and has developed resistance to several insecticide classes, which together make it a persistent challenge for smallholder farmers.

For African smallholders growing tomato, cotton, cowpea, common beans, sorghum, maize, chickpea, sunflower, and many vegetables, the African bollworm is a pest that requires an integrated, threshold-based approach. Because it hides inside fruits and pods for much of its life, and because it resists many chemicals, relying on routine calendar spraying is both wasteful and counterproductive: it kills the natural enemies that normally keep the pest in check while accelerating resistance. This document sets out how to identify the pest at each stage, scout effectively, and use prevention, biological control, and carefully targeted intervention to protect crops.

## Pest Identification
Accurate identification at each life stage allows farmers to detect the pest before larvae bore into produce, which is the point at which control becomes very difficult.

- **Eggs.** Laid singly, usually on or near flowers, growing points, young fruits, or the underside of tender leaves. Each egg is tiny (about 0.5 millimetres), dome-shaped, and ribbed. When freshly laid it is shiny white to yellowish, then develops a dark ring and turns brown just before hatching.
- **Larvae (caterpillars).** These are the damaging stage. Fully grown larvae reach 3.5 to 4 centimetres. Colour is highly variable, ranging from green and yellow to pink, brown, and almost black, often with darker and lighter lengthwise stripes running along the body and a paler stripe along each side. A useful diagnostic feature is that the body is covered with small, hardened, dark bumps (tubercles) bearing short bristles, giving a slightly rough texture, and the underside is usually pale. Alternating light and dark bands give many individuals a distinctly striped appearance.
- **Pupae.** Reddish-brown, smooth, and 1.5 to 2 centimetres long, formed in an earthen cell in the soil, typically a few centimetres below the surface.
- **Adults (moths).** Stout-bodied moths with a wingspan of about 3.5 to 4 centimetres. Forewings are pale yellowish-brown to olive in females and greenish-grey in males, marked with a faint darker kidney-shaped spot and a row of dark dots along the wing margin. Hind wings are pale cream with a broad dark band along the outer edge. Adults are active at night.

Distinguishing the African bollworm from other caterpillars matters: unlike the fall armyworm (see `pests/fall_armyworm.md`), which typically feeds in the maize whorl and has an inverted "Y" on the head and four dots in a square on the rear segment, the African bollworm targets flowers, pods, and fruits, and its body texture is rough with tubercles rather than smooth.

## Scientific Name
*Helicoverpa armigera* (Hübner). Order Lepidoptera, family Noctuidae (owlet moths). It is closely related to *Helicoverpa zea* (the American bollworm/corn earworm) and to *Helicoverpa punctigera*, and older literature may refer to it as *Heliothis armigera*. The common names cotton bollworm, tomato fruitworm, and maize earworm all refer to this same species attacking different crops.

## Life Cycle
The African bollworm undergoes complete metamorphosis with four stages: egg, larva, pupa, and adult.

- **Egg stage.** 2 to 5 days in warm weather. A single female is highly fecund and can lay several hundred to over a thousand eggs, spread singly across many plants, which is why infestations can build very quickly.
- **Larval stage.** 14 to 24 days, passing through five to six instars. Early instars feed on tender foliage, buds, and flowers; later instars bore into fruits, pods, bolls, and cobs. Larvae are cannibalistic, so usually only one large larva is found per fruit or boll.
- **Pupal stage.** 10 to 15 days in the soil under warm conditions. In cool or dry seasons, pupae can enter a resting state (diapause) and survive for several weeks to months, allowing the pest to bridge unfavourable periods.
- **Adult stage.** Moths live about 1 to 3 weeks. They are strong fliers capable of migrating tens to hundreds of kilometres, which allows sudden reinfestation of an area.

A complete generation takes roughly 4 to 6 weeks in warm conditions, so several overlapping generations occur per season, and populations can escalate rapidly where host crops and warm weather coincide.

## Crops Affected
The African bollworm is extremely polyphagous, recorded on well over one hundred plant species across many families. Priority crops for African smallholders include:

- Vegetables and fruiting crops: tomato, okra, and pepper (see `crops/tomato.md` and `crops/okra.md`).
- Legumes: cowpea, common beans, chickpea, pigeon pea, and groundnut (see `crops/cowpea.md`, `crops/common_beans.md`, and `crops/groundnut.md`).
- Cereals: maize (attacking cobs), sorghum (attacking grain heads) (see `crops/maize.md` and `crops/sorghum.md`).
- Fibre and oilseed crops: cotton (attacking bolls) and sunflower.

The pest tends to move between crops as they flower and fruit, so mixed cropping landscapes can sustain it throughout much of the year.

## Damage Caused
Damage is caused entirely by the larvae, and its economic importance comes from the fact that they attack the reproductive and harvestable structures.

- **On tomato and other fruiting vegetables:** larvae bore round holes into fruits, feeding inside and leaving entry holes and frass. Damaged fruits rot, are invaded by secondary fungi and bacteria, and are unmarketable. Bud and flower feeding causes them to drop.
- **On legumes such as cowpea and beans:** larvae feed on flowers and bore into pods, eating the developing seeds. A single larva can move between several pods.
- **On maize:** larvae feed on silks and then on kernels at the tip of the cob, opening the way for grain moulds.
- **On sorghum:** larvae feed within the developing grain head.
- **On cotton:** larvae bore into flower buds (squares) and bolls, causing them to shed or rot, directly reducing lint yield.

Because much of the feeding happens inside the produce, the damage is often hidden until harvest, and the entry wounds promote rots and aflatoxin-producing moulds that further reduce quality and safety.

## Monitoring Methods
Effective monitoring focuses on detecting eggs and young larvae before they bore into produce.

- **Regular scouting.** Inspect the crop at least once or twice a week from the onset of flowering, which is the most vulnerable stage. Examine flowers, buds, growing points, young fruits, and pods on a representative sample of plants (for example, 20 to 30 plants spread across the field).
- **What to look for.** Check flowers and terminal shoots for the small dome-shaped eggs, look for young larvae on buds and flowers, and inspect fruits and pods for entry holes and frass. Record the percentage of plants or fruiting structures infested.
- **Pheromone traps.** Traps baited with the species-specific sex pheromone catch male moths and give early warning of adult flights and the timing of egg-laying, helping to schedule scouting and any intervention. Traps indicate activity trends, not exact field densities.
- **Action thresholds.** Thresholds vary by crop, growth stage, and market. As general guidance, intervention is often considered when a small percentage of flowering or fruiting structures (commonly cited in the range of a few eggs or young larvae per given number of plants, or when a set percentage of fruits or pods show fresh infestation) is reached. Because these figures differ by crop and region, confirm the appropriate action threshold with your local agricultural extension officer rather than spraying on a fixed calendar.

## Prevention
- **Field sanitation.** Remove and destroy infested fruits, pods, and crop residues to eliminate larvae and reduce carry-over to the next crop. Deep ploughing after harvest exposes pupae in the soil to predators, birds, and drying, reducing the next generation.
- **Crop rotation.** Avoid planting successive susceptible crops (for example tomato after tomato, or a legume after another host legume) in the same field; rotate with non-host crops such as cereals in a legume rotation to break the cycle.
- **Timing and trap crops.** Adjust planting dates where extension guidance indicates a lower-risk window. Planting an attractive trap crop (for example, a border of a favoured flowering host) can concentrate egg-laying where it is easier to monitor and manage.
- **Resistant and appropriate varieties.** Where available, use varieties with some tolerance or with traits such as denser or less attractive fruiting structures, following local recommendations.
- **Field hygiene between plots.** Because moths migrate, community-wide coordination of planting and sanitation is more effective than isolated efforts.

## Integrated Pest Management
IPM for the African bollworm combines several tactics because no single method gives reliable, durable control, and because over-reliance on chemicals has driven widespread resistance.

- **Monitor first, then decide.** Base any action on scouting and thresholds, not on the calendar. Detecting eggs and early larvae is essential because control is far more effective before larvae bore into produce.
- **Conserve natural enemies.** A large complex of parasitoids and predators normally suppresses this pest; protecting them is one of the most valuable and cheapest control measures.
- **Cultural controls.** Combine rotation, sanitation, deep ploughing, trap crops, and appropriate planting dates.
- **Handpicking.** On small plots, regularly handpicking and destroying eggs and larvae from flowers and fruits can meaningfully reduce populations.
- **Biological and microbial controls first.** Where intervention is justified, prioritise selective biopesticides that spare natural enemies.
- **Chemicals as a last resort.** Reserve conventional insecticides for situations exceeding the threshold, rotate modes of action to manage resistance, and target young larvae.
- **Resistance management.** Because this pest resists many older chemistries, avoid repeated use of the same class, and integrate non-chemical tactics to reduce selection pressure.

## Biological Control
The African bollworm has a rich natural enemy complex, and conserving it is central to management.

- **Egg parasitoids:** tiny wasps of the genus *Trichogramma* lay their eggs inside bollworm eggs, killing them before larvae hatch. In some systems these are released augmentatively.
- **Larval parasitoids:** wasps such as *Cotesia* and *Campoletis* species, and tachinid flies, parasitise larvae.
- **Predators:** ladybird beetles, lacewing larvae, predatory bugs (such as *Orius* and assassin bugs), ants, spiders, and predatory earwigs feed on eggs and young larvae; birds take larvae and pupae.
- **Microbial biopesticides:** products based on *Bacillus thuringiensis* subsp. *kurstaki* (Bt) are effective against young larvae and are selective, sparing most beneficial insects; they must be applied to target early instars before larvae bore inside. A naturally occurring virus, the *Helicoverpa armigera* nucleopolyhedrovirus (HaNPV), is a highly specific biocontrol that infects and kills the larvae. Spinosad-based biopesticides (derived from soil bacteria) are also effective and relatively selective. Entomopathogenic fungi such as *Beauveria bassiana* contribute additional suppression.

Conserve these agents by avoiding unnecessary broad-spectrum sprays, maintaining flowering border plants and refuges that provide nectar and shelter, and choosing selective products when intervention is needed. Always confirm the local availability and registration of any biopesticide with a qualified agricultural extension officer.

## Chemical Control
Chemical control should be a last resort, used only when scouting shows the pest has exceeded the locally recommended action threshold, and applied while larvae are still young and exposed on the plant surface. Once larvae bore into fruits, pods, or bolls, they are protected from sprays, so timing is critical.

Because the African bollworm has developed resistance to several insecticide classes worldwide, including some pyrethroids and organophosphates in many regions, resistance management is essential. Where chemical intervention is justified, selective and reduced-risk options such as spinosad-based products, or specific classes advised by extension services, are generally preferred over broad-spectrum sprays that destroy natural enemies. Rotate between different modes of action across the season, never spray the same class repeatedly, and integrate biological and cultural methods to reduce reliance on chemicals.

Farmers must confirm which products are currently registered and approved for their specific crop, the correct dosage, the safe application method, and the pre-harvest interval with a qualified agricultural extension officer or national plant protection authority, because registrations, resistance status, and recommendations differ by country and change over time. See `regulations/pesticide_safety_and_handling.md` for detailed safety and handling guidance.

## Safety Considerations
- Always read and follow the product label for dosage, application method, protective equipment, and the pre-harvest interval, which is especially important for fruits and vegetables eaten fresh, such as tomato.
- Wear appropriate personal protective equipment (gloves, a mask or respirator, long sleeves, boots, and eye protection) when mixing and applying any pesticide.
- Do not spray during windy conditions to avoid drift onto other crops, water sources, and neighbouring fields.
- Observe the pre-harvest interval strictly so that produce sent to market or eaten at home is free of unsafe residues.
- Because bollworm damage promotes fungal invasion, do not consume or sell heavily bored, rotting produce, particularly maize and groundnut where mould can produce aflatoxins.
- Store pesticides securely away from food, water, and children, and dispose of empty containers safely as described in `regulations/pesticide_safety_and_handling.md`.
- If control repeatedly fails despite correct application, suspect resistance and consult an extension officer to change strategy rather than increasing dose or spray frequency.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: How do I tell the African bollworm apart from the fall armyworm on my maize?**
A: Fall armyworm mostly feeds inside the leaf whorl of young maize, has a distinctive inverted "Y" mark on its head and four dark dots in a square near its tail, and its body is fairly smooth. The African bollworm targets the cob tip, silks, and grain, its body is rough with small bristled bumps, and its colour varies widely with lengthwise stripes. Correct identification helps you choose the right timing and control, so check with your extension officer if unsure.

**Q: Why is calendar spraying a bad idea for this pest?**
A: Spraying on a fixed schedule wastes money, kills the natural enemies that normally control the pest, and speeds up insecticide resistance. Because the bollworm resists many chemicals and hides inside fruits and pods, routine spraying often fails while making the problem worse. Scout your crop and only intervene when it crosses the action threshold.

**Q: The larvae are already inside my tomatoes and pods. Will spraying help?**
A: Once larvae have bored inside fruits or pods they are protected from sprays, so chemical control at that point is largely ineffective. This is why early detection of eggs and young larvae is so important. Remove and destroy infested fruits and pods, and focus future sprays or biopesticide applications on the egg and young-larva stage.

**Q: What are the safest control options for a smallholder?**
A: Start with prevention and biological control: field sanitation, crop rotation, deep ploughing, handpicking eggs and larvae on small plots, and conserving natural enemies such as *Trichogramma* wasps and predatory bugs. Selective biopesticides based on *Bacillus thuringiensis* (Bt), the *Helicoverpa* nucleopolyhedrovirus, or spinosad target the pest while sparing beneficial insects. Confirm what is locally available with your extension officer.

**Q: Can I use the same insecticide I used last season?**
A: Repeatedly using the same insecticide class strongly promotes resistance, which is already common in this pest. Rotate between different modes of action across the season and integrate non-chemical methods. If a product that worked before is now failing, that is a warning sign of resistance, so consult your extension officer rather than increasing the dose.

**Q: Is bollworm-damaged grain or produce safe to eat?**
A: Light surface feeding may be cosmetic, but heavily bored fruits, pods, and cobs are prone to rot and mould. In maize and groundnut, mould in damaged produce can produce aflatoxins, which are harmful to people and livestock. Do not eat or sell badly damaged, mouldy produce, and store the good produce properly to prevent further contamination.

**Q: Do pheromone traps control the bollworm?**
A: Standard pheromone traps catch male moths and are primarily a monitoring tool that tells you when adults are flying and egg-laying is likely, so you can time your scouting and any intervention. They generally do not reduce the population enough on their own to serve as a control method, but they are a valuable early-warning aid within an IPM programme.
