# Mango Mealybug

## Pest Identification
- Common name "mango mealybug" refers to several mealybug species (family Pseudococcidae) that infest mango, with *Rastrococcus invadens* being a particularly significant invasive species introduced to West and Central Africa, alongside other mealybugs such as *Planococcus* and *Rastrococcus* species that also occur on mango and related fruit trees.
- Adults and nymphs are soft-bodied, oval insects covered in a white, waxy or cottony secretion that gives infested plant parts a distinctive white, powdery appearance.
- Colonies form dense clusters on the underside of leaves, along leaf veins and midribs, on young shoots, and on flower panicles and developing fruit clusters.
- Females are wingless and remain on the plant once settled; winged males are small, short-lived, and rarely seen.
- Heavy infestations are often accompanied by sticky honeydew on leaves and fruit, black sooty mould, and trails of ants moving along the trunk and branches to and from colonies.

## Life Cycle
- Eggs are laid within a cottony ovisac produced by the female, with each ovisac potentially containing large numbers of eggs.
- Newly hatched nymphs, called crawlers, are the main dispersal stage; they move short distances to new growth, are carried by wind, and are also transported between trees and orchards by ants and on infested planting material or fruit.
- Nymphs pass through several instars while feeding, taking roughly four to six weeks to complete development to the adult stage depending on temperature.
- Multiple overlapping generations occur per year in warm climates, with populations often building up most rapidly during periods of new leaf flush and flowering.

## Crops Affected
- Mango (*Mangifera indica*) is the primary host — see `crops/mango.md`.
- Related mealybug species and populations have also been recorded on citrus, guava, and various ornamental and shade trees, which can serve as alternate hosts or sources of reinfestation near mango orchards.

## Damage Caused
- Direct sap removal from leaves, shoots, and flower panicles weakens tree vigour and can reduce flowering and fruit set, particularly under heavy, sustained infestation.
- Copious honeydew excretion coats leaves, twigs, and fruit, supporting the growth of black sooty mould, which blocks light from leaf surfaces and reduces photosynthesis, and can render fruit visually unmarketable even without direct feeding damage to the fruit itself.
- Heavily infested flower panicles may fail to set fruit or may drop prematurely, directly reducing yield.
- Ants that tend mealybug colonies for their honeydew often interfere with biological control by attacking and driving away natural enemies, which can allow mealybug populations to persist at damaging levels.
- Cumulative infestation across multiple seasons can progressively weaken tree health and productivity if left unmanaged.

## Monitoring Methods
- Inspect new leaf flush, the underside of leaves, and flower panicles regularly, with particular attention at the start of the flowering and fruit-set period when damage has the greatest impact on yield.
- Look for white, waxy insect clusters, sticky honeydew, sooty mould, and ant trails on the trunk and main branches leading toward infested plant parts.
- Monitor orchard borders and trees near alternate host plants (such as ornamental or shade trees) that may harbour populations and act as a source of reinfestation.

## Prevention
- Prune and destroy heavily infested shoots, branches, and flower panicles to physically remove concentrated colonies and reduce the local population.
- Maintain orchard sanitation by controlling weeds and removing volunteer or alternate host plants that can sustain mealybug populations between mango flowering periods.
- Manage ants that protect mealybug colonies, for example by applying sticky barrier bands around the trunk, since reducing ant access allows natural enemies to reach and suppress the colonies more effectively.
- Avoid excessive nitrogen fertilisation, which stimulates the soft, tender new growth flushes that mealybug crawlers preferentially colonize.
- Use pest-free planting material when establishing new orchards or grafting, and inspect nursery stock carefully before transplanting.

## Integrated Pest Management
- Combine ant management, pruning and sanitation, and conservation of natural enemies as the foundation of mango mealybug management, reserving chemical treatment for situations where these measures are insufficient.
- Classical biological control has achieved substantial, lasting suppression of some invasive mango mealybug species in parts of Africa and should be supported and conserved rather than disrupted by unnecessary insecticide use.
- Time any cultural interventions, such as pruning of infested material, to coincide with periods of lower mealybug activity to maximize their impact on the overall population.

## Biological Control
- Parasitoid wasps, including *Gyranusoidea tebygi* and various *Anagyrus* species, were introduced for classical biological control of the invasive mango mealybug *Rastrococcus invadens* in West Africa and achieved substantial, sustained suppression of the pest across affected areas.
- Predatory ladybird beetles, including the mealybug destroyer (*Cryptolaemus montrouzieri*), along with lacewing larvae and hoverfly larvae, also feed on mealybug colonies and contribute to natural control.
- Conserve these natural enemies by controlling ants that protect mealybug colonies from predation and parasitism, and by limiting broad-spectrum insecticide use in and around the orchard.

## Chemical Control
- Horticultural mineral oils can be applied to smother mealybug colonies on leaves and shoots, generally with a lower impact on natural enemies compared with broad-spectrum insecticides, and can be a useful option within an IPM programme.
- Systemic insecticides may be considered for severe infestations that are not adequately controlled by cultural and biological measures, but should be reserved for such cases rather than used routinely.
- Applications should avoid periods of active flowering to protect bees and other pollinators visiting mango blossoms.
- Confirm currently approved and locally registered insecticide active ingredients with a national agricultural regulatory authority or extension officer, as registered products vary by country and change over time.
- Rotate insecticide modes of action (chemical classes) if repeated treatment is necessary, to reduce the risk of resistance development.

## Safety Considerations
- Always follow label instructions for any pesticide product, including dosage, application method, and pre-harvest interval, particularly important given that mango fruit is consumed fresh.
- Use appropriate personal protective equipment (gloves, mask, long sleeves) when handling and applying any chemical control product.
- Avoid spraying during flowering periods when bees and other pollinators are active in the orchard.
- Avoid spraying during windy conditions to prevent chemical drift onto non-target areas, water sources, or neighbouring farms.
- Store all pesticide products securely away from children, food, and water sources, and dispose of empty containers as directed on the label.
- Consult an agricultural extension officer if infestation persists despite treatment, as this may indicate insufficient ant control, disruption of natural enemies, or the need for a revised orchard sanitation plan.
