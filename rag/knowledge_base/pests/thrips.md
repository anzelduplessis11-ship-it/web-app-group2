# Thrips

## Pest Identification
- Scientific names: several species are important in Africa, including the onion thrips *Thrips tabaci*, the western flower thrips *Frankliniella occidentalis*, and the bean flower thrips *Megalurothrips sjostedti*.
- Adults are very small, slender insects, typically 1-2 mm long, ranging from pale yellow to brown or black, with narrow, fringed wings; they are easily overlooked without close inspection.
- Immature stages (larvae) are wingless, pale yellow to whitish, and found feeding in flowers, on young leaves, and in growing points.
- Thrips are most easily detected by tapping flowers or young shoots over a light-coloured surface and watching for the tiny insects that fall and move quickly.

## Life Cycle
- Egg stage: eggs are inserted into plant tissue and hatch in approximately 3-7 days.
- Larval stage: two feeding larval stages over roughly 1-2 weeks.
- Two non-feeding stages (prepupa and pupa) follow, often in the soil or leaf litter, lasting several days.
- Adult stage: from about one to several weeks; many species reproduce rapidly, and some can reproduce without mating.
- Warm, dry conditions favour rapid, overlapping generations and quick population buildup, particularly during flowering.

## Crops Affected
- Wide host range including onion, tomato (see `crops/tomato.md`), beans (see `crops/common_beans.md`), pepper, cabbage, cotton, and many other vegetables, legumes, and flowering crops.
- Some species are strongly associated with flowers of legumes and vegetables, where they concentrate during the flowering period.

## Damage Caused
- Feeding by rasping and sucking cell contents causes silvery or bronzed patches ("silvering"), fine pale flecking, and tiny dark spots of excrement on leaves, flowers, and pods.
- Damage to flowers and growing points can cause flower drop, distorted growth, and deformed or scarred fruit and pods, reducing both yield and market quality.
- In legumes such as beans, heavy thrips feeding on flowers can significantly reduce pod set.
- Some thrips species transmit plant viruses (such as tospoviruses), and this indirect virus damage can be more significant than direct feeding in susceptible crops.

## Monitoring Methods
- Inspect flowers, young leaves, and growing tips at least weekly, tapping them over a light-coloured surface or into the palm to dislodge and count the tiny insects.
- Look for silvering, flecking, dark excrement spots, and distorted flowers or new growth as signs of infestation.
- Use blue or yellow sticky traps to monitor adult thrips activity and movement into a field.
- Follow locally recommended action thresholds where available, and give particular attention to flowering, when thrips numbers and damage are usually highest.

## Prevention
- Remove and destroy heavily infested plant material and clear crop residues and weeds that can harbour thrips between crops.
- Avoid excessive nitrogen fertilisation, which encourages soft, attractive new growth.
- Maintain even soil moisture, since thrips outbreaks are often worse under hot, dry, water-stressed conditions (see `irrigation/` guidance where relevant).
- Use clean seedlings and, in nurseries, fine netting to protect young plants from colonisation.
- Where thrips-transmitted viruses are a concern, prioritise clean planting material and prompt removal of symptomatic plants.

## Integrated Pest Management
- Combine regular monitoring, conservation of natural enemies, cultural practices, and targeted control only when populations or virus risk exceed a damaging level.
- Because thrips shelter deep in flowers and growing points, thorough targeting and good spray coverage are essential when treatment is needed, and prevention plus biological control should come first.
- Reflective mulches are used in some vegetable systems to deter thrips from settling on plants.
- Rotate to non-host crops where practical to reduce carry-over, particularly where virus transmission is a problem.

## Biological Control
- Natural enemies include predatory mites, predatory bugs (such as *Orius* species), and lacewing larvae, which feed on thrips larvae and adults.
- Entomopathogenic fungi can infect thrips, particularly under humid conditions.
- Conserve these natural enemies by avoiding unnecessary broad-spectrum insecticide applications, since thrips recover quickly and can resurge when their predators are removed.

## Chemical Control
- Insecticidal soaps and horticultural oils, applied thoroughly to flowers and growing points, can reduce thrips with relatively low impact on beneficial insects.
- Chemical control should be reserved for infestations exceeding the locally recommended action threshold; thrips are prone to developing resistance, so overuse of chemicals often leads to poor control.
- Confirm currently approved and locally registered insecticide active ingredients with a national agricultural regulatory authority or extension officer, as registered products and resistance management recommendations vary by country and change over time.
- Rotate insecticide modes of action (chemical classes) across the season to reduce the risk of resistance development, and prioritise targeted applications over broad-spectrum whole-field spraying.

## Safety Considerations
- Follow label instructions for any pesticide product regarding dosage, application timing, and pre-harvest interval, especially on vegetables and legumes harvested frequently.
- Wear appropriate personal protective equipment (gloves, mask, long sleeves) when applying any chemical control product.
- Avoid spraying flowering crops during pollinator activity hours to protect bees and other pollinators, since thrips control often coincides with flowering.
- Avoid spraying during windy conditions to prevent chemical drift onto non-target areas, water sources, or neighbouring fields.
- Store all pesticide products securely away from children, food, and water sources, and dispose of empty containers as directed on the label.
- If thrips populations persist despite treatment, or if a viral disease is suspected in the crop, consult an agricultural extension officer or plant health specialist for further diagnosis, as virus problems require a different approach from direct pest control.
