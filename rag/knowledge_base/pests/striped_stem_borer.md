# Striped Stem Borer

## Pest Identification
The striped stem borer, *Chilo partellus* (also widely known as the spotted stem borer), is a moth pest originally native to Asia that has become well established across warmer, lower-altitude cereal-growing areas of eastern and southern Africa, where it is now one of the most economically important stem-boring pests of maize and sorghum, often dominating over indigenous stem borers in hotter, drier lowland zones.

- **Larvae** are creamy-white to pale yellowish with four longitudinal brownish stripes running the length of the body and a series of small, dark spots, giving the "striped" and "spotted" common names; the head capsule is brown. Mature larvae reach approximately 2-2.5 cm in length.
- **Adults** are small, straw-coloured to pale brown moths with a wingspan of roughly 2-2.5 cm; the forewings are elongated and somewhat pointed, with faint darker streaking, and females are generally larger than males. Adults are nocturnal and rarely seen during the day.
- **Eggs** are flat, oval, and creamy-white to pale yellow, laid in overlapping clusters resembling fish scales, typically on the underside of leaves close to the midrib, making them difficult to spot without deliberate inspection.
- Because larval feeding behaviour changes markedly with age, correct staging matters: young larvae feed openly in the leaf whorl, while older larvae are hidden inside the stem and cannot be reached by most sprays.

## Life Cycle
| Stage | Approximate duration | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Egg | 4-8 days | Laid in overlapping clusters on leaf undersides near the midrib |
| Larva | 2-3 weeks (longer in cooler conditions) | 5-6 instars; early instars feed in the whorl, later instars bore into the stem |
| Pupa | 1-2 weeks | Usually within the stem or at the base of the plant |
| Adult | 4-10 days | Nocturnal, mates and lays eggs shortly after emergence |

Under warm conditions typical of lowland tropical and subtropical cereal-growing areas, *Chilo partellus* can complete four to six generations in a year, allowing populations to build up rapidly across a single long cropping season. Between seasons, or during cooler or drier periods, mature larvae can enter a resting state (diapause) inside crop residues and stubble, re-emerging to continue development when conditions become favourable again; this carry-over in old stalks is a major source of the following season's infestation.

## Crops Affected
- **Primary hosts:** maize and sorghum.
- **Other cultivated hosts:** sugarcane, pearl millet, and rice.
- **Wild and cultivated grass hosts:** various wild grasses, as well as Napier grass and Sudan grass, serve as alternative hosts and, notably, are also used deliberately as trap crops in some integrated management systems because *Chilo partellus* moths are strongly attracted to them.

## Damage Caused
- Young larvae feeding in the whorl produce characteristic symptoms known as "window-paning" (thin, translucent patches where only one leaf layer has been eaten) and "shot-holing" (small, round holes across the leaf, visible in a row once the leaf unfurls).
- In young plants, larvae feeding down into the growing point can kill the central shoot, a symptom known as "deadheart," which is easily recognised as a wilted, dead central leaf that can be pulled out easily.
- Older larvae tunnel extensively inside the stem, disrupting the movement of water and nutrients, weakening the stem structurally, and greatly increasing the risk of lodging (stems breaking or falling over), especially in windy conditions.
- Larvae can also bore into developing cobs or panicles, directly damaging grain and creating entry points for storage pests and fungal pathogens.
- Because damage caused by older larvae occurs inside the stem, visible external symptoms often understate the extent of internal damage until the stem is split open for inspection.

## Monitoring Methods
- Begin scouting from the seedling stage, checking at least 20 plants at several points across the field on a weekly basis.
- Look specifically for window-paning and shot-hole damage on young whorl leaves, deadheart in young plants, and frass (sawdust-like larval excrement) accumulating in the whorl.
- Inspect the undersides of leaves near the midrib for the characteristic overlapping, fish-scale egg clusters before larvae hatch.
- Later in the season, split a sample of stems from plants showing external damage symptoms to check directly for tunnelling and the presence of live larvae, which gives a more accurate picture of infestation level than external symptoms alone.
- Where available, pheromone traps can be used to monitor adult moth activity and help time more intensive scouting; follow locally recommended action thresholds, as these vary by region, season, and crop stage.

## Prevention
- Plant early and as uniformly as possible within a community to reduce the period during which vulnerable young plants are available to egg-laying moths.
- Destroy or otherwise manage crop residues and stubble promptly after harvest, since surviving diapausing larvae in old stalks are a major source of the next season's infestation.
- Remove or manage volunteer maize, sorghum, and nearby wild grass hosts that can harbour the pest between cropping seasons.
- Where feasible, use known trap plants such as Napier grass or Sudan grass planted around field borders to draw egg-laying moths away from the main crop as part of a broader intercropping strategy.
- Maintain balanced soil fertility so that plants grow vigorously and can better tolerate a degree of larval feeding without severe yield loss.

## Integrated Pest Management
- The most extensively developed and validated approach for *Chilo partellus* in parts of eastern Africa is "push-pull" companion cropping, in which a repellent legume such as desmodium is intercropped between rows of the main cereal (the "push," deterring egg-laying moths from the crop) while an attractive trap grass, typically Napier grass or Sudan grass, is planted around the field border (the "pull," drawing moths away to lay eggs on a plant where larvae largely fail to survive well). This approach has shown measurable reductions in stem borer damage in trials and farmer fields where adopted, alongside additional benefits such as improved soil fertility and livestock fodder from the companion plants.
- Combine push-pull or other cultural measures with field sanitation (residue destruction), timely planting, resistant or tolerant maize varieties where locally available, conservation of natural enemies, and threshold-based chemical control reserved for infestations exceeding locally recommended levels.
- Because larvae become protected once they bore into the stem, the most effective window for any intervention, cultural or chemical, is while larvae are still young and exposed in the leaf whorl.
- Handpicking and destroying visible egg masses where infestations are localised and manageable can further reduce the population entering the vulnerable whorl-feeding stage.

## Biological Control
- The introduced parasitic wasp *Cotesia flavipes* (a braconid wasp originally from Asia) has been deliberately released and has become successfully established in parts of eastern and southern Africa as a classical biological control agent, parasitising *Chilo partellus* larvae and providing a significant, self-sustaining source of natural suppression in many areas.
- Egg parasitoids in the genus *Trichogramma* attack *Chilo partellus* eggs and contribute to reducing the number of larvae that hatch.
- Various generalist predators, including predatory beetles and ants, feed on eggs and young larvae in the whorl.
- Biopesticides based on *Bacillus thuringiensis* (Bt) can be effective against young larvae feeding in the whorl and have a much lower impact on these beneficial parasitoids and predators than broad-spectrum chemical insecticides, supporting the conservation of established biological control.
- Minimising unnecessary broad-spectrum insecticide use helps protect established populations of *Cotesia flavipes* and other natural enemies, preserving their long-term contribution to pest suppression.

## Chemical Control
- Chemical control should be reserved for infestations exceeding locally recommended action thresholds and is most effective when applied while larvae are still exposed in the whorl, since larvae that have already bored into the stem are largely protected from sprays.
- Whorl-targeted applications, directing granules or spray formulations into the funnel of the plant, reach young larvae more effectively than general foliar sprays applied across the whole plant.
- Historically important active ingredient classes for whorl-stage stem borer control include certain pyrethroids and other insecticide classes formulated as granules for whorl application, alongside Bt-based biopesticides as a lower-impact alternative for young larvae.
- Confirm currently approved and locally registered insecticide active ingredients, formulations, and application rates with a national agricultural regulatory authority or extension officer, as registered products, granule formulations, and resistance-management guidance vary by country and change over time.
- Rotate insecticide modes of action (chemical classes) across a season where repeated treatment is necessary, to reduce the risk of resistance developing in the local pest population.

## Safety Considerations
- Always follow label instructions for any pesticide product, including dosage, application method, and pre-harvest interval.
- Use appropriate personal protective equipment (gloves, mask, long sleeves) when handling and applying any chemical pesticide, taking particular care when placing granular or liquid product into the plant whorl by hand.
- Keep children and animals away from treated fields for the period specified on the product label.
- Avoid spraying during windy conditions to prevent chemical drift onto non-target areas, water sources, or neighbouring fields.
- Store pesticides securely, away from food, water sources, and children, and dispose of empty containers as directed on the label.
- Consult an agricultural extension officer if an infestation persists despite treatment, as this may indicate resistance, incorrect timing, or misapplication, and report severe or unusual outbreaks to local agricultural authorities for regional monitoring.
