# Tomato Leafminer (Tuta absoluta)

## Pest Identification
- Scientific name: *Phthorimaea absoluta* (widely known by its former name *Tuta absoluta*), also called the tomato leafminer or South American tomato moth.
- Larvae are the damaging stage: small caterpillars, cream to greenish and later pinkish, up to approximately 0.5-0.9 cm long, with a dark band behind the head in early stages; they feed inside leaves, stems, and fruit.
- Adults are small, silvery-grey to brown moths with a wingspan of roughly 1 cm and thread-like antennae; they are active mainly at dusk and night and hide among foliage during the day.
- Eggs are tiny, cream to yellowish, and laid singly on leaves, stems, and young fruit, making them very hard to see.

## Life Cycle
- Egg stage: approximately 4-7 days.
- Larval stage: about 1-3 weeks, passing through four instars; larvae mine into leaves and bore into stems and fruit, so they spend most of this stage protected inside plant tissue.
- Pupal stage: about 1-2 weeks, in mined leaves, on the plant surface, or in the soil.
- Adult stage: one to a few weeks; a single female can lay from around one hundred to several hundred eggs.
- The complete cycle can be as short as a few weeks in warm conditions, allowing many overlapping generations per season and very rapid population growth.

## Crops Affected
- Primarily tomato, its main host, on which it can be extremely damaging (see `crops/tomato.md`).
- Also attacks other solanaceous crops including potato, eggplant (aubergine), and pepper, as well as some solanaceous weeds that act as reservoir hosts.

## Damage Caused
- Larvae create irregular, blotch-shaped mines within the leaves; heavily mined leaves dry out and die, reducing photosynthesis and vigour.
- Larvae bore into stems and growing points, weakening plants and disrupting growth.
- Larvae tunnel into fruit, causing internal damage and entry points for rot organisms, which makes fruit unmarketable; fruit damage is the most economically serious effect.
- Severe infestations can cause very high yield and quality losses across the whole crop if not managed early.

## Monitoring Methods
- Scout the crop at least weekly from transplanting or early growth, inspecting leaves for the characteristic blotch mines and checking stems and fruit for boring holes and frass.
- Use pheromone traps to detect the arrival of adult males, monitor population trends, and time control decisions before damage becomes severe.
- Pay close attention to seedlings and young plants, since early infestation can carry through and intensify over the season.
- Follow locally recommended action thresholds where available and act promptly, as this pest builds up very quickly.

## Prevention
- Use clean, pest-free seedlings and inspect nursery stock before transplanting to avoid introducing the pest into a new field (see `crops/tomato.md`).
- Remove and destroy infested leaves, stems, and fruit, and clear crop residues promptly after harvest to reduce carry-over.
- Rotate away from tomato and other solanaceous crops, and control solanaceous weeds that serve as alternative hosts.
- Protect nurseries and seedbeds with fine netting to keep out egg-laying adults.
- Avoid continuous, overlapping tomato plantings in the same area, which sustain the pest year-round.

## Integrated Pest Management
- Combine clean planting material, field sanitation, crop rotation, pheromone-based monitoring, biological control, and carefully timed chemical control only when thresholds are exceeded.
- Pheromone mass-trapping and mating-disruption approaches are used in some systems to reduce populations and may be considered based on local extension recommendations.
- Because larvae are protected inside leaves, stems, and fruit for most of their development, target eggs and newly hatched larvae, and rely heavily on prevention and biological control.
- Remove and destroy heavily infested plants rather than leaving them as a source of continued spread.

## Biological Control
- Natural enemies include predatory bugs (such as *Nesidiocoris* and *Macrolophus* species) that feed on eggs and young larvae, and parasitic wasps (such as *Trichogramma* species that attack eggs).
- Biopesticides based on *Bacillus thuringiensis* (Bt) can be effective against young larvae before they tunnel deeply into plant tissue and generally have a lower impact on beneficial insects than broad-spectrum chemical insecticides.
- Conserve natural enemies by avoiding unnecessary broad-spectrum insecticide applications, which can worsen outbreaks by removing these beneficial species.

## Chemical Control
- Chemical control should be reserved for infestations exceeding the locally recommended action threshold and directed at eggs and early-stage larvae, since larvae inside mines, stems, and fruit are largely protected from sprays.
- This pest is well known for developing resistance rapidly, so over-reliance on chemicals commonly leads to control failure.
- Confirm currently approved and locally registered insecticide active ingredients with a national agricultural regulatory authority or extension officer, as registered products and resistance management recommendations vary by country and change over time.
- Rotate insecticide modes of action (chemical classes) strictly across the season to slow resistance development, and integrate chemicals with biological and cultural methods rather than using them alone.

## Safety Considerations
- Follow label instructions for any pesticide product regarding dosage, application timing, and pre-harvest interval; this is especially important for tomato, which is often harvested repeatedly over a long period.
- Wear appropriate personal protective equipment (gloves, mask, long sleeves) when applying any chemical control product.
- Observe the stated pre-harvest interval carefully so that treated fruit is safe to eat, and do not harvest earlier than the label allows.
- Avoid spraying during windy conditions to prevent chemical drift onto non-target areas, water sources, or neighbouring fields, and avoid spraying flowering crops during pollinator activity hours.
- Store all pesticide products securely away from children, food, and water sources, and dispose of empty containers as directed on the label.
- If infestations persist despite treatment, consult an agricultural extension officer or plant health specialist, as this may indicate resistance, and report severe or unusual outbreaks to local agricultural authorities for regional monitoring.
