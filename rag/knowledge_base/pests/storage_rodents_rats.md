# Storage Rodents (Rats and Mice)

## Pest Identification
Storage rodents are warm-blooded vertebrate pests, not insects, and their management differs fundamentally from insect pest control. The species most commonly responsible for damage to stored grain and food reserves in Africa include the house rat (*Rattus rattus*), the Norway or brown rat (*Rattus norvegicus*), the house mouse (*Mus musculus*), and the multimammate rat (*Mastomys natalensis*), a native African rodent that is especially important because its populations can erupt into large-scale outbreaks following good rains and abundant food.

- **Rats** are typically 15-25 cm in body length (excluding tail), with the tail often as long as or longer than the body; fur colour ranges from grey-brown to black depending on species.
- **Mice** are much smaller, generally 6-9 cm in body length, with proportionally large ears and a thin tail.
- **Multimammate rats** are medium-sized, greyish-brown, and named for the unusually high number of teats in females, reflecting their capacity for large litters.
- Because rodents are nocturnal and secretive, direct sightings are less reliable than indirect signs:
  - **Droppings**: dark, pellet-shaped, and often clustered near feeding sites, runways, or nesting areas; fresh droppings are soft and moist, old ones dry and crumbly.
  - **Gnaw marks**: fresh, pale wood or plastic scarring on sacks, bins, doors, and structural timber, since rodents must gnaw continuously to wear down their incisor teeth.
  - **Runways and rub marks**: greasy, dark smudges along walls and beams where rodents repeatedly travel, and visible tracks or footprints in dust or spilled grain.
  - **Burrows and nests**: holes at the base of walls, granaries, or field banks, and nests made of shredded material in quiet, undisturbed corners of stores.
  - **Damaged produce and packaging**: torn sacks, holes chewed into wooden or mud-brick store walls, and gnawed maize cobs or tubers.

## Life Cycle
Rodents do not undergo metamorphosis; they are mammals that reproduce continuously under favourable conditions, and their reproductive potential is the central reason populations can build up so quickly around a food source.

| Feature | Typical value |
|---|---|
| Gestation period | Approximately 19-23 days |
| Litter size | 4-12 young, depending on species |
| Age at sexual maturity | As early as 6-10 weeks in species such as *Mastomys natalensis* |
| Litters per year | Several, with breeding often continuing year-round where food and shelter are abundant |
| Lifespan | Typically less than one year in the wild, though a small proportion may survive longer |

Because a single breeding pair can theoretically give rise to a very large number of descendants within a year, rodent populations respond explosively to a combination of good rains (which boost wild food availability and breeding), the presence of standing crops or stored grain, and available shelter. In parts of East and Southern Africa, *Mastomys natalensis* is well documented to undergo periodic population outbreaks tied to rainfall and food pulses, leading to sudden, severe damage to both field crops and stored produce over a wide area.

## Crops Affected
Storage rodents are highly generalist feeders and will attack almost any stored food commodity as well as some standing crops:

- **Cereal grains**: maize, sorghum, millet, rice, and wheat, whether stored on the cob, as threshed grain, or as flour.
- **Legumes and oilseeds**: stored beans, cowpea, groundnut, and soybean.
- **Root and tuber products**: dried cassava chips and stored sweet potato.
- **Perishables and processed foods**: any accessible food item in a household store, including packaged foods once packaging is breached.
- **Standing field crops**, particularly maize and sugarcane near maturity, groundnut pods below ground, and germinating seed shortly after planting, where field populations cause losses before produce even reaches storage.

## Damage Caused
- **Direct consumption** of grain and other stored food reduces the quantity available for household use or sale, and rodents typically eat only a portion of each grain or cob while damaging much more through handling.
- **Contamination** with urine, droppings, hair, and body fluids renders far more grain unfit for human consumption than is actually eaten, and contaminated produce is difficult to fully clean.
- **Moisture introduction** from rodent activity and nesting can raise localised humidity within a grain heap, promoting mould growth and further spoilage.
- **Structural damage** from gnawing weakens storage containers, sacks, wooden structures, and even electrical wiring in some settings, creating entry points for other pests and moisture.
- **Disease transmission** is a serious indirect harm: rodents can carry pathogens responsible for diseases such as leptospirosis, salmonellosis, and, in the case of *Mastomys natalensis*, Lassa fever virus in parts of West Africa, making rodent contact and contaminated food a genuine human health risk in addition to an economic one.
- **Field losses** before harvest can also be substantial, particularly in maize and groundnut, and are often underestimated because damage is scattered and gradual.

## Monitoring Methods
- **Sign surveys.** Regularly inspect stores and field margins for fresh droppings, gnaw marks, runways, and burrow entrances; fresh, moist droppings indicate active, current infestation rather than an old, abandoned population.
- **Tracking plates or chew cards.** Placing a flat surface dusted with a fine material (such as flour or talc) or a plain piece of card overnight and checking for footprints or gnaw marks the next morning gives a simple relative index of rodent activity.
- **Trap indices.** Setting a standard number of snap traps for a fixed number of nights and recording the catch rate (captures per trap-night) provides a way to compare rodent pressure between sites or over time.
- **Bait uptake monitoring.** Placing unpoisoned bait at monitoring points and checking how quickly it disappears helps gauge population size and the best locations for later control before committing to rodenticide use.
- **Community and seasonal observation.** Because populations of species such as *Mastomys natalensis* can erupt following good rains, tracking rainfall patterns and any early reports of unusual rodent numbers in the district allows households and communities to prepare control measures ahead of a predicted outbreak rather than reacting after major losses occur.

## Prevention
- **Rodent-proof storage.** Store grain in raised granaries with metal guards ("rat guards") on the supporting legs, in sealed metal or heavy plastic silos, or in other containers that rodents cannot gnaw through or climb into.
- **Eliminate harbourage.** Clear grass, weeds, brush piles, and general debris from around stores and homesteads, since dense vegetation provides shelter and travel cover for rodents close to food sources.
- **Seal entry points.** Block holes and gaps in store walls, floors, and roofs, particularly at ground level, since rats can squeeze through surprisingly small openings.
- **General hygiene.** Keep storage areas clean, remove spilled grain promptly, and store food in rodent-proof containers rather than open sacks left directly on the floor.
- **Community-wide action.** Because rodents move freely between neighbouring homesteads and fields, coordinated, community-level prevention and control is far more effective and longer-lasting than isolated household efforts.
- **Timely harvesting and drying.** Harvest promptly at maturity and dry produce quickly to the recommended moisture content, since delayed harvest leaves crops exposed to rodent damage in the field for longer.

## Integrated Pest Management
Effective rodent management combines exclusion, habitat modification, monitoring, and targeted control rather than relying on poison alone, since rodenticides used in isolation are quickly overwhelmed by a population's reproductive capacity.

- Prioritise structural and hygiene measures (rodent-proof stores, sealed entry points, removal of harbourage) as the durable foundation of control.
- Use monitoring data to target trapping and baiting precisely where activity is highest, rather than applying control uniformly or reactively.
- Time community control campaigns to precede predicted population surges, particularly for species prone to outbreaks following good rains, since acting before a population boom is far more effective than attempting to reduce an already large population.
- Combine mechanical trapping with rodenticide baiting in tamper-resistant bait stations, rotating or limiting rodenticide use to reduce the risk of resistance and secondary poisoning.
- Conserve and encourage natural predators as part of the wider landscape approach, alongside farmer-level structural and hygiene measures.
- Coordinate action across neighbouring farms and the wider community, since rodents readily recolonise from untreated areas nearby.

## Biological Control
- **Owls**, particularly barn owls, are effective nocturnal predators of rats and mice; installing nesting boxes near stores and fields to encourage owl populations is a low-cost, low-risk way to support natural rodent suppression.
- **Other avian and reptile predators**, including birds of prey active by day and various snake species, contribute to natural population control, particularly in less disturbed field margins and bush areas.
- **Domestic cats** can help suppress rodent numbers around homesteads and small stores, though they are generally insufficient on their own against a significant infestation.
- Conserving these natural enemies by avoiding indiscriminate poisoning of non-target wildlife, and by protecting hedgerows, trees, and other predator habitat near farms, supports background rodent suppression between control campaigns.

## Chemical Control
Chemical rodent control relies on rodenticide active ingredient classes applied strategically as bait, always in combination with the structural and hygiene measures above.

- **Anticoagulant rodenticides** are the most widely used class and work by preventing normal blood clotting after repeated (first-generation active ingredients, such as warfarin or coumatetralyl) or single (second-generation active ingredients, such as bromadiolone or brodifacoum) feeding. Second-generation anticoagulants are more potent but also carry a higher risk of secondary poisoning to predators and scavengers that eat poisoned rodents.
- **Acute (non-anticoagulant) rodenticides**, such as zinc phosphide, act as a fast stomach poison after a single feed and are often used where anticoagulant resistance is suspected, but they generally require careful pre-baiting with untreated bait to overcome bait shyness.
- Bait should always be placed in secure, tamper-resistant bait stations positioned along runways and near burrows or entry points, out of reach of children, livestock, pets, and non-target wildlife.
- Rotate or limit the use of any single rodenticide class where resistance is suspected, and coordinate timing of baiting campaigns across a community for best effect.

Confirm currently approved, locally registered rodenticide products and correct application rates with a qualified agricultural extension officer or public health authority, since registrations, restrictions on more hazardous active ingredients, and resistance patterns vary between countries and change over time.

## Safety Considerations
- All rodenticides are toxic to humans and non-target animals; store products in original, clearly labelled containers, locked away from children, food, and animal feed.
- Use tamper-resistant bait stations rather than loose bait, both to protect non-target animals and to reduce the risk of accidental poisoning of children and livestock.
- Collect and safely dispose of rodent carcasses promptly after a baiting campaign to reduce the risk of secondary poisoning to predators, scavengers, and pets, and to limit odour and disease risk.
- Wear gloves when handling live or dead rodents, traps, or contaminated grain and droppings, since rodents can carry disease-causing organisms; wash hands thoroughly afterwards.
- Do not place rodenticide bait directly among food intended for human consumption or animal feed; use dedicated bait stations away from direct food contact.
- If accidental poisoning of a person, pet, or livestock animal is suspected, seek medical or veterinary help immediately and take the product label or container along for reference.
- Consult an agricultural extension officer or public health authority for guidance on safe rodenticide selection and use, and report unusually large or sudden rodent population increases so that coordinated community or regional responses can be organised in time.
