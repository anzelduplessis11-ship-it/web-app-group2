# Fall Armyworm

## Pest Identification
- Scientific name: *Spodoptera frugiperda*.
- Larvae (caterpillars) are the damaging stage: 3-4 cm long when fully grown, ranging from light green to nearly black, with a characteristic inverted "Y"-shaped marking on the head capsule and four raised dark spots arranged in a square pattern on the second-to-last abdominal segment.
- Adult moths are grey-brown with a wingspan of about 3.5-4 cm; females lay egg masses covered in greyish fuzz (scales) on leaves.
- Most active and damaging at night and during early morning/late afternoon; larvae often hide in the whorl of maize plants or in soil during the heat of the day.

## Life Cycle
- Egg stage: 2-4 days.
- Larval stage: 14-21 days, passing through six instars (growth stages), with the later instars causing the most damage.
- Pupal stage: 7-13 days, typically in the soil.
- Adult moth stage: 10-14 days; a single female can lay several hundred to over a thousand eggs.
- Complete life cycle: approximately 24-40 days depending on temperature, allowing multiple overlapping generations per season.

## Crops Affected
- Primarily maize, but also sorghum, rice, sugarcane, and various pasture grasses.
- Can also feed on a wide range of other crops including vegetables and cotton when preferred hosts are unavailable.

## Damage Caused
- Early instars cause characteristic "window paning" — small, translucent patches on leaves where surface tissue is scraped away.
- Later instars cause large, ragged holes in leaves, often concentrated in the whorl, along with visible frass (excrement) resembling sawdust.
- Can bore into the developing tassel and cob, directly damaging the reproductive parts of the plant and reducing yield.
- Severe infestations can destroy young plants entirely.

## Monitoring Methods
- Scout fields weekly from seedling emergence through the vegetative stage, checking at least 20 plants per field at multiple points.
- Look for window-pane damage on young leaves and frass in the whorl of older plants.
- Use pheromone traps to monitor adult moth activity and detect the start of an infestation period.
- Establish an action threshold (commonly cited as around 20% of plants showing damage with live larvae present, though local extension guidance may vary) to decide when control measures are warranted.

## Prevention
- Plant early and uniformly within a community to reduce the availability of young, vulnerable plants over an extended period.
- Use fall armyworm-tolerant maize varieties where available.
- Practice good field sanitation and destroy crop residues that may harbour pupae.
- Encourage natural enemies (parasitic wasps, predatory beetles, birds) by minimizing unnecessary broad-spectrum insecticide use.

## Integrated Pest Management
- Combine regular scouting, threshold-based decision-making, cultural practices, biological control, and chemical control (only when necessary) rather than relying on any single method.
- Intercropping with certain companion plants and push-pull systems (using repellent and trap plants) has shown effectiveness in some regional trials and may be considered based on local extension recommendations.
- Handpicking and destroying egg masses and young larvae can be effective for small-scale plots.

## Biological Control
- Natural enemies include parasitic wasps (e.g., *Telenomus remus*, various *Cotesia* species), predatory beetles, and entomopathogenic fungi and viruses.
- Conserve natural enemy populations by avoiding unnecessary broad-spectrum insecticide applications.
- Biopesticides based on *Bacillus thuringiensis* (Bt) or entomopathogenic fungi/viruses can be effective, particularly against early instar larvae, and generally have a lower impact on beneficial insects than broad-spectrum chemical insecticides.

## Chemical Control
- Chemical control should be reserved for infestations exceeding the locally recommended action threshold, and applied while larvae are still in early instars, since later instars are harder to control and hide deep in the whorl.
- Confirm currently approved and locally registered insecticide active ingredients with a national agricultural regulatory authority or extension officer, as registered products and resistance management recommendations vary by country and change over time.
- Rotate insecticide modes of action (chemical classes) across the season to reduce the risk of resistance development.
- Apply in early morning or late evening when larvae are more likely to be actively feeding and exposed.

## Safety Considerations
- Always follow label instructions for any pesticide product, including dosage, application method, and pre-harvest interval.
- Use appropriate personal protective equipment (gloves, mask, long sleeves) when handling and applying any chemical pesticide.
- Keep children and animals away from treated fields for the period specified on the product label.
- Avoid spraying during windy conditions to prevent chemical drift onto non-target areas, water sources, or neighbouring fields.
- Store pesticides securely, away from food, water sources, and children.
- Consult an agricultural extension officer if an infestation persists despite treatment, as this may indicate resistance or misapplication, and severe or unusual outbreaks should be reported to local agricultural authorities for regional monitoring.
