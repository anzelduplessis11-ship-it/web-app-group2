# Whitefly

## Pest Identification
- Scientific names: the tobacco or silverleaf whitefly *Bemisia tabaci* is the most important species in Africa; the greenhouse whitefly *Trialeurodes vaporariorum* is also found, particularly in cooler and protected cropping systems.
- Adults are tiny (approximately 1-2 mm long), moth-like insects with white, powdery wings, usually found on the underside of leaves; they fly up in a small cloud when the plant is disturbed.
- Immature stages (nymphs) are flat, oval, scale-like, and pale, attached to the underside of leaves and often overlooked because they do not move once settled.
- Eggs are minute and laid on the underside of young leaves, frequently in a circular or arc-shaped pattern.

## Life Cycle
- Egg stage: approximately 5-10 days depending on temperature.
- Nymph stage: several instars over roughly 2-4 weeks; early nymphs crawl briefly before settling and feeding in one place.
- A non-feeding pupal-like stage precedes the emergence of the adult.
- Adult stage: from about one to several weeks; females can lay dozens to a few hundred eggs over their lifespan.
- Warm conditions allow rapid, overlapping generations, so populations can build up quickly, especially in the dry season and in sheltered plantings.

## Crops Affected
- Very wide host range including tomato (see `crops/tomato.md`), cassava (see `crops/cassava.md`), beans (see `crops/common_beans.md`), cotton, okra, cabbage, sweet potato, and many other vegetable and field crops.
- Certain whitefly populations are strongly associated with particular crops, but *Bemisia tabaci* as a group is a broad generalist.

## Damage Caused
- Direct damage from sap-sucking feeding causes yellowing, leaf wilting, and reduced vigour, particularly when populations are high.
- Whiteflies excrete sugary honeydew that promotes black sooty mould on leaves, reducing photosynthesis and lowering the quality of the crop.
- Critically, whiteflies are important vectors of plant viruses, including cassava mosaic disease (see `diseases/cassava_mosaic_disease.md`) and various tomato leaf curl and yellowing viruses; this indirect virus transmission is often far more damaging than direct feeding.
- Because even low whitefly numbers can spread virus, the presence of virus-susceptible crops greatly increases the importance of managing this pest.

## Monitoring Methods
- Inspect the underside of young leaves weekly, gently turning or tapping foliage to disturb adults and estimate their numbers.
- Look for nymphs and eggs on the underside of leaves, sticky honeydew, sooty mould, and early symptoms of virus infection such as leaf curling, yellowing, or mosaic patterns.
- Use yellow sticky traps to detect and track adult whitefly movement into a field and to help time control decisions.
- Follow locally recommended action thresholds where available, and give extra weight to whitefly presence when virus-susceptible crops are grown.

## Prevention
- Use certified, virus-free planting material and clean seed or cuttings, especially for cassava, since infected planting material spreads virus regardless of whitefly control (see `diseases/cassava_mosaic_disease.md`).
- Remove and destroy heavily infested and virus-symptomatic plants promptly to reduce sources of pest and virus spread.
- Avoid excessive nitrogen fertilisation, which promotes lush growth attractive to whiteflies.
- Control weeds in and around fields that can serve as alternative hosts, and avoid overlapping successive susceptible crops that allow populations to carry over.
- Reflective or living mulches are used in some vegetable systems to deter whiteflies from settling.

## Integrated Pest Management
- Combine clean planting material, regular monitoring, conservation of natural enemies, cultural sanitation, and targeted control only when populations or virus risk warrant it.
- Give particular attention to virus management, since controlling whitefly numbers alone may not prevent virus spread once infected plants and vectors are present.
- Physical barriers such as fine netting can protect nurseries and seedbeds where young, virus-susceptible plants are most vulnerable.
- Rotate to non-host crops where practical to break the pest and virus cycle.

## Biological Control
- Natural enemies include parasitic wasps (such as *Encarsia* and *Eretmocerus* species) that attack whitefly nymphs, as well as predatory beetles, lacewings, and predatory bugs.
- Entomopathogenic fungi can infect whiteflies, particularly under humid conditions.
- Conserve these natural enemies by avoiding unnecessary broad-spectrum insecticide applications, which often kill beneficial insects and can trigger whitefly resurgence.

## Chemical Control
- Insecticidal soaps and horticultural oils, applied directly to the underside of leaves where nymphs feed, can reduce populations with relatively low impact on beneficial insects.
- Chemical control should be reserved for infestations exceeding the locally recommended action threshold; note that whiteflies are prone to developing resistance, so chemicals are frequently ineffective when overused.
- Confirm currently approved and locally registered insecticide active ingredients with a national agricultural regulatory authority or extension officer, as registered products and resistance management recommendations vary by country and change over time.
- Rotate insecticide modes of action (chemical classes) across the season to reduce the risk of resistance development, and use targeted, thorough underside-of-leaf coverage rather than broad-spectrum whole-field spraying.

## Safety Considerations
- Follow label instructions for any pesticide product regarding dosage, application timing, and pre-harvest interval, particularly on leafy and fruiting vegetables harvested frequently.
- Wear appropriate personal protective equipment (gloves, mask, long sleeves) when applying any chemical control product.
- Avoid spraying flowering crops during pollinator activity hours to protect bees and other pollinators.
- Avoid spraying during windy conditions to prevent chemical drift onto non-target areas, water sources, or neighbouring fields.
- Store all pesticide products securely away from children, food, and water sources, and dispose of empty containers as directed on the label.
- If whitefly populations persist despite treatment, or if a viral disease is suspected in the crop, consult an agricultural extension officer or plant health specialist for further diagnosis, as virus problems require a different management approach from direct pest control.
