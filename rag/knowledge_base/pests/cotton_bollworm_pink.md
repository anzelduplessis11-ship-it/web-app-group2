# Pink Bollworm

## Pest Identification
- Scientific name: *Pectinophora gossypiella*, a moth in the family Gelechiidae; one of the most economically significant insect pests of cotton worldwide.
- Larvae (caterpillars) are the damaging stage: young larvae are creamy-white, while later instars develop a distinctive pink banding across the body, which gives the pest its common name; fully grown larvae reach about 10-12 mm.
- Adult moths are small (wingspan roughly 15-20 mm), grey-brown with darker mottled markings and narrow, fringed wings; they are nocturnal and rarely seen during the day.
- A key identifying field symptom is the "rosette flower" — a cotton flower whose petals fail to open properly and remain twisted or flared together because a young larva is feeding and webbing inside the bud.
- Larvae also cause "double seeds" — two cotton seeds webbed together with silk inside a damaged boll, another distinctive diagnostic sign.

## Life Cycle
- Eggs are laid singly or in small groups on flower buds (squares), bolls, or leaves, hatching in roughly 4-6 days depending on temperature.
- Newly hatched larvae bore quickly into a square or boll, usually leaving very little external evidence of entry, and feed internally, passing through four instars over about 12-16 days.
- A notable survival trait is diapause: fully grown larvae can enter a dormant state within a seed inside harvested seed cotton, ginning trash, or crop residue, allowing them to survive for months between cropping seasons — this makes post-harvest sanitation critical.
- Non-diapausing larvae pupate either within the boll, in plant trash, or in the soil, with the pupal stage lasting about 7-12 days.
- Adults live roughly 1-2 weeks, and a full non-diapausing generation from egg to adult takes approximately 25-35 days, allowing four to eight or more overlapping generations per year in warm climates.

## Crops Affected
- Cotton (*Gossypium* species) is the primary and most economically important host — see `crops/cotton.md`.
- Other plants in the mallow family (Malvaceae), such as okra and ornamental hibiscus, can serve as occasional alternate hosts, particularly where they grow near cotton fields or bridge the gap between cotton seasons.

## Damage Caused
- Larvae boring into young flower buds (squares) cause bud shedding and the characteristic rosette flower symptom, directly reducing the number of flowers available to set bolls.
- Larvae boring into developing bolls feed on the seeds and surrounding lint, causing internal damage that is often invisible from the outside until the boll is opened.
- Feeding and webbing inside bolls stains and mats the lint together, lowering fibre grade and market value even where yield loss is modest.
- Damaged seed shows reduced germination viability, which is a particular concern for farmers saving their own seed for the next season.
- Bored bolls are also more prone to secondary fungal and bacterial infection (boll rot), compounding yield and quality losses.

## Monitoring Methods
- Deploy pheromone traps baited with the synthetic sex pheromone (commonly referred to by the trade term gossyplure) to monitor adult male moth activity and detect the onset and intensity of flight periods.
- Regularly scout fields for rosette flowers and dissect a sample of green bolls and squares from multiple plants to check for larvae, entry holes, and internal damage.
- Record the percentage of damaged squares/bolls or larvae per boll and compare against locally recommended action thresholds, which vary by region, crop stage, and cotton value; confirm current thresholds with an extension officer.
- Increase scouting frequency during boll development, since this is the period of greatest economic risk from internal feeding.

## Prevention
- Destroy cotton stalks and crop residues promptly and thoroughly immediately after the final harvest, since diapausing larvae surviving in old plant material and trash are a primary source of the following season's infestation.
- Avoid growing off-season, ratoon, or volunteer cotton, which provides a "green bridge" allowing the pest to survive between the main cropping seasons.
- Coordinate planting and crop termination dates across a growing area where possible, so that host plants are not continuously available across an extended period.
- Use certified, pest-free seed, and where locally approved, deep ploughing after harvest can help expose overwintering pupae to predation and desiccation.
- Rotate cotton with non-host crops to interrupt the pest's continuity on a given field.

## Integrated Pest Management
- Combine strict post-harvest sanitation (the single most important cultural practice for this pest, given its diapause survival strategy), pheromone-based monitoring and mating disruption, conservation of natural enemies, and threshold-based chemical use.
- Mating disruption, using multiple pheromone dispensers distributed through a field to confuse males and reduce successful mating, has been used effectively in some cotton-growing regions as a lower-impact alternative or complement to insecticide sprays.
- Where approved and available, Bt cotton varieties expressing proteins toxic to lepidopteran larvae can substantially reduce the need for insecticide sprays against this pest; confirm the availability and local regulatory status of such varieties with an extension officer.
- Time any intervention to target eggs and newly hatched larvae before they bore into squares or bolls, since larvae become very difficult to reach with contact treatments once inside.

## Biological Control
- Egg parasitoid wasps in the genus *Trichogramma* are mass-reared and released in some cotton production systems to attack pink bollworm eggs before larvae can bore into the plant.
- General predators, including lacewings, predatory bugs, spiders, and ants, contribute to natural suppression of eggs and young larvae in the field.
- Biopesticides based on *Bacillus thuringiensis* (Bt) can be applied against young larvae before they enter the boll, and generally have a lower impact on beneficial insects than broad-spectrum chemical insecticides.
- Conserve natural enemy populations by avoiding unnecessary broad-spectrum insecticide applications, which can trigger secondary pest outbreaks (for example, of mites or aphids) once beneficial insects are removed.

## Chemical Control
- Chemical control is most effective when targeted at eggs and early instar larvae before they bore into squares or bolls, since larvae feeding inside the plant are largely shielded from contact insecticides.
- Pink bollworm has developed resistance to several insecticide classes in various regions historically, so chemical control should be used judiciously and always guided by monitoring and threshold data rather than routine calendar spraying.
- Confirm currently approved and locally registered insecticide active ingredients with a national agricultural regulatory authority or extension officer, as registered products and resistance management recommendations vary by country and change over time.
- Where chemical treatment is warranted, rotate insecticide modes of action (chemical classes) across the season and avoid repeated use of the same class to reduce the risk of resistance development.
- Avoid unnecessary or excessive spraying frequency on cotton generally, since this crop is prone to secondary pest flare-ups (such as mites and whitefly) when natural enemy populations are disrupted by heavy insecticide use.

## Safety Considerations
- Always follow label instructions for any pesticide product, including dosage, application method, and pre-harvest interval.
- Use appropriate personal protective equipment (gloves, mask, long sleeves, eye protection) when handling and applying any chemical pesticide, particularly given the historically high spray frequency on cotton in some regions.
- Avoid spraying during periods of pollinator activity, since cotton flowers are visited by bees and other beneficial insects.
- Avoid spraying during windy conditions to prevent chemical drift onto non-target areas, water sources, or neighbouring fields.
- Store pesticides securely, away from food, water sources, and children, and dispose of empty containers as directed on the product label.
- Consult an agricultural extension officer if an infestation persists despite treatment, as this may indicate resistance, insufficient sanitation of crop residues, or reinvasion from neighbouring untreated fields.
