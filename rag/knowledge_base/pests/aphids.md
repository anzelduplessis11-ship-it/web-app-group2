# Aphids

## Pest Identification
- Small, soft-bodied insects, typically 1-4 mm long, ranging in colour from green, yellow, black, to brown or pink depending on species.
- Pear-shaped body with two characteristic tail-like tubes (cornicles) projecting from the rear of the abdomen.
- Found in clusters on the underside of leaves, on stems, and on developing shoots and flower buds.
- Both winged and wingless forms occur; winged forms appear when colonies are overcrowded or host quality declines, enabling dispersal to new plants.

## Life Cycle
- Aphids reproduce very rapidly; many species can reproduce asexually (without mating), with females giving birth to live young (nymphs) rather than laying eggs, in warm climates typical of most of Africa.
- Nymphs mature to reproductive adults in as little as 7-10 days under warm conditions.
- A single female can produce dozens of offspring over her lifespan, leading to rapid population buildup, especially in warm, dry weather.
- Populations can decline rapidly when natural enemies build up or when weather turns cool and wet, which favours fungal diseases of aphids.

## Crops Affected
- Very wide host range including maize, sorghum, beans, tomato, cabbage, citrus, cotton, and many vegetable and cereal crops.
- Certain aphid species show preference for specific crop families, but many are generalists.

## Damage Caused
- Direct damage from sap-sucking feeding causes leaf curling, yellowing, stunted growth, and distorted new growth.
- Heavy infestations can reduce yield directly through plant stress, particularly in young plants.
- Aphids excrete a sugary substance called honeydew, which promotes the growth of black sooty mould on leaves, reducing photosynthesis.
- Critically, aphids are vectors for numerous plant viruses (e.g., bean common mosaic virus, various potato and vegetable viruses), and this indirect damage is often more economically significant than direct feeding damage.

## Monitoring Methods
- Inspect the underside of leaves and growing tips weekly, especially during warm, dry periods when populations build up fastest.
- Look for clusters of aphids, sticky honeydew residue, sooty mould, and the presence of ants (which often tend aphid colonies for their honeydew).
- Use yellow sticky traps to monitor winged aphid movement into a field.

## Prevention
- Avoid excessive nitrogen fertilisation, which promotes lush new growth that is particularly attractive to aphids.
- Encourage natural enemy populations (ladybird beetles, hoverfly larvae, lacewings, parasitic wasps) by minimizing unnecessary broad-spectrum insecticide use.
- Use reflective mulches in some vegetable systems to deter winged aphids from landing.
- Remove and destroy heavily infested plant material promptly to prevent buildup and spread.

## Integrated Pest Management
- Combine regular monitoring, conservation of natural enemies, cultural practices, and targeted control only when populations exceed a damaging threshold.
- Consider the presence of virus-susceptible crops nearby, since even low aphid populations can cause significant indirect damage through virus transmission in these cases.
- Companion planting with strongly scented plants (e.g., certain alliums or aromatic herbs) is used in some traditional and small-scale systems, though effectiveness varies and should be considered a supplementary rather than primary control measure.

## Biological Control
- Ladybird beetles (both adults and larvae) are highly effective aphid predators.
- Lacewing larvae and hoverfly larvae also consume large numbers of aphids.
- Parasitic wasps lay eggs inside aphids, eventually killing them and leaving a characteristic swollen, bronze-coloured "mummy."
- Conserve these natural enemies by avoiding broad-spectrum insecticide applications when populations of natural enemies are already present and active.

## Chemical Control
- Insecticidal soaps and horticultural oils can control aphids with relatively low impact on beneficial insects when applied directly to aphid colonies.
- For severe infestations, confirm currently approved and locally registered insecticide active ingredients with a national agricultural regulatory authority or extension officer.
- Targeted, spot-treatment applications to infested areas are preferable to broad-spectrum whole-field spraying, to conserve natural enemies elsewhere in the field.

## Safety Considerations
- Follow label instructions for any pesticide product regarding dosage, application timing, and pre-harvest interval.
- Wear appropriate personal protective equipment when applying any chemical control product.
- Avoid spraying flowering crops during pollinator activity hours to protect bees and other pollinators.
- Store all pesticide products securely away from children, food, and water sources.
- If aphid populations persist despite treatment or if a viral disease is suspected in the crop, consult an agricultural extension officer or plant health specialist for further diagnosis.
