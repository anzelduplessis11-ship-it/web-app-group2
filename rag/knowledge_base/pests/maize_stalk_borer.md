# Maize Stalk Borer

## Pest Identification
- Scientific names: the African maize stalk borer *Busseola fusca* and the spotted stalk borer *Chilo partellus* are the two most important species affecting cereals in Africa; both are commonly referred to as stalk borers.
- Larvae are the damaging stage: *Busseola fusca* larvae are creamy-white to pinkish with a dark head, growing to approximately 2-3 cm long; *Chilo partellus* larvae are creamy-white with a pattern of dark spots along the body and a brown head.
- Adults are small, dull grey-brown to straw-coloured moths with a wingspan of roughly 2-3 cm; they are active at night and are rarely noticed by farmers.
- Eggs are laid in rows or clusters, often behind leaf sheaths or on the underside of leaves, and are difficult to see without close inspection.

## Life Cycle
- Egg stage: approximately 4-10 days.
- Larval stage: typically 2-5 weeks, passing through several instars; young larvae first feed in the leaf whorl before boring into the stem.
- Pupal stage: about 1-2 weeks during the growing season; late-season larvae may enter a resting (diapause) state inside crop stubble and stalks over the dry season.
- Adult moth stage: a few days to about two weeks; females can lay from dozens up to several hundred eggs.
- Multiple overlapping generations can occur in a single season, with populations often carried over between seasons in unharvested stalks and residues.

## Crops Affected
- Primarily maize and sorghum.
- Also attacks millet, sugarcane, rice, and various wild grasses, which can serve as reservoir hosts between crops.

## Damage Caused
- Young larvae produce characteristic "window paning" and rows of small holes ("shot-holing") in the leaves as they feed in the whorl before tunnelling.
- Older larvae bore into the stem, creating tunnels that disrupt the movement of water and nutrients, weaken the plant, and can cause the central shoot to die ("deadheart") in young plants.
- Tunnelled stems are prone to lodging (breaking and falling over), especially in wind.
- Larvae may also bore into the cob, damaging developing grain and creating entry points for storage pests and fungal infection.

## Monitoring Methods
- Scout fields weekly from the early whorl stage, checking at least 20 plants at several points across the field.
- Look for window paning, shot-hole damage, and frass (sawdust-like excrement) in the whorl, as well as deadheart in young plants.
- Split a sample of stems later in the season to check for tunnelling and the presence of live larvae.
- Use pheromone traps where available to track adult moth flight activity and time scouting, and follow locally recommended action thresholds, as these vary by region.

## Prevention
- Plough under, burn where permitted, or otherwise destroy crop residues and stubble after harvest to kill overwintering larvae, since carry-over in stalks is a major source of reinfestation (see `crops/maize.md`).
- Plant early and uniformly within a community to avoid a prolonged supply of young, vulnerable plants.
- Remove and destroy volunteer maize and nearby wild grass hosts that harbour the pest between seasons.
- Maintain balanced soil fertility so plants grow vigorously and can better tolerate some borer damage (see `crops/maize.md`).

## Integrated Pest Management
- Combine field sanitation, timely planting, biological control, resistant or tolerant varieties, and threshold-based chemical control rather than relying on any single method.
- Push-pull systems, which intercrop a repellent plant with the cereal and plant an attractive trap grass around the border, have shown effectiveness against stalk borers in some regional trials and may be considered based on local extension recommendations.
- Handpicking egg masses and applying control while larvae are still feeding in the whorl (before they bore into the stem) improves results, since larvae inside stems are protected from most treatments.
- Use stalk borer-tolerant maize varieties where locally available.

## Biological Control
- Natural enemies include parasitic wasps (such as *Cotesia* species that attack larvae and *Telenomus* species that attack eggs) and various predators; some parasitoids have been introduced and established in parts of Africa for classical biological control.
- Conserve natural enemy populations by avoiding unnecessary broad-spectrum insecticide applications.
- Biopesticides based on *Bacillus thuringiensis* (Bt) can be effective against young larvae in the whorl and generally have a lower impact on beneficial insects than broad-spectrum chemical insecticides.

## Chemical Control
- Chemical control should be reserved for infestations exceeding the locally recommended action threshold and applied while larvae are still in the whorl, since larvae that have bored into the stem are largely protected from sprays.
- Whorl applications (directing product into the funnel of the plant) are more effective than general foliar sprays for reaching young larvae.
- Confirm currently approved and locally registered insecticide active ingredients with a national agricultural regulatory authority or extension officer, as registered products and resistance management recommendations vary by country and change over time.
- Rotate insecticide modes of action (chemical classes) across the season to reduce the risk of resistance development.

## Safety Considerations
- Always follow label instructions for any pesticide product, including dosage, application method, and pre-harvest interval.
- Use appropriate personal protective equipment (gloves, mask, long sleeves) when handling and applying any chemical pesticide, and take particular care when placing product into the plant whorl by hand.
- Keep children and animals away from treated fields for the period specified on the product label.
- Avoid spraying during windy conditions to prevent chemical drift onto non-target areas, water sources, or neighbouring fields.
- Store pesticides securely, away from food, water sources, and children, and dispose of empty containers as directed on the label.
- Consult an agricultural extension officer if an infestation persists despite treatment, as this may indicate resistance or misapplication, and severe or unusual outbreaks should be reported to local agricultural authorities for regional monitoring.
