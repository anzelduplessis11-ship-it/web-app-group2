# Grain Weevils and Borers

## Pest Identification
- Scientific names: the maize weevil *Sitophilus zeamais* and the closely related rice weevil *Sitophilus oryzae*, together with the larger grain borer *Prostephanus truncatus*, are among the most important storage pests of maize and other cereals in Africa.
- Grain weevils (*Sitophilus* species) are small (approximately 3-4 mm long), reddish-brown to dark brown beetles with a distinct long snout (rostrum); the larvae are legless, whitish grubs that develop hidden inside individual grains.
- The larger grain borer (*Prostephanus truncatus*) is a dark brown, cylindrical beetle about 3-4 mm long with a head tucked under the body; it bores into grain and produces large quantities of grain dust as it tunnels.
- Signs of infestation include small round exit holes in grains, powdery grain dust, adult beetles moving in the grain, and grain that feels light and hollow (see `storage/grain_storage.md`).

## Life Cycle
- Grain weevils lay eggs inside individual grains; the female bores a small hole, deposits an egg, and seals it, so the developing larva feeds hidden within the grain.
- The larger grain borer bores into grain and stored produce, and both adults and larvae tunnel and feed, generating characteristic grain dust.
- Egg, larval, and pupal development takes place largely inside the grain over roughly 4-6 weeks under warm conditions, so early infestations are easily overlooked.
- Adults are relatively long-lived and continue to reproduce in storage, allowing populations to build up rapidly and continuously in warm store conditions.
- Because much of the life cycle is concealed inside grain, infestations often begin in the field or early in storage and intensify over the storage period.

## Crops Affected
- Primarily stored maize (see `crops/maize.md`); the maize weevil and larger grain borer are especially damaging to stored maize on the cob and as shelled grain.
- Also affect stored rice (see `crops/rice.md`), sorghum, wheat, and other cereals, and the larger grain borer can additionally damage dried cassava (see `crops/cassava.md`).

## Damage Caused
- Larvae and adults feed inside and on grain, hollowing out kernels, reducing grain weight, and lowering both food value and market quality.
- The larger grain borer is particularly destructive, capable of causing heavy losses in stored maize over a storage season and reducing much of the grain to dust.
- Damaged grain has reduced germination if intended for seed and is more prone to mould and further pest attack.
- Feeding damage and the resulting broken grain and dust can create conditions that favour mould growth, including moulds associated with food-safety concerns; see `storage/grain_storage.md` and relevant `emergency_guides/` documents regarding aflatoxin risk.

## Monitoring Methods
- Inspect stored grain regularly (for example, every couple of weeks) by taking samples from different depths and checking for live insects, exit holes, grain dust, and hollow or damaged kernels (see `storage/grain_storage.md`).
- Sieve grain samples to reveal insects and dust that are not visible on the surface, and note any warming or musty smell in the store, which can indicate active infestation.
- Where available, pheromone traps can help detect the larger grain borer and monitor its activity around stores.
- Act promptly at the first sign of infestation, since populations build up quickly and losses accelerate over time.

## Prevention
- Dry grain thoroughly before storage to a safe low moisture level, since insect and mould problems are much worse in damp grain (see `harvesting/` and `storage/grain_storage.md` guidance).
- Clean and, where appropriate, treat the empty store or granary before the new harvest, and remove old, infested grain and residues that carry pests over between seasons.
- Store grain in clean, dry, well-sealed containers; hermetic (airtight) storage bags and containers can control storage insects by depriving them of oxygen and are widely used by smallholders (see `storage/grain_storage.md`).
- Harvest and shell promptly and avoid leaving mature maize in the field, since field infestation, especially by the larger grain borer, can carry into storage.
- Store only clean, undamaged, well-dried grain, and keep new grain separate from older stocks.

## Integrated Pest Management
- Combine thorough drying, store hygiene, airtight or well-sealed storage, regular monitoring, and traditional and biological methods, reserving chemical grain protectants for situations where they are necessary and approved.
- Hermetic storage is a cornerstone of integrated storage pest management for smallholders because it controls insects without chemicals when bags or containers are properly sealed (see `storage/grain_storage.md`).
- Traditional practices such as storing grain on the cob in well-ventilated cribs, mixing grain with inert materials such as ash or sand, or using botanical materials are used in many smallholder systems and may be combined with other measures; their effectiveness varies and they are best treated as supplementary.
- Because grain is a food product, prioritise non-chemical methods and strictly limit any chemical use to products and rates approved for stored grain.

## Biological Control
- Natural enemies include predatory and parasitic insects (such as certain predatory beetles and parasitic wasps) that attack storage pests; the introduced predator *Teretrius nigrescens* has been used against the larger grain borer in parts of Africa.
- Biological control in farm-level storage is generally supplementary, since good hygiene, drying, and sealed storage are the most reliable smallholder tools.
- Avoid conditions (such as damp, dirty stores) that allow pest populations to overwhelm any natural regulation, and maintain the clean, dry, sealed conditions that best suppress storage pests.

## Chemical Control
- Only insecticides specifically registered and labelled for treatment of stored grain intended for consumption may be used on grain, since many field insecticides are unsafe on food and must never be applied to stored produce.
- Chemical grain protectants and store fumigation should be reserved for situations where non-chemical methods are insufficient, and fumigation in particular should generally be carried out only by trained, qualified operators because of its serious safety hazards.
- Confirm currently approved and locally registered stored-grain products and permitted uses with a national agricultural regulatory authority or extension officer, as registrations and resistance management recommendations vary by country and change over time.
- Where repeated treatment is needed, rotate approved modes of action (chemical classes) to reduce the risk of resistance development, and always observe the required interval between treatment and consumption.

## Safety Considerations
- Grain is food, so extreme care is required: never treat stored grain with insecticides that are not specifically registered and labelled as safe for stored grain intended for consumption, and always follow label dosage and pre-consumption waiting periods exactly.
- Fumigants used against storage pests are highly toxic and can be fatal if misused; fumigation should be handled only by trained, qualified operators using appropriate equipment, and treated stores must be properly sealed, signposted, and ventilated before re-entry.
- Use appropriate personal protective equipment (gloves, mask, long sleeves) when handling and applying any grain protectant, and keep children and animals well away from treatment areas.
- Store all pesticide products securely, away from food, water sources, and children, and keep them entirely separate from grain not being treated; dispose of empty containers as directed on the label.
- Do not consume or sell grain that has been treated with an unapproved product, and discard grain showing heavy mould, since mouldy grain may carry food-safety hazards such as aflatoxins (see `storage/grain_storage.md` and relevant `emergency_guides/` documents).
- Consult an agricultural extension officer if storage infestations persist despite good management, and seek qualified advice before any fumigation or if food-safety contamination such as aflatoxin is suspected.
