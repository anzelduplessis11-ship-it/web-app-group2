# Red-Billed Quelea

## Pest Identification
The red-billed quelea (*Quelea quelea*) is a small, sparrow-sized weaver bird (family Ploceidae) and is considered the most numerous wild bird species in the world, with the African continental population estimated in the billions. Its enormous numbers and habit of forming vast, dense flocks make it one of the most significant bird pests of small-grain cereals in sub-Saharan Africa.

- **Size and general appearance.** Adults are approximately 11-13 cm long, similar in size and build to a house sparrow, with a short, conical, seed-eating bill.
- **Breeding males** develop a distinctive black or, in some populations, white facial mask bordered by a pinkish wash on the head and breast, and a bill that turns bright red to orange-red during the breeding season.
- **Females and non-breeding males** are duller, streaked brown above and buff below, similar to many other small seed-eating birds, and are harder to distinguish at a distance; the bill is typically a duller yellowish or dull red.
- **Flocking behaviour** is the single most important identifying feature: quelea move, feed, roost, and breed in extremely dense flocks that can contain many thousands to millions of birds, creating a distinctive swirling, smoke-like appearance in flight and a loud, continuous chattering call at roosts and colonies.
- **Nests** are small, woven, oval structures built in dense colonies, often in thorny trees, shrubs, or reedbeds, with colonies sometimes containing hundreds of thousands of nests packed closely together.

## Life Cycle
Quelea are opportunistic colonial breeders whose reproduction is tightly linked to rainfall and the availability of ripening wild grass seed, rather than following a fixed calendar.

- **Breeding trigger.** Breeding is typically initiated by rainfall that stimulates a flush of grass growth, since quelea need abundant green grass seed and insects (for feeding chicks) to breed successfully.
- **Colony formation.** Flocks settle in suitable trees, shrubs, or reedbeds and build nests colonially over a period of a few days, with an entire colony often synchronising nest-building, egg-laying, and hatching closely in time.
- **Eggs.** A clutch typically consists of two to four eggs, incubated for approximately 12-13 days.
- **Nestling and fledging.** Chicks fledge roughly two weeks after hatching, and the entire cycle from colony settlement to fledged young can be completed in about four to six weeks.
- **Nomadic movement.** After breeding, flocks move on in search of the next area of ripening grass seed or, unfortunately for farmers, ripening cereal crops, and can travel long distances between roosting, breeding, and feeding sites within a season.
- **Population dynamics.** Because breeding success is closely tied to rainfall and grass seed abundance, quelea populations and the location of major outbreaks vary considerably from year to year and across regions, making forecasting and regional coordination central to management.

## Crops Affected
Quelea are primarily granivorous, favouring small, exposed grain heads that are easy to grip and husk:

- **Highly susceptible cereals**: sorghum, pearl and finger millet, wheat, rice, and teff, all of which have small grains presented in accessible panicles or heads.
- **Less susceptible cereals**: maize is generally less preferred once kernels harden, since the grain is enclosed in a husk and set in a hard cob, though quelea can damage maize at the earlier milk or soft-dough stage.
- **Pasture and wild grasses** are the natural food base for quelea outside of cropping seasons, and heavy reliance on wild grass seed means that flocks turn to cultivated cereals particularly when wild seed supplies decline or when crop ripening coincides with flock movements.

## Damage Caused
- Quelea flocks can descend on a ripening cereal field in vast numbers and strip grain heads within hours to a few days, and a large flock can destroy a smallholder's entire field in a single visit.
- Damage is concentrated at the grain-filling to ripening stage, when seed is soft enough to be husked and swallowed easily; earlier vegetative growth stages are largely unaffected.
- Because flocks are highly mobile and nomadic, damage is unpredictable in timing and location, often striking with little warning and moving on to a neighbouring field or district within days.
- Fields located close to quelea roosting or breeding colonies, or along known flight paths between roost and feeding sites, face substantially higher risk.
- Beyond direct grain loss, farmers may also incur costs and lost labour time from extended bird-scaring efforts during the vulnerable ripening period.

## Monitoring Methods
- **Locate roosts and breeding colonies.** Identifying nearby quelea roosting sites (often in dense trees or reedbeds) and breeding colonies gives early warning of a population that may later move onto ripening crops.
- **Track flock movements and timing.** Note the direction, size, and frequency of flocks passing over or near fields, especially as cereal crops approach grain-filling and ripening.
- **Follow rainfall and vegetation conditions.** Since breeding follows good rains and grass seed flushes, unusually good rains in known quelea breeding areas can signal a higher risk season regionally.
- **Coordinate with national and regional bird control agencies.** Several countries maintain quelea surveillance and control programmes (some coordinated regionally) that track roost and colony locations and issue advisories; reporting large flocks or new colonies to local agricultural or wildlife authorities feeds into this wider early-warning effort.
- **Time scouting to crop stage.** Increase vigilance specifically from the milk stage through to harvest of susceptible cereals, since this is the only period when the crop is at meaningful risk.

## Prevention
- **Synchronised, timely planting** within a community reduces the length of time that vulnerable, ripening grain is available in the landscape, making it harder for flocks to find an extended food supply and somewhat reducing the risk to any single field.
- **Crop and variety choice.** Where quelea pressure is chronic, growing less preferred crops such as maize, or bird-tolerant/less palatable cereal varieties where available, can reduce relative risk, though this must be balanced against other agronomic needs.
- **Avoid planting susceptible cereals immediately adjacent to known roosting or breeding sites**, where practical, since proximity greatly increases exposure.
- **Bird-scaring measures** such as noise-makers (drums, clappers, gas-fired bangers where available), visual deterrents (reflective tape, moving scarecrows, flags), and active human scaring at dawn and dusk during the ripening period can reduce, though rarely eliminate, losses from small to moderate flocks.
- **Netting** of small, high-value plots is effective but costly and generally practical only for limited areas.
- **Community bird-scaring rotas**, where neighbouring farmers coordinate scaring effort across a wider area during the risk period, are more effective than isolated individual efforts against a highly mobile flocking pest.

## Integrated Pest Management
Quelea management, like desert locust management, combines farm-level deterrence with coordinated regional surveillance and control, because the scale and mobility of major flocks exceed what any individual farmer can address alone.

- Farm-level bird-scaring, timely planting, and crop choice reduce exposure and losses from smaller, more localised flocks.
- Community-level coordination of scaring efforts and harvest timing improves protection against larger flocks moving through a district.
- Regional and national surveillance of roosting and breeding colonies allows authorities to identify high-risk colonies before birds disperse to feed on ripening crops, enabling preventive action at the source rather than reactive defence of individual fields.
- Conservation of natural predators and avoidance of unnecessary, uncoordinated chemical control support longer-term balance, while still allowing decisive government-led intervention against confirmed, large damaging colonies.
- As with other landscape-scale migratory pests, the guiding principle is that early, coordinated action against colonies is more effective and efficient than dispersed, last-minute defence of individual ripening fields.

## Biological Control
- **Birds of prey** (various hawks, falcons, and other raptors) prey on adult quelea and can take a meaningful toll at roosts and colonies, and their presence should be encouraged rather than disturbed where compatible with other farm activities.
- **Snakes and other predators** at nesting colonies also consume eggs and nestlings, contributing to natural population regulation.
- **Limits of natural control.** Given the extraordinary reproductive output and sheer population size of quelea, natural predation alone is not sufficient to prevent major crop-damaging flocks, particularly following a season of exceptionally successful breeding.
- Farmers can support natural regulation by conserving raptor habitat (tall trees, undisturbed perches) near farmland without expecting it to substitute for active crop protection during high-risk periods.

## Chemical Control
Large-scale chemical control of quelea, where undertaken, targets roosting or breeding colonies directly and is carried out exclusively by national or regional wildlife and plant protection authorities using specialised equipment; it is not a tool available to, or appropriate for, individual smallholders.

- Historically, control programmes in various countries have used organophosphate-class avicides applied by trained teams directly to nocturnal roosts or breeding colonies under strict regulatory oversight, aiming to reduce the population at its most concentrated and accessible point rather than attempting to protect individual fields.
- Any chemical intervention of this kind requires specific authorisation, trained personnel, and careful siting away from water bodies, human settlements, and non-target wildlife habitats, and is guided by current national wildlife and pesticide regulations.
- At the individual farm level, the appropriate and legal response to a quelea threat is non-chemical: scaring, timing, and reporting flock and colony sightings to the responsible agricultural or wildlife authority so that any warranted colony-level action can be assessed and carried out by qualified teams.
- Farmers must never attempt to poison or otherwise chemically control wild bird flocks or colonies independently, both because it is generally illegal without authorisation and because informally applied avicides pose serious risks to human health, non-target wildlife, and food safety.

## Safety Considerations
- Do not attempt independent chemical control of quelea flocks or colonies; report significant flocks, roosts, or colonies to the local agricultural extension office or wildlife authority instead.
- If official control operations are carried out near your farm, follow all instructions regarding safe distances, timing, and any restrictions on grazing livestock or harvesting crops in the vicinity during and after treatment.
- Avoid consuming quelea or other birds that may have been exposed to avicide treatments, as this poses a poisoning risk.
- Keep children and livestock away from areas where official bird control teams are actively working.
- When using noise-makers or other scaring devices, ensure they are operated safely, particularly around children, and avoid actions that could start fires in dry vegetation during scaring campaigns.
- Take care when working near or disturbing large quelea roosts or colonies, as extremely dense concentrations of birds and droppings can create localised health and hygiene concerns; wash hands after handling any equipment used near roosting sites.
- Report unusually large flocks, new roosting sites, or breeding colonies promptly to agricultural or wildlife authorities so that regional monitoring and, where necessary, coordinated colony-level control can be planned in time to protect ripening crops across the district.
