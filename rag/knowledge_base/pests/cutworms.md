# Cutworms

## Overview
Cutworms are the soil-dwelling caterpillars of several species of night-flying moths, and they are among the most damaging early-season pests of newly transplanted seedlings and young direct-seeded crops across Africa. Their common name comes from their most characteristic behaviour: at night they cut through the stems of young plants at or just below the soil surface, felling seedlings like tiny trees and often leaving the severed plant lying uneaten on the ground. Because a single larva can destroy several seedlings in one night, and because they attack at the vulnerable establishment stage, cutworms can cause sudden and severe gaps in a stand, sometimes forcing farmers to replant entire sections of a field.

Cutworms are especially problematic in fields recently cleared from grass or weeds, in the first crop after fallow, and where soil is moist and rich in organic matter. For African smallholders producing vegetables such as tomato, cabbage, and onion, as well as maize, beans, and other field crops, the greatest risk is in the first two to three weeks after emergence or transplanting. This document explains how to recognise cutworms, understand their hidden habits, scout for them, and manage them through cultural practices, biological control, and, only where necessary, targeted intervention. Because cutworms hide in the soil by day and feed only at night, prevention and timely detection are far more effective than reactive spraying.

## Pest Identification
Cutworms are secretive, so identification often depends as much on recognising the damage and finding the hidden larvae as on seeing the insect in the open.

- **Eggs.** Small, round to dome-shaped, and ribbed, laid singly or in small clusters on low-growing plants, weeds, crop residues, or the soil surface. They are white to pale yellow when laid, darkening before hatching. Eggs are rarely noticed by farmers because of their small size and hidden placement.
- **Larvae (cutworms).** The damaging stage. Fully grown larvae are 3 to 5 centimetres long, plump, smooth, and greasy or dull in appearance, ranging in colour from grey and dull brown to nearly black, sometimes with faint stripes or spots. The most reliable behavioural sign is that when disturbed they curl tightly into a characteristic "C" shape and lie still. They are soft-bodied and are usually found in the top few centimetres of soil close to a recently cut plant, especially during the day.
- **Pupae.** Shiny reddish-brown and formed in an earthen cell in the soil.
- **Adults (moths).** Medium-sized, stout, greyish-brown to dark brown night-flying moths with a wingspan of roughly 3.5 to 5 centimetres. The forewings are mottled with darker markings, often including a kidney-shaped and a rounded spot, while the hind wings are paler. They are strongly attracted to light and are active at night.

The combination of seedlings cut off at the base, the plant left lying nearby, and a plump, greasy larva that curls into a "C" when unearthed close to the damage is diagnostic of cutworms.

## Scientific Name
*Agrotis* species, order Lepidoptera, family Noctuidae (owlet moths). The most important species in African cropping systems include the black cutworm (*Agrotis ipsilon*), the common cutworm or turnip moth (*Agrotis segetum*), and other *Agrotis* species. The term "cutworm" is also loosely applied to some related noctuid genera with similar habits, but *Agrotis* species are the principal cutworms of concern.

## Life Cycle
Cutworms undergo complete metamorphosis with egg, larva, pupa, and adult stages. Duration depends strongly on temperature and moisture.

- **Egg stage.** Roughly 3 to 7 days. Females lay large numbers of eggs, often several hundred to well over a thousand, favouring weedy fields, moist soil, and areas with dense low vegetation or crop residue.
- **Larval stage.** The longest and most damaging stage, lasting approximately 3 to 5 weeks and passing through about six instars. Young larvae feed on leaves and cause minor surface damage, but from about the fourth instar they move to the soil, feed at night, and cut stems. Older, larger larvae cause the most severe cutting damage. During the day they shelter in the soil or under debris.
- **Pupal stage.** About 1 to 3 weeks in an earthen cell in the soil.
- **Adult stage.** Moths live roughly 1 to 2 weeks and are capable of migrating considerable distances, which allows sudden appearance in fields far from where the previous generation developed.

A complete generation takes about 6 to 9 weeks under warm conditions, and several generations can occur per year, though the damaging peaks usually coincide with the crop establishment period when young plants are abundant.

## Crops Affected
Cutworms are polyphagous and attack the seedlings of a very wide range of crops. Those most at risk for smallholders include:

- Vegetables: tomato, cabbage and other brassicas, onion, okra, pepper, and leafy greens (see `crops/tomato.md`, `crops/cabbage.md`, and `crops/onion.md`).
- Cereals: maize and sorghum seedlings (see `crops/maize.md` and `crops/sorghum.md`).
- Legumes: common beans, cowpea, and groundnut at the seedling stage (see `crops/common_beans.md`, `crops/cowpea.md`, and `crops/groundnut.md`).
- Root and tuber crops: young cassava and sweet potato transplants and the seedlings of many other crops.

Any young, tender-stemmed plant at the establishment stage is vulnerable, which is why fields planted soon after clearing grassland or weedy fallow are at particular risk.

## Damage Caused
Cutworm damage is distinctive and usually appears suddenly overnight.

- **Stem cutting.** The most typical and damaging symptom: young plants are severed at or just below the soil line, and the toppled seedling often lies on the surface nearby, wilting. A single larva may cut several plants in one night, so damage clusters in patches.
- **Stand gaps.** Because they attack at establishment, cutworms create bare patches and irregular gaps in the crop stand, reducing plant population and forcing costly replanting or gap-filling.
- **Leaf and surface feeding.** Younger larvae chew ragged holes and notches in leaves before they begin cutting stems, which can be an early warning of a building population.
- **Underground and stem-boring feeding.** Some species also feed on roots, tubers, and the base of stems, and certain "climbing" cutworm behaviour can damage foliage and buds higher on the plant.

Damage typically declines once plants pass the seedling stage and stems become woodier and too thick to cut, so the risk window is concentrated in the first few weeks after emergence or transplanting.

## Monitoring Methods
Because cutworms hide by day, monitoring relies on inspecting for damage, digging for larvae, and tracking adult activity.

- **Inspect at establishment.** From emergence or transplanting, walk the field regularly, especially in the early morning, looking for freshly cut or wilting seedlings and bare patches. Concentrate on fields recently cleared from grass or weeds and on low-lying, moist areas.
- **Dig for larvae.** Where a cut plant is found, gently scrape and dig the soil within a few centimetres around the base, ideally to a depth of 5 centimetres. Cutworms usually shelter close to the plants they have damaged. Finding curled "C"-shaped larvae confirms the pest and indicates continuing risk.
- **Count and map.** Record the number of cut plants and larvae found across several sample points to gauge the severity and whether damage is spreading.
- **Light and pheromone traps.** Because adults are strongly attracted to light and to species-specific pheromones, light traps and pheromone traps can detect moth flights and give advance warning of egg-laying so that scouting can be intensified before damage begins.
- **Thresholds.** A commonly cited guideline is to consider intervention when a small percentage of seedlings (for example, in the region of a few percent of plants cut) is affected and live larvae are present, but exact thresholds vary by crop and plant value. Confirm the appropriate action threshold for your crop with a qualified agricultural extension officer.

## Prevention
Cultural prevention is the backbone of cutworm management because it reduces egg-laying and starves young larvae before the crop is planted.

- **Clean seedbed and land preparation.** Prepare fields two to four weeks before planting and keep them free of weeds and volunteer plants during this period. Removing green vegetation deprives moths of egg-laying sites and starves any young larvae already present.
- **Ploughing and cultivation.** Ploughing exposes larvae and pupae to the sun, birds, and predators, and mechanically kills many. Cultivating a weedy field well before planting is one of the most effective preventive measures.
- **Control weeds around fields.** Because moths prefer weedy areas for egg-laying, controlling weeds in and around fields and along margins reduces the source population.
- **Avoid planting immediately after grassland.** The first crop after breaking grassland or long fallow is at highest risk, so allow time between clearing and planting and cultivate thoroughly.
- **Physical barriers on small plots.** For high-value transplants such as tomato and cabbage, protective collars (for example, a small ring of stiff paper, cardboard, or a cut plastic bottle) placed around the stem and pushed slightly into the soil can physically prevent cutting. Firming soil around transplants also helps.
- **Timely, vigorous seedlings.** Transplanting sturdy, well-hardened seedlings that establish quickly shortens the vulnerable window.

## Integrated Pest Management
IPM for cutworms integrates prevention, monitoring, and targeted response, recognising that the pest is most manageable before and during establishment.

- **Reduce the population before planting** through timely land preparation, ploughing, and weed control, which is more effective and cheaper than treating an active infestation.
- **Monitor closely at establishment** and dig to confirm larvae so that any intervention is justified and correctly timed.
- **Protect individual high-value plants** with collars and good transplanting practice.
- **Conserve natural enemies** that attack eggs and larvae by avoiding unnecessary broad-spectrum sprays.
- **Handpick at night on small plots.** Because larvae feed on the surface after dark, going out at night or in the early morning with a light to collect and destroy larvae around damaged plants can effectively protect small vegetable plots.
- **Use baits and targeted treatment** only where thresholds are exceeded, directing control to the soil surface around plants where larvae feed rather than blanket-spraying foliage.
- **Rotate crops** to avoid successive susceptible seedlings in the same field.

## Biological Control
A range of natural enemies helps suppress cutworm populations, and conserving them reduces the frequency of damaging outbreaks.

- **Parasitoids:** parasitic wasps (including braconids and ichneumonids) and tachinid flies attack cutworm larvae, and egg parasitoids attack the eggs.
- **Predators:** ground beetles (carabids) are important nocturnal predators of cutworm larvae in the soil; rove beetles, predatory ants, spiders, and birds (which follow ploughing to eat exposed larvae) also contribute. Domestic poultry allowed to forage on a field before planting can markedly reduce larvae.
- **Microbial and biopesticide options:** products based on *Bacillus thuringiensis* subsp. *kurstaki* (Bt) can control young larvae when they are still feeding on foliage, though they are less effective once larvae are large and feeding at the soil surface. Entomopathogenic nematodes (such as *Steinernema* and *Heterorhabditis* species) applied to moist soil can infect and kill cutworm larvae in the soil and are well suited to this soil-dwelling pest. Entomopathogenic fungi such as *Beauveria bassiana* and *Metarhizium anisopliae* also infect cutworms under suitable conditions.

Conserve these agents by ploughing to expose larvae to predators and birds, maintaining refuges for ground beetles, and avoiding unnecessary broad-spectrum insecticide use. Confirm the local availability and registration of any biopesticide or beneficial organism with a qualified agricultural extension officer.

## Chemical Control
Chemical control should be reserved for situations where scouting confirms live larvae and cutting damage exceeding the locally recommended action threshold, and it should be targeted rather than applied as a blanket foliar spray. Because cutworms feed at night on or near the soil surface and hide in the soil by day, treatments are most effective when directed at the base of plants and the surrounding soil surface, and when applied in the late afternoon or evening so that the product is present when larvae emerge to feed.

Where intervention is justified, poison bait formulations (a carrier such as bran mixed with an approved insecticide, broadcast around plants in the evening) are a targeted and efficient option that limits exposure of beneficial insects, and soil-surface-directed sprays or drenches around the base of transplants may also be used. Insecticide classes historically used against cutworms include certain pyrethroids and other soil-active products, but the specific active ingredients, formulations, and rates must be chosen according to current local registration.

Farmers must confirm which products are currently registered and approved for their crop, along with the correct dosage, application method, and pre-harvest interval, with a qualified agricultural extension officer or national plant protection authority, because registrations and recommendations vary by country and change over time. Rotate modes of action to reduce resistance risk, and never rely on chemicals alone. See `regulations/pesticide_safety_and_handling.md` for detailed safety guidance.

## Safety Considerations
- Always read and follow the product label, including dosage, protective equipment, application method, and the pre-harvest interval.
- Wear appropriate personal protective equipment (gloves, mask, long sleeves, boots) when handling and applying any pesticide, and when preparing poison baits.
- Poison baits are attractive and hazardous to poultry, other livestock, children, and wildlife; place baits carefully, keep children and animals away from treated areas, and do not leave bait accessible.
- Apply in calm conditions to avoid drift, and keep pesticides and baits away from water sources.
- Store all pesticides and prepared baits securely, away from food, water, and children, and dispose of empty containers safely.
- Because cutworm control often involves soil-directed treatments, take care to avoid contaminating irrigation water and follow all guidance in `regulations/pesticide_safety_and_handling.md`.
- If damage continues despite treatment, consult an extension officer rather than repeating or increasing applications.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: My tomato and cabbage seedlings are being cut off at the base overnight. What is causing it?**
A: This is the classic sign of cutworms. Confirm it by gently digging in the soil within a few centimetres of a cut plant in the morning; you will usually find a plump, greasy grey-to-black caterpillar that curls into a "C" shape when disturbed. Cutworms attack at night and hide in the soil by day, which is why you rarely see them in the act.

**Q: How can I protect my transplants without spraying?**
A: For high-value transplants, place a protective collar around each stem, such as a ring of stiff paper, cardboard, or a section of plastic bottle pushed slightly into the soil, to physically block cutting. Prepare the seedbed well ahead of planting, plough to expose larvae, control weeds, and handpick larvae at night around damaged plants. Letting poultry forage on the field before planting also reduces larvae.

**Q: Why does clearing grass or weeds before planting help so much?**
A: Cutworm moths prefer to lay eggs in weedy, grassy, moist ground, and young larvae feed on that vegetation. Preparing and weeding the field two to four weeks before planting starves the young larvae and removes egg-laying sites, while ploughing exposes larvae and pupae to birds and predators. This greatly reduces the population before your crop is even in the ground.

**Q: Is spraying the whole field the best solution?**
A: Usually not. Cutworms hide in the soil and feed at night at the base of plants, so blanket foliar spraying is often ineffective and wasteful, and it kills helpful ground beetles that eat cutworms. Where intervention is truly needed, targeted evening applications to the soil surface around plants, or poison baits, work better. Always confirm approved products and methods with your extension officer.

**Q: When are my crops safe from cutworms?**
A: The main risk is the first two to three weeks after emergence or transplanting, while stems are still tender. Once plants grow larger and their stems become woodier, cutworms can no longer cut them and damage drops off sharply. Getting seedlings through this early window with good establishment and protection is the key to avoiding losses.

**Q: Are there biological controls specifically suited to a soil pest like this?**
A: Yes. Because cutworms live in the soil, entomopathogenic nematodes (such as *Steinernema* and *Heterorhabditis* species) applied to moist soil are well suited to reaching and killing the larvae. Ground beetles are important natural predators, and *Bacillus thuringiensis* (Bt) can control young larvae feeding on foliage. Check local availability of these options with your extension officer.

**Q: Can cutworms come back later in the season?**
A: Cutworm moths can produce several generations a year and can migrate into fields, so later plantings and successive crops can be attacked, especially if fields are weedy. However, the most severe damage is almost always at crop establishment. Keeping fields clean, rotating crops, and monitoring each new planting will limit repeat problems.
