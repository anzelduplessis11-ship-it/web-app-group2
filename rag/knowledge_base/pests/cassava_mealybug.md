# Cassava Mealybug

## Pest Identification
- Scientific name: *Phenacoccus manihoti*, a small, soft-bodied insect in the family Pseudococcidae, native to South America and accidentally introduced to Africa in the early 1970s, where it caused one of the most serious food security crises in the continent's agricultural history before being brought under control.
- Adults and nymphs are pale pink to yellowish, soft, and covered with a white, mealy or powdery wax secretion, giving colonies a distinctive white, cottony appearance.
- Colonies form dense clusters at the growing points (shoot tips), young unfurled leaves, and leaf axils of the cassava plant, rather than spreading evenly across the whole plant.
- Winged males are small, fragile, short-lived, and rarely observed; females are wingless and immobile once settled, relying on wind, ants, and human movement of planting material for dispersal.

## Life Cycle
- Females produce eggs (commonly several hundred) within a cottony ovisac attached to the plant; this species can reproduce parthenogenetically (without mating), which allows populations to establish and expand rapidly even from a small founding population.
- Newly hatched nymphs, known as "crawlers," are the primary mobile and dispersal stage; they walk to new feeding sites, are carried by wind, are transported by ants that tend the colonies for honeydew, and are readily spread over long distances through infested cuttings used as planting material.
- Nymphs pass through several instars while feeding at growing points before reaching the sessile, wax-covered adult stage.
- A full generation takes roughly three to four weeks under warm conditions, and with continuous overlapping generations, populations can produce eight to ten or more generations per year in favourable climates.

## Crops Affected
- Cassava (*Manihot esculenta*) is essentially the exclusive host of this species — see `crops/cassava.md`.
- Because cassava is a staple food and income crop across much of sub-Saharan Africa, outbreaks of this pest have historically had severe consequences for household food security in affected regions.

## Damage Caused
- Sap removal at growing points causes shortened internodes, distorted and curled young leaves, and a characteristic stunted, compressed shoot-tip symptom often described as "bunchy top."
- Severe, sustained feeding can kill shoot tips outright, halting the plant's vertical growth and reducing the leaf area available for photosynthesis.
- Honeydew excreted by the colonies coats leaves and promotes the growth of black sooty mould, further reducing photosynthetic capacity.
- Before effective biological control was established, this pest was responsible for continent-wide cassava yield losses reported as severe as 60-80% in the most heavily affected fields, contributing to a major food security crisis across affected parts of Africa in the late 1970s and 1980s.
- Repeated infestation across successive growing seasons can also degrade the vigour of planting material (cuttings) taken from infested fields, carrying the problem forward into new plantings.

## Monitoring Methods
- Inspect the growing points, youngest unfurled leaves, and leaf axils of cassava plants regularly for white, waxy mealybug clusters, which is where colonies concentrate.
- Look for the characteristic bunchy top and stunted shoot symptom as an indicator of an established infestation, as well as sooty mould and honeydew on lower leaves.
- Check for ant activity around shoot tips, since ants often tend and protect mealybug colonies in exchange for honeydew, and their presence can indicate an active infestation.
- Inspect planting material (cuttings) before use, since infested cuttings are a major pathway for introducing the pest into previously clean fields.

## Prevention
- Use certified, pest-free planting material and avoid taking cuttings from fields known or suspected to be infested.
- Avoid moving cuttings from infested areas into clean fields or regions, since this is one of the primary ways the pest spreads over long distances.
- Remove and destroy severely infested shoot tips where practical to reduce the local source of crawlers.
- Maintain balanced soil fertility and adequate crop vigour, since healthy, actively growing plants can better tolerate moderate infestation levels than stressed plants.
- Where regionally recommended, participate in coordinated planting-material exchange programmes that verify cuttings are sourced from pest-free multiplication sites.

## Integrated Pest Management
- Cassava mealybug management in Africa is widely regarded as one of the most successful examples of classical biological control in the history of African agriculture, and this remains the cornerstone of management across the continent.
- Combine conservation of the established biological control agent with the use of clean planting material, field sanitation, and avoidance of unnecessary insecticide use, which could disrupt the natural enemy populations that now keep this pest at generally low levels across most of its African range.
- Continue routine monitoring even in areas with established biological control, since localized outbreaks can still occur, particularly where natural enemy populations have been disrupted by heavy pesticide use for other pests.

## Biological Control
- The parasitoid wasp *Anagyrus lopezi*, native to South America and deliberately introduced to Africa in the 1980s through an internationally coordinated classical biological control programme, parasitizes cassava mealybug nymphs and adults and has provided substantial, self-sustaining suppression of the pest across most of its African range without the need for ongoing chemical input.
- Predatory ladybird beetles (including species in the genera *Exochomus* and *Hyperaspis*) and lacewing larvae also feed on cassava mealybug colonies and contribute to natural control.
- Conserve these natural enemies, particularly *Anagyrus lopezi*, by avoiding unnecessary broad-spectrum insecticide applications on or near cassava fields, since disrupting this established biological control can lead to resurgence of the pest.

## Chemical Control
- Because biological control by *Anagyrus lopezi* is highly effective and self-sustaining across most affected regions, routine chemical control is rarely necessary or recommended for cassava mealybug and risks undermining the established natural enemy population.
- If a severe, localized outbreak occurs despite the presence of biological control (for example, following heavy insecticide use for another pest that disrupted natural enemies), consult an agricultural extension officer before considering any insecticide application.
- Where chemical treatment is genuinely warranted and locally approved, confirm currently registered active ingredients with a national agricultural regulatory authority or extension officer, and select products and application methods that minimize harm to established parasitoid and predator populations.
- Avoid routine or preventive insecticide spraying on cassava, since this crop's mealybug problem is best managed biologically, and unnecessary spraying can create new pest problems.

## Safety Considerations
- Always follow label instructions for any pesticide product, including dosage, application method, and pre-harvest interval, if chemical treatment is genuinely required.
- Use appropriate personal protective equipment (gloves, mask, long sleeves) when handling and applying any chemical control product.
- Avoid spraying during windy conditions to prevent chemical drift onto non-target areas, water sources, or neighbouring fields, and particularly to avoid harming established natural enemy populations in surrounding vegetation.
- Store all pesticide products securely away from children, food, and water sources.
- Consult an agricultural extension officer before treating any cassava mealybug outbreak, given the strong existing biological control programme, so that any intervention is appropriate and does not undermine long-term pest suppression across the wider area.
