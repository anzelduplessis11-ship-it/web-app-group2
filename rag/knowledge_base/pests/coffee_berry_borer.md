# Coffee Berry Borer

## Pest Identification
- Scientific name: *Hypothenemus hampei*, a small bark beetle in the family Curculionidae (subfamily Scolytinae), believed to be native to Central Africa and now distributed across virtually all coffee-growing regions of the world.
- Adult females are tiny, cylindrical, dark brown to black beetles approximately 1.4-1.8 mm long, with a hard, shiny exoskeleton that makes them difficult to see with the naked eye against dark, ripening berries.
- Adult males are considerably smaller, pale, wingless, flightless, and rarely leave the berry in which they developed; they are almost never seen outside a dissected berry.
- The insect spends nearly its entire life cycle concealed inside the coffee berry (cherry), which is the main reason it is so difficult to detect and control compared with pests that feed openly on foliage.
- Look for a single small, round entry hole (about the diameter of a pin) near the disc (calyx end) of the berry; a fine boring dust may be visible around the hole.

## Life Cycle
- Only mated adult females fly in search of new berries to colonize; they bore a single entrance tunnel near the disc end of the berry and begin excavating galleries inside the seed (bean).
- Once inside, the female lays a batch of eggs (typically 30-50, though this varies) within the gallery; the offspring develop entirely inside the berry.
- Reproduction is unusual: males are wingless and blind, mate with their sisters inside the same berry (sibling mating), and never disperse; the sex ratio produced is heavily skewed toward females (often around 10 females to 1 male), which greatly accelerates population growth.
- Complete development from egg to adult takes roughly 25-35 days under warm conditions (optimum around 27-30°C), with several overlapping generations possible within a single cropping season.

| Stage | Approximate duration | Location |
|---|---|---|
| Egg | 5-9 days | Inside gallery in the berry |
| Larva (3 instars) | 10-26 days | Boring through the seed |
| Pupa | 4-9 days | Inside the berry |
| Adult female | Several weeks to months | Inside berry, then dispersing to new berries once mated |

- Adult females can live for several months and continue laying eggs over an extended period, so a single colonized berry can seed further infestation for a long time if not removed.
- Because development occurs almost entirely inside the berry, the pest is largely protected from weather, contact insecticides, and many natural enemies once it has entered.

## Crops Affected
- Coffee is the only significant host: both Arabica (*Coffea arabica*) and Robusta (*Coffea canephora*) are attacked — see `crops/coffee.md`.
- All stages of berry development can be attacked, although the beetle strongly prefers berries that are mature or nearing maturity, since the seed tissue is soft enough to tunnel through but has begun to hold nutritional value.
- Berries left on the tree after the main harvest, and fallen berries on the ground, act as reservoirs that sustain populations between cropping cycles.

## Damage Caused
- The entry hole and internal tunnelling directly damage the coffee bean, reducing its weight, density, and market grade.
- Bored berries are prone to secondary infection by fungi (including *Fusarium* species) entering through the entrance hole, which can further degrade quality and, in some cases, contribute to mycotoxin contamination concerns.
- Heavily bored beans are frequently sorted out as defective ("black" or insect-damaged beans), directly reducing the proportion of exportable, high-grade coffee from an affected lot.
- Because damage occurs inside the bean, infestation is often underestimated until berries are cut open or processed, making it one of the most economically damaging pests of coffee worldwide.
- Cumulative losses across a season can be substantial where sanitation and monitoring are neglected, particularly in warm lowland coffee-growing areas where the beetle completes more generations per year.

## Monitoring Methods
- Set out alcohol-methanol baited traps (commonly a mixture resembling the beetle's aggregation cues) hung within the coffee canopy to monitor flight activity of dispersing females and detect the start and intensity of migration into a field.
- Regularly sample and dissect a representative number of berries per plant across the field (for example, a fixed number of berries from several plants at multiple points) and record the percentage that show entry holes or internal tunnelling.
- A commonly cited action threshold is around 5% berry infestation, though farmers should confirm current locally recommended thresholds with an extension officer, as these can vary by region and season.
- Pay close attention to field edges and areas near old, unharvested, or abandoned coffee trees, which often show the highest infestation levels first.

## Prevention
- Practice strict and timely harvesting: pick all ripe berries promptly rather than allowing them to remain on the tree, since over-ripe and lingering berries are prime breeding sites.
- Carry out a thorough gleaning round after the main harvest (sometimes called "pepena" or post-harvest picking) to remove any remaining ripe, overripe, or dried berries left on the trees.
- Collect and destroy fallen berries from the ground regularly throughout the season, as these continue to harbour developing beetles even after dropping.
- Maintain good field sanitation by removing volunteer coffee plants and abandoned or unmanaged coffee trees nearby that are not harvested and can serve as a continuous source of infestation.
- Moderate, well-managed shade and pruning that improves airflow and reduces excess humidity in the canopy can help reduce conditions favourable to the pest, although shade management should be balanced against other agronomic and quality goals for the specific growing region.

## Integrated Pest Management
- No single method reliably controls coffee berry borer; effective management combines strict harvest sanitation, monitoring with baited traps, conservation and augmentation of natural enemies, and targeted use of biological or chemical inputs only when needed.
- Because the pest is protected inside the berry for most of its life, prevention and sanitation (removing berries before or shortly after beetles enter) are far more effective than attempting to reach the insect once it has bored in.
- Regional and community-level coordination is valuable, since beetles readily disperse between neighbouring farms; area-wide sanitation and synchronized harvesting reduce the pool of available breeding sites across a landscape.
- Where available, some coffee genotypes and management practices that promote uniform, concentrated ripening can reduce the extended availability of susceptible berries that sustains high pest pressure.

## Biological Control
- Several parasitoid wasps native to Africa, including *Prorops nasuta*, *Cephalonomia stephanoderis*, and *Phymastichus coffea*, attack the borer's larvae, pupae, or adults inside the berry and have been used in classical biological control programmes in coffee-growing regions worldwide.
- The entomopathogenic fungus *Beauveria bassiana* is widely used as a biopesticide spray against adult beetles as they disperse to find new berries, since this is the brief window when the insect is exposed outside the fruit; effectiveness depends on humidity and thorough coverage timed to dispersal flights.
- Conserve these natural enemies by minimizing unnecessary broad-spectrum insecticide applications, which can disrupt established parasitoid populations built up over years.

## Chemical Control
- Chemical options are limited in effectiveness because larvae, pupae, and much of the adult stage are physically protected inside the berry; insecticides can only realistically target adult females during the short period when they are exposed on the berry surface searching for an entry point.
- Historically, organochlorine insecticides such as endosulfan were used against this pest, but these have been banned or heavily restricted in most countries due to environmental and health concerns; do not rely on outdated recommendations.
- Confirm currently approved and locally registered insecticide active ingredients with a national agricultural regulatory authority or extension officer before any chemical application, as registered products and resistance management guidance vary by country and change over time.
- Where chemical treatment is locally approved and warranted, timing applications to coincide with peak adult dispersal (identified through trap monitoring) improves effectiveness compared with calendar-based spraying.
- Rotate insecticide modes of action (chemical classes) across seasons if repeated treatment is necessary, to reduce the risk of resistance development.

## Safety Considerations
- Always follow label instructions for any pesticide product, including dosage, application method, and pre-harvest interval, which is particularly important on a crop where the bean itself is processed for human consumption.
- Use appropriate personal protective equipment (gloves, mask, long sleeves) when handling and applying any chemical or biological pesticide product.
- Avoid spraying during flowering periods when bees and other pollinators are actively visiting coffee blossoms, to minimize harm to pollinator populations.
- Avoid spraying during windy conditions to prevent drift onto neighbouring farms, water sources, or non-target areas.
- Store all pesticide and biopesticide products securely, away from food, water sources, and children.
- Consult an agricultural extension officer if infestation persists despite sanitation and trapping efforts, as this may indicate insufficient sanitation coverage, reinvasion from neighbouring unmanaged plots, or the need for a revised area-wide management plan.
