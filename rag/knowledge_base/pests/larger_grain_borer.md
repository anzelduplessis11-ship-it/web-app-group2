# Larger Grain Borer

## Overview
The larger grain borer is one of the most destructive storage pests of maize and dried cassava in sub-Saharan Africa. It is not native to the continent; it was accidentally introduced from Central America into Tanzania in the late 1970s and into Togo in West Africa in the early 1980s, and has since spread across much of eastern, southern, central, and western Africa. In some regions farmers gave it local nicknames referring to its speed and destructiveness, because it can reduce stored maize cobs to powder far faster than the more familiar grain weevils.

Unlike many storage insects, the larger grain borer bores aggressively into whole grain and even into the woody cob and dried cassava, generating large quantities of grain dust. It causes weight losses that can reach 30-40% or more in stored maize over a single storage season of a few months, and losses in dried cassava chips can be even higher. Because it can also breed in dry wood, it survives in the environment outside stores, making it difficult to eliminate. This document should be read together with `storage/grain_storage.md` and `storage/aflatoxin_prevention.md`, since insect damage and mould contamination are closely linked. It complements `pests/grain_weevils_and_borers.md`.

## Pest Identification
- The adult is a small, cylindrical, dark brown to almost black beetle about 3-4.5 mm long, with a hard, tube-shaped body.
- The head is bent downward and tucked under a hood-like front section of the body (the pronotum), so the head is not easily visible from above; the front of the pronotum carries rasp-like teeth.
- The rear end of the wing cases drops off steeply, giving the beetle a distinctly "cut-off" or truncated appearance at the back.
- Unlike grain weevils (*Sitophilus* species), the larger grain borer has no long snout; its cylindrical, snoutless shape is a key distinguishing feature.
- The larvae are small, white, C-shaped grubs with a brown head, found tunnelling inside grain, cobs, or the flour the pest produces.
- Signs of infestation: neat round bore holes in grains and cobs, and above all large amounts of fine, floury grain dust, which is far more abundant than the dust produced by weevils alone. Live beetles are often seen moving in and on the grain.

## Scientific Name
*Prostephanus truncatus* (Horn), family Bostrichidae (the auger or false powderpost beetles). It is commonly abbreviated as LGB.

## Life Cycle
- Eggs are laid in small chambers that the female bores into grain, cobs, or dried produce; a female can lay several dozen eggs over her life.
- Larvae hatch and feed within the grain or within the abundant flour created by the tunnelling adults, passing through several growth stages entirely hidden from view.
- Pupation takes place inside the grain or in the compacted flour, after which new adults emerge and immediately begin boring and reproducing.
- Under warm store conditions (around 30-32 degrees Celsius) the complete cycle from egg to adult takes roughly 25-40 days, so populations build up very quickly and generations overlap.
- Adults are long-lived and strong fliers, allowing them to disperse to new stores and to fields at harvest.
- Critically, the beetle can also breed in dry wood and woody plant material, so it maintains reservoir populations in the environment and in wooden granary structures even between harvests.

## Crops Affected
- Stored maize, especially maize stored on the cob, is the most seriously affected crop (see `crops/maize.md`); the beetle bores into both the grain and the cob.
- Dried cassava chips and dried cassava roots are highly susceptible and can suffer very heavy losses (see `crops/cassava.md`).
- Other stored cereals including sorghum, wheat, and to a lesser extent rice, as well as dried yam and some other dried starchy produce, can be attacked.
- The beetle also damages wooden storage structures, timber, and dried plant material, which helps it persist between seasons.

## Damage Caused
- Both adults and larvae bore into and tunnel through grain, hollowing out kernels and reducing them, and much of the surrounding grain, to fine powder.
- Weight losses in stored maize can be severe, commonly cited at 30-40% or more over several months of storage, and dried cassava losses can exceed this.
- Damaged grain loses food value, cooking quality, and market value, and grain intended for seed loses germination capacity.
- The large amount of dust, broken grain, and increased moisture created by feeding provides ideal conditions for storage moulds, including moulds linked to food-safety hazards such as aflatoxin (see `storage/aflatoxin_prevention.md`).
- Because the beetle attacks the cob directly, traditional cob storage in cribs, which offers some protection against weevils, is often heavily damaged by the larger grain borer.

## Monitoring Methods
- Inspect stored grain and cobs regularly, at least every one to two weeks, taking samples from different depths and locations in the store (see `storage/grain_storage.md`).
- Look specifically for abundant floury dust, round bore holes, hollow or powdery kernels, and live cylindrical beetles; the unusually large quantity of dust is a strong warning sign.
- Sieve grain samples to reveal beetles and dust hidden below the surface, and note any warming or musty smell.
- Aggregation pheromone traps are available and are useful for detecting the beetle's arrival and monitoring its activity around stores and communities; regional pest-monitoring programmes often use them.
- Act immediately at first detection, because this pest multiplies and destroys grain much faster than weevils.

## Prevention
- Harvest maize promptly once mature and do not leave cobs standing in the field, because field infestation carries the pest directly into storage.
- Dry grain thoroughly to a safe low moisture level before storage; shelling maize and drying the grain reduces the protection that the cob offers to the beetle (see `harvesting/harvesting_best_practices.md` and `storage/grain_storage.md`).
- Clean the empty store, granary, or crib thoroughly before each new harvest, removing all old grain, dust, cobs, and residues that carry the pest over between seasons.
- Repair or replace heavily infested wooden structures where possible, since the beetle breeds in wood.
- Store clean, undamaged, well-dried grain in sealed containers, and keep new grain separate from old stocks.
- Shelling maize and storing it in airtight (hermetic) bags or containers is one of the most effective preventive measures for smallholders.

## Integrated Pest Management
- Combine prompt harvest, thorough drying, strict store hygiene, airtight or well-sealed storage, regular monitoring, biological control, and, only where necessary, approved grain protectants.
- Hermetic (airtight) storage bags and containers are a cornerstone of control because they deprive the beetle of oxygen and can stop an infestation without chemicals when properly sealed (see `storage/grain_storage.md`).
- Shelling maize before storage and avoiding on-the-cob storage in areas with heavy larger grain borer pressure reduces losses substantially.
- Inert dusts such as food-grade diatomaceous earth, admixed with grain at labelled rates, can protect stored grain physically by damaging the insects' protective outer layer; confirm suitability and rates locally.
- Traditional supplementary practices (ash, sand, botanical materials, well-ventilated cribs) may help against weevils but are generally less effective against the larger grain borer, so they should not be relied upon alone.
- Because grain is food, prioritise non-chemical methods and treat any chemical use as a last resort at defined action points.

## Biological Control
- The main biological control agent is the predatory beetle *Teretrius nigrescens* (formerly *Teretriosoma nigrescens*), which was introduced from Central America into several African countries as a classical biological control agent and feeds on larger grain borer eggs and larvae.
- This predator establishes in the environment and around stores and can reduce beetle populations over time, although it does not eliminate the pest and works best alongside good storage hygiene and sealed storage.
- Naturally occurring predatory beetles and parasitic wasps also attack storage pests and should be conserved by avoiding unnecessary broad-spectrum chemical use in and around stores.
- Maintaining clean, dry, sealed store conditions supports the effectiveness of biological control by preventing pest populations from overwhelming natural regulation.

## Chemical Control
- Only insecticides specifically registered and labelled for treatment of stored grain intended for human consumption may ever be applied to grain; field insecticides must never be used on stored produce.
- Where grain protectants are justified, a common approach is an admixture of an organophosphate grain protectant combined with a synthetic pyrethroid, formulated and registered specifically for stored grain, because the pyrethroid component improves control of the larger grain borer, which is less well controlled by organophosphates alone.
- Phosphine fumigation can control heavy infestations but is highly hazardous and should be carried out only by trained, qualified operators in properly sealed stores.
- Confirm currently approved and locally registered stored-grain products, permitted uses, and dosages with a qualified agricultural extension officer or national regulatory authority, as registrations vary by country and change over time (see `regulations/pesticide_safety_and_handling.md`).
- Where repeated treatment is needed, rotate approved modes of action to reduce the risk of resistance, and always observe the required interval between treatment and consumption.

## Safety Considerations
- Grain is food: never treat stored grain with any product not specifically registered as safe for stored grain intended for consumption, and follow label dosage and pre-consumption waiting periods exactly.
- Fumigants are extremely toxic and can be fatal if misused; fumigation should be done only by trained operators, and treated stores must be sealed, clearly marked, and thoroughly ventilated before re-entry (see `regulations/pesticide_safety_and_handling.md`).
- Wear appropriate personal protective equipment (gloves, mask, long sleeves) when handling or admixing any grain protectant or inert dust, and keep children and animals away from treatment areas.
- Store all pesticide products securely, away from food, water, and children, and separate from grain that is not being treated; dispose of empty containers as directed on the label.
- Do not consume or sell grain treated with an unapproved product, and discard grain showing heavy mould, since mouldy grain may carry aflatoxins (see `storage/aflatoxin_prevention.md`).
- Consult an agricultural extension officer if infestations persist despite good management, or if aflatoxin contamination is suspected.

## Frequently Asked Questions
**Q: How is the larger grain borer different from ordinary maize weevils?**
A: The larger grain borer is a cylindrical, snoutless, dark beetle that bores aggressively into both grain and the woody cob, producing very large amounts of floury dust. Maize weevils have a long snout and produce far less dust. The larger grain borer usually destroys grain much faster and can attack cobs that weevils largely leave intact.

**Q: Why is storing maize on the cob no longer safe in my area?**
A: The larger grain borer bores directly into the cob as well as the grain, so traditional cob storage in cribs, which gives some protection against weevils, offers little protection against this pest. In affected areas, shelling maize and storing the dry grain in sealed or airtight containers is much safer.

**Q: Can hermetic (airtight) bags really control this pest without chemicals?**
A: Yes. When properly filled and tightly sealed, airtight bags and containers deprive the beetles of oxygen, stopping their development and killing existing insects over time. This makes hermetic storage one of the most reliable chemical-free options for smallholders (see `storage/grain_storage.md`).

**Q: Where does the beetle come from if my store was clean?**
A: The larger grain borer flies well and can breed in dry wood, old cobs, wooden structures, and residues, so it survives in the environment between harvests and can re-infest clean grain. Thorough cleaning, repairing wooden structures, and sealed storage all help reduce re-infestation.

**Q: The grain is full of powder. Is it still safe to eat?**
A: Lightly infested grain can often still be used after cleaning and sieving, but heavily damaged grain has low food value and is prone to mould. Discard any grain that is mouldy or smells musty, because it may contain aflatoxins (see `storage/aflatoxin_prevention.md`), and seek advice if you are unsure.

**Q: Is there a natural enemy that controls the larger grain borer?**
A: Yes, the predatory beetle *Teretrius nigrescens* was introduced into several African countries and feeds on the pest's eggs and larvae. It reduces populations over time but does not eliminate the pest, so it works best combined with drying, hygiene, and sealed storage.

**Q: When should I use a chemical grain protectant?**
A: Only as a last resort, using a product specifically registered for stored grain intended for consumption, when non-chemical methods are not enough. A combination protectant containing an organophosphate plus a pyrethroid is often recommended against this pest. Always confirm the approved product and rate with an extension officer (see `regulations/pesticide_safety_and_handling.md`).
