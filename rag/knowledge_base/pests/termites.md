# Termites

## Pest Identification
- Scientific names: several genera cause crop damage in Africa, most importantly the fungus-growing termites *Macrotermes* and *Odontotermes* species, and the harvester termites *Microtermes* and *Hodotermes* species.
- Termites are small, soft-bodied, pale (workers are whitish to cream) social insects living in colonies; workers are the caste responsible for feeding and crop damage.
- Colonies may be recognised by earthen mounds, soil sheeting or tunnels running up plant stems, and by hollowed, soil-filled stems and roots of damaged plants.
- Winged reproductive forms (alates) emerge in swarms, often after rains, to establish new colonies and are a useful sign of termite presence in the area.

## Life Cycle
- Termites are social insects with overlapping generations living together in a colony headed by a queen and king.
- The queen lays eggs more or less continuously once the colony is established, so colonies persist and grow over many years rather than following a short seasonal cycle.
- Eggs hatch into young that develop into workers, soldiers, or reproductives depending on colony needs.
- Winged reproductives are typically produced seasonally, often around the rains, and fly out to found new colonies; most perish, but a few establish successful new nests.
- Because colonies are long-lived and centred on a protected nest, management focuses on protecting crops and reducing colony pressure rather than on interrupting a short life cycle.

## Crops Affected
- Wide range including maize (see `crops/maize.md`), sorghum, millet, groundnut, cassava (see `crops/cassava.md`), sugarcane, and many tree and vegetable crops.
- Damage is generally most severe on crops that are moisture-stressed, on young seedlings, and on mature plants nearing harvest.

## Damage Caused
- Termites attack roots and the base of stems, causing plants to wilt, lodge (fall over), or die, with damage often patchy across a field.
- They may hollow out stems and cobs and cover plant surfaces with soil sheeting as they feed.
- Damage is frequently worst during dry spells and on drought-stressed crops, and around harvest when mature plants are more susceptible.
- Harvester termites also cut and remove leaves, stems, and crop residues, which can be significant in drier regions and on rangeland.

## Monitoring Methods
- Walk fields regularly to identify patches of wilting, lodged, or dead plants and check the stem base and roots for tunnelling, hollowing, and soil sheeting.
- Note the location of active mounds and swarming events, which indicate colony pressure in and around the field.
- Pay particular attention during dry periods and as the crop approaches maturity, when damage risk is highest.
- Because established action thresholds are not well defined for termites, base decisions on observed damage patterns and local extension guidance.

## Prevention
- Maintain vigorous crop growth and adequate soil moisture, since healthy, unstressed plants are far less susceptible; supplementary watering during dry spells can reduce damage where feasible (see `irrigation/` guidance where relevant).
- Incorporate crop residues and organic matter to improve soil condition and give termites an alternative food source to the crop, and avoid leaving large amounts of dry residue that attracts harvester species (see `soil/` guidance where relevant).
- Harvest promptly once the crop is mature to limit exposure of vulnerable plants in the field.
- Practise crop rotation and good field sanitation, and select locally adapted, more tolerant varieties where available.

## Integrated Pest Management
- Combine sound agronomy (vigorous growth, moisture management, timely harvest), field sanitation, cultural practices, and targeted control of destructive colonies only where damage is significant.
- Locating and destroying the nest of the responsible colony, including the queen where practical, is often more effective than treating individual plants, since the colony is the source of continued damage.
- Traditional practices such as physical destruction of mounds and encouraging natural predators are used in many smallholder systems and may be combined with other measures.
- Reserve chemical intervention for situations where cultural and physical methods do not adequately protect the crop.

## Biological Control
- Natural enemies include ants, birds, and other predators, and certain entomopathogenic fungi and nematodes can infect termites; the practical effectiveness of biological agents against established colonies is generally limited and variable.
- Conserve general predator populations by avoiding unnecessary broad-spectrum insecticide use.
- Because termites are protected within nests and tunnels, biological control is best viewed as one supporting element within an integrated approach rather than a stand-alone solution.

## Chemical Control
- Chemical control should be reserved for situations where termite damage is significant and cultural and physical measures are insufficient, and should be targeted at the nest or at protecting vulnerable crops rather than applied broadly.
- Confirm currently approved and locally registered insecticide active ingredients with a national agricultural regulatory authority or extension officer, as registered products, permitted uses, and resistance management recommendations vary by country and change over time.
- Rotate insecticide modes of action (chemical classes) where repeated treatment is needed to reduce the risk of resistance development.
- Take particular care with soil-applied treatments to avoid unnecessary contamination of soil and water, and use only products and rates approved for the intended use.

## Safety Considerations
- Always follow label instructions for any pesticide product, including dosage, application method, and pre-harvest interval.
- Use appropriate personal protective equipment (gloves, mask, long sleeves) when handling and applying any chemical pesticide, and take extra care with soil treatments and when working near nests.
- Keep children and animals away from treated areas for the period specified on the product label.
- Take special care to protect water sources when applying soil treatments, and avoid application during heavy rain that could wash chemicals into waterways.
- Store pesticides securely, away from food, water sources, and children, and dispose of empty containers as directed on the label.
- Consult an agricultural extension officer if termite damage is severe or persists despite control efforts, and report unusual or widespread outbreaks to local agricultural authorities for regional monitoring.
