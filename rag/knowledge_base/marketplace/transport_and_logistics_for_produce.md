# Transport and Logistics for Produce

## Overview
Getting produce from the farm to a buyer or market in good condition is as important to the final return a farmer receives as the quality achieved at harvest. Poor transport and logistics decisions can turn a well-grown, well-graded harvest into a lower-value or unsellable batch by the time it reaches the buyer. This document explains the general principles and factors involved in planning transport and logistics for agricultural produce, without recommending specific transport providers, routes, or costs, which vary by location and change over time.

## Why Post-Harvest Transport Matters
A significant share of post-harvest loss in many agricultural value chains occurs during transport and handling, rather than during growing or storage. Physical damage, moisture loss, temperature stress, and delays between harvest and sale can all reduce the volume and grade of produce that ultimately reaches the buyer, directly reducing the farmer's return regardless of how well the crop was grown. Transport planning is therefore an extension of quality management, not a separate afterthought once produce is ready for sale.

## Factors Affecting Transport-Related Loss
- **Handling practices**: Rough loading, unloading, and in-transit handling (dropping, stacking too high, or careless movement) can cause bruising, cracking, or breakage, particularly in fresh fruits, vegetables, and eggs.
- **Packaging adequacy**: Produce packed in containers unsuited to its fragility, weight, or ventilation needs is more prone to damage and quality loss in transit (see the Packaging section below).
- **Vehicle and mode suitability**: Different produce types have different tolerances for vibration, compression, temperature, and transit time; using an unsuitable vehicle or mode increases loss risk.
- **Road and route conditions**: Poor road surfaces increase vibration and jolting, extending effective transit time and increasing physical damage risk, particularly for produce transported in bulk or in inadequate packaging.
- **Time and temperature exposure**: The longer perishable produce is in transit, particularly under high ambient temperature and direct sun exposure, the more quality deterioration occurs, largely independent of physical handling quality (see `storage/perishable_storage.md`).
- **Loading density and mixing**: Overloading vehicles or mixing incompatible produce types (for example, ethylene-sensitive produce alongside ethylene-producing fruit) can accelerate quality loss for some or all of the load.

## Packaging for Transport
- **Rigid containers (crates, boxes)**: Generally provide better protection against compression and physical damage than sacks, particularly for produce prone to bruising, such as tomatoes, and are often required or preferred by formal and export buyers.
- **Sacks and bags**: Suitable for produce that tolerates some compression and abrasion, such as grains, tubers, and some root crops, but generally unsuitable for delicate fresh produce over any significant distance.
- **Ventilation**: Packaging for fresh produce should allow adequate airflow to manage heat and moisture buildup; sealed, non-ventilated packaging can accelerate spoilage through heat and condensation buildup.
- **Cushioning and layering**: Separating layers within a container, and avoiding excessive stacking height, reduces compression damage, particularly for soft fruits and vegetables.
- **Appropriate container size**: Very large containers or sacks increase the weight and compression pressure on produce at the bottom of the container; smaller, appropriately sized containers reduce this risk for fragile produce.
- **Labelling and identification**: Clear labelling of contents, quantity, and origin supports smoother handling, faster processing at the buyer's receiving point, and easier traceability if a quality issue arises.

## Transport Mode Considerations

| Mode | General Characteristics | Typical Suitability |
|---|---|---|
| Head-loading/animal transport | Very short distance, low capacity, gentle handling possible but slow | Farm-to-collection-point movement over short distances |
| Bicycle/motorcycle | Moderate distance, low-to-moderate capacity, exposed to weather unless covered | Local and rural market delivery, smaller volumes |
| Pick-up/small truck | Moderate to large capacity, moderate speed, common for local/regional trade | Local and regional market delivery, cooperative aggregation |
| Large truck | High capacity, suited to longer distances and bulk loads | Urban wholesale, processor, and export-bound produce |
| Rail (where available) | High capacity, generally lower cost per unit over long distances, less flexible scheduling | Bulk, storable commodities moved over long distances |
| Refrigerated transport | Temperature-controlled, higher cost, essential for many perishables over longer distances | Highly perishable produce for distant, formal, or export markets |
| Air freight | Very fast, high cost, used for high-value, highly perishable, or urgently needed produce | Premium export markets for high-value perishables |

Choosing an appropriate mode involves balancing produce perishability and value against transit time, handling gentleness, and cost considerations relevant to the specific shipment and market channel (see `marketplace/market_fundamentals.md` for related market channel considerations).

## Timing and Scheduling Considerations
- **Minimising time between harvest and transport**: Reducing the delay between harvest and dispatch generally reduces quality loss, particularly for highly perishable produce; harvesting and preparing produce with transport timing in mind (for example, harvesting early in the day and dispatching promptly) can improve outcomes.
- **Avoiding peak heat periods**: Where feasible, transporting perishable produce during cooler parts of the day, or overnight, reduces heat stress compared to transport during the hottest daytime hours.
- **Aligning with buyer receiving schedules**: Arriving at a market or buyer's receiving point when it is open, staffed, and not already congested with other deliveries reduces waiting time that can further degrade perishable produce.
- **Consolidating shipments where practical**: Combining produce from multiple farmers, such as through a cooperative or aggregation point, can improve vehicle utilisation and reduce the per-unit cost and frequency of trips needed, though this must be balanced against the risk of delays waiting for a full load.

## Cold Chain Considerations for Perishables
For highly perishable produce, maintaining an appropriate temperature from shortly after harvest through to the buyer is one of the most significant factors in preserving quality and shelf life. Key principles include:
- Cooling produce as soon as practical after harvest, rather than allowing it to sit at ambient temperature before transport begins.
- Minimising breaks in temperature control during loading, transit, and unloading, since even short exposure to high temperature can accelerate spoilage that cannot be reversed by later cooling.
- Where refrigerated transport is not available or affordable, using practical alternatives such as shade, evaporative cooling, or transport during cooler hours to reduce (though not eliminate) heat stress.
- Understanding that cold chain requirements are often stricter, and more strictly enforced, for export markets than for local markets (see `marketplace/export_market_requirements.md` and `storage/perishable_storage.md`).

## Route and Market Planning
- Understanding road conditions, distances, and typical journey times to alternative markets or buyers supports realistic planning of harvest timing and packaging investment.
- Identifying collection points, aggregation centres, or cooperative facilities along a route can reduce the distance an individual farmer needs to transport produce independently.
- Considering seasonal road conditions, such as rainy-season access difficulties, when planning harvest and sale timing for crops maturing during periods of poor road access.
- Building relationships with reliable local transporters, where transport is hired rather than owned, supports more predictable scheduling and handling quality over repeated transactions.

## Cost Considerations in Transport Decisions
While this document does not provide specific transport cost figures, farmers should weigh the following factors when deciding how to transport produce to market:
- The trade-off between transport cost and the price achievable at more distant markets, since more distant or formal markets often offer better prices but at higher transport cost and time investment (see `marketplace/market_fundamentals.md`).
- The cost of packaging and handling improvements against the reduction in loss and quality degradation they are likely to achieve.
- The potential for shared or cooperative transport arrangements to reduce the effective per-unit transport cost compared to individual, smaller shipments.
- The value of the produce being transported relative to the transport mode's cost, since higher-value or more perishable produce can often justify a more expensive but faster or more protective transport option.

## Common Mistakes
- Using packaging suited to grain or root crops (such as unventilated sacks) for delicate fresh produce, resulting in avoidable bruising and spoilage.
- Overloading vehicles or stacking produce too high, causing compression damage to the produce at the bottom of the load.
- Delaying dispatch after harvest, allowing perishable produce to lose quality before transport even begins.
- Failing to plan for seasonal road access issues that could delay transport of a time-sensitive harvest.
- Mixing incompatible produce types in the same transport load without considering ethylene sensitivity or odour transfer.

## When to Consult an Agricultural Extension Officer or Logistics/Post-Harvest Specialist
- To identify appropriate packaging and handling practices for a specific crop and target market.
- Before investing in transport equipment or cold chain infrastructure, to confirm it is well matched to the crops and volumes involved.
- When planning to access a more distant or formal market channel for the first time, to understand realistic transit times, road conditions, and handling requirements.
- If persistent, unexplained quality loss is occurring between harvest and sale, to help identify whether handling, packaging, timing, or transport conditions are the primary cause.
