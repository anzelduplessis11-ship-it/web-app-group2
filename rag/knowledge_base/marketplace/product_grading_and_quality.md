# Product Grading and Quality Standards

## Overview
Grading and quality standards allow buyers and sellers to communicate about produce characteristics consistently, and generally determine the price premium or discount a farmer receives. Meeting recognized quality standards, even informally, increases market access and typically improves the price received.

## General Grading Criteria Across Crop Types

### Physical Appearance
- Size uniformity within a batch.
- Shape regularity and absence of deformities.
- Colour, matched to expected ripeness or variety standard for the crop.
- Absence of visible pest damage, disease lesions, cuts, or bruises.

### Moisture Content (for Grains and Dried Products)
- Buyers, particularly formal/institutional buyers, often specify maximum acceptable moisture content, since grain above safe moisture thresholds is prone to mould, weight loss during storage, and quality decline.
- Grain within the recommended safe storage moisture range (see `storage/grain_storage.md`) generally meets baseline buyer requirements for quality.

### Foreign Matter and Impurities
- Presence of stones, soil, chaff, insect fragments, or other crop varieties mixed into a batch reduces grade and price.
- Thorough cleaning, sorting, and winnowing before sale improves grade.

### Uniformity and Sorting
- Batches sorted into consistent size/quality categories generally command better prices than unsorted, mixed-quality batches, even if the average quality is similar.
- Separating and selling lower-grade produce separately (e.g., for processing or animal feed markets) rather than mixing it with higher-grade produce protects the value of the higher-grade portion.

## Crop-Specific Grading Considerations

### Cereals (e.g., Maize, Rice)
- Grading commonly considers moisture content, foreign matter percentage, percentage of broken or damaged kernels, and presence of mould or discolouration.
- Aflatoxin and other mycotoxin testing is increasingly required by formal buyers and export markets, particularly for maize and groundnuts.

### Legumes (e.g., Beans)
- Grading commonly considers seed size uniformity, colour consistency (for varieties where uniform colour is expected), moisture content, and absence of weevil damage.

### Fresh Fruits and Vegetables
- Grading commonly considers size, shape, colour/ripeness stage, absence of blemishes or disease, and firmness.
- Perishables are often graded into categories such as export/premium grade, local market grade, and processing grade, each with different handling and price implications.

### Root and Tuber Crops
- Grading commonly considers size uniformity, absence of cuts/bruises from harvest, and absence of rot or pest damage.

## Quality Standards and Certification
- Some markets, particularly export and formal processing markets, require compliance with specific national or international quality and safety standards (e.g., maximum pesticide residue limits, aflatoxin limits, or certified organic production standards).
- Certification requirements and standards vary by destination market and buyer; farmers targeting these markets should confirm current specific requirements directly with the buyer or relevant regulatory body, since standards can change.
- Basic good agricultural practices (proper pesticide use following label instructions, adequate drying and storage, careful harvest handling) support compliance with most quality and safety standards even without formal certification.

## Practical Steps to Improve Grade and Market Access
- Harvest at correct maturity to avoid both under- and over-ripe produce in the same batch.
- Sort and clean produce promptly after harvest, removing damaged, diseased, or foreign material.
- Dry grain to recommended safe moisture levels before sale where relevant.
- Package appropriately for the target market (e.g., appropriately sized, ventilated containers for perishables; clean, labelled bags for grain).
- Maintain consistent quality across successive sales to build buyer trust and potentially secure better ongoing terms.

## Common Mistakes
- Mixing different quality grades in the same batch, which typically results in the entire batch being priced at the lower grade.
- Selling grain above safe moisture content to avoid drying costs, risking rejection or price discount, and food safety concerns.
- Rough handling during harvest and transport that introduces bruising or damage not present in the field, unnecessarily reducing grade.

## When to Consult an Agricultural Extension Officer or Market/Quality Specialist
- To understand specific grading standards required by a target buyer or market, particularly for export or certified markets.
- To access testing services (e.g., aflatoxin testing, pesticide residue testing) required for certain formal markets.
- Before investing in packaging, sorting, or processing equipment intended to access a higher-grade market segment.
