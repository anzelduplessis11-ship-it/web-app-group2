# Regional Planting Calendar: East Africa

## Overview
East Africa is characterized by a highly variable topography, from lowland arid/semi-arid areas to highland zones above 1,500 m, resulting in significant local variation in rainfall pattern. Much of the region (e.g., Kenya, Uganda, Rwanda, Burundi, northern Tanzania, parts of Ethiopia) experiences a bimodal rainfall pattern with two rainy seasons, while other areas (e.g., parts of Tanzania, parts of Ethiopia) have a single unimodal rainy season.

## Climatic Sub-Zones
- **Bimodal highland/mid-altitude zone** (e.g., central Kenya, Uganda, Rwanda, Burundi): long rains typically March-May, short rains typically October-December.
- **Unimodal zone** (e.g., much of southern Tanzania, parts of Ethiopia): single rainy season typically November-April or June-September depending on specific location.
- **Arid/semi-arid lowland zone** (e.g., northern Kenya, parts of Somalia, southeastern Ethiopia): low, unreliable rainfall, often 200-500 mm annually, requiring drought-tolerant crop choices or irrigation.

## General Planting Calendar by Crop

| Crop | Bimodal Zone (Long Rains) | Bimodal Zone (Short Rains) | Unimodal Zone | Arid/Semi-Arid Zone |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Maize | March-April | October-November | Timed to single rainy season onset | Limited; drought-tolerant varieties only, or irrigated |
| Beans | March-April | September-October | Timed to single rainy season onset | Limited |
| Sorghum/Millet | March-April | September-October | Timed to single rainy season onset | Preferred cereal choice; June-July or per local pattern |
| Rice (irrigated) | Flexible, based on scheme water availability | Flexible | Flexible | Limited to irrigation schemes |
| Cassava | March-May | September-November | Timed to single rainy season onset, or year-round in adequate rainfall areas | Limited; requires supplemental water for establishment |
| Tomato/Vegetables | March-April and Sept-Oct (2 seasons possible) | See long rains column | Aligned to single season, or irrigated | Under irrigation only |

## Key Regional Notes
- In bimodal zones, the long rains season is generally longer and more reliable than the short rains, making it the primary planting season for most staple crops; the short rains season is often used for a second crop or for shorter-duration varieties.
- Highland areas (above approximately 1,800-2,000 m) have cooler temperatures that can limit crop choice (e.g., maize varieties must be suited to cooler highland conditions) and slow crop development compared to mid-altitude zones.
- Arid and semi-arid lowland areas require drought-tolerant crop choices (sorghum, millet, cowpea, certain drought-tolerant maize varieties) or reliable irrigation access, since rainfed cropping carries high risk of failure.
- Rainfall onset timing has shown increasing variability in recent years across much of the region, reinforcing the importance of monitoring local conditions rather than relying solely on historical average dates.

## Adjusting for Local Conditions
- Altitude strongly affects both temperature and rainfall patterns even over short distances in East Africa's varied topography; farmers should calibrate planting windows to their specific altitude and local microclimate rather than broad zone averages alone.
- Proximity to major lakes (e.g., Lake Victoria) can create localized rainfall patterns that differ from the surrounding region.

## When to Consult an Agricultural Extension Officer
- To confirm current-season rainfall onset predictions for a specific district, given the significant year-to-year variability common in the region.
- For guidance on variety selection suited to specific altitude bands, particularly in highland areas.
- When considering irrigation investment in arid/semi-arid zones, to assess water source reliability and appropriate crop choices.
