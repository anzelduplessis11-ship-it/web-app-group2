# Regional Planting Calendar: The Sahel Region

## Overview
The Sahel is a semi-arid belt stretching across the width of Africa immediately south of the Sahara, spanning parts of Senegal, Mauritania, Mali, Burkina Faso, Niger, northern Nigeria, Chad, and Sudan. The zone is defined by a short single rainy season and low, highly variable rainfall, generally increasing from north to south. Millet and sorghum dominate rainfed cropping because of their drought tolerance, and agropastoral systems combining crops with livestock are central to local livelihoods. Irrigated and flood-recession agriculture along major rivers and lake basins (the Senegal River, the Niger River inland delta, the Lake Chad basin, and the Nile in Sudan) provides an important supplement that is largely decoupled from local rainfall timing.

## Climatic Sub-Zones
- **Sahelian zone proper** (roughly 200-400 mm annual rainfall): rainy season typically June-September and often only two to three months long; rainfed cropping is marginal and risk-prone, with pastoralism and drought-tolerant millet or cowpea as the main land use.
- **Sahelo-Sudanian transition zone** (roughly 400-600 mm annual rainfall): rainy season typically June-September/October, somewhat longer and more reliable; sorghum, millet, cowpea, and groundnut rainfed cropping is the primary land use.
- **River valley, flood-recession, and irrigated zone** (along the Senegal River, the Niger inland delta, the Lake Chad basin, and Sudan's Nile system): irrigated or flood-recession agriculture largely independent of local rainfall timing, allowing rice, wheat, and vegetable production, often in the dry season.
- **Oasis and far-northern fringe** (bordering the Sahara, e.g. northern Mali, northern Niger, northern Chad, northern Sudan): extremely low or no reliable rainfall; agriculture limited to oases and groundwater-fed irrigation.

## Key Regional Notes
- Rainfall onset is extremely variable from year to year across the Sahel, and a "false start" — an early rain event followed by a prolonged dry spell — is a well-recognized risk. Many farmers delay full-scale planting until a defined threshold rainfall event (commonly cited as around 15-20 mm within a short period) has occurred, to avoid losing seed and labour to a failed early planting.
- Intercropping of millet or sorghum with cowpea or groundnut is a traditional risk-management and soil-fertility practice throughout the region, spreading risk across crops with different drought sensitivities and improving nitrogen availability for the cereal component.
- Dry-season irrigated vegetable gardening — often called "off-season" or "counter-season" gardening — near rivers, wells, or small reservoirs is a critical source of income and dietary diversity given the short single rainy season, and is frequently organized around village-level garden schemes or floodplain access.
- Land degradation and desertification pressure at the Sahara's southern margin are long-standing structural challenges; water-harvesting and soil-restoration techniques such as *zaï* planting pits, half-moon (demi-lune) earthworks, contour bunding, and Farmer-Managed Natural Regeneration of trees are widely used to improve rainfed cropping conditions and rehabilitate degraded land (see `soil/soil_conservation_and_erosion.md`).
- Agropastoralism — the integration of livestock with cropping — is central to Sahelian farming systems: crop residues support livestock during the long dry season, while manure contributes to soil fertility for subsequent crops.
- The region has a documented history of severe drought cycles (notably in the 1970s and 1980s) that caused major production shocks, underscoring the importance of drought-tolerant crop and variety choice, water-harvesting practices, and diversified livelihoods including livestock and dry-season gardening.

## Adjusting for Local Conditions
- The north-south rainfall gradient across the Sahel is steep; a shift of even 100 km can noticeably change total seasonal rainfall and the length of the growing season, so broad zone averages should be checked against local experience.
- Proximity to rivers, lakes, or wetlands (the Senegal, Niger, and Chad basins in particular) dramatically expands agricultural options compared with purely rainfed areas nearby, decoupling cropping calendars from local rainfall onset.
- Soil type and water-holding capacity vary significantly even within short distances in the Sahel, affecting how much benefit water-harvesting structures provide in a given field.

## When to Consult an Agricultural Extension Officer
- To confirm whether the season's rainfall has reached the recommended threshold for full-scale planting in a specific locality, given the risk of a false start.
- For guidance on appropriate water-harvesting structures (*zaï* pits, half-moons, contour bunds) suited to specific soil and slope conditions.
- For support accessing irrigated dry-season gardening schemes or river/floodplain irrigation infrastructure.
- For guidance on drought-tolerant variety selection, particularly where seasonal rainfall forecasts point to a below-average or late-starting season.
