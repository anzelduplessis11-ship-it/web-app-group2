# Regional Planting Calendar: West Africa

## Overview
West Africa spans a range of climatic zones from the arid Sahel in the north to humid coastal and forest zones in the south, resulting in significant variation in rainfall onset, duration, and cropping patterns across the region. This calendar provides general guidance; actual planting dates should be adjusted based on observed local rainfall onset each season.

## Climatic Sub-Zones
- **Sahel zone** (e.g., northern Senegal, northern Mali, northern Niger, northern Nigeria): short single rainy season, typically June-September, 300-600 mm annual rainfall.
- **Sudan savanna zone**: single rainy season, typically May-October, 600-1000 mm annual rainfall.
- **Guinea savanna zone**: single, longer rainy season, typically April-October, 1000-1500 mm annual rainfall.
- **Humid forest/coastal zone** (e.g., southern Nigeria, Ghana, Côte d'Ivoire, coastal Guinea): often bimodal rainfall, allowing two cropping seasons, 1500-2500+ mm annual rainfall.

## General Planting Calendar by Crop

| Crop | Sahel Zone | Sudan Savanna | Guinea Savanna | Humid Forest/Coastal |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Maize | June-July | May-June | April-May and Aug-Sept (2 seasons) | March-April and Aug-Sept (2 seasons) |
| Sorghum/Millet | June-July | May-June | May-June | Less common; localized production |
| Rice (rainfed lowland) | Limited; irrigated schemes preferred | June-July | May-June | March-May and Aug-Sept |
| Cassava | Not well suited (too dry); irrigated pockets only | June-July | April-June | Year-round in humid areas |
| Cowpea | June-July | June-July | May-July | May-June and Sept-Oct |

| Tomato/Vegetables | Under irrigation, Oct-Feb (dry season) | Oct-Feb (dry season, irrigated) | Oct-Feb (dry season) and rainy season with disease management | Year-round with irrigation and disease management |

## Key Regional Notes
- In the Sahel and Sudan savanna zones, the short and sometimes erratic rainy season makes planting date decisions critical; planting too early risks seedling loss to a false start, while planting too late shortens the season for maturity.
- In humid forest/coastal zones with bimodal rainfall, two cropping seasons are possible for many crops, but the second (minor) season often has less reliable rainfall than the first (major) season.
- Dry-season vegetable production under irrigation is common and often more profitable in areas with reliable water access, since disease pressure (particularly fungal diseases favoured by humidity) is generally lower in the dry season.
- Rainfall onset varies by several weeks from year to year even within the same sub-zone; farmers should confirm actual local rainfall onset before committing to full-scale planting, using early, smaller test plantings where uncertain.

## Adjusting for Local Conditions
- Altitude, proximity to large water bodies, and local microclimate can shift effective planting windows earlier or later than the broad zone averages presented here.
- Farmers should combine this general calendar with locally observed rainfall patterns and, where available, seasonal forecast information from national meteorological services.

## When to Consult an Agricultural Extension Officer
- To confirm current-season rainfall onset predictions and recommended planting windows for a specific district or locality.
- When considering a second (minor) season planting in bimodal zones, to assess the reliability of the minor season in the specific local area.
- For guidance on drought-tolerant variety choices suited to increasingly variable rainfall patterns in a specific zone.
