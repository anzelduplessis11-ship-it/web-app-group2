# Pan-African Planting Calendar: Cereal Crops

## Overview
Cereals (maize, rice, sorghum, millet, wheat, and teff) are Africa's dominant staple food crops, but which cereal is grown, and when, depends heavily on the interaction between rainfall pattern, growing-season length, elevation-driven temperature, and access to irrigation. This calendar summarizes major cereal crops by broad climate zone rather than by country. It should be read alongside the region-specific calendars (`east_africa_calendar.md`, `west_africa_calendar.md`, `southern_africa_calendar.md`, `central_africa_calendar.md`, `north_africa_calendar.md`, `sahel_region_calendar.md`) and individual crop files such as `crops/maize.md`, `crops/rice.md`, `crops/sorghum.md`, `crops/pearl_millet.md`, `crops/finger_millet.md`, and `crops/wheat.md`.

## Climate Zones Used in This Calendar
- **Humid Tropical/Bimodal zone**: high or well-distributed, often bimodal rainfall (Congo Basin, coastal and forest West Africa, East African bimodal lowlands); maize and rice dominate.
- **Unimodal Savanna zone**: single, longer wet season (Guinea and Sudan savanna of West Africa, wetter summer-rainfall areas of Southern Africa); maize, sorghum, and rainfed lowland rice are common.
- **Semi-Arid/Sahelian zone**: short single rainy season and low rainfall (the Sahel belt and Sudan savanna); sorghum and millet are the dominant, most drought-tolerant cereals.
- **Highland Temperate zone**: cool, high-altitude areas, especially the Ethiopian and Eritrean highlands; wheat, barley, and teff are primary crops, alongside finger millet in parts of the East African highlands.
- **Mediterranean/Irrigated North African zone**: winter-rainfall and Nile-irrigated systems (Egypt, Morocco, Tunisia, Algeria, Libya); wheat and irrigated rice (Nile Delta) are major crops.
- **Southern Africa Winter-Rainfall zone**: the Western Cape of South Africa and similar pockets, where wheat is grown as a winter crop distinct from the rest of Sub-Saharan Africa's summer-rainfall cereal calendar.

## Key Regional Notes
- Cereal choice across Africa is strongly shaped by growing-season length and reliability: millet and sorghum dominate in short-season, low-rainfall areas because of their drought tolerance; maize is preferred where rainfall is moderate to high and more reliable; rice requires assured water, whether from high and well-distributed rainfall, river floodplains, or irrigation; wheat and teff are suited to cooler highland or Mediterranean/winter-rainfall conditions.
- Elevation is a major determinant of cereal suitability independent of rainfall amount: teff, wheat, and barley are adapted to cool highland or temperate conditions, while maize, sorghum, and millet are generally grown at warmer lowland-to-mid-altitude elevations.
- Where bimodal rainfall occurs (humid tropical zones and some highland areas), double-cropping of maize or short-duration cereals across the two rainy seasons is common; unimodal zones generally support only one main cereal crop per year, though a shorter secondary crop may be possible in wetter unimodal areas.
- Irrigation decouples cereal cropping from rainfall timing entirely in several important systems: Nile Valley wheat, maize, and rice in Egypt; river-valley and flood-recession rice and wheat in the Sahel; and winter wheat in South Africa's Western Cape. In these systems, planting is governed by irrigation scheme scheduling and agronomic optimum timing rather than rainfall onset.
- Frost risk in the Ethiopian and Eritrean highlands is an important constraint on sowing and harvest timing for teff, wheat, and barley, since frost occurring near crop maturity can severely damage grain fill; farmers in these areas time planting to avoid frost at the flowering and grain-filling stages.
- Post-harvest handling and storage strongly influence the value retained from a cereal harvest across all zones; see `storage/grain_storage.md` and `storage/aflatoxin_prevention.md` for guidance on safe storage, particularly for maize and other cereals prone to aflatoxin contamination under poor storage conditions.

## Adjusting for Local Conditions
- Altitude bands within the highland temperate zone strongly affect which cereal (teff, wheat, barley, or maize) is best suited to a specific site; broad zone averages should be adjusted to the specific altitude of a given farm.
- Access to irrigation, rather than climate zone alone, often determines whether wheat or rice can be grown outside their typical rainfed zones.
- Rainfall onset varies by several weeks from year to year even within the same sub-zone; farmers should confirm local rainfall onset before committing to full-scale cereal planting, especially for longer-duration varieties.

## When to Consult an Agricultural Extension Officer
- To confirm current-season rainfall onset predictions and recommended cereal planting windows for a specific district or altitude band.
- For guidance on variety selection suited to local growing-season length, altitude, and frost risk, particularly for teff, wheat, and barley in highland areas.
- When considering a shift between cereal crops (for example, from maize to a more drought-tolerant sorghum or millet) in response to observed rainfall variability or a forecast below-average season.
- When planning irrigated cereal production, to confirm water source reliability and scheme-specific scheduling.
