# Regional Planting Calendar: Central Africa

## Overview
Central Africa (e.g., the Democratic Republic of the Congo, Cameroon, Gabon, Republic of the Congo, and neighbouring areas) is dominated by humid and equatorial climates with generally high annual rainfall and warm temperatures throughout the year. Much of the region experiences a bimodal rainfall pattern, with two rainy seasons separated by two drier periods, which allows two cropping seasons per year for many crops. Areas closer to the equator often have rainfall distributed across much of the year with only short drier spells, while areas farther from the equator show more distinct wet and dry seasons. This calendar provides general guidance; actual planting dates should be adjusted based on observed local rainfall onset each season, as timing varies with location and from year to year.

## Climatic Sub-Zones
- **Equatorial humid zone** (e.g., much of the Congo Basin, parts of Gabon and northern DRC): rainfall spread across most of the year with two peaks and only short, weak dry periods; consistently warm and humid, allowing near year-round cropping of suitable crops.
- **Bimodal humid zone** (e.g., much of southern Cameroon, parts of DRC, and surrounding areas): two clear rainy seasons separated by two drier periods, supporting two distinct cropping seasons per year.
- **Wetter savanna/transition zone** (drier margins farther from the equator, e.g., parts of southern DRC and northern/interior areas): a more pronounced single main rainy season or a shorter second season, with a longer dry season requiring more careful planting-date decisions.

## The Two Rainy Seasons
- In bimodal areas, the year is broadly divided into a **first rainy season** and a **second rainy season**, separated by two drier periods (a shorter dry spell and a longer dry season). The exact months vary by location and by position relative to the equator; in general terms across much of the region the two rainy seasons fall broadly around **February/March to May/June** and **August/September to November/December**, with local variation.
- Note that in areas north and south of the equator the rainy seasons occur at different times of year, so neighbouring parts of the region can have opposite seasonal calendars. Farmers should rely on locally observed rainfall onset rather than a single regional date.
- The two-season pattern allows a main-season crop and a second-season crop for many food crops, though the reliability and length of each season vary locally; one season is often more dependable than the other in a given area.

## Key Regional Notes
- The bimodal rainfall pattern allows two cropping seasons per year for many food crops, but the two seasons are not always equally reliable or equally long; in many areas one season is the more dependable main season and the other a shorter secondary season. Farmers should confirm which season is stronger in their specific locality.
- High rainfall and humidity in much of the region increase pressure from fungal and bacterial crop diseases and from certain pests; disease-tolerant varieties, good drainage, and appropriate spacing help manage this (see `diseases/` and `emergency_guides/pest_outbreak_emergency_guide.md`).
- Perennial and flexible crops such as cassava, banana, and plantain suit the humid, near year-round growing conditions and provide food security across seasons, while shorter-duration crops such as beans and groundnut are fitted within the rainy seasons.
- Heavy rains can cause waterlogging on poorly drained soils; raised beds, ridges, or mounds and good field drainage are important for crops sensitive to waterlogging.
- Rainfall onset and the length of the dry periods vary from year to year; monitoring local conditions is more reliable than depending solely on historical average dates (see `weather/weather_impacts_on_farming.md`).

## Adjusting for Local Conditions
- Position relative to the equator strongly affects the timing and number of rainy seasons; areas north and south of the equator can have opposite calendars, so broad regional dates must be calibrated to the specific location.
- Altitude, proximity to large rivers and forests, and local microclimate can shift effective planting windows and rainfall reliability compared with the broad zone averages presented here.
- Farmers should combine this general calendar with locally observed rainfall onset and, where available, seasonal forecast information from national meteorological services, using early, smaller test plantings where the season's onset is uncertain.

## When to Consult an Agricultural Extension Officer
- To confirm current-season rainfall onset predictions and recommended planting windows for a specific district or locality, given local and year-to-year variability.
- When deciding whether to attempt a second (secondary) season crop, to assess the reliability and length of that season in the specific local area.
- For guidance on disease-tolerant and well-adapted varieties suited to the humid, high-rainfall conditions and local disease pressure.
- When planning drainage or field layout (raised beds, ridges, mounds) for crops sensitive to waterlogging on heavy or poorly drained soils.
