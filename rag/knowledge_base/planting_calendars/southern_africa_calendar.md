# Regional Planting Calendar: Southern Africa

## Overview
Southern Africa generally experiences a single rainy season during the austral summer (roughly October to April), with a distinct dry winter season. Rainfall reliability and total amount vary significantly from the wetter eastern and northern parts of the region to the drier western areas.

## Climatic Sub-Zones
- **Wetter summer rainfall zone** (e.g., much of Zambia, Malawi, Mozambique, eastern Zimbabwe, eastern South Africa): rainy season typically November-April, 800-1400+ mm annually.
- **Drier summer rainfall zone** (e.g., much of Zimbabwe, Botswana, western South Africa, southern Zambia): rainy season typically November-March, 400-800 mm annually, with higher year-to-year variability.
- **Semi-arid/arid zone** (e.g., Namibia, parts of Botswana, western South Africa): low, unreliable rainfall, often below 400 mm annually, requiring drought-tolerant crops or irrigation.
- **Winter rainfall zone** (e.g., southwestern South Africa/Western Cape): unusual for the region, with rainfall concentrated in the austral winter (May-August), supporting different cropping patterns including winter cereals and Mediterranean-type horticulture.

## General Planting Calendar by Crop (Summer Rainfall Zones)

| Crop | Wetter Summer Rainfall Zone | Drier Summer Rainfall Zone | Semi-Arid/Arid Zone |
|---|---|---|---|
| Maize | October-December | November-December | Limited; drought-tolerant varieties or irrigation |
| Sorghum/Millet | November-December | November-December | Preferred cereal choice; November-December |
| Groundnut | November-December | November-December | Limited |
| Common Beans | November-December | November-December (short-duration varieties) | Limited |
| Cassava | November-December (in suitable areas) | Limited | Not well suited |
| Cotton | October-November | November-December | Limited |
| Tomato/Vegetables | Year-round with irrigation; rainy season Aug-Oct planting also common | Aug-Oct or under irrigation | Under irrigation only |

## Winter Rainfall Zone (e.g., Western Cape, South Africa)
- Winter cereals (e.g., wheat) are typically planted April-June and harvested October-December, aligned with the winter rainfall pattern.
- Summer vegetable and fruit production in this zone generally relies on irrigation, since the summer months are dry.

## Key Regional Notes
- Late planting (after mid-December in most summer rainfall areas) is generally discouraged for long-duration maize varieties, since it risks the crop reaching critical flowering stages after peak rainfall has passed, and risks not reaching maturity before the onset of the dry, cool winter season.
- Drier zones and semi-arid areas face higher risk of mid-season dry spells (a "false start" to the season or extended dry periods during the season); drought-tolerant crop and variety choices, along with water conservation practices, are particularly important in these areas.
- El Niño and La Niña climate patterns have a documented influence on Southern African rainfall variability, with El Niño years often associated with below-average rainfall in large parts of the region; farmers in drought-prone areas may benefit from monitoring seasonal climate outlook information where available.

## Adjusting for Local Conditions
- Altitude and proximity to major water bodies can create local variation within each broad zone described here.
- Frost risk at the start (October-November) and end (March-April) of the season should be considered in higher-altitude or frost-prone localities, particularly for frost-sensitive crops.

## When to Consult an Agricultural Extension Officer
- To confirm current-season rainfall onset and seasonal outlook predictions (including El Niño/La Niña influence) for a specific district.
- For guidance on drought-tolerant variety selection suited to drier zones or forecast below-average rainfall seasons.
- When planning winter cereal or irrigated production in the winter rainfall zone, to align with locally appropriate planting windows.
