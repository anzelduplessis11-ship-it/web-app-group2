# Pan-African Planting Calendar: Vegetables

## Overview
Vegetable production occurs across nearly every climate zone in Africa, but the crops best suited and the planting windows available differ sharply between humid tropical lowlands, cooler highlands, drier savanna and Sahelian areas, and the Mediterranean and winter-rainfall zones at the continent's northern and southern extremes. This calendar summarizes major vegetable crops by broad climate zone rather than by country, since the same climate zone (for example, tropical highland or semi-arid savanna) recurs in multiple regions with broadly similar planting logic. It should be read alongside the region-specific calendars (`east_africa_calendar.md`, `west_africa_calendar.md`, `southern_africa_calendar.md`, `central_africa_calendar.md`, `north_africa_calendar.md`, `sahel_region_calendar.md`) and individual crop files in `crops/` for local and crop-specific detail.

## Climate Zones Used in This Calendar
- **Humid Tropical/Equatorial zone**: high, well-distributed or bimodal rainfall (Congo Basin, coastal West Africa, coastal East Africa); year-round warmth allows near-continuous vegetable cropping where disease pressure is managed.
- **Bimodal Highland/Mid-Altitude Tropical zone**: East African highlands and similar areas (Kenya, Uganda, Rwanda, Burundi, Ethiopian and Tanzanian highlands) with long and short rains and cooler temperatures that favour temperate-type vegetables alongside tropical ones.
- **Unimodal Savanna zone**: single, longer wet season (Guinea and Sudan savanna of West Africa, wetter summer-rainfall areas of Southern Africa).
- **Semi-Arid/Sahelian zone**: short single rainy season and low rainfall (the Sahel belt, Sudan savanna, drier Southern African summer-rainfall areas), where dry-season irrigated vegetable gardening is often as important as rainy-season cropping.
- **Mediterranean/Arid North African zone**: winter rainfall and hot dry summers, with irrigation supporting both winter and summer vegetable cycles (Egypt, Morocco, Tunisia, Algeria, Libya).
- **Temperate Winter-Rainfall zone**: the Western Cape of South Africa and similar small pockets, with rainfall concentrated in the austral winter, supporting a Mediterranean-type vegetable calendar distinct from the rest of Sub-Saharan Africa.

## Zone Notes
- **Humid tropical/equatorial**: warmth allows near-continuous vegetable cropping, but high humidity and rainfall raise fungal and bacterial disease pressure (see `diseases/tomato_early_blight.md`, `diseases/tomato_late_blight.md`, `diseases/tomato_bacterial_wilt.md`); staking, spacing for airflow, and disease-tolerant varieties are important management practices.
- **Bimodal highland**: cooler temperatures make this the most versatile zone, supporting both tropical vegetables (tomato, kale) and temperate crops (cabbage, carrot, Irish potato) that struggle in hotter lowland zones; altitude within this zone strongly affects which crops perform best.
- **Unimodal savanna and semi-arid/Sahelian zones**: the single rainy season concentrates rainfed vegetable production into a few months, so dry-season irrigated gardening near rivers, dams, or wells is frequently the more reliable and higher-value production window, particularly for onion, tomato, and leafy vegetables.
- **Mediterranean/North African zone**: distinct winter and summer vegetable cycles reflect the region's winter-rainfall, irrigation-dependent system; greenhouse and protected cultivation is common for extending seasons and managing frost or heat stress.
- **Winter-rainfall zone**: growing seasons are shifted relative to the rest of Sub-Saharan Africa because rainfall and mild temperatures occur in the austral autumn-winter rather than summer; irrigation is used to extend production into the dry summer months.

## Key Cross-Regional Notes
- Vegetable crop choice is driven primarily by temperature (governed by altitude and latitude) and by water availability (rainfall pattern and access to irrigation), more than by any single continent-wide calendar.
- Dry-season, irrigation-based vegetable production is common and often more profitable across drier zones because disease pressure — particularly fungal diseases favoured by humidity — is typically lower outside the rainy season.
- Short-duration leafy vegetables (kale, amaranth, and similar African leafy vegetables) can often be fitted into gaps between main-season plantings or grown as a quick income and nutrition source where longer-season vegetables are not yet mature.
- Good drainage and raised beds are important for most vegetable crops during the wettest part of any zone's rainy season, since many vegetable crops are sensitive to waterlogging.
- Where two rainy seasons exist (bimodal highland, some humid tropical areas), the first/long-rains window is generally more reliable for main-crop vegetable production, with the second season better suited to shorter-duration crops.

## Adjusting for Local Conditions
- Microclimate effects — altitude, slope aspect, proximity to large water bodies, and local shading — can shift effective vegetable planting windows earlier or later than the zone averages given here.
- Access to irrigation (rather than climate zone alone) is often the deciding factor in whether a dry-season vegetable crop is feasible in savanna, Sahelian, and North African zones.
- Local market demand patterns should be considered alongside agronomic planting windows, since vegetable prices can vary significantly by season (see `marketplace/buyer_preferences_and_seasonal_demand.md`).

## When to Consult an Agricultural Extension Officer
- To confirm recommended vegetable varieties and planting windows suited to a specific altitude band or local microclimate within a broad zone.
- For guidance on disease and pest management appropriate to local humidity and rainfall conditions, particularly in humid tropical and highland zones.
- When planning irrigated dry-season vegetable production, to assess water source reliability and appropriate crop choice.
