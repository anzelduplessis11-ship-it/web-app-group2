# Regional Planting Calendar: North Africa

## Overview
North Africa (Egypt, Morocco, Tunisia, Algeria, Libya) spans Mediterranean coastal climates in the north to hyper-arid Saharan interior further south, with agriculture shaped less by a single rainfall pattern than by the availability of water from two distinct sources: winter Mediterranean rainfall in coastal and near-coastal areas, and river or groundwater irrigation elsewhere, most notably the Nile in Egypt. Across most of the region, the farming year is organized around a cool winter season (roughly October/November to April/May) and a warm summer season (roughly April/May to October), rather than a single wet-season onset as in Sub-Saharan Africa.

## Climatic Sub-Zones
- **Mediterranean coastal/rainfed zone** (northern Morocco, northern Algeria, northern Tunisia, coastal Libya): winter rainfall roughly October-April, hot dry summers, 300-1000+ mm annual rainfall depending on distance from the coast and elevation.
- **Nile Valley and Delta irrigated zone** (Egypt): negligible reliable rainfall; agriculture depends almost entirely on Nile water delivered through canal systems, supporting up to three cropping windows per year rather than a single rain-fed season.
- **Oasis and Saharan interior zone** (southern Morocco, southern Algeria and Tunisia, most of Libya's interior, Egypt's oases): scattered irrigated agriculture around groundwater sources and oases, surrounded by extremely low or effectively no reliable rainfall.
- **High Atlas/interior mountain and steppe zone** (interior Morocco, high plateaus of Algeria and Tunisia): higher elevation, cooler temperatures, moderate winter rainfall or snow, supporting rainfed cereal and orchard production but with greater frost risk than the coastal zone.

## General Planting Calendar by Crop

| Crop | Mediterranean Rainfed Zone | Nile Valley/Delta Irrigated Zone | Oasis/Saharan Zone | Mountain/Steppe Zone |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Wheat/Barley | October-December (rainfed), harvest May-June | November-December (winter/*shitwi* season), harvest April-May | Limited; only where irrigation is available | October-November, harvest June-July (later, due to cooler conditions) |
| Maize | March-April (spring) or as a summer irrigated crop | May-June (summer/*sayfi* season), harvest September-October | Limited; irrigated, April-May | Limited by short warm season; May where grown |
| Rice (irrigated paddy) | Not a traditional crop outside the Nile system | May (main producing area, Nile Delta), harvest September-October | Not practiced | Not practiced |
| Cotton | Limited, irrigated pockets, April sowing | March-April (summer/*sayfi* season), harvest September-November | Limited | Not practiced |
| Olives (establishment) | November-December or February-March | Limited but possible with irrigation | Limited but possible with irrigation | November-December or February-March |
| Citrus (establishment) | February-April, with irrigation for establishment | February-April, with irrigation; major production zone | Limited to irrigated sites | Limited (frost risk) |
| Dates (palm offshoots/seedlings) | Not a primary zone | Limited (Upper Egypt oases) | February-April, primary zone; harvest September-November | Not practiced |
| Fava beans (winter legume) | October-November, harvest March-April | October-November (winter/*shitwi* season), harvest March-April | Limited, irrigated | October-November, harvest April-May |

## Egypt's Three-Season System
Egypt's Nile-fed agriculture is conventionally organized into three overlapping cropping windows rather than a single rain-defined season:
- **Winter (*shitwi*), roughly November-April**: wheat, fava beans, berseem (Egyptian clover) and other cool-season crops, relying on cooler temperatures and irrigation.
- **Summer (*sayfi*), roughly April/May-September/October**: cotton, maize, rice, and sesame, grown during the hot season with full irrigation.
- **Nili/autumn, roughly August-October**: a shorter transitional window for maize or vegetables squeezed between the summer harvest and winter planting, used where field rotation and water allocation allow.

## Key Regional Notes
- Water availability, not rainfall timing, is the primary constraint in the Nile Valley, oasis, and Saharan interior zones; irrigation scheduling, canal water release, and groundwater management typically determine planting dates more than climate does.
- In the Mediterranean rainfed zone, cereal and legume sowing is timed to the autumn/winter rainfall onset (roughly October-December); a late or weak autumn rain onset is a significant production risk, comparable in principle to the "false start" risk seen in rainfed farming systems further south in Africa.
- Frost can occur in interior highland and steppe areas, and even during cold winter nights in low-lying areas such as the Nile Delta; this can damage sensitive vegetables and young citrus or other frost-sensitive perennials, so timing and site selection should account for local frost risk.
- Perennial tree crops (olives, citrus, dates) represent long-term farm investments across the region and are established at the start of favourable growing conditions rather than replanted each year; once established, they are managed on a multi-year production cycle distinct from the annual field-crop calendar.
- Groundwater depletion in oasis systems and pressure on Nile water allocation are long-term structural concerns for the region's irrigated agriculture, reinforcing the importance of water-efficient irrigation methods (see `irrigation/irrigation_methods.md` and `irrigation/irrigation_scheduling.md`) alongside calendar timing.

## Adjusting for Local Conditions
- Distance from the coast and elevation cause sharp differences in rainfall totals and frost risk within relatively short distances across the Mediterranean and mountain/steppe zones.
- Specific irrigation scheme water release schedules, particularly in the Nile Valley and Delta and in oasis systems, may take precedence over the general calendar windows presented here.
- Local soil salinity, a recognized issue in parts of the Nile Delta and some oasis areas, can affect crop choice and timing independently of the broad climatic calendar.

## When to Consult an Agricultural Extension Officer
- To confirm the current-season Nile water release or canal irrigation schedule for a specific command area before committing to a summer or winter crop.
- To confirm autumn/winter rainfall onset predictions and recommended sowing windows for a specific locality in the Mediterranean rainfed zone.
- For guidance on frost-tolerant variety choice or protective measures in frost-prone highland, steppe, or Delta localities.
- When establishing perennial tree crops (olives, citrus, dates), to assess water source reliability and site suitability before planting.
