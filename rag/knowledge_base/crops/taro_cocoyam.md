# Taro / Cocoyam (Colocasia esculenta)

## Overview

Taro, widely known across Africa as cocoyam, dasheen, madumbe (Southern Africa), amadumbe (isiZulu), or coco/mankani in parts of West Africa, is a herbaceous perennial root crop grown as an annual for its starchy underground corms and edible leaves. It belongs to the aroid family (Araceae) and produces a central mother corm surrounded by smaller lateral corms called cormels. Both the corm and the young leaves are important foods for smallholder households, and the crop is prized because it thrives in wet, marginal, and shaded conditions where cereals and many other tubers perform poorly.

For African smallholders, taro fills several important roles. It is a reliable staple and famine-reserve crop that can be left in the ground and harvested progressively as needed, giving households food security through lean months. It grows well in swampy valley bottoms, riverbanks, and seasonally flooded land (locally called dambos, fadama, or vleis) that would otherwise be difficult to farm. The leaves provide a nutritious, iron- and vitamin-A-rich vegetable that can be picked repeatedly during the season. The corms are rich in easily digestible starch, contain useful potassium and dietary fibre, and are valued for infant and invalid diets because the fine starch granules are gentle on the stomach.

Taro should not be confused with tannia or "new cocoyam" (Xanthosoma sagittifolium), a related aroid that produces larger lateral cormels, prefers better-drained soils, and has arrow-shaped (sagittate) leaves rather than the peltate (shield-shaped, stalk-in-the-middle) leaves of true taro. Many African markets sell both under the single name "cocoyam," so buyers and extension officers should confirm which species a farmer is growing. This document focuses on Colocasia esculenta. For related root and tuber crops in the knowledge base, see `crops/cassava.md`, `crops/sweet_potato.md`, and `crops/yam.md`.

A critical safety point: all parts of raw taro contain needle-like calcium oxalate crystals (raphides) that cause severe mouth and throat irritation. Corms and leaves must always be thoroughly cooked (boiled, steamed, roasted, or fermented) before eating. This affects post-harvest handling and consumer advice throughout the value chain.

## Scientific Name

- **Botanical name:** *Colocasia esculenta* (L.) Schott
- **Family:** Araceae (the aroid or arum family)
- **Common synonyms in cultivation literature:** *Colocasia antiquorum*
- **Major cultivar groups:** the *esculenta* group (dasheen types, grown mainly for a large cylindrical mother corm) and the *antiquorum* group (eddoe types, grown mainly for numerous small cormels; generally more cold- and drought-tolerant and more suited to upland cultivation).
- **Common names:** taro, cocoyam, old cocoyam, dasheen, eddoe, madumbe/amadumbe, coco, mankani, kontomire (referring to the leaves in parts of West Africa).

Distinguishing dasheen from eddoe types matters agronomically because eddoe types tolerate drier, cooler upland fields, while dasheen types reward wetland cultivation with a single large corm.

## Suitable Climate

Taro is fundamentally a warm, humid, tropical and subtropical crop with a long growing season. Understanding its climatic needs prevents the most common cause of failure: planting it in conditions that are too dry.

- **Temperature:** Optimal growth occurs between 21 and 27 degrees Celsius. Growth slows markedly below 20 degrees Celsius, and the crop is highly frost-sensitive; any frost destroys foliage and can rot exposed corms. In cooler highland areas (for example the Kenyan and Ethiopian highlands, or the South African midlands), eddoe types are preferred and planting is timed to avoid frost.
- **Rainfall:** Taro needs high, well-distributed moisture, ideally 1,500 to 2,500 mm per year or the equivalent supplied through irrigation, wetland residual moisture, or flooding. It is one of the few staples that tolerates waterlogged and even shallow standing-water conditions, which is why it dominates valley bottoms and stream margins.
- **Growing period:** Most cultivars require a long frost-free season of roughly 6 to 12 months from planting to maturity, so a reliable extended wet season or supplementary water is essential.
- **Altitude:** Grown successfully from sea level to about 2,000 m, and eddoe types can be grown somewhat higher where frost is absent.
- **Light:** Taro tolerates partial shade better than almost any other root crop, making it valuable for intercropping under bananas, plantains, or young tree crops and for home gardens with dappled light.

Climate-smart note: because taro withstands flooding and waterlogging that kill maize and cassava, it is an important resilience crop in a changing climate, particularly for wetland margins. However, it is very vulnerable to drought and heat stress, so in drier zones it must be confined to naturally moist sites or irrigated land.

## Suitable Soil

Taro is adaptable but performs best on deep, fertile, moisture-retentive soils rich in organic matter.

- **Texture:** Heavy loams, silty loams, and clay loams are ideal because they hold moisture well. Sandy soils are only suitable where irrigation or a high water table maintains constant moisture.
- **pH:** A slightly acidic to near-neutral range of 5.5 to 6.5 is best. Taro tolerates mildly acidic soils better than many crops but yields decline on strongly acidic or alkaline soils.
- **Drainage and water regime:** Uniquely, taro can be grown in two distinct systems: flooded/wetland (paddy-style, dasheen types) and upland/dryland (rain-fed or irrigated, eddoe types). In flooded systems the soil must retain water; in upland systems it must stay consistently moist but not permanently waterlogged around the growing point.
- **Organic matter:** High organic matter improves both moisture retention and nutrient supply, and taro responds strongly to compost and manure.
- **Salinity:** Taro is sensitive to salinity, so brackish irrigation water and saline soils should be avoided.

Before choosing fertiliser inputs, farmers should assess soil fertility and pH. See `soil/soil_testing_and_fertility.md` for sampling methods and low-cost indicators, and `soil/soil_ph_management.md` for correcting acidity.

## Soil Preparation

Good land preparation determines corm size and ease of harvest.

1. **Clear and level the land.** Remove weeds, crop residues, and perennial grass rhizomes such as couch grass that compete aggressively with the slow-establishing crop. Composting residues rather than burning them conserves nutrients and organic matter.
2. **Cultivate deeply.** Because corms swell underground, loose, deep soil (worked to 25 to 30 cm) allows corms to expand and makes harvesting easier. Hand hoeing, animal-drawn ploughing, or minimum tillage on already-loose wetland soils are all used depending on resources.
3. **Incorporate organic matter.** Work in well-rotted manure or compost at roughly 10 to 20 tonnes per hectare (a few large wheelbarrows per bed for a home garden). This is the single most cost-effective input for smallholders.
4. **Form the growing surface to suit the water system.**
   - *Wetland/flooded system:* level the field and construct low bunds so it can hold a shallow layer of water, as in rice paddies.
   - *Upland system:* make ridges or mounds 20 to 30 cm high, or raised beds, to concentrate topsoil and organic matter around the plant while still retaining moisture. Ridging also eases hilling-up and harvest.
5. **Plan drainage channels** in upland fields to shed excess storm water while keeping the root zone moist, and inlet/outlet channels in wetland fields to manage water depth.

## Planting Season by African Region

Planting is timed to place the long growing period across the wet season (or the period of assured irrigation/wetland moisture).

| Region | Typical Planting Window | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| West Africa (Nigeria, Ghana, Cameroon) | March to May, at the onset of the main rains | Long single rains in the forest zone; planted in valley bottoms and fadama; leaves harvested progressively for kontomire dishes. |
| East Africa (Kenya, Uganda, Tanzania) | March to April (long rains); also October to November (short rains) in bimodal areas | Wetland margins around lakes and swamps allow year-round staggered planting; frost-free highlands favour eddoe types. |
| Central Africa (DR Congo, Congo, Gabon) | Onset of main rains, roughly September to November or March to April by zone | Abundant humidity suits taro; commonly intercropped under bananas and in forest gardens. |
| Southern Africa (South Africa, eSwatini, Zimbabwe, Mozambique) | September to December (spring/early summer, after frost risk passes) | Grown as amadumbe/madumbe along rivers and in irrigated gardens; must mature before winter frost. |
| Sahel and drier zones (with irrigation) | At start of rains or any time under reliable irrigation | Only viable on permanently moist sites, riverbanks, or irrigated schemes; not a rain-fed option in dry savanna. |
| Ethiopian and equatorial highlands | Onset of main rains (roughly March to June) | Eddoe/cool-tolerant types; time planting so bulking occurs during warm, moist months and harvest avoids frost. |

Because taro can remain in the ground and be harvested progressively, exact windows are flexible where moisture is continuous. The guiding rule is that the crop needs 6 to 12 months of warm, moist conditions from planting to maturity.

## Seed or Seedling Requirements

Taro is propagated vegetatively; there is no practical seed-based system for smallholders because flowering is irregular and true seed is rarely used outside breeding programmes. Common planting materials are:

- **Setts (huli):** the top 1 to 2 cm slice of the mother corm together with the base of the leaf petioles, trimmed to a short stub. This is the preferred, vigorous planting material.
- **Small whole corms or cormels:** especially for eddoe types.
- **Suckers or side shoots** detached from established plants.

Selection and preparation guidance:

- Choose planting material only from **healthy, vigorous, disease-free mother plants**, ideally the best-performing plants in the previous crop. Reject any material showing rot, water-soaked lesions, or leaf blight symptoms, because taro leaf blight and corm rots are carried on planting material.
- Use material of uniform size for even establishment.
- Where available, **clean/certified planting material** or tissue-cultured plantlets dramatically reduce the spread of diseases and viruses; extension services and multiplication gardens are the safest source.
- Setts can be pre-sprouted in a shaded, moist nursery bed to give a head start and allow rogueing of weak plants before transplanting.

Planting material requirement depends on spacing but typically ranges from about **10,000 to 30,000 setts per hectare**. For a home garden, a farmer can multiply from a handful of corms over one or two seasons.

## Plant Spacing

Spacing balances corm size against total yield and depends on cultivar type and water system.

- **Upland/ridge system:** commonly 60 to 90 cm between rows and 40 to 60 cm within the row. A widely used spacing is 75 cm by 50 cm, giving roughly 25,000 to 27,000 plants per hectare.
- **Wetland/flooded system:** often planted a little denser, 50 to 60 cm each way, because ample water supports more plants.
- **Eddoe types** (smaller plants) can be spaced more closely; large dasheen types need the wider end of the range.
- **Home gardens and intercrops:** taro is frequently planted at wider, irregular spacing between banana stools or along bed edges where moisture collects.

Planting depth is 5 to 10 cm. Closer spacing raises total tonnage but produces smaller individual corms; wider spacing produces fewer, larger, more marketable corms. Farmers should match spacing to whether the market rewards large corms or overall volume.

## Water Requirements

Water is the decisive factor for taro yield.

- Taro needs **continuous, abundant soil moisture** throughout its long season. Total water demand is high, on the order of 1,500 to 2,500 mm across the crop, whether from rain, a high water table, flooding, or irrigation.
- **Most sensitive stages** are establishment (the first 4 to 6 weeks) and the corm bulking period; moisture stress at bulking sharply reduces corm size and can cause corms to crack or become fibrous.
- In **flooded systems**, a shallow layer of moving or regularly refreshed water (a few centimetres) is maintained; stagnant deep water reduces oxygen and encourages rot, so gentle flow or periodic draining is better than deep standing water.
- In **upland systems**, irrigate whenever the topsoil begins to dry; mulching heavily conserves moisture and is one of the most valuable low-input practices.
- Toward maturity, water can be gradually reduced to help corms firm up and improve storage quality.

Taro's tolerance of waterlogging is a genuine advantage, but it is not a substitute for management: even in wetlands the growing point should not be permanently submerged, and drainage control prevents rot. For irrigation scheduling principles, see `water/irrigation_methods.md` and for moisture conservation see `soil/mulching_and_water_conservation.md`.

## Fertiliser Recommendations

Taro removes substantial nutrients, particularly potassium, over its long season, so fertility management pays off.

- **Organic base (recommended for all smallholders):** incorporate 10 to 20 t/ha of well-rotted manure or compost during land preparation. This supplies nutrients slowly, feeds soil life, and greatly improves moisture retention, which taro values highly.
- **Nitrogen (N):** promotes leaf growth and photosynthesis; important early to build a strong canopy. Excess late nitrogen delays maturity and favours leaf disease, so split applications (part at planting, part 6 to 10 weeks later) are better than a single heavy dose.
- **Phosphorus (P):** supports root and early corm development; usually applied fully at planting.
- **Potassium (K):** the key nutrient for corm bulking, starch filling, and storage quality. Taro is a heavy potassium feeder, and adequate K improves both yield and eating quality.
- **Micronutrients:** on very leached or sandy soils, watch for deficiencies; organic matter usually supplies enough.

General guidance where soil tests are unavailable: a moderate compound fertiliser providing balanced N-P-K at planting, followed by a nitrogen and potassium top-dress during active leaf growth and early bulking, is typical. **Exact product choice and rates must be based on a soil test and confirmed with a local agricultural extension officer**, because requirements vary widely with soil type, water system, and locality. Always match fertiliser to soil analysis rather than applying blanket rates. See `soil/soil_testing_and_fertility.md` and `soil/composting_and_manure.md` for practical low-input fertility building.

Prices of fertilisers vary considerably by country, season, and subsidy programmes, so budget using local current rates; organic sources are often the most economical route for smallholders.

## Weed Management

Taro establishes slowly and its early canopy is sparse, so weeds compete strongly during the first two to three months and can drastically cut yield if uncontrolled.

- **Timely early weeding is essential.** Keep the crop weed-free for roughly the first 8 to 12 weeks until the canopy closes and begins to shade out weeds.
- **Hand weeding and hoeing** are the mainstay for smallholders; work shallowly near the plant to avoid damaging surface corms and feeder roots.
- **Mulching** with crop residues, grass, or leaf litter is highly effective: it suppresses weeds, conserves moisture, moderates soil temperature, and adds organic matter as it breaks down. This is a leading IPM-aligned practice for taro.
- **Earthing-up (hilling)** during weeding covers the swelling corms, controls weeds, and improves corm quality; it is usually done once or twice during the season.
- **Flooded systems** naturally suppress many weeds because standing water excludes them, giving wetland taro an advantage.
- **Herbicides:** chemical weed control is rarely necessary or economical for smallholder taro and is complicated near waterways where flooded taro is grown. If herbicides are considered, only locally registered products appropriate for aquatic-margin or root-crop use should be used, and the choice, timing, and dose must be confirmed with an agricultural extension officer to protect water sources and the crop.

## Pest Management

Taro suffers fewer pests than many staples, but several are important. An integrated pest management (IPM) approach — combining clean planting material, field hygiene, resistant/tolerant types, natural enemies, and only carefully targeted chemical control — is both cheapest and safest.

- **Taro beetles (Papuana and related genera):** large beetles whose adults tunnel into corms, causing holes that reduce quality and open entry for rots. Manage by field sanitation, destroying breeding sites in decaying organic matter and manure heaps near fields, hand collection, and crop rotation. Where infestations are severe, a locally registered soil insecticide may be advised — confirm with an extension officer.
- **Aphids:** cluster on young leaves and shoots, weaken plants, and, importantly, transmit viruses such as Dasheen mosaic virus. Encourage natural enemies (ladybird beetles, hoverflies, parasitic wasps), remove badly infested shoots, and avoid excess nitrogen that produces soft, aphid-attractive growth. See `pests/aphids.md`.
- **Taro leafhopper and planthoppers:** suck sap and can spread disease; managed similarly through natural enemies and healthy vigorous plants.
- **Spider mites:** flare in hot, dry, dusty conditions; maintaining moisture and canopy humidity and conserving predatory mites usually keeps them in check.
- **Root-knot nematodes (Meloidogyne spp.):** cause galled roots, stunted plants, and corm blemishes, especially in upland sandy soils. Manage with crop rotation (avoid following other susceptible root crops), organic matter, fallowing, and clean planting material. See `pests/nematodes.md`.
- **Snails and slugs and army worms:** may damage leaves, particularly in wet conditions; hand collection, removing hiding places, and protecting natural predators help.

IPM principles for taro:
1. Start with **disease- and pest-free planting material** from healthy mother plants.
2. Practise **field sanitation and crop rotation** to break pest cycles.
3. **Monitor regularly** and act early while populations are small.
4. **Conserve beneficial insects**; avoid unnecessary broad-spectrum sprays that kill natural enemies.
5. If chemical control is genuinely needed, use only **locally registered, approved active-ingredient classes** (for example, appropriate insecticide classes for beetles or sap-feeders) at the correct dose and pre-harvest interval, and always confirm the specific approved product and rate with a qualified agricultural extension officer. Never use unlabelled or brand-unverified chemicals, and observe all safety and water-protection precautions. See `pests/integrated_pest_management.md`.

## Disease Management

- **Taro leaf blight (Phytophthora colocasiae):** the most serious disease of taro worldwide and a major threat in humid African production zones. It causes water-soaked spots that enlarge into large brown lesions with yellow halos, rapidly destroying leaves in warm, wet, humid weather, and can also rot corms. Management is integrated: plant **tolerant or resistant varieties** where available, use **clean planting material**, widen spacing and orient rows for airflow to reduce leaf wetness, remove and destroy infected leaves, rotate fields, and avoid overhead irrigation late in the day. Where disease pressure is high, protectant fungicides from appropriate active-ingredient classes may be used preventively — the registered product, timing, and dose must be confirmed with an agricultural extension officer, and resistance is best managed by alternating fungicide classes and prioritising cultural control.
- **Corm and soft rots (Pythium, Phytophthora, Sclerotium, and bacterial soft rots such as Erwinia/Pectobacterium):** cause mushy, foul-smelling decay of corms in the field and in storage, worst under poor drainage and waterlogging combined with wounding. Manage with good drainage, avoiding mechanical injury during weeding and harvest, careful curing and handling, rotation, and clean, undamaged planting material.
- **Dasheen mosaic virus (DsMV):** spread by aphids and through infected planting material; causes mosaic patterns, feathery leaf distortion, and gradual yield decline. There is no cure, so control relies on using **virus-free (ideally tissue-cultured) planting material**, roguing infected plants, and controlling aphid vectors.
- **Fungal leaf spots:** minor compared with leaf blight; managed by sanitation and healthy plant nutrition.

Overarching disease strategy: the majority of taro disease loss is prevented by (1) clean planting material, (2) resistant/tolerant varieties, (3) good drainage and spacing for airflow, (4) field sanitation and rotation, and only then (5) judicious, correctly advised fungicide use. For general principles see `diseases/fungal_disease_management.md` and `diseases/plant_disease_prevention.md`.

## Growth Stages

Taro passes through recognisable stages over its 6-to-12-month cycle:

1. **Establishment / sprouting (0 to 4 weeks):** the sett or corm sprouts and the first roots and shoot emerge. Needs constant moisture and weed-free conditions.
2. **Vegetative growth / canopy development (about 1 to 4 months):** rapid leaf and petiole growth builds the large shield-shaped leaves; the plant accumulates the photosynthetic capacity that will later fill the corm. Nitrogen and weed control are most important here.
3. **Corm initiation and bulking (about 3 to 8 months):** the mother corm and cormels swell and fill with starch. This is the most water- and potassium-demanding phase; moisture stress now cuts yield most severely.
4. **Maturation (about 6 to 12 months):** leaf production slows, older leaves yellow and die back, and the corms firm up. Water is gradually reduced to improve storage quality.
5. **Senescence / harvest readiness:** most foliage yellows and collapses, signalling that corms have reached maximum size and are ready to lift.

Because taro can be left in the ground, farmers often harvest progressively from stage 4 onward as household or market needs dictate.

## Harvest Indicators

Taro is ready to harvest when:

- **Leaves turn yellow and begin to dry and die back**, indicating the plant has finished bulking and is translocating reserves into the corm. This is the primary visual signal.
- The expected **maturity period for the cultivar has elapsed** (commonly 6 to 8 months for early eddoe types, up to 9 to 12 months for large dasheen types).
- **Corms feel firm and full** on gentle probing, and a test dig reveals well-developed corms of marketable size.
- For **leaf harvesting**, young unfurling to just-mature leaves are picked selectively throughout the season without over-stripping the plant, since removing too many leaves reduces corm yield.

Harvest by loosening the soil with a fork or hoe and lifting the whole plant to avoid cutting or bruising the corms. Corms that are wounded during harvest rot quickly in storage, so careful lifting directly protects later quality and market value. In flooded fields, the water is usually drained a week or two before harvest to firm the soil and corms.

## Post-Harvest Handling

Taro corms are perishable and injury-prone, so careful handling is essential to reduce losses.

- **Clean gently:** remove adhering soil by hand or with minimal water; avoid scrubbing that breaks the skin.
- **Trim** roots and the top, leaving the corm intact and unwounded. Reserve the best tops as setts for the next crop.
- **Cure** freshly harvested corms in a shaded, well-ventilated place for a few days to heal minor skin wounds and reduce rot; avoid direct sun, which causes greening and heat damage.
- **Sort and grade** by size and quality, discarding damaged, rotten, beetle-bored, or diseased corms so they do not spread rot to sound ones.
- **Handle in shade and cool of the day,** and use padded or well-ventilated containers (baskets, crates) rather than tightly packed sacks that bruise and heat the corms.
- **Leaves** are highly perishable and should be marketed or consumed within a day or two, kept cool and out of the sun; light misting keeps them fresh briefly.

Safety and consumer advice: because raw corms and leaves contain calcium oxalate raphides that irritate the mouth and throat, all taro must be **thoroughly cooked** before eating. Handlers with sensitive skin may find peeling raw corms irritating and can peel under water or wear gloves. See `postharvest/reducing_postharvest_losses.md` for general principles.

## Storage

Taro corms do not store as long as yams or cassava and are prone to rot, sprouting, and weight loss.

- **Short-term (weeks):** store cured, undamaged corms in a cool, dry, shaded, well-ventilated place. In a cool room, sound corms typically keep for a few weeks to about a month; quality declines steadily as they lose moisture and begin to sprout.
- **In-ground storage:** the most practical smallholder method is to **leave mature corms in the ground and harvest progressively** as needed, provided pest and rot pressure is low and the soil is not waterlogged. This avoids storage losses altogether but ties up the land.
- **Cool storage:** where refrigeration is available, corms keep longer at cool (not freezing) temperatures of roughly 7 to 10 degrees Celsius with high humidity; below about 7 degrees they suffer chilling injury.
- **Processing for longer storage:** peeling, slicing, and **drying** corms into chips or milling into flour greatly extends shelf life and adds value; **fermented and dried products** are traditional in several regions. Drying also reduces oxalate content.
- **Reject and separate** any rotting corm immediately, as soft rot spreads rapidly through a stored heap.

Store **planting setts** separately in a cool, airy, shaded place, or simply take them fresh from newly harvested healthy plants at the next planting.

## Expected Yield

Yields vary widely with cultivar, water supply, soil fertility, and management:

- **Smallholder rain-fed / low-input:** commonly 5 to 12 t/ha of fresh corms; poorly managed or moisture-stressed crops can fall below this.
- **Well-managed with adequate water and fertility:** 15 to 25 t/ha.
- **Optimal irrigated or wetland conditions with good cultivars:** 25 to 40 t/ha or more is achievable.

The single largest lever for yield is **assured moisture throughout bulking**, followed by soil fertility (especially potassium and organic matter), clean disease-free planting material, and timely weeding. Leaf harvesting, if excessive, reduces corm yield, so farmers growing primarily for corms should harvest leaves sparingly.

## Common Mistakes

- **Planting in soil that is too dry.** Taro's greatest failure cause is insufficient moisture; growing it on dry upland without irrigation gives poor, fibrous corms.
- **Using diseased or poor planting material,** which spreads leaf blight, corm rots, and Dasheen mosaic virus and locks in low yields season after season.
- **Neglecting early weed control,** allowing weeds to smother the slow-establishing crop during the critical first two to three months.
- **Over-harvesting leaves** from plants intended for corm production, starving the corm of photosynthate.
- **Wounding corms at harvest** and then storing them, leading to rapid rot and heavy post-harvest losses.
- **Poor drainage in upland fields,** causing corm and soft rots, while conversely allowing deep stagnant water in wetland fields, which suffocates roots.
- **Applying excessive late nitrogen,** which produces lush leaves, delays maturity, and worsens leaf disease and aphid attack.
- **Continuous cropping of taro (or other root crops) on the same land,** building up nematodes and soil-borne rots; rotation is neglected.
- **Eating undercooked taro,** causing painful mouth and throat irritation from calcium oxalate.

## Best Farming Practices

- **Match the site to the crop:** grow taro on naturally moist land — valley bottoms, riverbanks, wetlands, or irrigated plots — and choose dasheen types for wetlands and eddoe types for cooler or drier uplands.
- **Start clean:** use vigorous, disease-free setts from the best mother plants, or certified/tissue-cultured material where available, to control blight and virus.
- **Build the soil:** incorporate generous compost or manure and maintain organic matter; base fertiliser decisions on a soil test and extension advice, prioritising potassium for bulking.
- **Mulch heavily** to conserve moisture, suppress weeds, and feed the soil — one of the highest-value low-cost practices for taro.
- **Weed early and earth-up** the corms once or twice during the season.
- **Practise IPM:** monitor regularly, conserve natural enemies, maintain sanitation and rotation, and reserve chemical control for genuine need using only locally registered products confirmed with an extension officer.
- **Rotate** with non-aroid crops such as legumes or cereals to break pest and disease cycles and restore fertility; intercrop under bananas or young trees to use partial shade productively.
- **Manage water at both ends:** ensure steady moisture through bulking, then reduce water before harvest to firm corms.
- **Harvest and handle gently,** cure corms, sort out damaged ones, and store cool and ventilated or leave in the ground for progressive harvest.
- **Add value** by drying corms into chips or flour to extend storage and improve market returns, and always advise consumers to cook taro thoroughly.

For cross-cutting guidance see `soil/crop_rotation_and_intercropping.md`, `climate/climate_smart_agriculture.md`, and `postharvest/value_addition_and_processing.md`.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: What is the difference between taro and cocoyam, and between old and new cocoyam?**
A: In Africa "cocoyam" is used loosely. True taro (Colocasia esculenta), often called "old cocoyam," has shield-shaped leaves with the stalk joining in the middle and produces one large mother corm plus small cormels. "New cocoyam" or tannia (Xanthosoma sagittifolium) has arrow-shaped leaves, prefers better-drained soil, and produces larger lateral cormels. Both are sold as cocoyam, so confirm the species when buying planting material.

**Q: Can taro be grown without a wetland or irrigation?**
A: Only where rainfall is high (roughly 1,500 mm or more, well distributed) and the soil holds moisture. In drier zones taro must be grown on naturally moist sites such as valley bottoms or riverbanks, or under irrigation. Without reliable moisture, especially during corm bulking, yields are poor and corms become small and fibrous.

**Q: How long does taro take from planting to harvest?**
A: Typically 6 to 8 months for early eddoe types and 9 to 12 months for large dasheen types. Harvest when the leaves yellow and die back and the corms feel firm and full. Mature corms can be left in the ground and dug progressively as needed if pests and rot are not a problem.

**Q: Why does raw taro make the mouth itch, and is it safe to eat?**
A: Raw taro corms and leaves contain needle-like calcium oxalate crystals that irritate the mouth and throat. It is safe and nutritious once **thoroughly cooked** — boiled, steamed, roasted, or fermented — which breaks down the crystals. Never eat it raw, and cook leaves well before eating.

**Q: What is the most damaging disease of taro and how do I manage it?**
A: Taro leaf blight, caused by Phytophthora colocasiae, is the most serious, destroying leaves rapidly in warm, humid, wet weather. Manage it with tolerant varieties, clean planting material, wider spacing for airflow, removal of infected leaves, rotation, and, where pressure is high, preventive protectant fungicides from an appropriate active-ingredient class applied on the advice of an agricultural extension officer.

**Q: Can I harvest taro leaves and still get a good corm yield?**
A: Yes, but sparingly. The leaves are a valuable vegetable, but they also feed corm growth. Pick a few young-to-mature leaves per plant without stripping it bare. Heavy leaf harvesting noticeably reduces corm size, so plants grown mainly for corms should give up only a modest share of their leaves.

**Q: How should I store taro after harvest?**
A: Cure the cleaned, undamaged corms in the shade for a few days, sort out any damaged or rotten ones, and keep them cool, dry, and well ventilated, where they last a few weeks to about a month. For longer keeping, leave mature corms in the ground and harvest as needed, or dry and mill them into chips or flour. Handle gently, because wounded corms rot fast.

**Q: What fertiliser does taro need?**
A: Start with plenty of compost or manure worked into the soil. Taro is a heavy feeder, especially of potassium for corm filling, with nitrogen for early leaf growth and phosphorus for roots. Split nitrogen applications rather than applying it all at once. Base exact rates on a soil test and confirm suitable, locally available products with your agricultural extension officer rather than using blanket rates.
