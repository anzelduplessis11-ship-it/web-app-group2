# Sorghum

## Overview
Sorghum is a drought-tolerant cereal crop of major importance in the drier regions of Africa, where it is a staple food and a reliable choice when rainfall is uncertain. The grain is used for porridge, flour, local beers, and animal feed, while the stalks provide fodder and building material. Sorghum withstands heat and dry spells better than maize, making it a key food-security crop in semi-arid zones. It responds well to good management but is particularly vulnerable to bird damage and the parasitic weed Striga, both of which require deliberate management.

## Scientific Name
*Sorghum bicolor* (L.) Moench

## Suitable Climate
- Optimal growing temperature: 25-32°C; tolerates high heat well.
- Grows with approximately 400-800 mm of rainfall per season and tolerates dry spells better than maize.
- Sensitive to frost; grown in the warm season.
- Adapted mainly to low and mid-altitude areas, generally below approximately 1,500-2,000 m depending on variety.
- Can recover and continue growth after temporary drought stress better than many cereals.

## Suitable Soil
- Grows on a wide range of soils, from sandy loams to heavier clays, provided drainage is reasonable.
- Tolerates a pH range of approximately 5.5-7.5.
- More tolerant of temporary waterlogging and heavy soils than maize, though prolonged waterlogging still harms it.
- Tolerates moderately low fertility but responds well to improved fertility and organic matter.

## Soil Preparation
- Plough and harrow to produce a firm, moderately fine seedbed suitable for small cereal seed.
- Because sorghum seed is small, avoid an overly cloddy surface, which leads to poor and uneven emergence.
- Incorporate crop residues or organic manure ahead of planting to improve soil structure and fertility.
- On sloped land, use contour ridging or tied ridges to conserve moisture and reduce erosion, which is especially valuable in dry areas.
- Conduct a soil test where possible to guide fertiliser use; see `soil/soil_testing_and_fertility.md`.

## Planting Season by African Region
| Region | Typical Planting Window | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| West Africa (Sahel/savanna) | May-July (onset of rains) | A major staple in the semi-arid belt; photoperiod-sensitive local varieties are timed to the rains |
| East Africa | March-April (long rains); Oct-Nov (short rains) | Grown in drier lowland zones where maize is riskier |
| Southern Africa | Nov-Dec (start of summer rains) | Favoured in drought-prone districts as a maize alternative |
| Central Africa | Bimodal zones (Sept-Nov and March-April) | Less dominant where rainfall is high and humid |
| North Africa | Under irrigation: April-June | Rainfed production is limited to wetter margins |

## Seed or Seedling Requirements
- Use certified or good-quality seed of a variety adapted to local rainfall, season length, and Striga pressure.
- Seed rate: approximately 8-12 kg/ha for row planting; adjust for seed size and germination.
- Test germination before planting if seed is farm-saved or older than one season.
- A seed dressing against soil pests and seed-borne diseases can be beneficial where these are a known risk; confirm locally registered products with an extension officer.
- Sow into moist soil at a shallow depth (about 2-4 cm), as deep planting reduces emergence of the small seed.

## Plant Spacing
- Common spacing: 60-75 cm between rows, with plants thinned to approximately 10-20 cm within the row.
- Thin seedlings early to the target population to avoid overcrowding and competition for limited moisture.
- Wider spacing is used in drier areas to reduce competition for water; closer spacing suits higher-rainfall areas.

## Water Requirements
- Total seasonal water requirement: approximately 400-650 mm depending on climate and variety.
- Critical water-sensitive stages: establishment, panicle initiation (booting), flowering, and grain filling.
- Sorghum can pause growth under drought and resume when moisture returns, but stress at flowering still reduces grain set.
- In dry areas, moisture-conservation practices such as tied ridges and mulching help buffer dry spells; see `irrigation/irrigation_methods.md`.

## Fertiliser Recommendations
- Basal application at planting: a compound fertiliser supplying phosphorus and some nitrogen, guided by local soil test recommendations.
- Topdress with nitrogen (such as a urea topdress) once the crop is established and growing actively, splitting where feasible to reduce loss.
- Match fertiliser rates to realistic yield targets and rainfall; heavy nitrogen without adequate moisture is wasteful and can encourage lodging.
- Apply organic manure or compost where available to build soil fertility over time; see `fertilisers/fertiliser_basics.md`.
- Avoid over-fertilising in very dry seasons, as excess nitrogen without moisture provides little benefit.

## Weed Management
- First weeding: 2-3 weeks after emergence, when young sorghum competes poorly with weeds.
- Second weeding: around 5-6 weeks after emergence, before canopy development.
- Striga (witchweed) is a serious parasitic weed of sorghum in many regions and requires an integrated approach including tolerant varieties, rotation, and improved soil fertility; see `weeds/striga.md`.
- Hand hoeing, mechanical cultivation, or approved herbicides can be used; confirm registered products locally.
- See `weeds/general_weed_management.md` for integrated control principles.

## Pest Management
- Key pests: birds (a major cause of grain loss, especially for non-bird-resistant varieties), stalk borers, shoot fly, midge, and storage weevils.
- Bird damage can be severe; measures include bird-scaring, community-level coordinated planting, and choosing varieties with some bird tolerance where available.
- Scout for stalk borers and other pests during vegetative growth; see `pests/fall_armyworm.md` for a related caterpillar pest and general scouting principles.
- Use integrated pest management combining cultural practices, scouting, and chemical control only when thresholds are exceeded, using locally registered products.

## Disease Management
- Key diseases: grain mould (especially in humid conditions at maturity), anthracnose, leaf blights, smuts, and downy mildew.
- Use resistant or tolerant varieties where available and healthy, clean seed.
- Practice crop rotation with non-cereal crops such as legumes to break disease cycles.
- Avoid dense stands in humid areas, and where possible choose varieties and planting dates so that grain maturity does not coincide with heavy rain, reducing grain mould.
- For suspected severe or unfamiliar disease outbreaks, consult a qualified agricultural extension officer.

## Growth Stages
1. Germination and emergence (0-10 days after planting).
2. Vegetative growth: leaf development and stem elongation (approximately 10-45 days).
3. Panicle initiation and booting: the developing head forms within the stem (approximately 40-65 days).
4. Flowering, grain filling, and maturity: the panicle flowers and grain fills, reaching physiological maturity typically 90-140 days after planting depending on variety.

## Harvest Indicators
- Grain reaches its full variety colour and becomes hard; a dark layer forms at the base of the grain at physiological maturity.
- Panicles dry and the crop as a whole turns from green to a mature straw colour.
- Grain moisture at physiological maturity is high; allow the crop to dry down in the field where weather permits before harvesting for storage.
- Harvest promptly once mature to reduce losses to birds, weathering, and grain mould, especially in humid conditions.

## Post-Harvest Handling
- Cut and gather the heads (panicles) and dry them thoroughly before threshing.
- Dry grain to a safe moisture level (comparable to other cereals, approximately 12-13%) before storage to prevent mould and insect damage.
- Thresh only after adequate drying to reduce grain damage.
- Sort out mouldy, discoloured, or insect-damaged grain before storage, as mouldy sorghum can pose a food safety risk.

## Storage
- Store dried grain in a cool, dry, well-ventilated place protected from rain, rodents, and insects.
- Use hermetic storage bags or metal silos to protect against storage weevils and grain borer without chemical treatment where possible.
- If using chemical grain protectants, follow label instructions and use only locally approved products.
- Monitor stored grain regularly for moisture, insects, and mould.
- See `storage/grain_storage.md` for full storage guidance.

## Expected Yield
- Smallholder rainfed systems with basic management: approximately 0.5-2 tonnes/ha.
- Improved management with good seed, fertiliser, and pest/bird control: approximately 2-4 tonnes/ha.
- Higher yields are achievable under favourable conditions with adequate management.
- Actual yield depends heavily on rainfall, variety, Striga and bird pressure, and soil fertility.

## Common Mistakes
- Planting seed too deep, leading to poor emergence of the small seed.
- Failing to thin seedlings, causing overcrowding and competition for scarce moisture.
- Ignoring Striga, allowing the parasitic weed to build up and severely reduce yields over time.
- Underestimating bird damage and failing to protect maturing grain.
- Storing grain before it is properly dried, leading to mould and quality loss.

## Best Farming Practices
- Rotate sorghum with legumes (such as groundnut or cowpea) to improve soil fertility and help manage Striga and diseases.
- Use certified or good-quality seed of varieties adapted to local rainfall and Striga pressure.
- Practice moisture-conservation measures such as tied ridges in dry areas.
- Manage Striga through integrated methods including tolerant varieties, rotation, and improved fertility.
- Coordinate planting within a community where possible to reduce concentrated bird damage.
- Harvest and dry promptly to protect grain quality; see `sustainability/sustainable_farming_practices.md`.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Why is sorghum often recommended over maize in my dry area?**
A: Sorghum is generally more drought-tolerant and heat-tolerant than maize and can pause growth during dry spells and recover when rain returns. In areas with low or unreliable rainfall, it is typically a more dependable food-security crop, though local variety choice matters.

**Q: What can I do about the striga weed strangling my sorghum?**
A: Striga is best managed through an integrated approach: growing tolerant varieties, rotating with legumes, improving soil fertility (Striga is worse on poor soils), and removing Striga plants before they set seed. See `weeds/striga.md` and consult an extension officer for locally suited methods.

**Q: How do I reduce bird damage to my sorghum?**
A: Options include bird-scaring during grain filling, choosing varieties with some bird tolerance where available, and coordinating planting with neighbours so the crop matures together, spreading the risk rather than concentrating birds on a few early fields.

**Q: My sorghum grain has mould on the heads. Is it safe?**
A: Grain mould commonly develops when maturity coincides with wet, humid conditions. Mouldy grain can carry a food safety risk and should be sorted out and not stored or consumed if heavily affected. Where mould is significant or its safety is unclear, consult a qualified agricultural or food safety authority.
