# Soybean (Soya Bean)

## Overview
Soybean is a high-protein, high-oil grain legume that has expanded rapidly across Africa as demand grows for cooking oil, poultry and livestock feed, and protein-rich foods such as soy flour, soy milk, and processed products. As a legume, soybean fixes atmospheric nitrogen through symbiosis with *Bradyrhizobium* bacteria, improving soil fertility and making it a valuable rotation crop that boosts the yield of following cereals such as maize. Soybean seed is roughly 35-40% protein and 18-20% oil, giving it strong and reliable markets. Major and growing producers include Nigeria, South Africa, Zambia, Malawi, Zimbabwe, Uganda, and Ethiopia. Soybean suits smallholders because it can be grown with modest inputs, improves the soil, and offers cash and nutrition, but it has specific requirements that must be met for good yields: correct inoculation (or previous soybean history) for nitrogen fixation, a variety adapted to local daylength, timely planting, and careful management of pod shattering at harvest. The most important input decision for many African soybean farmers is whether the variety needs rhizobium inoculant and whether it is a "promiscuous" type that nodulates with local soil bacteria.

## Scientific Name
*Glycine max* (L.) Merr., family Fabaceae (Leguminosae), subfamily Faboideae.

## Suitable Climate
- Warm-season crop; optimal temperatures are approximately 20-30°C.
- Grown from the lowlands up to about 2,000 m altitude, from humid to sub-humid and moist savanna zones.
- Requires approximately 450-700 mm of well-distributed rainfall per season; sensitive to drought at flowering and pod filling but also to waterlogging.
- Sensitive to frost; grown in the warm, rainy season.
- Soybean is strongly photoperiod-sensitive (a short-day plant): each variety flowers within a specific range of daylength, so varieties are adapted to particular latitudes and must be matched to the local zone, or they may flower too early (stunted) or too late.
- Matures in roughly 90-150 days depending on variety and conditions.

## Suitable Soil
- Grows best on well-drained loams and sandy loams with good organic matter.
- Optimal pH is approximately 6.0-7.0; soybean is sensitive to strong acidity (below about pH 5.2) and to aluminium and manganese toxicity, and also dislikes strongly alkaline soils.
- Requires good drainage; waterlogging kills nodules and roots and promotes disease.
- Moderately tolerant of low fertility once well nodulated, but responds to phosphorus and adequate potassium.
- Lime acidic soils based on a soil test to improve nodulation and nutrient availability.
- Test soil to guide lime and fertiliser decisions; see `soil/soil_testing_and_fertility.md`.

## Soil Preparation
- Prepare a firm, reasonably fine, weed-free seedbed for good seed-to-soil contact and even emergence.
- Plough and harrow to break clods; soybean seedlings are moderately strong but establish best on a clean, mellow seedbed.
- On acidic soils, incorporate agricultural lime several weeks before planting based on a soil test to raise pH toward 6.0-6.5, which greatly improves nodulation.
- Ensure good drainage; on heavy or flood-prone soils, plant on ridges or raised beds.
- Incorporate crop residues, compost, or well-rotted manure to build organic matter; see `fertilisers/organic_fertilisers.md`.
- Where soybean has not been grown before, plan for rhizobium inoculation at planting (see below), as the correct bacteria are usually absent from the soil.

## Planting Season by African Region

## Seed or Seedling Requirements
- Soybean is direct-sown from seed and is never transplanted.
- Use certified or good-quality seed of a variety adapted to your zone's daylength and maturity length; this is critical because of soybean's photoperiod sensitivity.
- Soybean seed loses viability quickly in storage, so use fresh, well-stored seed and always test germination before planting; discard cracked, wrinkled, or mouldy seed.
- Seed rate: approximately 60-100 kg/ha depending on seed size and spacing; aim for a target plant population of roughly 300,000-400,000 plants/ha.
- Inoculate seed with the correct *Bradyrhizobium* strain at planting where soybean has not been grown recently and the variety is not "promiscuous"; handle inoculant carefully (keep cool, out of direct sun, use before expiry) and plant inoculated seed the same day.
- Sow 3-5 cm deep into moist soil; handle inoculated seed gently to protect the bacteria.

## Plant Spacing
- Row spacing is typically 45-60 cm between rows and 5-10 cm between plants within the row.
- Narrower rows (around 45 cm) with the correct density improve canopy closure, weed suppression, and yield.
- Bushy, branching varieties can use slightly wider spacing than erect, less-branched types.
- Aim for an even, dense stand; gaps encourage weeds and reduce yield, while over-thick stands cause thin, lodging-prone plants.

## Water Requirements
- Total seasonal water requirement is approximately 450-700 mm.
- The most water-sensitive stages are flowering, pod formation, and pod (seed) filling; stress during these stages sharply reduces pod number and seed size.
- Establishment also needs adequate moisture for even emergence and early nodulation.
- Soybean tolerates short dry spells during vegetative growth better than during reproduction.
- Excess water and waterlogging kill nodules and roots and promote root rots and foliar disease.
- Mostly grown rainfed; supplemental irrigation at flowering and pod fill greatly stabilises yield where available.

## Fertiliser Recommendations
- Well-nodulated soybean fixes most of its own nitrogen and usually needs little or no nitrogen fertiliser; a small "starter" nitrogen dose is sometimes used on poor soils before nodulation establishes, but heavy nitrogen suppresses nodulation and wastes money.
- Ensure effective nodulation through correct inoculation (or a promiscuous variety plus previous soybean history) rather than relying on nitrogen fertiliser.
- Phosphorus is the key nutrient: apply a basal dressing (commonly 20-40 kg P/ha) at planting to support rooting, nodulation, and pod filling, especially on deficient soils.
- Potassium is needed where soils are deficient; soybean removes significant potassium in the grain.
- On acidic soils, lime and, where indicated by soil tests, correct molybdenum and other micronutrients; molybdenum is important for nitrogen fixation.
- Base rates on a soil test; see `soil/soil_testing_and_fertility.md` and `fertilisers/fertiliser_basics.md`.
- Organic manure and compost improve soil fertility and structure over time; see `fertilisers/organic_fertilisers.md`.

## Weed Management
- Weeds compete most strongly in the first 4-6 weeks, before the soybean canopy closes; early control is essential for both yield and easy harvesting.
- The first weeding at 2-3 weeks after emergence and a second at 5-6 weeks are the standard approach on smallholdings.
- Once the canopy closes in a well-spaced, vigorous crop, soybean suppresses many later weeds.
- Hoe or hand weed carefully to avoid damaging the shallow roots and nodules, and avoid throwing soil over young plants.
- Pre-emergence and post-emergence herbicides are used in larger systems; correct product choice and timing are essential and some herbicides can injure soybean. Confirm locally registered, approved products and rates with a qualified agricultural extension officer.
- Rotation with cereals helps break weed cycles. See `weeds/general_weed_management.md`.

## Pest Management
- Key field pests include aphids, whiteflies, thrips, leaf-eating caterpillars and loopers, pod borers, pod-sucking bugs (stink bugs), and, in some areas, bean flies/stem maggots at the seedling stage.
- Pod-sucking bugs and pod borers directly damage the harvestable seed and should be scouted from flowering onward.
- Storage pests are relatively minor compared with other pulses, but the crop should still be protected; see the Storage section.
- Practise integrated pest management (IPM): scout weekly, conserve natural enemies (ladybirds, parasitic wasps, spiders), use tolerant varieties, and rotate crops. See `pests/aphids.md`.
- Where chemical control is justified, use the appropriate active-ingredient class only when scouting thresholds are exceeded, and confirm locally registered, approved products and dosages with a qualified agricultural extension officer.

## Disease Management
- Important diseases include soybean rust (an aggressive foliar disease that can cause severe losses in humid conditions), frogeye leaf spot, bacterial pustule and blight, various root and stem rots, anthracnose, and pod and stem blight.
- Soybean rust is the most serious; manage it with resistant/tolerant varieties where available, timely planting, and appropriate fungicides applied at first signs in high-risk humid seasons.
- Use clean, disease-free, certified seed and treat seed where seed-borne diseases are a risk.
- Rotate with non-legume crops (cereals) to reduce root rots and residue-borne diseases, and avoid waterlogged soils.
- Ensure adequate spacing and avoid excess nitrogen to reduce humid, disease-favouring canopies.
- Triazole and strobilurin fungicide classes are used against rust and leaf spots when justified; confirm locally registered, approved fungicides, timing, and dosages with a qualified agricultural extension officer, and rotate fungicide groups to slow resistance.
- For unfamiliar or severe outbreaks, consult a qualified agricultural extension officer.

## Growth Stages
1. Germination and emergence (0-10 days): the seedling emerges with the seed leaves (cotyledons).
2. Vegetative growth (approximately 10-40 days): leaves and nodes develop, and root nodules form and begin fixing nitrogen.
3. Flowering (approximately 35-60 days): small flowers appear at the nodes; onset depends strongly on variety and daylength.
4. Pod formation (approximately 50-75 days): pods set and begin to develop.
5. Seed (pod) filling (approximately 65-110 days): seeds enlarge and fill the pods; this is the key yield-determining and water-sensitive stage.
6. Maturity (approximately 90-150 days): leaves yellow and drop, pods dry, and seeds harden to their mature colour.

## Harvest Indicators
- Leaves turn yellow and drop, and pods turn from green to their mature brown or tan colour and become dry.
- Seeds rattle in the dry pods and are hard, no longer soft or green inside.
- Grain moisture at harvest should be around 13-15%; harvest promptly once pods are dry.
- Timeliness is critical: over-dry pods shatter readily, scattering seed and causing heavy field losses, especially in hot dry weather. Harvest in the cooler morning to reduce shattering.
- Do not delay harvest waiting for the last pods, as shattering losses of the bulk of the crop outweigh the gain.

## Post-Harvest Handling
- Harvest by cutting or pulling whole plants when pods are dry, then dry them further on clean surfaces before threshing.
- Thresh gently by beating or machine; soybean seed cracks easily, and cracked seed germinates poorly and stores badly, so avoid rough handling and over-drying before threshing.
- Dry threshed grain to a safe storage moisture (approximately 12-13%).
- Clean and sort grain to remove pods, trash, and damaged, split, or discoloured seed.
- Seed intended for planting must be handled especially gently and dried carefully, as soybean seed loses viability quickly.
- Haulms (dried plant residues) make useful livestock fodder and, if returned to the soil, add organic matter and nitrogen.

## Storage
- Store thoroughly dried grain (approximately 12-13% moisture) in a cool, dry, well-ventilated, rodent-proof store.
- Hermetic (airtight) bags or containers protect grain from insects and moisture and, importantly for seed, help preserve germination.
- Soybean grain for planting is short-lived; store seed cool and dry and use it within one season for best germination, testing before sowing.
- Inspect stored grain regularly for insects, moisture, and mould, and remove affected stock.
- Use only locally approved grain protectants if needed, and confirm with a qualified agricultural extension officer.
- See `storage/grain_storage.md`.

## Expected Yield
- Smallholder rainfed systems with basic management: approximately 0.8-1.5 tonnes/ha.
- Improved varieties with correct inoculation, phosphorus, good spacing, and pest/disease control: approximately 1.8-3 tonnes/ha.
- Well-managed crops under favourable conditions can exceed 3 tonnes/ha.
- Yield depends heavily on variety-zone matching, effective nodulation, water at flowering and pod fill, disease (especially rust) control, and timely, low-shatter harvesting.

## Common Mistakes
- Growing a variety not matched to the local daylength, so it flowers too early (tiny plants) or too late.
- Failing to inoculate on soils without soybean history (and not using a promiscuous variety), resulting in poor nodulation, yellow plants, and low yield.
- Applying heavy nitrogen fertiliser, which suppresses nodulation and wastes money.
- Planting old, cracked, or poorly stored seed with low germination.
- Delaying harvest so pods shatter, or threshing roughly and cracking the seed.
- Growing susceptible varieties in humid conditions and losing yield to soybean rust.

## Best Farming Practices
- Choose a certified variety matched to your zone's daylength and season length, and use fresh, high-germination seed.
- Inoculate with the correct *Bradyrhizobium* strain where soybean is new to the field, or use a promiscuous variety; handle inoculant carefully.
- Apply phosphorus based on a soil test and lime acidic soils; avoid unnecessary nitrogen.
- Plant early in the rains at the correct spacing for a dense, even, canopy-closing stand.
- Scout for pests and diseases, especially soybean rust and pod pests, and act early using IPM, confirming any chemicals with extension advice.
- Harvest promptly when pods are dry (in the cool morning) to minimise shattering, and thresh gently to avoid cracking seed.
- Rotate soybean with cereals such as maize to exploit its nitrogen contribution and break disease cycles; see `crops/maize.md` and `sustainability/sustainable_farming_practices.md`.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: My soybean plants are yellow and stunted even though the soil looks fine. Why?**
A: The most likely cause is poor nitrogen fixation from a lack of nodulation. If soybean is new to the field, the correct *Bradyrhizobium* bacteria are usually absent, so the crop cannot fix nitrogen. Inoculate the seed at planting with the right strain, or grow a "promiscuous" variety that nodulates with local bacteria. Yellowing can also come from waterlogging or very acidic soil.

**Q: Do I need to inoculate my soybean seed?**
A: Yes, if soybean has not been grown in that field recently and you are not using a promiscuous variety, because the specific rhizobium bacteria are usually not present in the soil. Inoculation is cheap and greatly improves yield and soil nitrogen. Keep inoculant cool and out of the sun, apply it at planting, and sow the same day.

**Q: Why did my soybean flower when the plants were tiny and yield almost nothing?**
A: Soybean is very sensitive to daylength. A variety not adapted to your latitude can flower far too early, before the plant has grown enough to carry a crop. Always choose a variety recommended for your specific zone and season length; matching the variety to daylength is one of the most important decisions in soybean.

**Q: My pods keep splitting open and dropping seed before I harvest. What can I do?**
A: That is pod shattering, worst when over-ripe pods are exposed to hot, dry weather. Harvest promptly once pods are dry rather than waiting for the very last pods, and harvest in the cooler morning when pods are less brittle. Choosing shatter-tolerant varieties also helps.

**Q: Do I need to add nitrogen fertiliser to soybean?**
A: Usually no, if nodulation is good, because the crop fixes most of its own nitrogen. Focus instead on effective inoculation and on phosphorus. Heavy nitrogen actually suppresses nodulation and wastes money. A very small starter dose is occasionally used on poor soils before nodules establish.

**Q: Why does my saved soybean seed germinate so poorly?**
A: Soybean seed loses viability faster than most grains, and it cracks easily during threshing. Handle seed gently, avoid over-drying before threshing, store it cool and dry (ideally airtight), use it within one season, and always test germination before planting.

**Q: Is soybean good for my soil?**
A: Yes. As a well-nodulated legume it fixes nitrogen and, in rotation, raises the yield of following cereals such as maize while reducing their fertiliser needs. Returning the haulms adds organic matter and nitrogen. This makes soybean a valuable, soil-improving cash crop.
