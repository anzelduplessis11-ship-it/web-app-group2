# Finger Millet

## Overview
Finger millet is a hardy small-grained cereal grown widely across the highlands and semi-arid zones of East and Southern Africa, where it is a valued food-security and nutrition crop. Its name comes from the finger-like branches (the "hand") that make up the seed head. Finger millet is prized for its exceptionally nutritious grain, which is high in calcium, iron, and dietary fibre, and for its outstanding storability: well-dried finger millet can be stored for several years with minimal insect damage, making it a classic famine-reserve crop. It is milled into flour for thick and thin porridges, flatbreads, and traditional fermented foods and beverages, and is especially important as a weaning and convalescent food because of its calcium content. Finger millet tolerates drought, poor soils, and a range of altitudes, and requires few purchased inputs, which suits low-resource smallholders. Its main agronomic challenges are the tiny seed (which demands a fine seedbed and careful sowing), heavy early weed competition, the disease blast, and the labour of weeding and threshing. Key producing areas include Uganda, Kenya, Tanzania, Ethiopia, Zambia, Zimbabwe, Malawi, and parts of the Democratic Republic of Congo.

## Scientific Name
*Eleusine coracana* (L.) Gaertn., family Poaceae (Gramineae). It is sometimes called African finger millet, ragi, or wimbi.

## Suitable Climate
- Warm to moderately cool crop; optimal temperatures are approximately 20-30°C.
- Grown from the lowlands up to about 2,400 m altitude, performing especially well in mid- and high-altitude areas of East Africa.
- Requires approximately 400-750 mm of rainfall per season; more drought-tolerant than maize and able to yield where maize fails, though less drought-hardy than pearl millet and sorghum in the driest zones.
- A short-day crop that flowers in response to shortening daylength; matures in roughly 3-5 months depending on variety and altitude.
- Sensitive to waterlogging and to very heavy continuous rain during flowering and grain filling, which promotes blast and grain moulds.
- Tolerates dry spells well once established, thanks to a fibrous, efficient root system.

## Suitable Soil
- Grows on a wide range of soils, from sandy loams to clays, but prefers well-drained loams with reasonable fertility and organic matter.
- Optimal pH is approximately 5.0-7.5; tolerates moderately acidic soils better than many cereals.
- Tolerates lower fertility than maize but responds well to improved fertility and organic matter.
- Does not tolerate waterlogging; poorly drained soils cause poor stands and root problems.
- Suited to the lighter, shallower highland soils where maize is unreliable.
- Test soil to guide any fertiliser and lime use; see `soil/soil_testing_and_fertility.md`.

## Soil Preparation
- Prepare a fine, firm, weed-free seedbed; this is essential because finger millet seed is extremely small and needs shallow, even placement and good soil contact.
- Plough and then harrow one or more times to break clods to a fine tilth; a rough, cloddy seedbed gives thin, patchy stands.
- Level the seedbed so the tiny seed is not washed into hollows or buried too deep.
- Incorporate crop residues, compost, or well-rotted manure ahead of planting to raise fertility and improve moisture retention; see `fertilisers/organic_fertilisers.md`.
- On sloped highland land, use contour cultivation, terraces, or tied ridges to control erosion and conserve moisture.
- Because early weed competition is severe, start with the cleanest possible seedbed, ideally after a well-managed fallow or a competitive previous crop.

## Seed or Seedling Requirements
- Finger millet is usually direct-sown from seed; occasionally seedlings are raised in a nursery and transplanted (a traditional practice in parts of East Africa that improves stand establishment and reduces seed rate).
- Use clean, good-quality seed of an adapted variety; improved blast-tolerant, high-yielding varieties are available from research and extension services.
- Seed rate for row sowing: approximately 4-8 kg/ha; broadcasting uses more, roughly 8-12 kg/ha, because distribution is less efficient.
- Because the seed is tiny, mix it with dry sand, fine soil, or sieved ash to help spread it evenly and avoid over-thick sowing.
- Sow shallow, about 1-2.5 cm deep; deeper sowing prevents the small seed from emerging.
- Test germination of farm-saved seed and discard mouldy or discoloured grain.

## Plant Spacing
- Row sowing is preferred over broadcasting because it eases weeding and improves yield.
- Rows are typically spaced 22-30 cm apart, with seed drilled continuously along the row and later thinned if very dense.
- For transplanted crops, seedlings are set at roughly 15-25 cm between plants and 22-30 cm between rows.
- Broadcasting is common and simpler but makes weeding much harder and gives uneven stands.
- Aim for a dense but not overcrowded stand that closes the canopy to suppress weeds while allowing good tillering.

## Water Requirements
- Total seasonal water requirement is approximately 400-600 mm.
- The most sensitive stages are establishment (because the small seedlings are fragile), flowering, and grain filling.
- Moisture stress at flowering and grain filling reduces head size and grain set.
- Finger millet tolerates dry spells well once established but yields best with well-distributed rainfall.
- Excess water and prolonged wetness, especially at flowering, favour blast disease and grain moulds and can cause lodging.
- Mostly grown rainfed; supplemental irrigation is uncommon on smallholdings but can stabilise yields where available.

## Fertiliser Recommendations
- Finger millet responds well to moderate fertility but is often grown with few inputs; base rates on a soil test where possible. See `soil/soil_testing_and_fertility.md` and `fertilisers/fertiliser_basics.md`.
- Nitrogen: approximately 20-60 kg N/ha raises yield markedly; split into a basal dose and a top-dressing at tillering to early stem elongation. Avoid excess nitrogen, which causes lodging and delays maturity.
- Phosphorus: a basal application (commonly 10-20 kg P/ha) supports rooting and early growth, especially on deficient soils.
- Organic manure and compost are particularly valuable and well-suited to this low-input crop, improving fertility and moisture retention; see `fertilisers/organic_fertilisers.md`.
- Where finger millet follows a legume in rotation, it benefits from residual nitrogen and needs less fertiliser; see `crops/pigeon_pea.md` and `crops/soybean.md`.
- Wood ash traditionally supplies some potassium and helps the tiny seedbed, but should not replace balanced fertility management.

## Weed Management
- Weed control is the single biggest management challenge, because young finger millet grows slowly, is tiny, and competes very poorly with weeds for the first 4-6 weeks.
- The most damaging weed is often the parasitic striga (witchweed) in some areas, plus fast-growing grasses and broadleaf weeds that easily smother the seedlings.
- Row sowing greatly eases weeding compared with broadcasting; the first weeding at 2-3 weeks after emergence is critical, followed by a second at 5-6 weeks.
- Hand weeding and hoeing are the norm; take care to distinguish young finger millet from grassy weeds, which look similar early on.
- A fine, clean seedbed, good fertility, and appropriate density help the crop close its canopy and suppress later weeds.
- Rotation and, where present, striga-management practices (trap crops, improved fertility, resistant varieties) reduce weed and parasite pressure.
- Selective herbicides for finger millet are limited and must be used with care given the small seedlings; confirm any locally registered, approved products with a qualified agricultural extension officer. See `weeds/general_weed_management.md`.

## Pest Management
- Finger millet suffers relatively few serious field pests, which is one of its advantages.
- Occasional field pests include stem borers, aphids, armyworms, shoot flies, and birds; bird damage at ripening (including from quelea) can be significant and needs scaring.
- The grain's tough seed coat makes it highly resistant to storage insects, giving finger millet its excellent long-term storability.
- Practise integrated pest management (IPM): scout regularly, conserve natural enemies, use clean fields, and time planting to avoid peak pest periods. See `pests/aphids.md` for aphid guidance.
- Where chemical control is justified, use the appropriate active-ingredient class only when scouting thresholds are exceeded, and confirm locally registered, approved products and dosages with a qualified agricultural extension officer.

## Disease Management
- Blast (caused by a fungus) is the most important disease, attacking leaves, the neck of the head (neck blast), and the fingers (finger blast); neck and finger blast can cause severe grain loss.
- Other diseases include leaf spots, downy mildew, and grain smut.
- Manage blast by growing resistant or tolerant varieties, using clean seed, avoiding excessive nitrogen and overly dense stands, rotating crops, and removing infected residues.
- Avoid planting so that the susceptible flowering and heading stages coincide with prolonged wet, humid weather.
- Ensure good drainage and air movement within the crop to reduce leaf wetness.
- Triazole and other fungicide classes can manage blast on susceptible crops when disease appears, but resistant varieties and cultural control are the mainstay for this low-input crop. Confirm locally registered, approved fungicides and dosages with a qualified agricultural extension officer.

## Growth Stages
1. Germination and emergence (0-10 days): the tiny seedling emerges; this fragile stage needs a fine, moist, weed-free seedbed.
2. Seedling and early vegetative growth (approximately 10-30 days): slow early growth during which weed competition is most damaging.
3. Tillering (approximately 25-50 days): the plant produces multiple tillers, each potentially bearing a head.
4. Stem elongation and booting (approximately 45-70 days): stems lengthen and heads develop.
5. Heading and flowering (approximately 60-90 days): the characteristic finger-like heads emerge and flower.
6. Grain filling (approximately 85-120 days): grains fill and change colour.
7. Maturity (approximately 100-150 days depending on variety and altitude): heads and grain dry and harden.

## Harvest Indicators
- The finger-like heads change from green to their mature brown, reddish, or straw colour depending on variety.
- Grains harden and the crop leaves dry down.
- Because tillers mature at different times, heads may be harvested in stages, or the crop cut when most heads are mature.
- Harvest by cutting the heads (with a small knife or by hand) rather than the whole plant, or cut the whole plant and dry it before threshing.
- Harvest promptly once mature to reduce bird damage, shattering, and the risk of grain moulds if wet weather continues.

## Post-Harvest Handling
- Dry the harvested heads thoroughly in the sun on clean surfaces (mats or tarpaulins, never bare soil) before threshing.
- Thresh by beating, treading, or rubbing the dried heads, then winnow to separate the tiny grain from chaff.
- Because the grain is so small, use fine sieves and take care during winnowing to avoid losses.
- Dry the threshed grain further to a safe storage moisture (approximately 12-13%).
- Clean and sort the grain to remove chaff, sand, and foreign matter; the fine grain is otherwise gritty and lower value.
- The straw and threshing residues make useful livestock fodder.

## Storage
- Finger millet is one of the best-storing cereals; well-dried grain resists storage insects thanks to its hard seed coat and can be kept for several years, making it an excellent food reserve.
- Store thoroughly dried grain (approximately 12-13% moisture) in clean, dry, well-ventilated, rodent-proof containers, pots, gourds, granaries, or sealed bags.
- Even with its natural resistance, keep grain dry to prevent mould, and protect it from rodents, which will still attack it.
- Hermetic (airtight) storage further protects grain and preserves quality.
- Inspect stored grain periodically for moisture and rodents.
- See `storage/grain_storage.md`.

## Expected Yield
- Smallholder rainfed systems with basic management and broadcasting: approximately 0.5-1.2 tonnes/ha.
- Row-sown crops with improved varieties, good weeding, and moderate fertility: approximately 1.5-3 tonnes/ha.
- Well-managed improved crops under favourable conditions can exceed 3 tonnes/ha.
- Yield depends heavily on weed control, variety, blast management, seedbed quality, and rainfall distribution.

## Common Mistakes
- Sowing the tiny seed too deep or on a rough, cloddy seedbed, giving thin, patchy stands.
- Broadcasting instead of row sowing, then failing to weed adequately, so weeds smother the slow-growing crop.
- Delaying the first weeding beyond the critical early weeks, causing large yield losses.
- Applying too much nitrogen, causing lodging and worse blast.
- Growing susceptible varieties in wet conditions and losing grain to neck and finger blast.
- Harvesting or threshing damp heads, leading to grain moulds despite the crop's good storability.

## Best Farming Practices
- Prepare a fine, firm, level, weed-free seedbed and sow shallow, mixing the tiny seed with sand for even distribution.
- Sow in rows rather than broadcasting to make weeding easier and raise yield.
- Weed early and thoroughly, especially in the first 3-6 weeks when competition is most damaging.
- Use adapted, blast-tolerant improved varieties and clean seed where available.
- Rotate finger millet with legumes such as pigeon pea, groundnut, or soybean to improve soil fertility and break disease and striga cycles; see `crops/pigeon_pea.md` and `crops/groundnut.md`.
- Use organic manure and compost to build the fertility of this low-input crop.
- Dry heads and grain thoroughly and store in clean, dry, rodent-proof, ideally airtight containers to exploit finger millet's excellent storability; see `sustainability/sustainable_farming_practices.md`.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Why is my finger millet stand so thin and patchy?**
A: The most common causes are sowing the tiny seed too deep or on a rough, cloddy seedbed. Finger millet seed is very small and must be sown shallow (about 1-2.5 cm) on a fine, firm, level, moist seedbed. Mixing the seed with dry sand helps spread it evenly and prevents both gaps and over-thick clumps.

**Q: Weeds always smother my finger millet. What can I do?**
A: Young finger millet grows slowly and competes poorly, so weeding is critical. Sow in rows rather than broadcasting to make weeding easier, start with a clean seedbed, and weed at 2-3 weeks and again at 5-6 weeks after emergence. Take care to tell young finger millet from grassy weeds, which look similar early on.

**Q: My finger millet heads have dark, shrivelled patches on the neck and fingers. What is it?**
A: That is blast, the crop's most damaging disease, especially neck and finger blast in wet, humid conditions. Grow resistant varieties, use clean seed, avoid excess nitrogen and over-dense stands, rotate crops, and remove infected residues. Fungicides can help on susceptible crops but resistant varieties are the best defence.

**Q: How long can I store finger millet?**
A: Finger millet is one of the best-storing cereals. Because of its hard seed coat, well-dried grain resists storage insects and can be kept for several years, which is why it is a classic food-reserve crop. Keep it thoroughly dry (about 12-13% moisture) and protect it from rodents and moisture.

**Q: Is finger millet more nutritious than maize?**
A: Finger millet is notably rich in calcium, iron, and fibre, more so than maize, which is why it is valued as a weaning food and for people needing extra minerals. Its long storability also makes it more reliable for food security, though maize often yields more grain per hectare on good land.

**Q: How much seed do I need per hectare?**
A: For row sowing, roughly 4-8 kg/ha; broadcasting needs more, about 8-12 kg/ha. Because the seed is so fine, mix it with dry sand or fine soil to spread it evenly and avoid sowing too thickly.

**Q: Can finger millet grow where maize fails from drought?**
A: Often yes. Finger millet is more drought-tolerant than maize and yields on poorer, lighter soils, though it is less drought-hardy than pearl millet and sorghum in the very driest zones. It is a good choice for uncertain mid- and high-altitude rainfall areas. See `crops/pearl_millet.md` and `crops/sorghum.md`.
