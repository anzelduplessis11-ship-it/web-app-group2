# Cowpea

## Overview
Cowpea is a versatile grain legume grown widely across Africa, especially in the drier savanna and Sahel regions. Both the dry seeds and the fresh leaves are eaten, making it an important source of protein for many households, while the haulms provide valuable livestock fodder. As a legume, cowpea fixes atmospheric nitrogen and improves soil fertility, making it an excellent crop for rotation and intercropping with cereals such as maize, sorghum, and millet. Cowpea is drought-tolerant and fast-maturing, but it is highly susceptible to insect pests both in the field and in storage, which is its main management challenge.

## Scientific Name
*Vigna unguiculata* (L.) Walp.

## Suitable Climate
- Optimal growing temperature: 25-35°C; tolerates heat well.
- Grows with approximately 300-700 mm of rainfall per season and tolerates dry spells.
- Sensitive to frost; grown in the warm season.
- Adapted mainly to low and mid-altitude areas, generally below approximately 1,500 m.
- Drought-tolerant and quick to mature, allowing it to fit into short or uncertain rainy seasons.

## Suitable Soil
- Grows on a wide range of soils but prefers well-drained sandy loams.
- Tolerates a pH range of approximately 5.5-7.0; slightly acidic to neutral soils are suitable.
- Tolerates low fertility and sandy soils better than many crops, thanks to nitrogen fixation.
- Does not tolerate waterlogging; poorly drained soils promote root rots and reduce yield.

## Soil Preparation
- Prepare a firm, reasonably fine seedbed for good seed-to-soil contact.
- Because cowpea tolerates lighter soils, heavy tillage is often unnecessary; minimum tillage can conserve moisture in dry areas.
- Incorporate crop residues or organic matter ahead of planting to improve soil structure.
- On sloped land, use contour cultivation to reduce erosion and conserve moisture.
- Where possible, conduct a soil test to guide any fertiliser use; see `soil/soil_testing_and_fertility.md`.

## Planting Season by African Region
| Region | Typical Planting Window | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| West Africa (Sahel/savanna) | June-August (during/after cereal establishment) | Commonly intercropped with millet and sorghum; late-season sole crops are also grown |
| East Africa | March-April (long rains); Oct-Nov (short rains) | Fits well into either rainy season due to quick maturity |
| Southern Africa | Nov-Jan (summer rains) | Can be planted through the season because of its short cycle |
| Central Africa | Bimodal zones (Sept-Nov and March-April) | Two crops possible in suitable zones |
| North Africa | Under irrigation: April-June | Rainfed production is limited by aridity |

## Seed or Seedling Requirements
- Use certified or good-quality seed of a variety adapted to local conditions and end use (grain, dual-purpose grain-and-fodder, or vegetable/leaf type).
- Seed rate: approximately 15-30 kg/ha for sole cropping, lower when intercropped; adjust for seed size and spacing.
- Test germination before planting if seed is farm-saved; discard damaged, discoloured, or weevil-holed seed.
- A seed dressing against soil-borne fungi and early insects can help where these are a known risk; confirm locally registered products with an extension officer.

## Plant Spacing
- Sole crop spacing: approximately 40-60 cm between rows and 15-20 cm between plants, adjusted for spreading versus erect variety types.
- Erect/bush varieties can be planted more closely; spreading (trailing) varieties need wider spacing.
- When intercropped with cereals, cowpea is planted between or within the cereal rows at reduced density.

## Water Requirements
- Total seasonal water requirement: approximately 300-500 mm depending on climate and variety.
- Critical water-sensitive stages: establishment, flowering, and pod filling.
- Cowpea tolerates dry spells well but water stress during flowering and pod filling reduces grain yield.
- Excessive moisture, especially late in the season, encourages disease and can reduce grain quality.

## Fertiliser Recommendations
- As a legume, cowpea fixes much of its own nitrogen and generally needs little or no nitrogen fertiliser.
- Phosphorus supports root growth, nodulation, and pod filling; a basal phosphorus application benefits deficient soils.
- Where soils are known to be low in specific nutrients, apply based on a soil test; see `soil/soil_testing_and_fertility.md` and `fertilisers/fertiliser_basics.md`.
- Inoculating seed with appropriate rhizobia can improve nitrogen fixation where cowpea has not been grown recently.
- Organic manure or compost improves soil fertility over time and is valuable where available; see `fertilisers/organic_fertilisers.md`.

## Weed Management
- First weeding: 2-3 weeks after emergence, when young cowpea competes poorly with weeds.
- Second weeding: around 5-6 weeks after emergence, before spreading varieties cover the ground.
- Once trailing types close their canopy, they suppress later weeds, reducing weeding needs.
- Hand hoeing is the most common method; take care not to damage shallow roots or trailing stems.
- See `weeds/general_weed_management.md` for integrated weed control principles.

## Pest Management
- Key pests: aphids, flower thrips, pod-sucking bugs, pod borers, and the storage bruchid (cowpea weevil), which is a major storage pest.
- Field pests attacking flowers and pods can cause heavy losses; scout regularly from flowering onward and see `pests/aphids.md` for aphid management.
- The storage bruchid can rapidly destroy stored grain and often begins infestation in the field, so timely harvest and good storage are critical.
- Use integrated pest management combining scouting, cultural practices, and chemical control only when thresholds are exceeded, using locally registered products confirmed with an extension officer.

## Disease Management
- Key diseases: cowpea aphid-borne mosaic and other viruses, bacterial blight, anthracnose, and various leaf spots and root rots.
- Use resistant or tolerant varieties where available and healthy, clean, disease-free seed.
- Control aphids to help limit the spread of aphid-borne viruses.
- Practice crop rotation with non-legume crops (such as cereals) and avoid waterlogged soils to reduce root and foliar diseases.
- For suspected severe or unfamiliar disease outbreaks, consult a qualified agricultural extension officer.

## Growth Stages
1. Germination and emergence (0-7 days after planting).
2. Vegetative growth: development of leaves and branches (approximately 7-35 days).
3. Flowering: flowers form and set pods (approximately 30-55 days).
4. Pod filling and maturity: pods fill and dry, reaching maturity typically 60-100 days after planting depending on variety and use.

## Harvest Indicators
- For dry grain, pods turn from green to dry, papery, and their mature colour, and seeds harden inside.
- For fresh (vegetable) use, pods or immature seeds are picked while still tender, before drying.
- Leaves can be picked young throughout vegetative growth for use as a vegetable, in moderation.
- Because pods do not all mature at once, several pickings may be needed, or the crop is harvested when the majority of pods are dry.

## Post-Harvest Handling
- Harvest dry pods promptly once mature to reduce shattering and field losses, and to limit storage bruchid infestation that starts in the field.
- Dry pods and threshed grain thoroughly to a safe moisture level (comparable to other pulses/grains, approximately 12-13%) before storage.
- Thresh only after adequate drying to reduce grain damage.
- Sort out damaged, discoloured, or insect-holed seed before storage, as these often carry bruchid infestation.

## Storage
- Store well-dried grain in a cool, dry, well-ventilated place protected from moisture, rodents, and insects.
- The storage bruchid (cowpea weevil) is the main storage threat and can destroy grain quickly; hermetic (airtight) storage bags or containers are very effective at controlling it without chemicals by depriving the insects of oxygen.
- If using chemical grain protectants, follow label instructions and use only locally approved products.
- Monitor stored grain regularly for insects and moisture, and remove affected stock.
- See `storage/grain_storage.md` for full storage guidance.

## Expected Yield
- Smallholder rainfed systems with basic management: approximately 0.3-1 tonne/ha of dry grain.
- Improved management with good seed, spacing, and pest control: approximately 1.5-2.5 tonnes/ha.
- Higher yields are achievable under favourable conditions with effective field and storage pest management.
- Actual yield depends heavily on variety, rainfall, insect pest pressure, and management.

## Common Mistakes
- Neglecting field pest control at flowering and podding, when insect damage causes the greatest losses.
- Storing grain without protection against the bruchid weevil, leading to rapid destruction of the harvest.
- Applying nitrogen fertiliser unnecessarily, wasting inputs since cowpea fixes its own nitrogen.
- Delaying harvest of dry pods, increasing shattering and storage insect carry-over.
- Planting weevil-holed or damaged seed, resulting in poor stands and early infestation.

## Best Farming Practices
- Rotate or intercrop cowpea with cereals such as maize, sorghum, or millet to benefit from its nitrogen fixation and improve total land productivity.
- Use good-quality, well-adapted seed matched to the intended use (grain, fodder, or vegetable).
- Scout and manage field pests carefully from flowering through pod filling.
- Harvest promptly and use hermetic storage to control the bruchid weevil without chemicals.
- Consider rhizobium inoculation on soils where cowpea has not recently been grown; see `sustainability/sustainable_farming_practices.md`.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Weevils keep destroying my stored cowpeas. What can I do?**
A: The cowpea storage bruchid is a major pest and often begins infesting pods in the field. Harvest promptly, dry grain thoroughly, sort out damaged seed, and store in hermetic (airtight) bags or containers, which suffocate the insects and are very effective without chemicals.

**Q: Do I need to add nitrogen fertiliser to my cowpeas?**
A: Generally no, because cowpea fixes much of its own nitrogen as a legume. Focus on phosphorus on deficient soils and consider rhizobium inoculation where cowpea has not been grown recently. Adding nitrogen is usually an unnecessary expense.

**Q: Can I harvest cowpea leaves for food without ruining my grain harvest?**
A: Yes, young cowpea leaves are widely eaten as a nutritious vegetable. Pick them in moderation, especially earlier in growth, so you do not remove too much leaf area needed for pod and grain production later.

**Q: My cowpea flowers and pods are being damaged and dropping. Why?**
A: Flower and pod loss is commonly caused by insect pests such as thrips, pod-sucking bugs, and pod borers, and can be worsened by moisture stress. Scout regularly from flowering onward and use integrated pest management, applying locally registered products only when needed.
