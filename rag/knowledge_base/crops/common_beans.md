# Common Beans

## Overview
Common beans are a major legume crop across Africa, valued as a protein source for human consumption and for their nitrogen-fixing contribution to soil fertility. They are grown as a sole crop or intercropped with cereals such as maize.

## Scientific Name
*Phaseolus vulgaris* L.

## Suitable Climate
- Optimal growing temperature: 18-24°C.
- Sensitive to temperatures above 30°C, particularly during flowering, which reduces pod set.
- Requires 300-500 mm of well-distributed rainfall per season.
- Sensitive to frost and waterlogging.
- Best suited to mid-altitude zones (1,000-2,000 m), though lowland-adapted varieties exist.

## Suitable Soil
- Well-drained loam or sandy loam soils.
- Optimal pH: 6.0-7.0; poor growth and nodulation occur below pH 5.5.
- Avoid heavy clay soils prone to waterlogging, which increase root rot risk.

## Soil Preparation
- Plough and harrow to a fine tilth.
- Avoid deep burial of crop residue that could harbour disease inoculum.
- Apply lime if soil pH is below 5.5 and lime is available, to improve nodulation and nutrient availability.

## Planting Season by African Region
| Region | Typical Planting Window | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| West Africa | June-July and Sept-Oct in some zones | Timed with rainfall onset |
| East Africa | Mar-Apr (long rains) and Sept-Oct (short rains) | Two cropping seasons common |
| Southern Africa | Nov-Dec (start of rains) | Avoid late planting due to frost risk at end of season |
| Central Africa | Sept-Nov and Mar-Apr | Bimodal rainfall allows two seasons |

## Seed or Seedling Requirements
- Use certified, disease-free seed; avoid seed from fields with a history of bacterial or viral disease.
- Seed rate: approximately 60-90 kg/ha depending on seed size and spacing.
- Inoculate seed with rhizobium inoculant where beans have not been grown recently in the field, to enhance nitrogen fixation.

## Plant Spacing
- Standard spacing: 40-50 cm between rows, 10-15 cm between plants for bush varieties.
- Climbing varieties: wider spacing (60-75 cm rows) with staking or support structures.
- Seeding depth: 3-5 cm.

## Water Requirements
- Moderate water requirement; avoid waterlogging, which causes root rot.
- Critical water-sensitive stages: flowering and pod filling.
- Drought stress during flowering significantly reduces pod set and yield.

## Fertiliser Recommendations
- Beans fix atmospheric nitrogen via root nodules but still benefit from starter nitrogen and adequate phosphorus.
- General guideline: 20-30 kg N/ha (starter dose only), 40-60 kg P2O5/ha, 20-40 kg K2O/ha.
- Phosphorus is particularly important for root and nodule development.
- Avoid excessive nitrogen fertilisation, which promotes vegetative growth at the expense of pod formation and can suppress nodulation.

## Weed Management
- Weed at 2-3 weeks and again at 5-6 weeks after emergence.
- Beans are relatively poor competitors with weeds due to low canopy height; early weeding is essential.

## Pest Management
- Key pests: bean stem maggot, aphids, pod borers, and storage bruchids (weevils).
- Scout regularly during vegetative and flowering stages.
- Practice crop rotation to reduce soil-borne pest carryover.
- Dry and store seed properly to prevent bruchid weevil damage in storage.

## Disease Management
- Key diseases: common bacterial blight, angular leaf spot, bean anthracnose, and bean common mosaic virus.
- Use certified disease-free seed as the primary control method.
- Avoid working in fields when foliage is wet, to reduce disease spread.
- Rotate with non-legume crops for at least 2 seasons.

## Growth Stages
1. Germination and emergence (0-10 days).
2. Vegetative growth (2-5 weeks).
3. Flowering (5-7 weeks).
4. Pod filling and maturity (7-10 weeks for most bush varieties; longer for climbing types).

## Harvest Indicators
- For dry beans: pods turn yellow/brown and dry, and rattle when shaken.
- For fresh green beans: harvest pods while still tender and before seeds fully swell.
- Harvest promptly once mature to avoid pod shattering and seed loss in the field.

## Post-Harvest Handling
- Dry harvested pods further on a clean surface or tarpaulin before threshing.
- Thresh carefully to minimize seed damage, which reduces both germination quality and storage life.
- Winnow to remove debris and damaged seed before storage.

## Storage
- Dry seed to 12-14% moisture content before storage.
- Use hermetic storage (sealed bags or containers) to prevent bruchid weevil infestation without chemical treatment.
- Store in a cool, dry location away from moisture and pests.

## Expected Yield
- Traditional smallholder systems: 400-800 kg/ha.
- Improved management with good seed, fertiliser, and pest control: 1.2-2 tonnes/ha.
- High-input systems: 2.5-3.5 tonnes/ha.

## Common Mistakes
- Planting uncertified seed carrying seed-borne bacterial or viral disease.
- Excessive nitrogen fertiliser application, reducing pod set.
- Delayed harvest, leading to pod shattering and seed loss.
- Poor drying before storage, leading to mould and weevil infestation.
- Working in wet fields, spreading bacterial diseases.

## Best Farming Practices
- Rotate beans with cereals to balance soil nitrogen and reduce disease carryover.
- Use rhizobium inoculant on fields without recent bean history.
- Weed early, as beans compete poorly with weeds.
- Harvest promptly at maturity and dry thoroughly before storage.
- Intercrop with maize or other cereals to maximize land use and soil fertility benefits.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Why are my bean plants growing well but producing few pods?**
A: Excess nitrogen (from fertiliser or preceding legume crops) can cause excessive vegetative growth at the expense of flowering and pod set. Heat stress during flowering is another common cause.

**Q: What causes brown, angular lesions on bean leaves?**
A: This is a typical symptom of angular leaf spot or common bacterial blight. Confirm with a close inspection and consult `diseases/` reference material or an extension officer, since management differs between fungal and bacterial causes.

**Q: How do I prevent weevils in stored beans?**
A: Ensure seed is dried to the correct moisture content (12-14%) before storage and use hermetic (airtight) storage bags or containers, which suffocate any weevils present without needing chemical treatment.
