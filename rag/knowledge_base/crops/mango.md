# Mango

## Overview
Mango is one of the most important fruit tree crops across tropical and sub-tropical Africa, valued for fresh consumption, drying, juice, and export. It is a long-lived perennial tree that, once established, can produce fruit for several decades with relatively low annual input, making it well suited to smallholder farms, homestead plots, and agroforestry systems. Mango tolerates a defined dry season better than most fruit trees, which is why it thrives across the Sahel margins, East African lowlands, and the drier parts of Southern Africa. For smallholders it offers several advantages: it stabilises income across years, provides shade and windbreak value, and its fruiting peak often coincides with periods when annual crops are scarce, giving farmers a seasonal cash injection. The main challenges are the long wait to first commercial harvest (typically 3-5 years for grafted trees), heavy losses to fruit flies and anthracnose, and gluts during peak season that depress farm-gate prices. Grafted improved varieties, good orchard sanitation, integrated pest management, and better post-harvest handling are the levers that turn mango from a low-value backyard tree into a profitable enterprise.

## Scientific Name
*Mangifera indica* L. (family Anacardiaceae).

## Suitable Climate
- Mango grows in warm tropical and sub-tropical climates, from sea level up to about 1,200 m altitude; above this, flowering and fruit set become unreliable.
- Optimal mean temperatures are 24-30°C during active growth. Trees tolerate brief heat above 40°C but sustain flower and young-fruit damage.
- A distinct cool, dry period of 2-4 months is important because it induces flowering. Continuously humid, rainy climates promote vegetative growth at the expense of flowers and greatly increase anthracnose.
- Rainfall of 600-1,500 mm per year is adequate. A dry spell during flowering and early fruiting improves fruit set and reduces disease.
- Mango is sensitive to frost, especially young trees. Temperatures near 0°C damage flowers, young fruit, and shoots.
- Strong winds cause fruit drop and limb breakage; windbreaks are valuable in exposed sites.

## Suitable Soil
- Mango is adaptable but performs best on deep (at least 1 m), well-drained loam or sandy loam soils.
- Optimal pH is 5.5-7.5. Highly alkaline or saline soils reduce vigour and cause micronutrient (iron, zinc) deficiencies.
- Good drainage is essential; mango does not tolerate waterlogging, which causes root rot and tree decline.
- Deep soils allow the taproot to reach moisture during the dry season, which supports the tree's drought tolerance.
- See `soil/soil_types_africa.md` for guidance on matching mango to local soil conditions.

## Soil Preparation
- Clear the site of perennial weeds, old tree stumps, and termite mounds well before planting.
- Dig planting holes at least 60 x 60 x 60 cm (larger, 1 x 1 x 1 m, on hard or shallow soils) at the start of the rainy season.
- Separate topsoil from subsoil when digging. Mix the topsoil with 10-20 kg of well-decomposed manure or compost plus phosphorus-supplying fertiliser, and use this to refill the hole.
- Allow holes to weather for a few weeks where possible, and refill so the soil settles before planting.
- On sloping land, establish contour bunds or plant on the contour to conserve water and reduce erosion; see `soil/soil_conservation_and_erosion.md`.

## Planting Season by African Region
| Region | Typical Planting Window | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| West Africa | Start of the rains (Apr-Jun) | Plant early in the wet season so roots establish before the dry period |
| East Africa | Onset of long rains (Mar-Apr) or short rains (Oct-Nov) | Bimodal rainfall gives two establishment windows |
| Southern Africa | Warm wet season (Nov-Jan) | Avoid planting into frost-prone months |
| Central Africa | Onset of main rains (Mar-May or Sept-Oct) | Choose the drier end of the wet season to limit young-tree disease |

## Seed or Seedling Requirements
- Use grafted seedlings of an improved, named variety rather than seedlings grown from seed. Grafted trees come true to type, bear earlier (3-5 years vs. 6-8 years), and stay smaller and easier to manage.
- Buy from a reputable nursery; the graft union should be clean and well healed, the stem straight, and the plant free of pests and leaf disease.
- Choose varieties for the local market and season: early, mid, and late types spread harvest and reduce exposure to gluts. Consider fibreless dessert types for fresh market and firm types for drying and transport.
- Rootstock raised from polyembryonic local seed gives a hardy, drought-tolerant base; the scion determines fruit quality.
- Harden nursery plants under partial shade and reduced watering for one to two weeks before field planting.

## Plant Spacing
- For perennial mango orchards, spacing depends on variety vigour, soil fertility, and whether the trees will be pruned to a managed size.
- Traditional large-tree spacing: 10 x 10 m to 12 x 12 m (about 70-100 trees/ha).
- Semi-intensive spacing with regular pruning: 8 x 8 m (about 150 trees/ha).
- High-density planting with dwarfing varieties and strict pruning: 5 x 5 m or 6 x 4 m (400-500 trees/ha), giving earlier per-hectare yields but requiring skilled canopy management.
- Wider spacing suits dry, low-fertility sites; closer spacing suits fertile, well-watered land with active pruning. Orient rows to allow light penetration and airflow, which reduces disease.

## Water Requirements
- Established mango trees are drought tolerant, but young trees (first 2-3 years) need regular watering to survive the dry season: roughly 15-30 litres per tree per week during dry spells.
- The critical periods for water are flowering and fruit development. Water stress at fruit set causes heavy fruit drop; sudden watering after stress can cause fruit cracking.
- A deliberate dry period before flowering encourages flower initiation. Avoid irrigating heavily just before the expected flowering flush, as this promotes leaf growth instead of flowers.
- Resume regular watering once fruit has set to size the fruit and prevent premature drop.
- Drip or basin irrigation around the root zone is efficient and keeps the trunk dry. See `irrigation/irrigation_methods.md` and `irrigation/irrigation_scheduling.md`.

## Fertiliser Recommendations
- Young trees benefit from small, frequent applications. A common guideline is to apply nitrogen, phosphorus, and potassium in increasing amounts each year for the first four to five years, roughly scaling with tree age.
- Split fertiliser into two or three doses per year, applied at the start of the rains and after harvest, spread in a ring under the canopy edge (drip line) and lightly worked in.
- Bearing trees need higher potassium to support fruit quality and sugar content, along with adequate nitrogen and phosphorus.
- Apply 20-50 kg of well-decomposed manure or compost per bearing tree per year to maintain soil organic matter and micronutrient supply.
- Watch for zinc, boron, and iron deficiencies on alkaline soils, which cause small leaves, poor fruit set, and misshapen fruit; see `fertilisers/nutrient_deficiencies_guide.md` and `fertilisers/organic_fertilisers.md`.
- Confirm rates against a soil test where possible; see `soil/soil_testing_and_fertility.md`.

## Weed Management
- Keep a weed-free ring around young trees (at least 1 m radius) to reduce competition for water and nutrients during establishment.
- Mulch the tree basin with dry grass, crop residue, or leaves to suppress weeds, conserve moisture, and moderate soil temperature; keep mulch a hand's width away from the trunk to avoid collar rot.
- Slash or mow between rows rather than leaving bare soil, which reduces erosion and can support a cover crop or intercrop in the early years.
- Avoid deep cultivation near the tree, which damages surface feeder roots. See `weeds/general_weed_management.md`.

## Pest Management
- Fruit flies are the single most damaging pest, laying eggs in ripening fruit; larvae render fruit unmarketable and cause premature drop. Manage with strict orchard sanitation (collect and destroy fallen and infested fruit), protein bait spots, male-annihilation traps, and early harvesting at the mature-green stage. See `pests/fruit_flies.md`.
- Mango seed weevil and mango stone weevil bore into the seed; sanitation and destroying infested fruit reduce carryover, and some export markets reject infested fruit.
- Mealybugs and scale insects colonise shoots and fruit stalks; encourage natural enemies, use sticky bands on trunks against mango mealybug, and prune out heavily infested material.
- Thrips and hoppers (mango leafhoppers) damage flowers and reduce fruit set; monitor at flowering. See `pests/thrips.md`.
- Follow integrated pest management: monitor weekly, conserve beneficial insects, and use only locally registered products as a last resort. Confirm product choice with a qualified agricultural extension officer and follow `regulations/pesticide_safety_and_handling.md`.

## Disease Management
- Anthracnose (*Colletotrichum*) is the major disease, causing black spots on leaves, flower blight, and sunken lesions on fruit that develop in storage. It is worst in humid, wet flowering seasons. Manage with good airflow (pruning), sanitation, resistant varieties, and protectant fungicide sprays timed at flowering where justified.
- Powdery mildew attacks flowers and young fruit in cool, dry conditions, causing flower drop and poor set.
- Bacterial black spot causes angular leaf spots and raised, cracked lesions on fruit; use clean planting material and windbreaks (wind-driven rain spreads it), and prune out affected wood.
- Mango malformation distorts flower panicles and shoots; remove and destroy affected panicles and avoid propagating from affected trees.
- Practise field sanitation, prune for an open canopy, and avoid overhead wetting of foliage during flowering. Consult an extension officer to confirm locally registered fungicide classes and safe use.

## Growth Stages
For grafted trees, the orchard develops through establishment to full bearing over several years:
1. **Establishment (Year 1-2):** Root and canopy development. Focus on watering, weed control, formative pruning to build a strong open framework of 3-4 main branches, and protecting from pests, livestock, and frost.
2. **Juvenile/pre-bearing (Year 2-4):** Rapid canopy growth. Remove any early flowers so the tree invests in structure. Continue training and light pruning.
3. **Early bearing (Year 3-5 onward):** First commercial crops. Yields rise each year as the canopy fills.
4. **Full bearing (Year 6-10 onward):** Peak, stable production for many years with annual cycles of flowering (in the cool/dry season), fruit set, fruit development (roughly 3-5 months from flowering to harvest depending on variety), harvest, and post-harvest recovery pruning.
5. **Ageing/renovation:** Old, tall trees can be rejuvenated by heavy pruning to restore productivity and ease of harvest. Alternate (biennial) bearing, where a heavy year is followed by a light year, is common and can be reduced by thinning fruit and balanced nutrition.

## Harvest Indicators
- Mango is harvested at the mature-green (physiologically mature but firm) stage for distant markets and export, and closer to ripe for local sale.
- Maturity signs: fullness or "shoulders" developing beside the stalk, a change in skin colour (variety dependent), development of the characteristic bloom, and flesh colour turning from white to cream/yellow near the seed.
- The specific gravity test (mature fruit tend to sink in water) and days-from-flowering records help time harvest.
- Harvest with a portion of stalk attached and avoid letting fruit fall, which bruises it and lets the milky sap stain and burn the skin (sap burn).

## Post-Harvest Handling
- Harvest during cooler parts of the day and keep fruit out of direct sun in the field.
- De-sap the fruit: cut the stalk long, invert the fruit on a rack so the sap drains away from the skin, or wash promptly, to prevent sap burn that lowers grade.
- Handle gently; mango bruises easily. Use padded, ventilated crates rather than sacks.
- Sort and grade by size, maturity, and freedom from blemish, fruit fly damage, and disease. Remove any punctured or soft fruit. See `marketplace/product_grading_and_quality.md`.
- Hot-water treatment (dipping fruit in warm water at controlled temperature) is used in commercial chains to reduce anthracnose and disinfest fruit fly larvae for some markets; follow tested protocols.
- See `harvesting/harvesting_best_practices.md` and `storage/perishable_storage.md`.

## Storage
- Mango is perishable. Mature-green fruit can be held around 10-13°C at 85-90% relative humidity for two to three weeks; ripe fruit last only a few days at ambient temperature.
- Do not store below about 10-12°C, as mango suffers chilling injury (pitting, dull skin, failure to ripen).
- Mango produces and responds to ethylene; keep it away from ethylene-sensitive produce during storage.
- Controlled ripening in a warm, ventilated room produces even colour and flavour for market.
- Where cold storage is unavailable, drying (solar or improved dryers) and processing into juice, leather, or pickle add value and reduce glut losses. See `storage/perishable_storage.md`.

## Expected Yield
- Yields rise with tree age and management. A young grafted tree may bear a few dozen fruit; a mature tree can carry several hundred to over a thousand fruit.
- Smallholder orchards with limited management: 5-10 tonnes/ha.
- Well-managed orchards with good varieties, nutrition, pruning, and pest control: 15-25 tonnes/ha or more.
- Alternate bearing, fruit fly losses, and poor pollination weather are the main reasons actual yields fall short of potential.

## Common Mistakes
- Planting seedling (non-grafted) trees, then waiting many years for low-quality, variable fruit.
- Ignoring fruit flies until fruit is already infested, leading to heavy market rejection.
- Over-irrigating or fertilising with nitrogen just before flowering, which drives leaf growth instead of flowers.
- Letting fruit fall and bruise, and failing to de-sap, causing sap burn and rapid spoilage.
- Never pruning, resulting in tall, dense, shaded trees that are hard to harvest, spray, and that carry more disease.
- Planting too densely without a pruning plan, so trees crowd, shade each other, and yields drop.

## Best Farming Practices
- Establish grafted, market-preferred varieties with a mix of early, mid, and late types to spread harvest and income.
- Prune annually after harvest to keep an open, manageable canopy that admits light and air and lowers disease pressure.
- Practise integrated pest management with sanitation, traps, and baits against fruit flies, and conserve natural enemies.
- Maintain soil health with mulch, organic matter, and balanced, split fertiliser applications.
- Use windbreaks on exposed sites and manage water carefully around the flowering-to-fruiting period.
- Invest in careful harvesting, de-sapping, grading, and cool handling to protect quality and price. See `sustainability/sustainable_farming_practices.md` and `weather/climate_smart_agriculture.md`.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: How long before a mango tree bears fruit?**
A: Grafted improved trees usually begin bearing in 3-5 years and reach full production by about year 6-10. Trees grown from seed take longer (6-8 years or more) and give variable, often lower-quality fruit.

**Q: Why does my mango tree flower heavily but set very little fruit?**
A: Common causes are cool or wet weather during flowering (disrupting pollination and promoting anthracnose or powdery mildew), water stress at fruit set, poor nutrition (especially boron and zinc), and pest damage to flowers by thrips or hoppers. Improve pollination-season conditions, nutrition, and flower protection.

**Q: What causes worms inside my mangoes?**
A: These are usually fruit fly larvae. Manage by collecting and destroying fallen and infested fruit, using baits and traps, and harvesting at the mature-green stage. See `pests/fruit_flies.md`.

**Q: Why do my mango fruits have black spots that spread after picking?**
A: This is typically anthracnose, which infects fruit in the orchard and develops during ripening and storage. Improve canopy airflow through pruning, practise sanitation, and use a protectant programme confirmed with an extension officer.

**Q: Why does my tree bear heavily one year and hardly at all the next?**
A: This is alternate (biennial) bearing, common in mango. Reduce it by thinning fruit in heavy years, maintaining balanced nutrition, controlling pests, and pruning to keep the tree vigorous.

**Q: Can I grow mango where there is no distinct dry season?**
A: It is difficult. Continuously wet, humid conditions favour vegetative growth over flowering and greatly increase anthracnose. Mango performs best where there is a cool, dry period of two to four months to trigger flowering.

**Q: Should I pick mangoes ripe or green?**
A: For distant markets and to reduce fruit fly and handling losses, harvest at the mature-green stage and ripen off the tree. For immediate local sale, you can harvest closer to ripe. Never let fruit fall to the ground.

**Q: How can I avoid losing money when everyone's mangoes ripen at once?**
A: Plant a spread of early, mid, and late varieties, harvest at mature-green to widen the selling window, and add value through drying, juice, or other processing to sell beyond the fresh-market glut. See `marketplace/buyer_preferences_and_seasonal_demand.md`.
