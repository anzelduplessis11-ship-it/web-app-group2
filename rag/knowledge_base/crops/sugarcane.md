# Sugarcane

## Overview
Sugarcane is a tall, perennial tropical grass grown across many African countries, including South Africa, Sudan, Kenya, Ethiopia, Malawi, Zambia, Eswatini, Mozambique, and Mauritius, primarily for sucrose (sugar) production and, increasingly, for ethanol and bioenergy. Unlike most field crops, sugarcane is propagated vegetatively from stem cuttings rather than true seed, and a single planting supports several successive harvests, known as ratoons, before the field needs to be replanted. The crop has a long growth cycle of 10-24 months and is highly capital- and labour-intensive, requiring careful attention to variety choice, irrigation scheduling, and prompt delivery to a mill after harvest, since cut cane deteriorates rapidly. Sugarcane is grown both by large commercial estates with associated mills and by outgrower smallholders supplying those mills, making it an important cash crop in many rural economies.

## Scientific Name
*Saccharum officinarum* L.; most commercially grown cane today consists of interspecific hybrids classified as *Saccharum* spp. hybrids.

## Suitable Climate
- Tropical and subtropical crop; optimal growing temperature: 20-32°C.
- Requires a long frost-free growing period of 10-24 months depending on variety and whether the crop is rainfed or irrigated.
- Total water requirement over the growing cycle: 1,200-2,500 mm, from rainfall, irrigation, or both.
- Benefits from a distinct drier, cooler period before harvest, which slows vegetative growth and encourages sucrose accumulation ("ripening").
- Grows from sea level up to approximately 1,600 m altitude; cooler high-altitude sites extend the ripening period but slow overall growth.
- Sensitive to frost, which damages standing cane and reduces sucrose quality.

## Suitable Soil
- Deep, well-drained, fertile soils ranging from loam to clay loam are ideal, supporting the crop's extensive root system and heavy above-ground biomass.
- Optimal pH: 5.5-7.5.
- Moderate tolerance to salinity compared with many field crops, but yield and sucrose content decline on strongly saline or sodic soils.
- Poor tolerance for prolonged waterlogging, which restricts root development and encourages root and stalk rot diseases.

## Soil Preparation
- Deep ploughing or ripping to 30-40 cm is required to break compaction and allow the extensive root system to develop, since the crop will occupy the same land for several years across ratoons.
- Sub-soil compacted layers where present, particularly on land previously used for continuous cropping or heavy machinery traffic.
- Form furrows or ridges 1.0-1.5 m apart for laying setts (planting material).
- Level the field, especially under irrigation, to ensure even water distribution and avoid waterlogged patches.
- Incorporate organic matter, filter cake, or mill by-products where available to improve soil structure ahead of planting.

## Seed or Seedling Requirements
- Propagated vegetatively from stem cuttings called "setts," each containing 2-3 healthy buds, rather than from true botanical seed.
- Seed rate: approximately 6-10 tonnes of seed cane per hectare, depending on sett size and spacing.
- Use disease-free planting material sourced from certified seed cane nurseries to avoid introducing systemic diseases such as ratoon stunting disease and sugarcane smut.
- Treat setts with hot-water treatment (around 50°C for approximately two hours) or an approved fungicide dip before planting to reduce seed-borne disease carryover.
- Lay setts end-to-end or slightly overlapping in furrows and cover with 5-10 cm of soil.

## Plant Spacing
- Row spacing: 1.0-1.5 m, depending on variety vigour, mechanisation needs, and irrigation method.
- Setts are laid continuously or with minor overlap along the furrow rather than at fixed individual plant spacings.
- Wider row spacing facilitates mechanised weeding, fertiliser application, and harvesting equipment where used.

## Water Requirements
- Total seasonal water requirement: 1,500-2,500 mm over the full growing cycle, depending on climate and variety.
- Critical water-sensitive stages: tillering and the "grand growth" phase of rapid stem elongation, when water stress most reduces final stalk length and tonnage.
- Reduce or withhold irrigation for 4-6 weeks before harvest (a practice known as "drying off") to slow vegetative growth and concentrate sucrose in the stalks.
- Excessive irrigation close to harvest lowers sucrose content and delays ripening.

## Fertiliser Recommendations
- Nitrogen demand is high due to the crop's large biomass: 100-150 kg N/ha for the plant crop, rising to 120-180 kg N/ha for ratoon crops, which draw more heavily on soil reserves.
- Phosphorus: 60-90 kg P2O5/ha applied basally at planting to support root and tiller establishment.
- Potassium: 100-150 kg K2O/ha; potassium is particularly important for stalk strength and sucrose accumulation.
- Split nitrogen applications across the early tillering and grand growth stages rather than applying all at planting.
- Recycle mill by-products such as filter cake and mill mud onto fields where available, as they supply organic matter and residual nutrients.
- See `fertilisers/fertiliser_basics.md` and `fertilisers/organic_fertilisers.md` for general application guidance.

## Weed Management
- Weed control is most critical during the first 3-4 months after planting or ratooning, before the canopy closes and shades out competition.
- Inter-row cultivation, hand weeding, and pre-emergence or post-emergence herbicides are all used depending on scale and mechanisation.
- Mulching with dried cane trash after harvest suppresses weeds in ratoon crops, conserves soil moisture, and reduces erosion.
- Two to three hand weedings are typical in smallholder systems without herbicide access.

## Pest Management
- Key pests: sugarcane stalk borers, termites, white grubs, and aphids (which also transmit viral disease).
- Scout fields regularly, particularly during early establishment and the grand growth phase, for signs of stem tunnelling or wilting shoots ("dead heart") caused by borers.
- Maintain field sanitation by removing and destroying severely infested stools to reduce carry-over populations between seasons.
- See `pests/termites.md` and `pests/aphids.md` for related management guidance.

## Disease Management
- Key diseases: sugarcane smut, mosaic virus, ratoon stunting disease, red rot, and eyespot.
- Use certified, disease-free setts and resistant varieties as the primary line of defence, since several key diseases (notably ratoon stunting disease) spread through infected planting material and are difficult to control once established.
- Rogue and destroy infected stools promptly to limit spread within a field.
- Avoid ratooning a field beyond its economic limit (typically 3-5 ratoons), since disease and yield decline accumulate with each successive ratoon cycle.
- Disinfect cutting tools between fields where smut or other systemic disease is suspected.

## Growth Stages
1. Germination and sprouting from setts (0-6 weeks after planting).
2. Tillering, during which multiple shoots develop from each planted sett (6-12 weeks).
3. Grand growth or stem elongation, the period of most rapid biomass accumulation (approximately 3-8 months).
4. Maturation and ripening, during which stalk elongation slows and sucrose accumulates in the stalk (approximately 8-12+ months, depending on variety and whether the crop is a plant crop or ratoon).

## Harvest Indicators
- Sucrose content is the primary harvest indicator and is commonly checked in the field using a hand refractometer to measure Brix (a measure of dissolved sugar content) in juice squeezed from sample stalks.
- Lower and older leaves senesce and dry as the stalk approaches maturity, and the cane rind hardens.
- Plant crops are typically harvested 12-14 months after planting; ratoon crops are usually ready in 10-12 months.
- Harvesting before flowering is preferred, since flowering diverts sugars away from the stalk and can reduce sucrose yield.
- Harvest timing is also coordinated with mill delivery schedules, since cut cane must be processed promptly.

## Post-Harvest Handling
- Cut stalks as close to the ground as possible to maximise recoverable tonnage and to promote even ratoon regrowth from the remaining stubble.
- Remove leafy trash from harvested stalks before transport, either by hand or, where practised, by pre-harvest burning (which eases manual cutting but has air-quality and biomass trade-offs compared with green, unburnt harvesting).
- Deliver cut cane to the mill as quickly as possible, ideally within 24-48 hours, since sucrose begins to invert (break down) soon after cutting, reducing recoverable sugar and juice quality.
- Handle stalks carefully during loading and transport to minimise stalk damage, which accelerates deterioration.

## Storage
- Sugarcane is not suited to long-term storage after cutting; the stalks deteriorate rapidly due to sucrose inversion and microbial contamination at the cut ends, so processing within 24-72 hours of harvest is standard practice.
- Store harvested stalks in shade where any delay before milling is unavoidable, and minimise handling and stacking damage.
- Seed cane (setts) intended for planting can be stored briefly in a cool, moist, shaded location if planting is delayed, but freshly cut setts give the best germination and are strongly preferred.

## Expected Yield
- Smallholder rainfed systems: 40-60 tonnes/ha for the plant crop.
- Well-managed irrigated commercial systems: 80-120 tonnes/ha.
- Ratoon crops typically yield 10-20% less than the preceding plant crop, with yield declining further across successive ratoons.
- Sucrose recovery (the proportion of cane weight converted to recoverable sugar) depends on variety, maturity at harvest, and processing efficiency at the mill, in addition to raw cane tonnage.

## Common Mistakes
- Planting uncertified or diseased setts, introducing systemic diseases such as ratoon stunting disease that are difficult to eradicate once established.
- Delaying delivery of cut cane to the mill, resulting in significant sucrose loss before processing.
- Ratooning a field for too many cycles without renewal, leading to declining yields and increasing disease pressure.
- Over-irrigating close to harvest, which lowers sucrose concentration in the stalks.
- Neglecting weed control during the establishment phase, when the crop is most vulnerable to competition.

## Best Farming Practices
- Source certified, disease-free planting material and use hot-water or fungicide treatment on setts before planting.
- Plan field renewal after 3-5 ratoon cycles rather than ratooning indefinitely.
- Withhold irrigation for several weeks before harvest to maximise sucrose content.
- Coordinate harvest timing closely with mill capacity and transport logistics to minimise the delay between cutting and processing.
- Apply mill by-products such as filter cake back onto fields to recycle nutrients and organic matter.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Why is sugarcane planted using stem cuttings instead of seed?**
A: Commercial sugarcane varieties are hybrids that do not breed true from botanical seed, and true seed germination is unreliable for commercial planting. Vegetative propagation from stem cuttings ("setts") with healthy buds ensures genetically uniform, true-to-type plants and faster establishment.

**Q: How many times can a sugarcane field be ratooned before replanting?**
A: Most commercial operations ratoon a field for 3-5 cycles before yields and disease pressure make replanting more economical. The exact number depends on variety, soil health, and disease incidence, particularly ratoon stunting disease.

**Q: Why does sugarcane need a dry period before harvest?**
A: Withholding water in the weeks before harvest slows vegetative growth and shifts the plant's physiology toward accumulating sucrose in the stalk rather than producing new growth, which raises the sugar content of the harvested cane.

**Q: How quickly must cut sugarcane be processed?**
A: Ideally within 24-48 hours of cutting. Once cane is cut, enzymatic and microbial activity begins converting sucrose to other sugars (inversion), reducing recoverable sugar yield and juice quality the longer the cane sits before milling.

**Q: Can sugarcane be grown without irrigation?**
A: Yes, in areas with reliable rainfall of 1,200 mm or more well distributed across the growing season. However, irrigation significantly improves and stabilises yield in drier regions and allows more precise control of the pre-harvest drying-off period that boosts sucrose content.
