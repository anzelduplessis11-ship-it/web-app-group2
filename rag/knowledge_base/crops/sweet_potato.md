# Sweet Potato

## Overview
Sweet potato is a hardy root crop widely grown across Africa as a food-security and cash crop. The starchy storage roots are eaten boiled, roasted, fried, or processed into flour, and the nutritious leaves are consumed as a vegetable in many regions. Orange-fleshed varieties are particularly valued because they are rich in provitamin A (beta-carotene). Sweet potato is drought-tolerant, adaptable to marginal soils, and produces a harvest relatively quickly, making it an important crop for households managing rainfall risk. Yields respond strongly to good vine selection, clean planting material, and timely weeding.

## Scientific Name
*Ipomoea batatas* (L.) Lam.

## Suitable Climate
- Optimal growing temperature: 20-30°C.
- Requires approximately 500-1000 mm of rainfall per season, or supplemental irrigation in drier areas.
- Very sensitive to frost; the crop cannot tolerate freezing temperatures at any stage.
- Grows from sea level up to approximately 2,000 m altitude, with maturity extending at higher, cooler elevations.
- Tolerates short dry spells better than many crops, but prolonged drought during root bulking reduces yield.

## Suitable Soil
- Well-drained sandy loam soils are ideal, as they allow roots to expand and are easy to harvest.
- Tolerates a pH range of approximately 5.5-6.5; slightly acidic soils are generally suitable.
- Heavy clay or waterlogged soils promote root rot and misshapen roots and should be avoided or raised into ridges/mounds.
- Performs reasonably on low-fertility soils, but excessive nitrogen encourages vine growth at the expense of root formation.

## Soil Preparation
- Prepare loose, friable soil to allow storage roots to develop freely.
- Form ridges or mounds approximately 25-40 cm high to improve drainage and ease harvesting, especially on heavier soils.
- Incorporate well-decomposed manure or compost 2-3 weeks before planting rather than fresh manure, which can cause forking of roots.
- On sloped land, align ridges along the contour to reduce soil erosion.
- Conduct a soil test where possible to guide fertiliser decisions; see `soil/soil_testing_and_fertility.md`.

## Planting Season by African Region
| Region | Typical Planting Window | Notes |

| West Africa | May-July (main rains) | Second crop possible in bimodal zones; valued in the "hunger gap" before cereals mature |
| East Africa | March-May (long rains); Oct-Nov (short rains) | Staggered planting spreads harvest and food supply |
| Southern Africa | Oct-Dec (start of summer rains) | Can extend into January in areas with a reliable late season |
| Central Africa | Two windows in bimodal zones (Sept-Nov and March-April) | Humid conditions allow near year-round cultivation with drainage |

## Seed or Seedling Requirements
- Sweet potato is propagated vegetatively from vine cuttings, not from true seed.
- Use healthy vine cuttings approximately 25-35 cm long, taken from the top and middle portions of vigorous, disease-free vines.
- Source cuttings from a clean nursery or a virus-free multiplication plot where possible, as diseases accumulate in continuously recycled material.
- Approximately 30,000-40,000 cuttings/ha are needed depending on spacing.
- Allow freshly cut vines to stand in a cool, shaded place for a day before planting to reduce transplanting shock.

## Plant Spacing
- Standard spacing: ridges 90-100 cm apart, with cuttings placed 25-30 cm apart along the ridge, giving approximately 33,000-44,000 plants/ha.
- Insert two to three nodes of each cutting into the soil, leaving the growing tip exposed.
- Closer spacing can improve ground cover and weed suppression; wider spacing may produce larger individual roots.

## Water Requirements
- Total seasonal water requirement: approximately 450-700 mm depending on climate and variety.
- Critical water-sensitive stages: establishment (first 2-3 weeks after planting) and the root bulking stage.
- Avoid heavy irrigation or waterlogging close to harvest, as this can cause root cracking and rot.
- The crop tolerates moderate dry spells once established, but consistent moisture during bulking improves root size and uniformity.

## Fertiliser Recommendations
- Sweet potato needs relatively modest fertiliser; excess nitrogen promotes vine growth at the expense of roots.
- Where soils are poor, a basal application of a phosphorus- and potassium-containing compound fertiliser supports root development.
- Potassium is particularly important for storage root formation and quality; ensure adequate potassium supply on deficient soils.
- Apply moderate nitrogen only if vines are visibly weak; avoid heavy late nitrogen applications.
- Well-decomposed organic manure or compost (5-10 tonnes/ha) improves soil structure and nutrient supply; see `fertilisers/organic_fertilisers.md` and `fertilisers/fertiliser_basics.md`.

## Weed Management
- First weeding: 2-3 weeks after planting, before weeds compete with young cuttings.
- Second weeding: 5-6 weeks after planting, before the vines spread and cover the ground.
- Once vines close over the ridges, they suppress most weeds naturally, reducing later weeding needs.
- Hand hoeing and careful earthing-up of ridges are the most common methods; take care not to damage developing roots.
- See `weeds/general_weed_management.md` for integrated approaches.

## Pest Management
- Key pests: sweet potato weevil (the most damaging pest), aphids, and root-feeding grubs.
- Sweet potato weevil is best managed through clean planting material, crop rotation, timely harvesting, and destroying crop residues after harvest; see `pests/aphids.md` for aphid-related guidance.
- Practice field sanitation and avoid leaving old roots and vines in the field, as they harbour weevils.
- Use integrated pest management combining clean material, cultural practices, scouting, and chemical control only when necessary, using locally registered products confirmed with an extension officer.

## Disease Management
- Key diseases: sweet potato virus disease (often a complex of viruses), Fusarium wilt, and various root and stem rots.
- Use virus-free planting material from a reliable source, as viruses are the main cause of yield decline over successive seasons.
- Practice crop rotation with non-related crops (e.g., cereals or legumes) to break disease cycles.
- Avoid waterlogged conditions that favour root rots by planting on well-drained ridges or mounds.
- For suspected severe or unfamiliar disease outbreaks, consult a qualified agricultural extension officer for diagnosis.

## Growth Stages
1. Establishment: rooting of cuttings and initial sprouting (0-3 weeks after planting).
2. Vine growth: rapid development of leaves and trailing vines (approximately 3-8 weeks).
3. Root initiation and bulking: storage roots form and enlarge (approximately 6-14 weeks, overlapping vine growth).
4. Maturity: root enlargement slows and skins firm up, typically 3-5 months after planting depending on variety and climate.

## Harvest Indicators
- Roots reach a marketable size and the skin firms so it does not scrape off easily when rubbed.
- Leaves may begin to yellow in some varieties as the crop matures, though this varies.
- Maturity is typically reached 3-5 months after planting; early varieties mature sooner in warm conditions.
- Test-dig a few plants to check root size before committing to a full harvest.

## Post-Harvest Handling
- Harvest carefully with a hand hoe or fork to avoid bruising, cutting, or breaking roots, as damage invites rot and shortens storage life.
- Harvest in dry conditions and avoid leaving lifted roots exposed to direct sun for long periods.
- Cure roots after harvest by holding them in a warm, humid, well-ventilated place for several days to heal skin wounds and improve storability.
- Sort out damaged, diseased, and pest-infested roots before storage or sale.

## Storage
- Sweet potato roots are perishable and store less well than grain; handle and store them like a perishable crop.
- Store cured roots in a cool, dry, well-ventilated place away from direct sunlight and rodents.
- In warm climates, roots keep for a limited period; local practices such as leaving mature roots in the ground until needed ("in-ground storage") are common but increase weevil risk.
- Do not store damaged or diseased roots, as rot spreads quickly in a store.
- See `storage/perishable_storage.md` for detailed guidance on handling perishable produce.

## Expected Yield
- Smallholder rainfed systems with basic management: approximately 5-10 tonnes/ha of fresh roots.
- Improved management with clean planting material and good agronomy: approximately 15-25 tonnes/ha.
- High-input or irrigated systems can achieve higher yields under favourable conditions.
- Actual yield depends heavily on variety, planting material health, soil, and moisture during bulking.

## Common Mistakes
- Recycling old, virus-infected vine cuttings season after season, causing steady yield decline.
- Applying too much nitrogen, producing lush vines but few or small roots.
- Leaving old roots and vines in the field after harvest, allowing weevil populations to build up.
- Delaying harvest excessively, increasing weevil damage and root cracking.
- Storing bruised or diseased roots together with sound roots, spreading rot.

## Best Farming Practices
- Start each season with clean, healthy planting material from a reliable source.
- Rotate sweet potato with cereals or legumes to break pest and disease cycles.
- Plant on well-drained ridges or mounds to reduce root rot and ease harvesting.
- Prioritise orange-fleshed varieties where improving dietary vitamin A is a goal, balanced with local taste and market preferences.
- Practice good field sanitation, removing crop residues promptly after harvest.
- Consider intercropping or staggered planting to spread harvest and reduce risk; see `sustainability/sustainable_farming_practices.md`.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: My sweet potato vines are very leafy but the roots are small. Why?**
A: This is commonly caused by excess nitrogen or very fertile soil, which favours vine growth over root formation. Reduce nitrogen inputs and ensure adequate potassium; shading and overly dense planting can also contribute.

**Q: How do I get good planting material without buying new vines every season?**
A: Maintain a small, well-managed multiplication plot using the healthiest, most vigorous vines, and rogue out any diseased or stunted plants. Even so, periodically refreshing material from a clean source helps prevent long-term virus build-up.

**Q: Why are my stored roots rotting quickly?**
A: Rot is often due to harvest damage, storing diseased or bruised roots, or poor ventilation. Handle roots gently, cure them after harvest, sort out damaged roots, and store in a cool, dry, airy place.

**Q: Can I eat the leaves as well as the roots?**
A: Yes, sweet potato leaves are widely eaten as a nutritious vegetable in many African regions. Harvest young leaves and tips in moderation so you do not excessively reduce the vine area needed for root production.
