# Citrus / Orange

## Overview
Sweet orange is the most widely grown citrus fruit in Africa and a valuable perennial crop for smallholders, supplying fresh fruit and juice to local, urban, and regional markets. Citrus orchards, once established, produce for two to three decades, giving farmers a durable income stream and household nutrition rich in vitamin C. Oranges are grown across a wide belt from the Mediterranean-influenced north, through the West African forest-savanna transition, the East African mid-altitudes, to the sub-tropical parts of Southern Africa. For smallholders, citrus offers steady demand, a long harvest window per variety, and good compatibility with mixed farming, but it demands patience (several years to full bearing), careful attention to grafting and clean planting material, disciplined disease control, and reliable water in the dry season. The greatest threats to African citrus are Huanglongbing (citrus greening) and its psyllid vector, along with tristeza virus, gummosis, and fruit flies. Building an orchard on certified disease-free grafted trees, an appropriate rootstock, good drainage, and an integrated pest and disease programme is essential for long-term productivity.

## Scientific Name
*Citrus sinensis* (L.) Osbeck (sweet orange, family Rutaceae). Related citrus include mandarin (*Citrus reticulata*), lemon (*Citrus limon*), lime (*Citrus aurantiifolia*), and grapefruit (*Citrus paradisi*), which share much of this management guidance.

## Suitable Climate
- Citrus grows in tropical and sub-tropical climates, from near sea level to about 1,800 m in the tropics.
- Optimal temperatures are 25-30°C during the day with cooler nights. Fruit colour and sweetness develop best where there is a cool, dry period before harvest; in warm tropical lowlands fruit may stay green even when ripe and sweet.
- Rainfall of 900-1,200 mm well distributed is adequate; supplementary irrigation is needed in the dry season and in drier zones.
- Citrus is sensitive to frost (young trees especially) and to strong, hot, dry winds that cause fruit drop and leaf desiccation.
- A distinct dry period helps trigger flowering; in some areas a controlled dry spell ("bahar" management) is used to time flowering and harvest.

## Suitable Soil
- Deep (at least 1 m), well-drained, medium-textured loam or sandy loam soils are best. Citrus roots are very sensitive to waterlogging, which causes root rot and gummosis.
- Optimal pH is 6.0-7.0. Highly alkaline soils cause iron, zinc, and manganese deficiencies; strongly acid soils reduce nutrient availability.
- Avoid saline and sodic soils; citrus is salt-sensitive, with rootstock choice affecting tolerance.
- Good internal drainage matters more than high fertility. See `soil/soil_types_africa.md`.

## Soil Preparation
- Choose a well-drained site; avoid low, water-collecting ground.
- Dig planting holes about 60 x 60 x 60 cm at the onset of the rains, separating topsoil.
- Mix topsoil with 10-20 kg well-decomposed manure or compost and a phosphorus source; refill and allow to settle before planting.
- On heavy or wet soils, plant on ridges or mounds to lift the root crown above waterlogging.
- On slopes, plant on the contour with conservation structures; see `soil/soil_conservation_and_erosion.md`.

## Planting Season by African Region
| Region | Typical Planting Window | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| West Africa | Start of the rains (Apr-Jun) | Establish before the dry season; irrigate young trees |
| East Africa | Onset of long rains (Mar-Apr) or short rains (Oct-Nov) | Mid-altitude sites give good colour and quality |
| Southern Africa | Warm wet season (Oct-Dec) | Protect young trees from frost; sub-tropical zones favoured |
| Central Africa | Onset of main rains (Mar-May or Sept-Oct) | Ensure drainage in high-rainfall areas |

## Seed or Seedling Requirements
- Always plant certified disease-free grafted (budded) trees. Seedling citrus is slow to bear, variable, and often carries disease. Grafting onto a selected rootstock controls vigour, disease tolerance, soil adaptation, and fruit quality.
- Choose the rootstock for local conditions: some rootstocks tolerate tristeza virus, some suit heavy or saline soils, others improve cold or drought tolerance. Follow local recommendations.
- Select scion varieties for the market: navel oranges for fresh dessert, Valencia and common oranges for juice and late-season supply, and blood or pigmented types where cool conditions allow.
- Buy from a certified nursery; the plant should have a clean healed graft union, straight stem, healthy roots, and no signs of greening, canker, or scab.

## Plant Spacing
- Orchard spacing depends on variety, rootstock vigour, and management.
- Standard spacing: 6 x 6 m to 7 x 7 m (about 200-280 trees/ha).
- Semi-intensive: 5 x 5 m (about 400 trees/ha) with pruning.
- High-density on dwarfing rootstock with strict management: 4 x 2 m to 5 x 3 m, giving earlier per-hectare yields.
- Orient rows for light and airflow, which improves fruit colour and reduces fungal disease. Leave access lanes for spraying and harvesting.

## Water Requirements
- Citrus needs regular water but must not be waterlogged. Establish young trees with careful, frequent watering through dry spells.
- Critical periods are flowering, fruit set, and fruit expansion. Water stress at these stages causes flower and fruit drop and small fruit; erratic watering after stress causes fruit splitting.
- A controlled dry period before flowering can be used to concentrate and time flowering.
- Reduce watering slightly before harvest to improve sugar and colour, but avoid severe stress.
- Drip or micro-sprinkler irrigation is efficient and keeps the trunk dry, reducing gummosis. See `irrigation/irrigation_methods.md` and `irrigation/irrigation_scheduling.md`.

## Fertiliser Recommendations
- Citrus is a heavy feeder, especially of nitrogen and potassium. Apply in two to four splits per year: before flowering, after fruit set, and during fruit development.
- Scale rates with tree age; a mature bearing tree needs substantially more than a young tree. Follow local guidelines and adjust with a soil or leaf test where possible.
- Potassium supports fruit size and quality; balance with nitrogen and phosphorus.
- Micronutrient deficiencies (zinc, manganese, iron, boron) are very common in citrus, showing as leaf mottling and small fruit; correct with targeted supply, sometimes as foliar sprays. See `fertilisers/nutrient_deficiencies_guide.md`.
- Apply 20-40 kg well-decomposed manure or compost per bearing tree per year and maintain mulch. See `fertilisers/fertiliser_basics.md`, `fertilisers/organic_fertilisers.md`, and `soil/soil_testing_and_fertility.md`.

## Weed Management
- Keep the tree basin weed-free during establishment to reduce competition for water and nutrients.
- Mulch under the canopy to conserve moisture and suppress weeds; keep mulch away from the trunk to prevent collar rot and gummosis.
- Slash or mow between rows and consider a managed cover crop in the inter-row to protect soil.
- Avoid deep cultivation near trees, which damages surface feeder roots. See `weeds/general_weed_management.md`.

## Pest Management
- The citrus psyllid transmits Huanglongbing (greening), the most destructive citrus disease; controlling the psyllid and removing infected trees is central. Monitor young flush closely.
- Fruit flies attack ripening fruit; use sanitation, baits, traps, and timely harvest. See `pests/fruit_flies.md`.
- Aphids curl young leaves and spread viruses; monitor flush and conserve natural enemies. See `pests/aphids.md`.
- Scale insects, mealybugs, and citrus mites damage leaves, fruit, and shoots; encourage beneficials and use horticultural oils where appropriate.
- Leaf miners tunnel young leaves, creating entry points for canker. Follow integrated pest management, scout weekly, conserve natural enemies, and use only locally registered products confirmed with a qualified agricultural extension officer, following `regulations/pesticide_safety_and_handling.md`.

## Disease Management
- Huanglongbing (citrus greening) causes blotchy leaf mottle, lopsided bitter fruit, and eventual decline; there is no cure. Manage by planting certified clean trees, controlling the psyllid vector, and promptly removing and destroying infected trees.
- Citrus tristeza virus causes decline and stem pitting; use tolerant rootstock and clean budwood.
- Gummosis and root rot (*Phytophthora*) cause bark oozing, girdling, and root decay; manage with good drainage, high budding (graft union well above soil), avoiding trunk injury and wet mulch against the trunk, and using tolerant rootstock.
- Citrus canker and citrus scab blemish leaves and fruit; use clean material, windbreaks, sanitation, and copper-based protectants where registered.
- Prevention rests on clean planting stock, drainage, sanitation, and vector control. Confirm all product choices with an extension officer.

## Growth Stages
For grafted citrus, the orchard develops through establishment to bearing over several years:
1. **Establishment (Year 1-2):** Root and canopy development. Priorities are watering, mulching, weed control, formative pruning to a strong open framework, removing rootstock suckers below the graft, and frost/wind protection.
2. **Juvenile/pre-bearing (Year 2-3):** Canopy growth; remove early flowers so the tree builds structure.
3. **Early bearing (Year 3-5):** First commercial crops; yields rise each year.
4. **Full bearing (Year 5-8 onward):** Peak, stable production for many years. The annual cycle is flowering, fruit set, fruit development over several months (typically 7-12 months from flowering to maturity depending on variety), and harvest, which can extend over a long window.
5. **Mature/renovation:** Old trees can be pruned to rejuvenate and control disease; dead and diseased wood is removed regularly.

## Harvest Indicators
- Oranges are non-climacteric: they do not ripen further after picking, so they must be harvested when internally mature.
- Maturity is judged by taste (sugar-acid balance), juice content, and the total soluble solids to acid ratio used in commercial standards, plus skin colour where cool nights allow colouring. In warm tropical zones fruit can be fully mature while still green-skinned.
- Fruit should be firm and full; clip fruit from the tree with a short stalk rather than pulling, to avoid tearing the rind (plugging), which causes rot.

## Post-Harvest Handling
- Harvest in dry, cool conditions; wet fruit spoils faster. Clip fruit carefully and avoid nail and clipper injuries to the rind.
- Handle gently; rind injuries and bruises are entry points for rots.
- Sort and grade by size, colour, and freedom from blemish, fruit fly damage, and disease. Remove injured or decaying fruit. See `marketplace/product_grading_and_quality.md` and `harvesting/harvesting_best_practices.md`.
- Washing and light curing reduce decay; degreening with controlled ethylene is used commercially to improve colour of mature green fruit for market.

## Storage
- Oranges store relatively well for citrus. Cool storage around 3-8°C (variety dependent) at 85-90% relative humidity extends shelf life to several weeks; some varieties are prone to chilling injury below a threshold.
- Good ventilation prevents moisture build-up and rot in storage.
- At ambient tropical temperatures, fruit holds for one to two weeks if handled well and kept out of the sun.
- For gluts and surplus, processing into juice, marmalade, or dried peel adds value. See `storage/perishable_storage.md`.

## Expected Yield
- Yields rise with age. A young bearing tree may carry a few dozen fruit; a mature orange tree can yield several hundred fruit (roughly 50-150 kg) per year.
- Smallholder orchards with basic management: 10-20 tonnes/ha.
- Well-managed orchards with good varieties, nutrition, water, and disease control: 25-40 tonnes/ha or more at full bearing.
- Greening, tristeza, poor nutrition, and water stress are the main causes of decline and low yield.

## Common Mistakes
- Planting seedling or uncertified trees, risking greening and tristeza and delaying bearing.
- Planting the graft union too low or piling mulch against the trunk, inviting gummosis and root rot.
- Ignoring the psyllid and keeping visibly greening-infected trees in the orchard as a reservoir of disease.
- Neglecting micronutrients, leading to chronic zinc/manganese deficiency and small, poor fruit.
- Waterlogging trees or letting them go into severe drought, causing root rot or heavy fruit drop and splitting.
- Pulling fruit off (plugging the rind) instead of clipping, causing storage rots.

## Best Farming Practices
- Plant certified disease-free grafted trees on a rootstock matched to local soil and disease pressure.
- Bud high and keep the trunk dry and mulch-free at the collar to prevent gummosis.
- Run an integrated programme against the psyllid, fruit flies, and other pests, and remove greening-infected trees promptly.
- Feed regularly with balanced nutrition including micronutrients, and manage water to avoid both stress and waterlogging.
- Prune for an open, airy canopy, practise sanitation, and harvest, grade, and cool fruit carefully. See `sustainability/sustainable_farming_practices.md` and `weather/climate_smart_agriculture.md`.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Why are the leaves on my orange tree yellow and blotchy, with small, lopsided, bitter fruit?**
A: These are classic symptoms of Huanglongbing (citrus greening), spread by the citrus psyllid. There is no cure. Plant certified clean trees, control the psyllid, and remove and destroy infected trees to protect the rest of the orchard.

**Q: My oranges are sweet and ripe but still green. Are they bad?**
A: No. In warm tropical lowlands oranges often stay green-skinned even when internally mature and sweet, because rind colour needs cool nights. Judge maturity by taste, juice content, and firmness rather than skin colour.

**Q: What is the gum oozing from my tree's trunk?**
A: This is gummosis, usually caused by *Phytophthora* and worsened by wet soil, a low graft union, or mulch and soil piled against the trunk. Improve drainage, keep the collar dry, avoid trunk injury, and use tolerant rootstock.

**Q: Why do my oranges split before harvest?**
A: Fruit splitting is linked to irregular water supply and nutrient imbalance, especially heavy watering after a dry period. Maintain even soil moisture and balanced potassium and calcium nutrition.

**Q: How long until a grafted orange tree bears fruit?**
A: Grafted trees usually begin bearing in about 3-4 years and reach full production by roughly year 5-8. Seedling trees take much longer and give variable fruit.

**Q: Why is my tree full of shoots from the base with different-looking leaves?**
A: These are rootstock suckers growing from below the graft union. Remove them promptly; if left, they take over from the fruiting variety.

**Q: Should I pick oranges early to ripen at home?**
A: No. Oranges are non-climacteric and do not sweeten after picking. Harvest only when internally mature, judged by taste and juice content.

**Q: How can I get better fruit size and colour?**
A: Ensure adequate potassium and micronutrients, maintain steady water with a mild dry period before harvest, thin excessive fruit in heavy years, and grow in sites with cool nights for better colour. Prune for light penetration.
