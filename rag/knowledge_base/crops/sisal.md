# Sisal

## Overview
Sisal is a hardy, drought-tolerant perennial fibre crop grown across the semi-arid tropics of Africa, most prominently in Tanzania (the world's leading producer), Kenya, Madagascar, Angola, and Mozambique. Unlike most crops in this knowledge base, sisal is not a food crop: it is grown for the long, strong leaf fibre extracted from its sword-shaped leaves, which is spun into twine, rope, doormats, geotextiles, dartboards, and increasingly used in composite materials and paper. Sisal is a member of the agave family and stores water in thick, fleshy leaves arranged in a rosette, allowing it to thrive on marginal land that is too dry, shallow, or infertile for most food crops. This makes it an important land-use option that does not compete directly with food security, while providing rural employment through both large estates and smallholder outgrower schemes linked to central decortication factories. Sisal is a long-lived plantation crop: it takes two to three years to establish before the first commercial leaf cut, then remains productive for seven to ten years or more, being cut in regular rounds throughout its life before it eventually flowers once and dies. Beyond fibre, the crop produces valuable by-products, including biogas potential from the large volume of wet pulp waste generated during decortication, and pulp or juice that can be composted or used in animal feed formulations, supporting broader sustainability and circular-economy value chains. See `sustainability/sustainable_farming_practices.md` for broader context on integrating perennial fibre crops into diversified farming systems.

## Scientific Name
*Agave sisalana* Perrine, family Asparagaceae (previously classified in the family Agavaceae). Commercial plantations often use selected hybrids, most notably hybrid 11648 (a cross involving *Agave sisalana*, *A. angustifolia*, and *A. amaniensis*), which is widely grown in East Africa for its higher fibre yield and improved disease tolerance compared with pure *A. sisalana*.

## Suitable Climate
- Sisal is adapted to semi-arid to sub-humid tropical and subtropical climates, with an optimal growing temperature of approximately 25-30°C.
- It performs well with 600-1,200 mm of annual rainfall but tolerates areas with as little as 500 mm, thanks to its succulent, water-storing leaves and efficient (CAM) photosynthesis.
- Sisal is highly drought-tolerant once established and can survive extended dry periods that would kill most annual crops, making it valuable in drought-prone regions.
- It is sensitive to frost and prolonged cold, and is best suited to low and mid-altitude areas, generally below approximately 1,500 m.
- Full sunlight is required for vigorous leaf growth; shading reduces fibre yield.
- Excessively wet, humid conditions with poor air movement increase the risk of fungal leaf and stem diseases, so a climate with a reasonable dry season is preferable to constant humidity.

## Suitable Soil
- Sisal tolerates a wide range of soils, including poor, shallow, and infertile soils where few other commercial crops can be grown profitably.
- Well-drained sandy loams to sandy clay loams, including the red lateritic soils common in East African sisal-growing areas, are ideal.
- Tolerates a pH range of approximately 5.5-7.5.
- The single most important soil requirement is good drainage; sisal does not tolerate waterlogging, which quickly leads to root and bole (stem base) rot.
- Because plantations remain in the ground for many years, avoid sites prone to seasonal flooding or with a shallow water table.

## Soil Preparation
- Clear bush, stumps, and existing vegetation thoroughly, since the crop will occupy the land for many years and competing woody growth is difficult to remove later.
- Deep plough or rip compacted layers to improve root penetration and drainage ahead of planting.
- Level the land and mark out planting rows accurately, as row alignment affects long-term field operations such as weeding, cutting, and transport of leaves to the decortication point.
- On sloping land, establish contour rows, terraces, or grass strips to control erosion, since the crop will be cultivated on the same land for a decade or more; see `soil/soil_conservation_and_erosion.md`.
- Incorporate well-decomposed organic matter into planting holes or furrows where soils are particularly poor, to aid early establishment.
- Ensure surface and sub-surface drainage channels are in place before planting on soils prone to waterlogging.

## Planting Season by African Region
| Region | Typical Planting Window | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| East Africa (Tanzania, Kenya - major producers) | Onset of rains, Oct-Dec or Mar-May | Plant early in the rains to establish root systems before any dry spell |
| Southern Africa (Angola, Mozambique, Madagascar) | Nov-Jan (start of summer rains) | Ensure adequate moisture during the slow initial establishment phase |
| West Africa | Limited commercial production; where grown, plant at the start of the rainy season (May-Jul) | Mainly small-scale or trial plantings; land suitability should be confirmed locally |
| Central Africa | Start of rains in bimodal zones (Sept-Nov) | Ensure good drainage given higher rainfall in parts of the region |

Because sisal is a long-lived perennial, the exact planting month is less critical than ensuring sufficient soil moisture during the first several months after field planting, when the young plant is most vulnerable to drought and weed competition.

## Seed or Seedling Requirements
- Sisal is propagated vegetatively, not from true seed, using two main types of planting material:
  - **Bulbils**: small plantlets that develop naturally on the long flowering pole ("inflorescence") a mature plant sends up once, near the end of its life.
  - **Suckers (rhizome offshoots)**: shoots that emerge from the base of an established mother plant and can be separated and replanted.
- Bulbils are the most common source of planting material on estates; they are collected and raised in a nursery bed for approximately 12-18 months until they reach a suitable size (a well-developed rosette with several leaves) before being transplanted to the field.
- Suckers can sometimes be planted with less nursery time, but supply is more limited than bulbils.
- Select planting material from healthy, disease-free mother plants of a recommended variety, such as hybrid 11648 where disease pressure (particularly zebra disease) is a concern.
- Handle and transport planting material carefully to avoid damaging the growing point, and plant promptly after lifting to reduce moisture loss.

## Plant Spacing
- Sisal is commonly planted in a double-row (paired-row) system to allow efficient movement of harvesting and transport equipment between the wide inter-row gaps.
- A typical arrangement is approximately 1 m between plants within a row, 0.6-1 m between the two rows of a pair, and 3-4 m between successive double rows, giving a population of roughly 2,500-3,700 plants per hectare.
- A simpler single-row system with spacing of approximately 1 m within the row and 3 m between rows is also used, particularly on smaller holdings.
- Consistent spacing supports uniform leaf development, eases access for cutting rounds, and allows sunlight to reach the base of the plant.

## Water Requirements
- Sisal is exceptionally drought-tolerant once established, owing to its thick, water-storing leaves and efficient use of available moisture.
- During the first 6-12 months after field planting, adequate soil moisture is important to support root establishment; supplementary watering can be valuable in very dry establishment periods, particularly on smallholder plots.
- Mature stands in semi-arid areas with 600-800 mm of annual rainfall typically survive on rainfall alone without irrigation.
- Prolonged waterlogging is far more damaging to sisal than drought stress and should be avoided at all growth stages.

## Fertiliser Recommendations
- Sisal is generally regarded as a low-input crop and is grown with minimal fertiliser on many estates and smallholdings, particularly where soils, while poor, are adequate for basic survival and fibre production.
- Where higher fibre yield and quality are targeted, nitrogen and potassium applications can meaningfully improve leaf growth rate and fibre strength; apply based on a soil test where possible.
- Incorporating well-decomposed manure or compost at planting benefits establishment on very poor soils.
- Boron and other micronutrient deficiencies have been reported on some depleted soils and can affect leaf and fibre quality; address based on a soil test or local extension guidance.
- See `soil/soil_testing_and_fertility.md` and `fertilisers/fertiliser_basics.md` for general guidance on matching nutrient supply to soil condition.

## Weed Management
- Weed control is most critical during the first one to two years after planting, when young sisal plants grow slowly and compete poorly against weeds for light, water, and nutrients.
- Hand weeding, inter-row slashing, and mechanical cultivation between the wide row gaps are the most common control methods.
- Cover crops or controlled inter-row cropping with low-growing legumes can sometimes be used during establishment to suppress weeds and add organic matter, provided they do not compete strongly with young sisal.
- Once established, the dense rosette of leaves and closed canopy along the row substantially reduce weed pressure, lowering the weeding burden in mature stands.
- See `weeds/general_weed_management.md` for general integrated weed control principles.

## Pest Management
- **Sisal weevil**: a soil-dwelling weevil whose larvae bore into the base (bole) of the plant, weakening or killing it; manage by removing and destroying severely infested plants, avoiding mechanical wounding of the bole, and maintaining field sanitation.
- **Termites**: can attack the root system and bole, particularly on poor, sandy soils; manage through field sanitation, removal of dead wood and stumps before planting, and, where necessary, locally approved soil treatments.
- **Sisal mite and mealybugs**: can infest leaf bases and reduce vigour; scout regularly and remove heavily infested leaf material.
- **Red spider mites**: occasionally cause leaf stippling in hot, dry conditions; generally a minor issue in well-managed stands.
- Integrated management relying on regular field scouting, sanitation, and removal of severely affected plants is preferred over routine chemical spraying, given the scale and perennial nature of sisal plantations; confirm any locally registered chemical options with a qualified agricultural extension officer.

## Disease Management
- **Zebra disease (leaf and stem streak, associated with *Phytophthora nicotianae*)**: causes purplish-red streaking on leaves and can progress to bole rot, particularly in wet, poorly drained conditions; it is a major reason hybrid varieties such as 11648, which show improved tolerance, are preferred over pure *Agave sisalana* in many areas.
- **Bole rot**: a rotting of the plant base associated with waterlogging and fungal or bacterial infection; prevented primarily through good site selection and drainage rather than curative treatment.
- **Leaf spot diseases** (including species of *Colletotrichum* and other fungi): cause discrete spots or lesions on leaves, generally worsened by prolonged leaf wetness and poor airflow.
- Manage diseases primarily through the use of tolerant hybrid planting material, careful site selection with good drainage, avoidance of mechanical damage to leaves and the bole during cutting, and prompt removal of severely diseased plants to limit spread.
- For unusual or severe outbreaks, consult a qualified agricultural extension officer or the relevant sisal research or industry body.

## Growth Stages
1. Nursery stage: bulbils or suckers are raised in a nursery bed for approximately 12-18 months before field planting.
2. Field establishment (approximately years 1-2): slow initial rosette development as the root system establishes; this is the period of highest vulnerability to weeds and drought stress.
3. Vegetative growth and early leaf production (years 2-3): leaf production accelerates, and the plant approaches a size suitable for the first commercial cut.
4. Mature productive phase (approximately years 3-4 to 8-10): the plant is cut in regular rounds, typically every 8-18 months, with each round removing the oldest, most mature outer leaves while younger leaves continue developing.
5. Flowering ("poling") and death: near the end of its productive life, the plant sends up a tall flowering stalk (the "pole"), which can reach several metres in height and produces bulbils; sisal is monocarpic, meaning the individual plant dies after flowering, after which the pole is harvested for bulbils and the old stand is eventually cleared and replanted.

## Harvest Indicators
- Leaves are harvested selectively and repeatedly over the life of the plant rather than in a single cut; a leaf is ready to cut once it has matured and dropped from an upright to a more horizontal position.
- The first commercial cutting round typically occurs approximately two-and-a-half to three years after field planting, once the plant has developed sufficient leaf area.
- At each cutting round, only the oldest, outermost mature leaves are removed, leaving a sufficient number of younger leaves (commonly around 30 or more, depending on variety and estate practice) on the plant to sustain continued growth and future cutting rounds.
- Cutting rounds are repeated roughly every 8-18 months throughout the plant's productive life, giving multiple harvests (commonly seven to ten or more) from a single planting before the plant eventually flowers and dies.
- Leaves should be firm, fully elongated, and free of significant disease or pest damage at the time of cutting for the best fibre quality.

## Post-Harvest Handling
- Cut leaves must be transported to the decortication point and processed as quickly as possible, ideally within 24-48 hours of cutting, because delays allow the leaf pulp to begin fermenting, which weakens and discolours the fibre.
- Decortication is the mechanical process of scraping away the fleshy leaf pulp to leave behind the long fibre strands; this is typically done at a central factory or decorticator serving an estate or group of outgrowers.
- After decortication, fibre is washed to remove residual plant sap and pulp, which also reduces the risk of fibre discolouration and weakening.
- Washed fibre is then dried, traditionally in the sun on raised racks or lines, taking care not to over-expose it, since excessive or uneven sun exposure can weaken or discolour the fibre.
- Dried fibre is brushed or combed to remove remaining trash and align the strands, then graded by length, colour, and cleanliness, as these factors determine market value; see `marketplace/product_grading_and_quality.md`.

## Storage
- Store dried, graded fibre in a dry, well-ventilated warehouse, protected from rain, humidity, and direct ground contact, as damp storage promotes mould, discolouration, and loss of fibre strength.
- Bale and compress fibre for efficient storage and transport, using clean baling materials that will not contaminate the fibre.
- Keep stored fibre away from sources of fire, as dried plant fibre is highly flammable; maintain good housekeeping and fire-prevention measures in storage areas.
- Ensure fibre moisture content is low (well-dried) before baling and storage to prevent heating, mould, and rot during storage or transport.
- Protect stored fibre from rodents and other pests that may nest in or damage baled material.

## Expected Yield
- Smallholder and lower-input estate systems: approximately 700 kg to 1.5 tonnes of dry fibre per hectare per year over the productive life of the stand.
- Well-managed estates with good variety choice (such as hybrid 11648), adequate spacing, and efficient decortication: approximately 2-2.5 tonnes of dry fibre per hectare per year.
- Because sisal is cut repeatedly over seven to ten years or more, cumulative lifetime fibre output per hectare is substantial even where annual yield appears modest compared with annual crops.
- Actual yield depends on variety, planting density, soil quality, cutting frequency and discipline, and the efficiency and timeliness of the decortication process.

## Common Mistakes
- Planting sisal on poorly drained sites, leading to bole rot and reduced stand life.
- Delaying decortication after cutting, allowing leaves to ferment and produce weak, discoloured fibre of lower market value.
- Over-harvesting leaves at each cutting round (removing too many at once), which weakens the plant, reduces future leaf production, and shortens the productive lifespan of the stand.
- Neglecting weed control during the critical first one to two years of establishment, resulting in poor early growth and delayed first harvest.
- Poor field drainage planning on sloped or flood-prone land, leading to erosion or waterlogging over the long life of the plantation.

## Best Farming Practices
- Reserve sisal for marginal, semi-arid, or poor-soil land that is not well suited to food crop production, so it contributes to rural income and land use without displacing food security priorities.
- Use disease-tolerant hybrid planting material, such as hybrid 11648, particularly where zebra disease is a known risk.
- Decorticate cut leaves as quickly as possible, ideally within a day or two, to protect fibre quality.
- Leave sufficient young leaves on the plant at every cutting round to sustain long-term productivity rather than maximising a single harvest.
- Make use of processing by-products, such as pulp waste for composting, animal feed, or biogas generation, to improve the overall sustainability and value of the enterprise; see `sustainability/sustainable_farming_practices.md`.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Can I grow sisal from seed like most other crops?**
A: No. Sisal is propagated vegetatively, using either bulbils (small plantlets that form on the flowering pole at the end of a mature plant's life) or suckers from the base of an established plant. Bulbils are raised in a nursery for about 12-18 months before being transplanted to the field.

**Q: Why does my sisal fibre come out weak and discoloured?**
A: This is almost always caused by delayed decortication after cutting. Leaves that sit too long before processing begin to ferment, which weakens and discolours the fibre. Process cut leaves within 24-48 hours and dry the extracted fibre carefully without over-exposure to sun.

**Q: How many times can I harvest leaves from one sisal plant?**
A: A single plant can be cut in regular rounds roughly every 8-18 months over a productive life of seven to ten years or more, commonly giving seven to ten or more cutting rounds before the plant eventually flowers once (poling) and dies. Leaving enough young leaves at each cut is essential to sustain this long productive life.

**Q: My sisal plants are dying at the base even though rainfall has been normal. What is happening?**
A: Sudden death or rotting at the base (bole) is often caused by zebra disease or bole rot, both worsened by poor drainage and waterlogging. Improve site drainage, avoid mechanical damage to the bole during cutting, remove severely affected plants, and consider disease-tolerant hybrid varieties for future planting.

**Q: Is sisal a good choice for land that cannot support food crops?**
A: Yes. Sisal is one of the most drought-tolerant and soil-tolerant commercial crops available, thriving on shallow, sandy, or infertile soils in semi-arid climates where most food crops would fail or yield poorly. This makes it a practical option for generating income from marginal land without competing with food production.

**Q: What happens to a sisal plant once it flowers?**
A: Sisal is monocarpic, meaning each individual plant flowers only once, sending up a tall pole that produces bulbils, and then dies. This is a natural end point of the plant's productive life; the bulbils produced on the pole can be collected and used as planting material for new stands.
