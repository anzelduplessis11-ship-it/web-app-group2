# Grapes

## Overview
Grapes are grown commercially across Africa's Mediterranean and warm-temperate climate zones, most notably in South Africa's Western Cape (one of the world's major wine and table grape export regions) and across North Africa in Morocco, Tunisia, Algeria, and Egypt, where both table and wine grapes have been cultivated for centuries. The vine is a deciduous, woody perennial trained onto a trellis or support system, requiring a distinct winter chilling period for proper dormancy and bud break, which restricts commercial production to areas with a Mediterranean or highland subtropical climate rather than the humid tropics. Grapes are valued for fresh table consumption, raisin production, juice, and wine, each requiring different cultivar choices and management approaches. The crop rewards precise canopy management, irrigation scheduling, and disease control, since humid conditions during the growing season sharply increase fungal disease pressure. Establishment costs (trellising, irrigation infrastructure, quality planting material) are relatively high compared with field crops, but a well-managed vineyard can remain productive for 25-30 years or longer. The main production risks are fungal diseases (downy mildew, powdery mildew, and botrytis bunch rot), inconsistent winter chilling in marginal areas, and bird damage to ripening bunches.

## Scientific Name
*Vitis vinifera* L. (family Vitaceae). Most African commercial cultivars are grafted onto phylloxera-resistant American *Vitis* rootstocks.

## Suitable Climate
- Grapes require a Mediterranean-type or warm-temperate climate with hot, dry summers for ripening and a cool winter period providing sufficient chilling hours for proper dormancy and bud break.
- Optimal growing-season temperatures are 20-30°C; excessive heat above 35°C during ripening can scorch fruit and reduce quality, while temperatures below 10°C slow vine growth.
- Grapes need at least some winter chilling (commonly 100-400 hours below about 7°C depending on cultivar) to break dormancy uniformly; in the highland tropics of East Africa, table grapes can be grown at higher altitude but with less predictable dormancy behaviour.
- Annual rainfall of 500-800 mm is adequate if well timed, but grapes are highly sensitive to rain and high humidity during flowering and ripening, which sharply increase fungal disease incidence and can cause berry splitting; most commercial production relies on irrigation with dry-season ripening.
- Vines are moderately frost tolerant in dormancy but young shoots and flowers are highly frost sensitive in spring.

## Suitable Soil
- Grapevines prefer deep, well-drained soils and are notably tolerant of poor, stony, or gravelly soils that would be marginal for many other crops; excessive fertility can actually reduce fruit quality by promoting excessive vegetative growth.
- Optimal soil pH is 6.0-7.5; the vine tolerates moderately alkaline soils better than strongly acidic ones.
- Good drainage is essential, as grapevines are highly susceptible to root diseases (including Phytophthora and various rots) in waterlogged soils.
- Soil depth of at least 1 m is preferred to support the deep root system that helps the vine withstand dry-season water stress. See `soil/soil_types_africa.md`.

## Soil Preparation
- Deep rip or subsoil the planting area to at least 60-80 cm before establishing a vineyard, breaking up any compacted layers to encourage deep rooting.
- Incorporate well-decomposed compost or manure and correct soil pH based on a soil test well ahead of planting, since vineyards are long-term investments and pre-plant correction is far easier than later amendment.
- Install trellis posts and wire support systems before or immediately after planting, since grapevines require permanent structural support from an early stage.
- On sloping land, establish contour rows or terraces to control erosion and facilitate machinery access where used. See `soil/soil_conservation_and_erosion.md` and `soil/soil_testing_and_fertility.md`.

## Seed or Seedling Requirements
- Commercial vineyards are established using certified, virus-tested grafted vines, combining a fruiting cultivar (scion) with a rootstock selected for phylloxera resistance, soil adaptability, and vigour control.
- Never propagate commercial vines from seed, as seedlings do not come true to type and seed propagation is used only in breeding programmes.
- Select scion cultivars matched to the intended product (table grape, raisin, or specific wine style) and to local climate, since ripening period and disease susceptibility vary widely between cultivars.
- Source planting material only from reputable, certified nurseries to avoid introducing viruses (such as leafroll virus) or phylloxera into new plantings.

## Plant Spacing
- Row spacing typically ranges from 2.5-3.5 m to allow machinery or cart access, with within-row vine spacing of 1.2-2.5 m depending on cultivar vigour, trellis system, and irrigation.
- Table grape vineyards often use wider spacing and overhead or specialised trellis systems (such as a pergola or T-trellis) to maximise light exposure for bunch quality and ease of hand harvesting.
- Wine grape vineyards commonly use a vertical shoot positioning trellis with closer in-row spacing to manage canopy density for balanced fruit ripening.
- Vine density commonly ranges from 1,600 to 4,000 vines per hectare depending on system and market.

## Water Requirements
- Grapevines are moderately drought tolerant once established but require careful irrigation scheduling for commercial yield and quality, particularly in the dry-summer climates where most African production occurs.
- Deficit irrigation (deliberately limiting water) during the ripening period is a standard practice to concentrate sugars and improve fruit and wine quality, but water stress must be carefully managed to avoid excessive stress that reduces yield.
- Critical periods for adequate water are shoot growth and berry set; water stress at these stages reduces yield potential.
- Drip irrigation is the standard method in commercial African vineyards, allowing precise control of water volume and timing. See `irrigation/irrigation_methods.md` and `irrigation/irrigation_scheduling.md`.

## Fertiliser Recommendations
- Grapevines generally require moderate rather than high fertility; excessive nitrogen promotes vigorous vegetative growth at the expense of fruit quality and increases disease susceptibility through dense canopies.
- Apply nitrogen in modest, split doses in spring at bud break and after fruit set, guided by petiole or leaf analysis rather than fixed rates.
- Potassium is important for fruit quality, sugar accumulation, and colour development in wine grapes, and should be supplied based on soil test results.
- Apply well-decomposed compost or manure periodically to maintain soil organic matter, particularly on poorer soils, but avoid over-application, which can excessively boost vigour. See `fertilisers/nutrient_deficiencies_guide.md` and `soil/soil_testing_and_fertility.md`.

## Weed Management
- Maintain a weed-free strip under the vine row, particularly during establishment, to reduce competition for water and nutrients; inter-row cover crops or natural vegetation are often managed separately, mown periodically, to control erosion and support beneficial insects.
- Mulching under young vines conserves soil moisture and suppresses weeds while the root system establishes.
- Avoid deep cultivation near vine rows, which can damage surface roots; shallow cultivation or herbicide strips are more commonly used commercially. See `weeds/general_weed_management.md`.

## Pest Management
- Mealybugs and scale insects infest bunches and canes, reducing fruit quality and vectoring viral diseases such as leafroll virus; monitor and manage through biological control and, where necessary, targeted treatment.
- Thrips can damage young shoots and flowers, affecting fruit set and causing scarring on berries. See `pests/thrips.md`.
- Birds cause significant losses to ripening bunches in many African vineyards; netting is the most reliable protection method.
- Nematodes can damage root systems, particularly in sandy soils, and are managed primarily through resistant rootstock selection.
- Follow integrated pest management: scout regularly through the growing season, conserve natural predators, and use locally registered products only where necessary, per `regulations/pesticide_safety_and_handling.md`.

## Disease Management
- Downy mildew (*Plasmopara viticola*) causes yellow oil-spot lesions on leaves and can destroy young shoots and bunches in wet, humid conditions; manage through canopy management for airflow, resistant cultivars where available, and protectant fungicide programmes timed to rainfall risk.
- Powdery mildew (*Erysiphe necator*) causes a white powdery coating on leaves, shoots, and berries, thriving in warm, dry conditions with high humidity at night; sulfur-based treatments and canopy management are standard controls.
- Botrytis bunch rot (grey mould) attacks ripening bunches in humid conditions or after rain, causing significant fruit loss; canopy management for airflow, leaf removal around bunches, and timely harvest reduce risk.
- Bacterial and viral diseases, including leafroll virus (spread by mealybugs), reduce vine vigour and fruit quality over time; use only certified virus-free planting material and manage mealybug vectors.
- Practise disciplined canopy management (shoot positioning, leaf removal, bunch thinning) to maximise airflow and light penetration, which is the single most effective non-chemical disease control strategy in grapes.

## Growth Stages
Grapevines follow both an annual growth cycle and a multi-year orchard development pattern:
1. **Establishment (Year 1-2):** Root and trunk development on the trellis system; formative pruning to establish the desired training system (cordon, cane, or other structure).
2. **Early bearing (Year 2-3):** First light crops as the vine matures onto its permanent framework; excessive early cropping should be limited to avoid stressing the young vine.
3. **Full bearing (Year 3-4 onward):** Stable annual production for 20-25 years or more, following an annual cycle of winter dormancy and pruning, spring bud break, flowering, fruit set, berry development (véraison, or colour change, marking the start of ripening), and harvest.
4. **Ageing/renovation:** Older vineyards can decline in yield and vigour over decades; renewal pruning, replanting, or trunk renewal techniques can extend productive life.

## Harvest Indicators
- Grape maturity is assessed primarily by sugar content (measured as degrees Brix with a refractometer), acidity, berry colour, and taste, since these determine suitability for the table, raisin, or wine market.
- Véraison (the onset of berry softening and colour change) marks the beginning of the ripening phase, typically 6-8 weeks before harvest depending on cultivar and climate.
- Table grapes are picked at full flavour and sugar development, since grapes do not continue to ripen after harvest (unlike climacteric fruit).
- Wine grapes are harvested at a sugar and acid balance suited to the intended wine style, which varies by cultivar and winemaking objective.

## Post-Harvest Handling
- Harvest during cooler parts of the day to reduce field heat and preserve fruit firmness and shelf life, particularly for table grapes destined for fresh market or export.
- Handle bunches gently to avoid berry drop, bruising, and stem damage, which accelerate spoilage and reduce grade.
- Pre-cool table grapes promptly after harvest to remove field heat before packing and transport.
- Sort and grade bunches by size, colour uniformity, and freedom from damage, disease, or pest marks. See `harvesting/harvesting_best_practices.md` and `marketplace/product_grading_and_quality.md`.
- Wine grapes are typically transported to the winery for processing (crushing, fermentation) within hours of harvest to preserve fruit quality.

## Storage
- Table grapes are highly perishable and should be cooled to near 0°C with high humidity (90-95%) as soon as possible after harvest to extend shelf life to several weeks under proper cold-chain conditions.
- Sulfur dioxide generator pads are commonly used in commercial export packaging to control botrytis and other storage rots in table grapes.
- Grapes are sensitive to ethylene exposure from other produce, which can accelerate stem browning and berry drop; store separately from ethylene-producing fruit.
- Raisins (dried grapes) offer a shelf-stable alternative to fresh storage and should be dried to a safe moisture content and stored in a cool, dry, pest-proof container. See `storage/perishable_storage.md`.

## Expected Yield
- Yield depends heavily on cultivar, training system, and management intensity; well-managed table grape vineyards in South Africa commonly achieve 15-25 tonnes per hectare, while premium wine grape vineyards are often managed for lower yields (8-12 tonnes/ha) to concentrate fruit quality.
- Yield builds over the first three to four years as the vine reaches full canopy size and stable annual bearing.
- Bunch and berry thinning are commonly used to balance yield against fruit quality, particularly for premium table grape and wine markets.
- Disease pressure, inadequate winter chilling, poor pollination weather, and bird damage are common reasons actual yields fall short of potential.

## Common Mistakes
- Planting uncertified or ungrafted vines, risking phylloxera damage and virus introduction into the vineyard.
- Over-fertilising with nitrogen, producing excessive vegetative growth, dense canopies, and increased disease pressure at the expense of fruit quality.
- Neglecting canopy management (shoot positioning, leaf removal), leading to poor airflow, shaded fruit, and higher disease incidence.
- Delaying harvest past optimal maturity, risking bunch rot, especially if rain occurs near ripening.
- Failing to protect ripening bunches from birds, resulting in significant losses just before harvest.
- Planting in a climate without adequate winter chilling, leading to erratic, staggered bud break and reduced yield uniformity.

## Best Farming Practices
- Use certified, virus-tested grafted planting material suited to the local climate and intended market (table, raisin, or wine).
- Design and install a suitable trellis system before planting and train vines from the first year for the desired canopy architecture.
- Practise disciplined canopy management throughout the season to maximise airflow, light penetration, and fruit quality while minimising disease pressure.
- Schedule irrigation carefully, including planned deficit irrigation during ripening where appropriate, to balance yield and quality.
- Monitor for downy mildew, powdery mildew, and botrytis proactively, especially around flowering and ripening, and apply protectant treatments before disease establishes.
- Protect ripening bunches with netting where bird pressure is high, and harvest promptly at optimal maturity. See `sustainability/sustainable_farming_practices.md`.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Can grapes be grown in tropical Africa?**
A: Commercial-quality grape production is difficult in the humid lowland tropics because grapevines need a period of winter chilling for uniform dormancy and dry conditions for good fruit quality; production is concentrated in Mediterranean-climate areas of South Africa and North Africa, with some niche production possible at higher, cooler tropical altitudes.

**Q: Why do my grapevines grow lots of leaves but produce little fruit?**
A: Excessive vegetative growth relative to fruit is usually caused by over-fertilisation with nitrogen, inadequate pruning, or insufficient light penetration into the canopy. Reduce nitrogen inputs, prune to an appropriate training system, and manage the canopy (shoot positioning, leaf removal) to redirect the vine's energy toward fruit.

**Q: What causes grey, fuzzy mould on my ripening grape bunches?**
A: This is botrytis bunch rot, favoured by humid conditions and dense canopies around ripening fruit. Improve airflow through leaf removal and canopy management, avoid overhead irrigation near harvest, and harvest promptly at maturity, especially before or after rain events.

**Q: How do I know when to harvest table grapes?**
A: Since grapes do not continue ripening after picking, harvest is timed using sugar content (measured with a refractometer), berry colour, and taste. Wait until the fruit reaches full flavour and sugar development for the target cultivar before harvesting.

**Q: Do grapevines need irrigation in Mediterranean climates?**
A: Most commercial African vineyards rely on irrigation, particularly drip irrigation, even in Mediterranean climates, because the dry summer ripening period typically does not provide enough natural rainfall for optimal yield and quality; controlled deficit irrigation during ripening is also a deliberate quality practice.

**Q: How can I protect my vineyard from bird damage?**
A: Netting placed over rows or individual bunches before ripening begins is the most reliable method. Some growers also use scare devices, but these tend to lose effectiveness over time and are best combined with netting for valuable crops.
