# Pumpkin

## Overview
Pumpkin is a hardy, drought-tolerant, sprawling cucurbit grown throughout Africa and valued as an important food-security crop because it is easy to grow, stores well, and provides food in more than one form. It is a dual-purpose crop: the tender leaves and young shoots are harvested repeatedly and eaten as a nutritious leafy vegetable, while the mature, hard-rinded fruit is stored for its flesh and its edible, protein-rich seeds. Pumpkin is usually direct-seeded in hills or planting stations at wide spacing to allow the vines to spread, and it is very commonly intercropped with maize and other staples, using space between rows that would otherwise be idle. As a related cucurbit it shares many pests, diseases, and management principles with cucumber; see `crops/cucumber.md`. Because its flowers depend on bees and other insects for pollination, protecting pollinators is central to good fruit set.

## Scientific Name
*Cucurbita* spp. (mainly *Cucurbita moschata*, *Cucurbita maxima*, and *Cucurbita pepo*)

## Suitable Climate
- Optimal growing temperature: 18-30°C; growth is best in warm, frost-free conditions.
- Pumpkin is sensitive to frost, which kills vines, so it must be grown in the frost-free season.
- More drought-tolerant than many vegetables once established, thanks to deep roots and a spreading canopy, but yields improve greatly with adequate moisture.
- Grows from lowland to mid-altitude areas; very high humidity increases foliar disease pressure.
- Requires roughly 400-600 mm of water over the growing cycle, from rainfall and/or irrigation.
- Hot, dry weather during flowering can reduce pollinator activity and fruit set; extreme heat may cause flower drop.

## Suitable Soil
- Grows on a wide range of soils but performs best on well-drained, fertile sandy loam to loam soils rich in organic matter.
- Optimal pH: 5.8-6.8, tolerating mildly acidic to near-neutral conditions.
- Avoid waterlogged soils, which cause root and fruit rots; good drainage is essential.
- Deep, fertile soils with good moisture retention support both vigorous leaf production and large fruit.
- See `soil/soil_types_africa.md` for regional soil guidance and `soil/soil_testing_and_fertility.md` for testing.

## Soil Preparation
- Prepare a well-loosened seedbed; pumpkin roots deeply, so breaking any hardpan helps drought tolerance.
- Dig planting holes or stations and work in generous quantities of well-decomposed manure or compost at each station, as pumpkin is a heavy feeder that responds strongly to organic matter. See `fertilisers/organic_fertilisers.md`.
- Where the crop is grown on mounds, ridges, or hills, these improve drainage and concentrate fertility at the planting point.
- In intercropping with maize, prepare the main crop rows normally and establish pumpkin stations in the inter-row spaces.
- On sloping land, follow contour layouts to conserve soil and water; see `soil/soil_conservation_and_erosion.md`.
- Conduct a soil test where possible to guide fertiliser decisions; see `soil/soil_testing_and_fertility.md`.

## Planting Season by African Region
| Region | Typical Planting Window | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| West Africa | Onset of the rains (Apr-Jun) | Commonly intercropped with maize, cassava, or yam; needs warm, moist establishment |
| East Africa | With the long rains (Mar-May) and short rains (Oct-Dec) | Bimodal zones allow two crops; mid-altitude areas suit vigorous vine growth |
| Southern Africa | Warm rainy season (Oct-Dec) | Must be planted after the last frost; harvest mature fruit before cold, wet weather |
| Central Africa | With the main rains in bimodal zones | High humidity raises disease risk; ensure spacing and airflow |
| North Africa | Spring planting (Feb-Apr) under irrigation | Frost-free warm season; irrigation essential in dry conditions |

## Seed or Seedling Requirements
- Pumpkin is normally **direct-seeded** into planting hills or stations in the field, as it establishes quickly and dislikes root disturbance from transplanting.
- Use good-quality seed of a variety suited to the intended use (leaf production, fresh fruit, storage fruit, or seed) and to local conditions and market preference.
- Sow 2-3 seeds per station at a depth of about 2-4 cm, then thin to the 1-2 strongest seedlings after emergence.
- Seed rate: approximately 2-4 kg/ha depending on spacing, variety, and seed size; wider-spaced, large-fruited types need less seed.
- Where an early start is wanted, seedlings can be raised in pots or sleeves and transplanted young with minimal root disturbance, but direct seeding is the norm for smallholders.
- Farmers commonly save seed from good, fully mature fruit of open-pollinated types; select from healthy, high-yielding plants and avoid saving seed from diseased fruit.

## Plant Spacing
- Pumpkin needs wide spacing because the vines sprawl extensively; typical spacing is 1.5-3 m between stations, arranged in rows or hills 1.5-3 m apart.
- Bush and semi-bush types can be spaced more closely than fully trailing, vigorous types.
- In maize intercrops, pumpkin stations are spread widely among the maize so vines run between the rows without smothering the main crop.
- Wide spacing improves airflow, reduces foliar disease, and gives each plant room for both leaf harvesting and fruit development.
- Training or directing vines into pathways helps keep fields accessible and fruit clean.

## Water Requirements
- Pumpkin is relatively drought-tolerant once established but responds strongly to adequate, even moisture, especially during flowering and fruit development.
- Critical water-sensitive stages are establishment, flowering/fruit set, and fruit enlargement; moisture stress at flowering causes flower and young-fruit drop.
- Reduce watering as fruit approaches maturity to help the rind harden and the fruit cure for storage.
- Water at the base of plants; wetting foliage encourages powdery and downy mildew and other leaf diseases.
- Mulching conserves moisture, keeps fruit clean, and moderates soil temperature; see `irrigation/irrigation_methods.md` and `irrigation/irrigation_scheduling.md`.

## Fertiliser Recommendations
- Pumpkin is a heavy feeder; base fertility on generous well-decomposed manure or compost worked into planting stations before sowing.
- Apply a basal dressing supplying phosphorus for root and early growth, plus some nitrogen.
- Topdress with nitrogen during vine growth to support leaf production, which is especially important where leaves and shoots are harvested as a vegetable; balance this against excessive vine growth at the expense of fruit.
- Ensure adequate potassium and phosphorus for good fruit set, fruit size, and seed development.
- Where leaves are harvested repeatedly, additional nitrogen supports continued shoot regrowth, but always maintain balanced nutrition.
- In the absence of a soil test, aim for moderate, balanced nutrition adjusted to soil fertility and the intended balance of leaf versus fruit production.
- See `fertilisers/fertiliser_basics.md` for methods and timing and `fertilisers/nutrient_deficiencies_guide.md` for deficiency symptoms.

## Weed Management
- Keep young plants weed-free during the first several weeks, before the vines spread and shade the ground.
- Weed by hand or with shallow cultivation near the plants to avoid damaging shallow feeder roots and vines.
- Once the vine canopy closes, pumpkin suppresses many weeds naturally, reducing later weeding needs.
- Mulching between stations suppresses weeds, conserves moisture, and keeps developing fruit clean and off wet soil.
- See `weeds/general_weed_management.md` for integrated weed control principles.

## Pest Management
- Practice integrated pest management (IPM): use tolerant varieties, rotate away from cucurbits, scout regularly, encourage natural enemies, and treat chemically only when monitoring shows it is justified.
- Key pests:
  - **Aphids** cluster on shoots and leaf undersides, suck sap, and transmit mosaic viruses; see `pests/aphids.md`.
  - **Whitefly** feeds on foliage and also spreads viruses, particularly in warm, dry conditions; see `pests/whitefly.md`.
  - **Epilachna / melon ladybird beetle** adults and larvae scrape and skeletonise leaves.
  - **Cucumber/pumpkin beetles** (including spotted and striped types) feed on leaves, flowers, and seedlings and can spread disease; seedlings are especially vulnerable.
  - **Fruit flies** lay eggs in developing fruit, causing internal rot and premature fruit drop.
  - **Red spider mites** cause stippling and bronzing of leaves in hot, dry weather.
- **Protect pollinators:** because pumpkin depends on bees and other insects for fruit set, avoid applying insecticides during flowering, and if treatment is unavoidable, apply in the late afternoon or evening when bees are least active and choose products least harmful to pollinators.
- Field sanitation, removal of crop residues, and rotation away from cucurbits (cucumber, melon, squash, watermelon) reduce pest carry-over.
- Where chemical control is needed, refer to active-ingredient CLASSES rather than brand products, rotate classes to manage resistance, and **always confirm locally registered, approved products and dosages with a qualified agricultural extension officer**.
- For sudden severe infestations, see `emergency_guides/pest_outbreak_emergency_guide.md`.

## Disease Management
- Use tolerant varieties where available, clean seed, wide spacing for airflow, base watering, and rotation away from cucurbits as core preventive measures.
- Key diseases:
  - **Powdery mildew** appears as white powdery patches on leaves and stems, common in warm conditions with high humidity but dry leaf surfaces.
  - **Downy mildew** causes angular yellow-to-brown leaf lesions in wet, humid weather and can rapidly defoliate plants.
  - **Fusarium wilt** is a soil-borne disease causing wilting and vascular browning; manage with rotation, clean seed, and good drainage.
  - **Anthracnose** produces sunken dark spots on leaves, stems, and fruit, worsened by wet conditions.
  - **Gummy stem blight** causes stem cankers and fruit rot in humid weather.
  - **Mosaic viruses** cause mottled, distorted leaves and deformed fruit and are spread by aphids and whitefly, so controlling these vectors and roguing infected plants are the main defences.
- Rogue out and destroy severely diseased or virus-infected plants, do not save seed from diseased fruit, and avoid working among plants when foliage is wet.
- Where fungicides are considered, refer to active-ingredient classes such as copper-based fungicides for downy mildew and anthracnose, and always confirm registered products and dosages with an extension officer.
- For severe or unclear disease situations, see `emergency_guides/crop_emergency_guide.md`.

## Growth Stages
1. Germination and emergence (seedlings emerge from planting stations; about 1-2 weeks).
2. Seedling and early vine establishment (first true leaves and initial vine growth).
3. Vine development and ground cover (rapid runner growth; tender leaves and shoots can begin to be harvested).
4. Flowering (separate male and female flowers are produced on the same plant — a monoecious habit — and require insect pollination for fruit set).
5. Fruit set and fruit enlargement (pollinated female flowers develop into fruit that swell over several weeks).
6. Fruit maturation and rind hardening (fruit colour deepens, the rind hardens, the stem corks off, and the crop is ready for mature harvest).

## Harvest Indicators
- **Leaves and shoots:** harvest young, tender leaves and shoot tips repeatedly once vines are growing vigorously, taking a portion at a time so plants continue to produce; regular harvesting encourages new shoots.
- **Immature fruit:** small, tender fruit can be picked young and used like courgette, with soft skin and a glossy surface.
- **Mature fruit for storage:** harvest when the rind is hard and cannot easily be dented with a thumbnail, the fruit has developed its full colour, the stem has begun to dry and cork, and the vine near the fruit is drying back.
- Cut mature fruit with a portion of stalk attached using a sharp knife; do not tear it off, as a broken stem invites rot.
- Harvest mature fruit in dry weather and before frost or cold, wet conditions arrive.
- Time from planting to first leaf harvest can be a few weeks, while mature fruit is typically ready about 3-5 months after planting depending on variety.
- See `harvesting/harvesting_best_practices.md` for general harvesting guidance.

## Post-Harvest Handling
- Handle mature fruit gently to avoid cuts and bruises, which become entry points for storage rots; leave the stalk attached.
- **Curing:** cure mature, hard-rinded fruit in a warm, dry, airy place for about 1-2 weeks to further harden the rind and heal minor surface wounds, greatly improving storage life.
- Harvested leaves and shoots are highly perishable and should be kept cool, shaded, and moist and moved to market or eaten quickly.
- Sort fruit by size and quality, and separate any bruised, cut, or diseased fruit for immediate use rather than storage.
- Grade fruit for market presentation; clean, unblemished, well-shaped fruit attracts better prices. See `marketplace/product_grading_and_quality.md`.
- For seed production, scoop seeds from fully mature fruit, wash, and dry thoroughly in shade before storing in a cool, dry place.

## Storage
- Well-cured, mature, sound pumpkin fruit stores for several months (often 3-6 months or more) in a cool, dry, well-ventilated place, making it an excellent food-security reserve.
- Keep fruit off the ground on shelves, racks, or dry bedding, with space between fruit for airflow; avoid stacking heavily, which causes bruising and rot.
- Avoid damp, poorly ventilated stores, which promote rot, and avoid very cold storage, which can cause chilling injury.
- Inspect stored fruit regularly and remove any that develop soft spots or rot.
- Leaves and immature fruit do not store and must be consumed or sold quickly; see `storage/perishable_storage.md` for handling perishable produce.
- Good storage lets farmers hold fruit for higher off-season prices; see `marketplace/buyer_preferences_and_seasonal_demand.md`.

## Expected Yield
- Yields depend on whether the crop is grown mainly for leaves, fruit, or both, and on spacing and management.
- Smallholder fruit yields with basic management: approximately 8-15 tonnes/ha of mature fruit.
- Improved management with adequate organic matter, fertiliser, and moisture: approximately 15-30 tonnes/ha.
- High-input systems with good varieties and full management: 30-40+ tonnes/ha of fruit.
- In addition, repeated leaf and shoot harvests provide a valuable, continuous vegetable yield throughout the vine's active growth, and mature fruit also yields edible seeds.

## Common Mistakes
- Spacing plants too closely, causing crowded vines, poor airflow, more foliar disease, and reduced fruit size.
- Spraying insecticides during flowering, killing bees and other pollinators and causing poor fruit set.
- Overhead watering and working among wet vines, spreading powdery mildew, downy mildew, and anthracnose.
- Over-harvesting leaves and shoots to the point that the plant cannot support fruit development when both are wanted.
- Harvesting fruit for storage before the rind has hardened, leading to rapid rot in store.
- Tearing fruit off the vine and breaking the stem, creating an entry point for rot.
- Saving seed from diseased or immature fruit, carrying over disease and giving weak, variable plants.

## Best Farming Practices
- Direct-seed into well-manured stations at wide spacing suited to the variety's vine vigour, and thin to the strongest seedlings.
- Use pumpkin's spreading habit productively by intercropping with maize or other tall crops, letting vines cover the inter-row space and suppress weeds.
- Protect pollinators by avoiding insecticide use at flowering; encourage bees for strong fruit set.
- Maintain even moisture through flowering and fruit set, then reduce watering to help fruit cure.
- Harvest tender leaves and shoots regularly for a continuous vegetable supply while leaving enough foliage to support fruit.
- Harvest mature fruit only when the rind is hard, cure it, and store sound fruit in a cool, dry, airy place.
- Rotate away from cucurbits and practice field sanitation to reduce pests and diseases; confirm any chemical controls with a qualified extension officer.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Can I really eat both the leaves and the fruit from the same pumpkin plant?**
A: Yes. Pumpkin is a dual-purpose, food-security crop. You can harvest tender young leaves and shoot tips repeatedly as a leafy vegetable while the plant grows, and still allow fruit to develop for its flesh and seeds. When you want both, harvest leaves moderately so enough foliage remains to support the fruit.

**Q: My plants flower a lot but set very few fruit. What is wrong?**
A: Pumpkin has separate male and female flowers on the same plant and depends on bees and other insects to move pollen between them. Poor fruit set is often caused by too few pollinators, frequently because insecticides were sprayed during flowering, or by hot, dry stress at flowering. Avoid spraying at flowering, protect bees, and keep moisture even; the first flush of flowers is usually male, so some early flower drop is normal.

**Q: How far apart should I plant pumpkins?**
A: Pumpkin vines sprawl widely, so space stations about 1.5-3 m apart depending on how vigorous the variety is. Wide spacing gives room for the vines, improves airflow to reduce disease, and allows both leaf harvesting and good fruit development. Bush types can be spaced more closely than fully trailing types.

**Q: Can I grow pumpkin together with my maize?**
A: Yes, this is a very common and effective intercrop. Establish pumpkin stations spread widely among the maize so the vines run through the inter-row spaces. The vines cover the ground, suppress weeds, and conserve moisture, while the maize provides the main grain crop.

**Q: How do I know when a pumpkin is mature enough to store?**
A: A mature pumpkin has a hard rind that you cannot easily dent with a thumbnail, full skin colour for its variety, and a stem that has begun to dry and cork, with the vine near the fruit drying back. Cut it with a piece of stalk attached, cure it in a warm dry place for a week or two, and it will store for months.

**Q: Why did my stored pumpkins rot after a short time?**
A: Common causes are harvesting before the rind hardened, breaking or tearing off the stalk, bruising fruit during handling, skipping curing, or storing in a damp, poorly ventilated place. Harvest only fully mature fruit with the stalk intact, cure it, sort out any damaged fruit, and store on racks with airflow in a cool, dry place.

**Q: Is pumpkin a good choice if my rainfall is unreliable?**
A: Yes. Pumpkin is relatively drought-tolerant once established, thanks to deep roots and a spreading canopy, which makes it a valuable food-security crop. It still yields best with adequate moisture, especially at flowering and fruit set, so provide water at those stages if you can, and use mulch to conserve soil moisture.

**Q: Can I save my own pumpkin seed for next season?**
A: You can save seed from open-pollinated (not hybrid) types by scooping seed from healthy, fully mature fruit of good, high-yielding plants, washing it, and drying it thoroughly in the shade before storing it cool and dry. Do not save seed from diseased fruit, as some diseases are carried on seed. Note that pumpkins cross-pollinate readily, so saved seed may vary if different types were grown nearby.
