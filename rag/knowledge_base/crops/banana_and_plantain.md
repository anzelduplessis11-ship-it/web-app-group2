# Banana and Plantain

## Overview
Banana and plantain are important perennial food and cash crops across humid and sub-humid Africa. Bananas are usually eaten fresh as a sweet dessert fruit, while plantains and cooking bananas are starchy staples that are boiled, roasted, fried, or pounded. Both belong to the same genus and are managed similarly, though plantains generally prefer hotter, more humid lowland conditions. As perennial crops grown from a "mat" of related plants, they produce continuously over several years, providing farmers with a steady source of food and income between seasonal crops.

## Scientific Name
*Musa* spp. (dessert bananas are largely *Musa acuminata* (AAA group); plantains and cooking types are largely *Musa* × *paradisiaca* / AAB and ABB groups).

## Suitable Climate
- Optimal growing temperature: 25-30°C; growth slows below 16°C and is damaged by frost.
- Requires 1,200-2,500 mm of well-distributed rainfall per year, or supplemental irrigation in drier zones.
- Prefers high humidity and sheltered sites; strong winds tear leaves and can topple fruiting plants.
- Plantains are best suited to hot, humid lowlands; some dessert and cooking bananas tolerate cooler mid-altitude highlands up to approximately 1,800 m.
- Prolonged dry spells reduce bunch size and delay fruiting.

## Suitable Soil
- Deep, well-drained, fertile loam soils rich in organic matter are ideal.
- Tolerates a pH range of 5.5-7.5; optimal pH is approximately 6.0-7.0.
- Very sensitive to waterlogging; standing water encourages root rot and toppling.
- High demand for potassium and nitrogen means soils should be fertile or regularly amended with organic matter.

## Soil Preparation
- Clear the land and dig planting holes approximately 40-60 cm wide and deep, keeping topsoil separate.
- Mix topsoil with well-decomposed manure or compost (5-15 kg per hole) and refill before planting.
- On sloped land, plant along contours and establish cover to reduce soil erosion.
- Improve drainage on heavy soils by mounding or forming raised beds to prevent waterlogging.
- Conduct a soil test where possible to guide lime and fertiliser decisions; see `soil/soil_testing_and_fertility.md`.

## Planting Season by African Region
| Region | Typical Planting Window | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| West Africa | Start of the rains (Apr-Jun) | Plantain is a major lowland staple; establish before peak rains for good root growth |
| East Africa (highlands) | Onset of long rains (Mar-Apr) or short rains (Oct-Nov) | Cooking bananas widely grown; align with bimodal rains |
| Southern Africa | Start of warm rains (Oct-Dec) | Frost-free sites only; irrigate in dry winter months |
| Central Africa | Early rains in bimodal zones (Mar-Apr and Sept-Oct) | Humid conditions favour year-round establishment with adequate moisture |
| North Africa | Under irrigation in frost-free coastal/oasis areas (spring) | Rainfed production is limited by aridity |

## Seed or Seedling Requirements
- Banana and plantain do not produce true seed for planting; they are propagated vegetatively.
- **Suckers:** Use healthy sword suckers (narrow, pointed leaves) from vigorous, disease-free mother plants rather than broad-leaved water suckers. Trim roots and pare off outer tissue to reduce pest and disease carry-over.
- **Tissue-culture plantlets:** Clean, laboratory-raised plantlets are the preferred planting material where available, as they are free of nematodes, weevils, and many diseases and give more uniform stands. Harden plantlets in a nursery before field planting.
- **Corms/corm pieces:** Sections of healthy corm with at least one bud can also be used.
- Select planting material only from mats with no symptoms of banana bunchy top, banana Xanthomonas wilt, or Panama disease.
- Where pest or disease pressure is high, tissue-culture plantlets or hot-water-treated suckers are strongly recommended.

## Plant Spacing
- Standard spacing: approximately 3 m × 3 m, giving roughly 1,100 plants/ha, for tall vigorous varieties.
- Closer spacing (2 m × 2 m to 2.5 m × 2.5 m) may be used for shorter varieties or higher-density plantings, roughly 1,600-2,500 plants/ha.
- Wider spacing improves airflow, reduces disease, and eases movement for management and harvesting.
- Manage each mat by keeping a limited number of followers (e.g., one fruiting plant, one large sucker, one small sucker) to avoid overcrowding.

## Water Requirements
- Requires consistent soil moisture throughout the year; banana has shallow roots and is sensitive to drought.
- Critical periods for water stress are during flowering (bunch emergence) and bunch filling.
- In areas with a distinct dry season, supplemental irrigation is important to maintain bunch size and continuous production; see `irrigation/irrigation_methods.md`.
- Mulching heavily around mats conserves moisture, moderates soil temperature, and suppresses weeds.

## Fertiliser Recommendations
- Banana and plantain are heavy feeders, particularly of potassium and nitrogen.
- Apply well-decomposed manure or compost regularly (e.g., several kilograms per mat, one to two times per year) to build soil fertility and structure.
- Supplement with mineral fertiliser supplying nitrogen and potassium in split applications through the year, guided by local soil test recommendations.
- General guideline in the absence of a soil test: prioritise potassium and nitrogen, with moderate phosphorus, split into several applications rather than a single dose.
- Apply fertiliser in a ring around each mat, not against the pseudostem, and cover lightly or mulch over it.
- See `fertilisers/fertiliser_basics.md` and `fertilisers/organic_fertilisers.md` for application methods and timing principles.

## Weed Management
- Keep young plantations weed-free during establishment, as weeds compete strongly with shallow banana roots.
- Mulching heavily with crop residues or dry grass is the most effective and sustainable weed control, and also conserves moisture.
- Hand weeding or shallow slashing is preferred over deep hoeing, which damages surface roots.
- Once the canopy closes, shading suppresses most weeds.
- See `weeds/general_weed_management.md` for integrated weed control principles.

## Pest Management
- Key pests: banana weevil (borer), plant-parasitic nematodes, and aphids (which transmit banana bunchy top virus).
- Use clean planting material (tissue-culture plantlets or pared/hot-water-treated suckers) to avoid introducing weevils and nematodes.
- Practice field sanitation: split and spread harvested pseudostems to deny weevils breeding sites, and remove crop debris.
- Trapping (e.g., split-pseudostem traps) helps monitor and reduce weevil numbers.
- Where chemical control is needed, use only locally registered products and confirm active-ingredient choices with an extension officer.
- See `pests/aphids.md` for guidance on aphids as virus vectors, and `emergency_guides/pest_outbreak_emergency_guide.md` for outbreak situations.

## Disease Management
- Key diseases: banana Xanthomonas wilt (BXW), Panama disease (fusarium wilt), black and yellow Sigatoka (leaf spot), and banana bunchy top virus.
- Use disease-free planting material and resistant/tolerant varieties where available.
- For BXW, remove and destroy infected plants, disinfect cutting tools between plants, and remove male buds to limit insect transmission.
- Improve airflow through proper spacing and remove old, diseased leaves to reduce Sigatoka pressure.
- Suspected wilt diseases can spread rapidly and destroy whole plantations; consult a qualified extension officer promptly if you suspect BXW or Panama disease. See `emergency_guides/crop_emergency_guide.md`.

## Growth Stages
1. Establishment: rooting and early leaf production after planting (0-3 months).
2. Vegetative growth: rapid leaf and pseudostem development (3-6+ months).
3. Flowering: emergence of the inflorescence (bunch) from the top of the pseudostem, typically 6-12 months after planting depending on variety and climate.
4. Bunch filling and maturity: fingers develop and fill over approximately 3-4 months from flowering to harvest.

## Harvest Indicators
- Fingers become plump and rounded, losing their sharp angular ridges (fingers "fill out").
- Fruit colour begins to lighten from dark green toward pale green as maturity approaches.
- For distant markets, harvest at the mature-green ("three-quarter full") stage so fruit ripens in transit; for local sale, harvest closer to full maturity.
- Time from flowering to harvest is typically 3-4 months, varying with temperature and altitude.
- Cut the bunch carefully, avoiding damage; the plant is then cut down after harvest as each pseudostem fruits only once.

## Post-Harvest Handling
- Handle bunches gently to avoid bruising, which turns dark and lowers market value.
- Cut bunches into hands or clusters in a shaded area, not in direct sun.
- Wash off latex stains and allow cut surfaces to dry before packing.
- Pack in ventilated crates or lined containers with padding (e.g., dry banana leaves) to cushion the fruit during transport.
- Keep fruit out of direct sunlight and heat, which accelerate ripening and spoilage.

## Storage
- Banana and plantain are highly perishable and cannot be stored for long at ambient tropical temperatures.
- Mature-green fruit can be held for a longer period in cool, well-ventilated conditions; avoid temperatures below approximately 13°C, which cause chilling injury and dull, dark skins.
- Bananas release ethylene, which speeds ripening; keep them away from other ethylene-sensitive produce during storage.
- For longer shelf life, plantains and cooking bananas can be processed into flour, chips, or dried products.
- See `storage/perishable_storage.md` for full guidance on handling perishable produce.

## Expected Yield
- Smallholder plantings with basic management: approximately 10-20 tonnes/ha per year.
- Improved management with clean planting material, adequate fertiliser, mulching, and irrigation: approximately 30-50 tonnes/ha per year.
- High-input commercial plantations: 50-60+ tonnes/ha per year.
- Actual yield depends heavily on variety, soil fertility, water availability, and pest/disease pressure; well-managed mats also remain productive for more years.

## Common Mistakes
- Planting suckers taken from diseased or weevil-infested mats, spreading pests and diseases to new fields.
- Allowing too many suckers per mat, causing overcrowding, small bunches, and toppling.
- Neglecting mulching and letting the shallow root zone dry out or overheat.
- Under-fertilising, especially with potassium, leading to small bunches and weak plants.
- Failing to remove and destroy plants affected by wilt diseases, allowing rapid spread through the plantation.

## Best Farming Practices
- Use clean planting material, ideally tissue-culture plantlets or well-treated sword suckers from healthy mats.
- Mulch heavily and apply organic manure regularly to build soil fertility and conserve moisture.
- Manage each mat to a limited number of followers to maintain bunch size and continuous production.
- Disinfect cutting tools between plants to prevent spreading bacterial and fungal diseases.
- Remove old leaves and post-harvest pseudostems to improve airflow and deny pests breeding sites.
- Establish windbreaks in exposed areas to protect plants from wind damage.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: My banana plants look healthy but the bunches are very small. What is wrong?**
A: Small bunches are commonly caused by overcrowded mats (too many suckers competing), low soil fertility (especially potassium), or moisture stress. Reduce the number of followers per mat, apply organic manure and a potassium-containing fertiliser, and mulch to conserve moisture.

**Q: Can I plant suckers from any banana plant in my garden?**
A: It is safest to take suckers only from vigorous, disease-free mats and to use sword suckers rather than broad-leaved water suckers. Suckers from diseased plants can carry weevils, nematodes, and diseases such as bunchy top or wilts into your new field. Tissue-culture plantlets are the cleanest option where available.

**Q: One of my banana plants is wilting and the cut stem shows dark staining inside. What should I do?**
A: Internal staining with wilting can indicate a serious wilt disease such as Panama disease or banana Xanthomonas wilt, which can spread through a plantation. Avoid moving planting material from the affected area, disinfect any tools used, and consult a qualified extension officer promptly for diagnosis and guidance.

**Q: How long after planting will I get my first harvest?**
A: This varies with variety, altitude, and climate, but the first bunch is typically ready roughly 9-15 months after planting, with flowering around 6-12 months and a further 3-4 months to fill the bunch. Warmer lowland conditions generally give faster maturity than cool highlands.
