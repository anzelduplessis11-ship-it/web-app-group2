# Pearl Millet

## Overview
Pearl millet is one of the most drought-tolerant cereals grown in Africa and is a vital staple in the hottest, driest farming regions, particularly the Sahel and other semi-arid zones. The grain is used for porridge, flour, and local foods, while the stalks and residues provide fodder and fuel. Pearl millet thrives on poor, sandy soils and under low rainfall where most other cereals fail, making it a cornerstone food-security crop for many smallholder households. It is well adapted to heat and drought but, like sorghum, requires attention to Striga and bird damage.

## Scientific Name
*Cenchrus americanus* (L.) Morrone (also widely known as *Pennisetum glaucum* (L.) R.Br.)

## Suitable Climate
- Optimal growing temperature: 25-35°C; highly tolerant of heat.
- Grows with as little as approximately 250-600 mm of rainfall per season, making it suited to very dry areas.
- Sensitive to frost; grown in the warm season.
- Adapted mainly to low and mid-altitude semi-arid zones.
- Among the most drought- and heat-tolerant cereals, able to produce a harvest where maize and even sorghum may fail.

## Suitable Soil
- Grows well on light, sandy soils that are often too poor or droughty for other cereals.
- Tolerates a pH range of approximately 5.0-7.0 and copes with mildly acidic, low-fertility soils.
- Requires good drainage; does not tolerate waterlogging.
- Tolerant of low fertility but still responds to modest improvements in soil nutrients and organic matter.

## Soil Preparation
- Prepare a firm, reasonably fine seedbed suitable for the very small millet seed.
- Because the seed is tiny, avoid a rough, cloddy surface, which causes poor and uneven emergence.
- Incorporate available organic matter to improve moisture retention on sandy soils, which are prone to drying out.
- On sloped or wind-prone land, use moisture-conservation and erosion-control measures such as contour ridging.
- Where possible, conduct a soil test to guide modest fertiliser use; see `soil/soil_testing_and_fertility.md`.

## Planting Season by African Region
| Region | Typical Planting Window | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| West Africa (Sahel) | June-July (onset of short rains) | A dominant staple of the Sahel; local varieties are timed closely to the rains |
| East Africa | March-April (long rains); Oct-Nov (short rains) | Grown in the driest lowland zones |
| Southern Africa | Nov-Dec (start of summer rains) | Favoured in the most drought-prone districts |
| Central Africa | Limited; drier margins only | High-rainfall humid zones are less suitable |
| North Africa | Under irrigation or wetter margins: April-June | Rainfed cultivation is confined to semi-arid fringes |

## Seed or Seedling Requirements
- Use certified or good-quality seed of a variety adapted to local rainfall, season length, and Striga pressure.
- Seed rate: approximately 3-6 kg/ha; the small seed goes a long way, so avoid over-sowing.
- Test germination before planting if seed is farm-saved or older than one season.
- Sow shallowly (about 2-3 cm deep) into moist soil, as deep planting greatly reduces emergence of the tiny seed.
- A seed dressing against soil pests or seed-borne disease may help where these are a known risk; confirm locally registered products with an extension officer.

## Plant Spacing
- Common spacing: approximately 60-75 cm between rows, with plants or hills thinned to about 15-30 cm within the row.
- Because millet is often planted in hills and then thinned, remove surplus seedlings early to leave a vigorous stand.
- Wider spacing suits the driest areas to reduce competition for scarce water; slightly closer spacing suits areas with more reliable rainfall.

## Water Requirements
- Total seasonal water requirement: approximately 300-500 mm depending on climate and variety, lower than most cereals.
- Critical water-sensitive stages: establishment, heading (panicle emergence), and grain filling.
- Pearl millet is exceptionally drought-tolerant and can still yield under dry conditions, but stress at heading and grain filling reduces yield.
- Moisture-conservation practices help stabilise yields in very dry areas; see `irrigation/irrigation_methods.md`.

## Fertiliser Recommendations
- Pearl millet is grown largely on poor soils and needs modest fertiliser matched to low, uncertain rainfall.
- A basal application of phosphorus supports early root growth and establishment on deficient soils.
- A light nitrogen topdress once the crop is established and growing can improve yield where moisture allows; avoid heavy nitrogen in very dry seasons.
- Organic manure or compost improves moisture retention and fertility on sandy soils and is valuable where available; see `fertilisers/fertiliser_basics.md` and `fertilisers/organic_fertilisers.md`.
- Match all inputs to realistic yield expectations for the rainfall available.

## Weed Management
- First weeding: 2-3 weeks after emergence, when young millet competes very poorly with weeds.
- Second weeding: around 5-6 weeks after emergence, before the crop closes in.
- Thin and weed together at the first weeding to establish a clean, evenly spaced stand.
- Striga (witchweed) affects pearl millet in many regions and is managed through tolerant varieties, rotation, and improved fertility; see `weeds/striga.md`.
- Hand hoeing is the most common method for smallholders; see `weeds/general_weed_management.md` for integrated principles.

## Pest Management
- Key pests: birds (a major cause of grain loss), stem borers, millet head miner, and storage pests.
- Bird damage to maturing heads can be severe; bird-scaring and coordinated community planting help spread the risk.
- Scout during vegetative growth for stem borers and other pests; see `pests/fall_armyworm.md` for a related caterpillar pest and general scouting principles.
- Use integrated pest management combining cultural practices, scouting, and chemical control only when necessary, using locally registered products confirmed with an extension officer.

## Disease Management
- Key diseases: downy mildew (a major disease of pearl millet), smut, ergot, and rust.
- Use resistant or tolerant varieties where available and healthy, clean seed, as downy mildew can be seed- and soil-borne.
- Rogue out (remove) plants showing downy mildew symptoms to reduce spread.
- Practice crop rotation with non-cereal crops such as legumes to break disease cycles.
- For suspected severe or unfamiliar disease outbreaks, consult a qualified agricultural extension officer.

## Growth Stages
1. Germination and emergence (0-7 days after planting; often rapid in warm soils).
2. Vegetative growth: leaf development and tillering (approximately 7-40 days).
3. Heading and flowering: the cylindrical panicle (head) emerges and flowers (approximately 35-60 days).
4. Grain filling and maturity: grain fills on the head, reaching maturity typically 75-120 days after planting depending on variety.

## Harvest Indicators
- Grain becomes hard and reaches its full variety colour, and the heads dry down.
- The crop turns from green to a mature straw colour as it ripens.
- Because pearl millet often matures quickly, monitor heads closely and harvest promptly once mature.
- Timely harvesting is important to reduce losses to birds and to prevent grain shattering or weathering.

## Post-Harvest Handling
- Cut and gather the heads and dry them thoroughly before threshing.
- Dry grain to a safe moisture level (comparable to other cereals, approximately 12-13%) before storage to prevent mould and insect damage.
- Thresh only after adequate drying to minimise grain damage.
- Sort out mouldy, discoloured, or insect-damaged grain before storage.

## Storage
- Store dried grain in a cool, dry, well-ventilated place protected from rain, rodents, and insects.
- Use hermetic storage bags or metal silos to protect against storage pests without chemical treatment where possible.
- If using chemical grain protectants, follow label instructions and use only locally approved products.
- Monitor stored grain regularly for moisture, insects, and mould.
- See `storage/grain_storage.md` for full storage guidance.

## Expected Yield
- Smallholder rainfed systems with basic management: approximately 0.4-1.2 tonnes/ha.
- Improved management with good seed, modest fertiliser, and pest/bird control: approximately 1.5-3 tonnes/ha.
- Higher yields are achievable under favourable conditions, though pearl millet is often grown where conditions are marginal.
- Actual yield depends heavily on rainfall, variety, Striga and bird pressure, and soil fertility.

## Common Mistakes
- Planting the very small seed too deep, leading to poor emergence.
- Failing to thin hills early, causing overcrowding and weak stands.
- Ignoring Striga and downy mildew, allowing them to build up over seasons.
- Underestimating bird damage to maturing heads.
- Storing grain before it is properly dried, leading to mould and losses.

## Best Farming Practices
- Choose pearl millet for the driest, sandiest fields where other cereals struggle, using well-adapted local or improved varieties.
- Rotate with legumes (such as cowpea or groundnut) to improve fertility and help manage Striga and disease.
- Thin and weed early to establish a vigorous, evenly spaced stand.
- Manage Striga and downy mildew through tolerant varieties, rotation, roguing, and improved fertility.
- Harvest and dry promptly to protect grain quality and reduce bird losses; see `sustainability/sustainable_farming_practices.md`.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: My soil is very sandy and rainfall is low. Is pearl millet a good choice?**
A: Yes. Pearl millet is among the most drought- and heat-tolerant cereals and is specifically well suited to poor, sandy soils and low rainfall where maize and even sorghum may fail. Local variety choice and timely planting with the rains remain important.

**Q: Why did my pearl millet emerge poorly and unevenly?**
A: The most common cause is planting the very small seed too deep or into a rough, cloddy, or dry seedbed. Sow shallowly into a firm, moist, reasonably fine seedbed, and thin early to even out the stand.

**Q: What is the whitish, distorted growth I see on some millet plants?**
A: Distorted, discoloured growth on pearl millet often indicates downy mildew, a major disease of the crop. Remove affected plants, use tolerant varieties and clean seed, rotate with non-cereals, and consult an extension officer if it is widespread.

**Q: How can I protect my millet grain from birds?**
A: Bird-scaring during grain filling, choosing varieties with some tolerance where available, and coordinating planting with neighbours so fields mature together all help reduce concentrated bird damage on individual plots.
