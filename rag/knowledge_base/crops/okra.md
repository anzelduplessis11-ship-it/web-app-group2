# Okra

## Overview
Okra (also called lady's finger or, in parts of West Africa, gombo) is a warm-season vegetable grown widely across Africa for its tender immature pods, which are used fresh, dried, or in soups and stews. It is well adapted to hot conditions and relatively tolerant of heat and short dry spells, making it a dependable crop for smallholders in warm lowland areas. Okra is quick to start bearing and is harvested repeatedly over several weeks, providing a steady supply of pods and income once it comes into production.

## Scientific Name
*Abelmoschus esculentus* (L.) Moench.

## Suitable Climate
- Optimal growing temperature: 25-35°C; okra is a warm-season crop and is very sensitive to cold.
- Requires 400-600 mm of well-distributed water over the growing cycle, through rainfall and/or irrigation.
- Growth and germination are poor below approximately 18°C; frost kills the plant.
- Well suited to hot lowland and savanna areas; tolerates heat better than many vegetables.
- Prolonged drought reduces pod set and quality, though okra tolerates short dry spells better than most vegetables.

## Suitable Soil
- Well-drained, fertile loam or sandy loam soils rich in organic matter are ideal.
- Tolerates a pH range of 6.0-7.5; optimal pH is approximately 6.0-6.8.
- Avoid waterlogged soils, which cause root diseases and poor growth.
- Tolerates a range of soils, including moderately poor ones, but responds well to fertile, well-structured soil.

## Soil Preparation
- Plough and harrow to a reasonably fine, well-drained seedbed to a depth of 20-30 cm.
- Incorporate well-decomposed manure or compost before planting to improve fertility and structure.
- Form ridges or raised beds in heavy soils or high-rainfall areas to improve drainage.
- On sloped land, plant along contours to reduce soil erosion.
- Conduct a soil test where possible to guide fertiliser decisions; see `soil/soil_testing_and_fertility.md`.

## Seed or Seedling Requirements
- Okra is usually **direct-seeded** in the field, as it establishes quickly in warm soil and does not transplant as readily as some vegetables.
- Use certified seed of a variety suited to local conditions and market (pod colour, length, and spininess vary by variety).
- Seed rate: approximately 5-10 kg/ha depending on spacing and whether one or more seeds are sown per station.
- Soaking seed for several hours to overnight before sowing can improve and speed germination, as okra has a hard seed coat.
- Sow 2-3 seeds per station about 2-3 cm deep in warm, moist soil, and thin to the strongest one or two seedlings after emergence.
- Warm soil is important; germination is slow and poor in cold soil.

## Plant Spacing
- Standard spacing: approximately 60-90 cm between rows and 30-45 cm between plants, depending on variety vigour and soil fertility.
- Closer spacing can be used for smaller varieties or where earlier, concentrated harvests are wanted; wider spacing suits tall, vigorous varieties on fertile soil.
- Adequate spacing improves airflow, eases repeated harvesting, and reduces disease.

## Water Requirements
- Requires adequate moisture for good germination, flowering, and continuous pod set, though okra tolerates short dry spells.
- Critical water-sensitive stages are germination/establishment and the flowering and pod-setting period.
- Moisture stress during flowering reduces pod set and causes tough, misshapen pods.
- Where irrigation is available, water regularly during pod production for continuous, tender pods; see `irrigation/irrigation_methods.md`.

## Fertiliser Recommendations
- Basal application at planting: a compound fertiliser supplying phosphorus and some nitrogen to support establishment.
- Topdress with nitrogen once or twice during growth, but avoid excessive nitrogen, which promotes leafy growth at the expense of pods.
- Ensure adequate potassium and phosphorus for good flowering and pod set.
- General guideline in the absence of a soil test: moderate, balanced nutrition adjusted to soil fertility, avoiding over-application of nitrogen.
- Apply well-decomposed manure or compost to improve soil structure and nutrient supply over time.
- See `fertilisers/fertiliser_basics.md` for application methods and timing principles.

## Weed Management
- Keep the crop weed-free during the first few weeks, before it establishes and shades the ground.
- Weed by hand hoeing or shallow cultivation; avoid deep cultivation close to the plant that damages roots.
- Mulching between rows suppresses weeds and conserves moisture.
- The critical weed-free period is the early establishment phase; once plants are large, competition is reduced.
- See `weeds/general_weed_management.md` for integrated weed control principles.

## Pest Management
- Key pests: aphids, whiteflies, cotton/spotted bollworm (pod borers), flea beetles, and jassids (leafhoppers).
- Scout regularly, especially for pod borers that tunnel into pods and for sucking pests that transmit viruses.
- Practice field sanitation, remove crop debris, and rotate away from related crops (e.g., cotton and other malvaceous crops) to reduce pest carry-over.
- Where chemical control is needed, use only locally registered products, rotate active-ingredient classes, and confirm choices with an extension officer.
- See `pests/aphids.md` for guidance on aphids and related sucking pests, and `emergency_guides/pest_outbreak_emergency_guide.md` for outbreak situations.

## Disease Management
- Key diseases: okra mosaic and yellow vein mosaic viruses (spread by whiteflies), powdery mildew, damping-off, and fusarium wilt.
- Use certified seed, tolerant varieties where available, and rotation to reduce disease pressure.
- Manage virus diseases indirectly by controlling whitefly vectors and removing severely infected plants.
- Ensure good drainage and airflow through proper spacing to reduce fungal diseases.
- See `emergency_guides/crop_emergency_guide.md` for guidance on severe or unclear disease situations.

## Growth Stages
1. Germination and emergence (approximately 5-14 days after sowing, faster in warm soil).
2. Vegetative growth: leaf and stem development (2-5 weeks).
3. Flowering: first flowers typically appear around 5-8 weeks after sowing.
4. Pod development: pods form and enlarge rapidly after flowering.
5. Continuous bearing: the plant flowers and sets pods repeatedly over several weeks with regular harvesting.

## Harvest Indicators
- Harvest pods young and tender, typically 4-7 days after flowering, when they are still soft and snap easily.
- Pods are usually harvested at approximately 5-10 cm long, depending on variety; over-mature pods become fibrous, tough, and unmarketable.
- Harvest frequently, every 2-3 days, to keep pods tender and to encourage continued flowering and pod set.
- Leaving mature pods on the plant reduces further flowering, so remove any oversized pods promptly.
- First harvest usually begins around 6-9 weeks after sowing and continues over several weeks.

## Post-Harvest Handling
- Handle pods gently, as they bruise easily and bruised areas darken and spoil quickly.
- Harvest in the cool part of the day and keep pods out of direct sun to slow moisture loss and toughening.
- Some varieties have small spines or irritant hairs on the pods; harvesters may wear gloves and long sleeves for comfort.
- Sort out damaged, over-mature, or diseased pods and pack tender pods in ventilated containers.
- Cool the pods as soon as possible after harvest to preserve tenderness and shelf life.

## Storage
- Okra pods are highly perishable and lose quality quickly; they are best sold or used soon after harvest.
- Fresh pods keep for only a few days at ambient temperature and somewhat longer under cool, humid conditions; avoid very cold storage, which causes chilling injury and darkening.
- Keep pods out of direct sun and heat, which cause rapid wilting and toughening.
- For longer preservation, pods can be dried or sliced and dried for use in soups and stews.
- See `storage/perishable_storage.md` for general guidance on handling perishable produce.

## Expected Yield
- Smallholder systems with basic management: approximately 5-10 tonnes/ha of fresh pods.
- Improved management with good fertility, water, and pest control: approximately 10-20 tonnes/ha.
- High-input, intensively managed systems: 20+ tonnes/ha.
- Actual yield depends heavily on variety, harvesting frequency, water availability, soil fertility, and pest/disease control.

## Common Mistakes
- Sowing in cold soil or too early in the season, resulting in poor, slow germination.
- Harvesting pods too late, so they become fibrous and tough and further flowering is suppressed.
- Harvesting too infrequently, reducing both pod quality and total yield.
- Applying excessive nitrogen, producing leafy plants with poor pod set.
- Neglecting whitefly control, allowing mosaic virus diseases to spread.

## Best Farming Practices
- Sow in warm soil after frost risk has passed, using an adapted variety and, where helpful, pre-soaked seed.
- Harvest young, tender pods frequently (every 2-3 days) to maintain quality and encourage continued bearing.
- Maintain adequate but not excessive nitrogen, and ensure good moisture during flowering and pod set.
- Scout for and manage sucking pests and pod borers using integrated pest management.
- Rotate away from related malvaceous crops and practice field sanitation to reduce pest and disease build-up.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: My okra pods are tough and stringy. What am I doing wrong?**
A: Tough, stringy pods are almost always caused by harvesting too late. Okra pods become fibrous very quickly, so harvest them young and tender (usually within about a week of flowering) and pick frequently, every 2-3 days, to keep the pods soft and to encourage continued production.

**Q: My okra plants are large and leafy but produce few pods. Why?**
A: Excessive leafy growth with poor pod set is often caused by too much nitrogen, sometimes combined with moisture stress during flowering. Reduce nitrogen applications, ensure steady moisture during flowering and pod set, and confirm that pests are not damaging flowers.

**Q: The leaves of my okra are turning yellow with a mottled pattern. Is this a disease?**
A: A yellow, mottled or vein-yellowing pattern can indicate a mosaic virus disease, which is spread by whiteflies. There is no cure once a plant is infected, so focus on controlling whiteflies, removing severely affected plants, and using tolerant varieties and certified seed. Consult an extension officer if the problem is widespread.

**Q: Can I transplant okra seedlings like tomato or cabbage?**
A: Okra is usually direct-seeded because it establishes quickly in warm soil and its taproot does not transplant as easily as vegetables such as tomato, onion, or cabbage. If you must raise seedlings, use individual containers to minimise root disturbance and transplant while young.
