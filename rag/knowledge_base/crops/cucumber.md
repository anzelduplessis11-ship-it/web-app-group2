# Cucumber

## Overview
Cucumber is a fast-growing, warm-season cucurbit vine grown across Africa for the fresh and salad market, where tender young fruit fetches a good price and turns over quickly. It is a high-value crop that can move from planting to first harvest in about seven to nine weeks, making it attractive to smallholders who need rapid returns and to farmers supplying urban markets, hotels, and the restaurant trade. The plant produces long trailing or climbing vines with tendrils, large leaves, and yellow flowers, and it can be grown either sprawling on the ground or trained up trellises and stakes. Trellised culture keeps fruit clean and straight, improves airflow to reduce disease, and makes picking easier, while ground culture is cheaper but raises the risk of fruit rot, disease, and misshapen fruit.

Cucumber is sensitive to frost and cold and needs warmth and steady, even moisture to produce well. Water stress is the single most common cause of bitter, misshapen, or hollow fruit, so irrigation management is central to quality. Most traditional types are monoecious, carrying separate male and female flowers on the same plant and depending on bees and other insects for pollination; because of this, insecticide sprays during flowering must be avoided or timed carefully to protect pollinators. Gynoecious (mostly female-flowering) and parthenocarpic (fruit-set without pollination) hybrids are increasingly available and can raise yields. Cucumber is a close relative of pumpkin and other cucurbits and shares many of their pests and diseases; see `crops/pumpkin.md`. Frequent harvesting of young, tender fruit every two to three days is essential to keep the plant productive and the fruit marketable.

## Scientific Name
*Cucumis sativus* L. It belongs to the family Cucurbitaceae, alongside pumpkin, squash, melon, and watermelon, and shares much of their pest and disease complex.

## Suitable Climate
- Optimal growing temperature: 18-30°C; cucumber is a true warm-season crop and grows fastest in warm, sunny weather.
- Very sensitive to frost and cold; growth stalls below about 15°C and plants are killed by frost.
- Requires warm days and warm nights for good pollination, fruit set, and rapid fruit expansion.
- Sustained temperatures above roughly 35°C, especially with water stress, cause flower drop, poor fruit set, and bitter fruit.
- Needs roughly 400-600 mm of water over the growing cycle, supplied evenly through rainfall and/or irrigation.
- Grows well in warm lowlands and warm seasons; in cool highlands it should be grown in the warm season or under protection.

## Suitable Soil
- Prefers fertile, well-drained, loose loam or sandy loam soils rich in organic matter that warm up quickly and drain freely.
- Optimal pH: 6.0-7.0; cucumber is sensitive to both strongly acidic and waterlogged conditions.
- Good drainage is essential, as waterlogging quickly causes root disease and wilting.
- Deep, friable soils with high organic matter hold moisture evenly, which is critical for smooth, well-shaped fruit.
- See `soil/soil_types_africa.md` for matching cucumber to local soils and `soil/soil_testing_and_fertility.md` for testing before planting.

## Soil Preparation
- Plough and harrow to a loose, fine tilth, then form raised beds or ridges to ensure good drainage and warm the root zone.
- Incorporate well-decomposed manure or compost (15-25 tonnes/ha) before planting; cucumber responds strongly to organic matter.
- On heavy or high-rainfall soils, plant on ridges or mounds to keep roots out of standing water.
- Prepare planting stations or furrows and, where trellising is planned, install stakes, poles, or wire supports before or soon after planting.
- Avoid fields recently cropped with other cucurbits to reduce carry-over of Fusarium wilt and cucurbit diseases; see `soil/soil_conservation_and_erosion.md` for protecting loose, prepared beds.

## Planting Season by African Region

## Seed or Seedling Requirements
- Cucumber is usually **direct-seeded** where it will grow, because its roots resent disturbance; it can also be **raised in the nursery in individual pots or blocks** and transplanted carefully with the root ball intact.
- Use certified or good-quality seed of a variety suited to the market (slicing/salad types, or specific types for the local trade) and, where available, disease-resistant hybrids.
- Choose the flowering type to suit conditions: monoecious open-pollinated types need bees for pollination; gynoecious hybrids set heavily and often include a pollinator seed; parthenocarpic types set fruit without pollination and suit protected culture.
- Seed rate: approximately 1.5-3 kg/ha depending on spacing and whether hills or single stations are used.
- Sow 2-3 seeds per station about 2-3 cm deep and thin to the strongest 1-2 seedlings after emergence.
- If transplanting, use young seedlings (about 2-3 weeks, 2-3 true leaves), handle the root ball gently, and transplant in the cool of the day.

## Plant Spacing
- Ground (trailing) culture: approximately 100-150 cm between rows and 30-60 cm between plants, allowing vines to spread.
- Trellised (vertical) culture: rows about 60-100 cm apart with plants about 30-45 cm apart, training one or two main stems up the support.
- Trellising raises plant density, improves airflow and light, keeps fruit clean and straight, and makes spraying and picking easier.
- Avoid overcrowding, which reduces airflow and greatly increases downy and powdery mildew.

## Water Requirements
- Cucumber has **high and even water needs**; the plant is more than 95% water and fruit expands rapidly.
- Maintain consistently moist (but not waterlogged) soil throughout growth, especially from flowering through fruiting.
- **Water stress is the leading cause of bitter, misshapen, curved, or hollow fruit**; alternating dry spells and heavy watering is particularly damaging.
- Drip irrigation is ideal because it delivers steady moisture and keeps foliage dry, reducing mildew and leaf spot; furrow irrigation also works. Avoid overhead watering, which prolongs leaf wetness and spreads disease. See `irrigation/irrigation_methods.md`.
- Mulching conserves moisture and buffers soil temperature; for timing and amounts see `irrigation/irrigation_scheduling.md`.

## Fertiliser Recommendations
- Cucumber grows and fruits fast and needs a balanced, readily available nutrient supply, with strong potassium demand during fruiting.
- Basal application at planting: a compound fertiliser supplying phosphorus and nitrogen to support root and early vine growth, worked into planting stations.
- Topdress with nitrogen and potassium as vines begin to run and again at the onset of fruiting to sustain continuous fruit production.
- Excess nitrogen produces lush vines with poor fruit set; balance nitrogen with adequate potassium and phosphorus.
- Ensure adequate calcium and magnesium to prevent fruit and leaf disorders; well-decomposed manure or compost improves both nutrition and moisture retention. See `fertilisers/organic_fertilisers.md`.
- In the absence of a soil test, follow balanced nutrition with split topdressing matched to soil fertility and crop vigour. See `fertilisers/fertiliser_basics.md` and `fertilisers/nutrient_deficiencies_guide.md`.

## Weed Management
- Keep the crop weed-free during the first few weeks, before the vines cover the ground, as young cucumbers compete poorly with weeds.
- Weed by hand or with shallow cultivation to avoid damaging the shallow, spreading root system.
- Mulching between rows and along beds suppresses weeds, conserves moisture, and keeps fruit clean in ground culture.
- Control cucurbit-family volunteer plants and weeds that host shared pests, viruses, and mildews.
- Once vines spread or climb the trellis, weed competition falls sharply. See `weeds/general_weed_management.md`.

## Pest Management
- Key pests: **aphids** and **whitefly** (both major virus vectors), cucumber beetles, red spider mites, thrips, fruit flies, and the epilachna/melon ladybird beetle (a leaf-eating beetle).
- Aphids and whitefly suck sap and transmit mosaic and other viruses; manage them early and conserve natural enemies. See `pests/aphids.md` and `pests/whitefly.md`.
- Thrips scar leaves and flowers and can transmit viruses; see `pests/thrips.md`. Red spider mites cause fine stippling and webbing in hot, dry conditions.
- Cucumber beetles and the epilachna beetle chew leaves and can spread bacterial disease; fruit flies sting young fruit, causing rot and deformity.
- **Protect pollinators:** because monoecious and gynoecious types depend on bees, avoid insecticide sprays during flowering; if control is unavoidable, spray late in the evening when bees are inactive, and prefer selective, low-toxicity options.
- Scout at least twice weekly, checking leaf undersides, growing tips, flowers, and young fruit; use yellow sticky traps to monitor whitefly and aphids.
- Because fruit is harvested frequently and eaten fresh, observe pre-harvest intervals strictly. Use only locally registered products and confirm active-ingredient choices, dosages, and safe intervals with a qualified agricultural extension officer. For severe outbreaks see `emergency_guides/pest_outbreak_emergency_guide.md`.

## Disease Management
- Key diseases: **downy mildew**, **powdery mildew**, angular leaf spot (bacterial), anthracnose, Fusarium wilt, gummy stem blight, and mosaic viruses transmitted by aphids and whitefly.
- Downy mildew shows angular yellow patches with greyish growth on leaf undersides in humid, wet weather; powdery mildew shows white powdery patches on leaf surfaces, worst in warm, dry days with humid nights.
- Angular leaf spot and anthracnose cause spots and lesions on leaves and fruit; they spread in wet, splashing conditions.
- Fusarium wilt and gummy stem blight cause wilting, stem cankers, and plant collapse and persist in soil, making rotation essential.
- Mosaic viruses cause mottled, distorted leaves and deformed fruit; because they are spread by aphids and whitefly, control those vectors, remove infected plants, and use resistant varieties.
- **Cultural control is the foundation:** use clean seed and resistant hybrids, rotate away from cucurbits for at least 2-3 seasons, trellis for airflow, avoid overhead watering, space adequately, and remove infected debris.
- Where copper-based fungicides or other products are considered, confirm locally registered, approved products and dosages with a qualified agricultural extension officer, and respect pre-harvest intervals on this frequently picked crop. See `emergency_guides/crop_emergency_guide.md` for severe or unclear situations.

## Growth Stages
1. Germination and emergence (about 4-10 days after sowing in warm soil).
2. Seedling stage: cotyledons and first true leaves; establishment of the young plant.
3. Vegetative vine growth: rapid runner and leaf development, with tendrils and staking/trellising.
4. Flowering: male flowers usually appear first, followed by female flowers (which carry a small fruit behind the petals); bee pollination occurs here in non-parthenocarpic types.
5. Fruit set and expansion: fertilised (or parthenocarpic) fruit swells rapidly and reaches picking size within days.
6. Continuous harvest phase: frequent picking of young fruit every 2-3 days sustains flowering and further fruit set over several weeks.
7. Decline: with age, heat, disease, or over-mature fruit left on the vine, flowering and fruit quality fall, ending the productive cycle.

## Harvest Indicators
- Harvest fruit **young and tender**, at the size and colour typical for the variety, before seeds harden and the skin toughens or yellows.
- First harvest usually begins about 6-9 weeks after sowing, depending on variety and temperature.
- Pick frequently, every **2-3 days**, because over-mature fruit turns bitter, seedy, and yellow and signals the plant to stop setting new fruit.
- Cut or clip fruit from the vine with a short piece of stalk rather than pulling, to avoid tearing the vine and bruising the fruit.
- Harvest in the cool part of the day to preserve firmness and shelf life.

## Post-Harvest Handling
- Harvest in the cool early morning to reduce field heat and slow softening.
- Handle fruit gently to avoid bruises, scratches, and pressure marks, which cause rapid decay and downgrade quality.
- Cucumber is sensitive to water loss and shrivels quickly; keep fruit shaded and cool and avoid exposure to sun and wind.
- Sort and grade by size, straightness, colour, and freedom from blemishes; remove yellowed, damaged, or diseased fruit. See `marketplace/product_grading_and_quality.md`.
- Wash with clean, safe water if required for market, and pack in ventilated crates to prevent bruising during transport.
- Cucumber is chilling-sensitive: do not store below about 10°C for long, as it develops pitting and decay. See `harvesting/harvesting_best_practices.md`.

## Storage
- Cucumber is highly perishable and best sold within a few days of harvest; it does not store well for long periods.
- Optimal short-term storage is cool and humid, around 10-12°C with high humidity; colder temperatures cause chilling injury (pitting, water-soaked spots, and rapid rot).
- Under ambient tropical conditions, fruit keeps only a few days and shrivels quickly without shade and humidity.
- Keep fruit out of direct sun, provide good airflow, and avoid packing warm fruit tightly, which causes heating and decay.
- Because of short shelf life and frequent picking, stagger plantings and market fruit rapidly. See `storage/perishable_storage.md` for general guidance on storing perishable produce.

## Expected Yield
- Smallholder systems with basic management, often ground culture: approximately 8-20 tonnes/ha.
- Improved management with trellising, quality or hybrid seed, drip irrigation, and good pest control: approximately 20-40 tonnes/ha.
- High-input systems, including protected cultivation with gynoecious or parthenocarpic hybrids: 40-80+ tonnes/ha.
- Actual yield depends heavily on variety and flowering type, trellising, water and nutrient management, pollination, and pest/disease control, especially virus pressure.

## Common Mistakes
- Planting into cold soil or before frost risk has passed, causing poor germination and stunted, frost-damaged plants.
- Irregular or insufficient watering, the leading cause of bitter, curved, and misshapen fruit.
- Spraying insecticides during flowering, killing bees and reducing fruit set in pollinator-dependent types.
- Growing on the ground without mulch in wet conditions, leading to fruit rot, disease, and dirty, misshapen fruit.
- Continuous cucurbit cropping without rotation, building up Fusarium wilt and other soil and foliar diseases.
- Picking too infrequently and leaving over-mature fruit on the vine, which turns bitter and shuts down further fruit set.
- Ignoring aphids and whitefly early, allowing mosaic viruses to spread through the crop.

## Best Farming Practices
- Grow cucumber in warm, frost-free conditions on fertile, well-drained beds rich in organic matter, and choose varieties and flowering types matched to the market and to whether pollinators are available.
- Trellis or stake the crop where possible to keep fruit clean and straight, improve airflow, and reduce disease.
- Provide steady, even moisture, ideally by drip irrigation, and mulch to conserve water and stabilise soil temperature.
- Protect pollinators by avoiding sprays during flowering and, where control is essential, applying selective, low-toxicity products in the evening.
- Use integrated pest management focused on aphids and whitefly to limit virus spread, conserving natural enemies and monitoring with sticky traps.
- Rotate away from cucurbits for at least 2-3 seasons, use clean seed and resistant hybrids, and remove infected debris.
- Harvest young fruit frequently, every 2-3 days, handle gently, cool quickly, and market rapidly because cucumber is highly perishable and chilling-sensitive.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Why are my cucumbers bitter?**
A: Bitterness is most often caused by water stress, heat, and irregular watering, which raise natural bitter compounds in the fruit, especially near the stem end and skin. Maintain steady, even soil moisture, mulch to conserve water, avoid heat stress, and pick fruit young. Some varieties are naturally less bitter, so choose accordingly.

**Q: Why are my cucumber fruits crooked, pinched, or misshapen?**
A: Misshapen fruit results from uneven watering, poor or partial pollination, nutrient imbalance, or feeding damage. Keep moisture even, ensure good pollination by protecting bees, balance nutrition (especially potassium), and control pests. Removing the first few small or deformed fruits can improve later fruit shape.

**Q: Should I spray insecticide when my cucumbers are flowering?**
A: Avoid spraying insecticides during flowering, because monoecious and gynoecious cucumbers depend on bees for pollination and sprays kill pollinators, reducing fruit set. If control is unavoidable, spray late in the evening when bees are inactive, use selective low-toxicity products, and confirm safe choices with an extension officer. See `pests/aphids.md` and `pests/whitefly.md`.

**Q: Do I need male and female plants, or a pollinator, to get cucumbers?**
A: Most cucumbers are monoecious, carrying both male and female flowers on the same plant, and need bees to move pollen; a single plant can therefore fruit if pollinators are present. Gynoecious hybrids are mostly female and often include a pollinator seed, while parthenocarpic types set fruit without any pollination and suit protected culture.

**Q: How often should I harvest cucumbers?**
A: Pick every 2-3 days while fruit is young and tender. Frequent harvesting keeps fruit at marketable size and quality and signals the plant to keep flowering and setting new fruit. Leaving over-mature fruit on the vine turns it bitter and seedy and shuts down further production.

**Q: Should I grow cucumbers on the ground or on a trellis?**
A: Trellising is generally better: it keeps fruit clean and straight, improves airflow to reduce mildew and leaf spot, raises plant density, and makes picking and spraying easier. Ground culture is cheaper but risks fruit rot, disease, and misshapen fruit; if growing on the ground, use mulch to keep fruit clean and dry.

**Q: My cucumber leaves have yellow patches or a white powdery coating. What is it?**
A: Yellow angular patches with greyish growth underneath indicate downy mildew, while a white powdery coating on the leaf surface indicates powdery mildew; both thrive in humid, crowded conditions. Improve airflow by trellising and spacing, avoid overhead watering, remove affected leaves, use resistant varieties, and confirm any approved copper-based or other fungicide options with an extension officer. See `emergency_guides/crop_emergency_guide.md`.

**Q: Cucumber is a cucurbit like pumpkin, so can I plant them together every season?**
A: They share pests and diseases such as Fusarium wilt, mildews, and mosaic viruses, so growing cucurbits together or repeatedly in the same plot increases pressure. Rotate away from all cucurbits (cucumber, pumpkin, melon, squash) for at least 2-3 seasons and separate cucurbit plantings; see `crops/pumpkin.md`.
