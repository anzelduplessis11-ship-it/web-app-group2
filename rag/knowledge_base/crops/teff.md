# Teff

## Overview
Teff is a small-grained cereal crop native to the Horn of Africa and the dominant staple grain in Ethiopia and Eritrea, where it is ground into flour to make injera, a fermented flatbread central to the regional diet. It is also grown as a fast-maturing forage and cover crop in parts of East and Southern Africa, and interest is growing in South Africa, Kenya, and beyond because of its gluten-free grain and drought tolerance. Teff performs well on marginal land where other cereals struggle, tolerates both moisture stress and short periods of waterlogging, and requires relatively low input levels compared to maize or wheat. Its extremely small seed size makes precise sowing, weeding, and threshing more demanding than for larger-seeded cereals, and lodging (stems falling over) is a persistent management challenge. The grain is valued nutritionally for its high mineral content (particularly iron and calcium) and its use as a naturally gluten-free staple, which has supported growing export and niche-market interest outside its traditional growing areas. Because a single hectare can be planted with only a few kilograms of seed, teff is also comparatively cheap to establish relative to other cereals, which appeals to smallholder farmers with limited access to purchased inputs.

## Scientific Name
*Eragrostis tef* (Zucc.) Trotter (synonym: *Eragrostis abyssinica*).

## Suitable Climate
- Grows across a wide range of altitudes, from lowland areas up to 2,800 m, but performs best at 1,800-2,400 m in its native highland range.
- Optimal growing temperature: 15-27°C; growth slows sharply below 10°C and above 30°C.
- Requires 450-650 mm of rainfall over the growing season; short-season varieties can produce a crop with as little as 300 mm.
- Frost-sensitive at all growth stages.
- More tolerant of both drought and temporary waterlogging than maize, wheat, or sorghum, making it a reliable option in areas with erratic rainfall distribution.

## Suitable Soil
- Highly adaptable; grows on soils ranging from light sandy loams to heavy clay (vertisol) soils that are difficult to manage for other cereals.
- Tolerates a wide pH range of 5.0-8.0.
- Performs best on well-drained, fertile soils, but is one of the few cereals that can produce a reasonable crop on waterlogged vertisols during the rainy season.
- Poor tolerance for prolonged, severe waterlogging at the seedling stage, which causes poor establishment and patchy stands.

## Soil Preparation
- Because teff seed is extremely small (roughly 1.3-1.7 million seeds per kg), a very fine, firm, and level seedbed is essential for even germination.
- Plough 2-3 times and harrow to break clods and remove crop residue that could bury the tiny seed too deep.
- On vertisols, prepare broad beds and furrows to manage drainage in wet periods and retain moisture in dry spells.
- Level the field carefully; uneven ground causes water to pool in low spots while leaving high spots too dry, both of which reduce germination uniformity.
- Apply organic manure or compost during the final tillage pass where soil fertility is low.

## Seed or Seedling Requirements
- Direct-seeded; no transplanting is practised.
- Traditional broadcasting uses a high seed rate of 25-50 kg/ha to compensate for uneven distribution, but this often causes overcrowding and lodging.
- Row planting with a calibrated seed rate of 3-5 kg/ha, mixing seed with fine sand, ash, or dry soil at roughly a 1:3 or 1:4 ratio, greatly improves distribution, seedling vigour, and final yield.
- Use clean, certified seed of an improved, lodging-tolerant variety suited to local altitude.
- Sow at a shallow depth of 1-2 cm; deeper sowing severely reduces emergence because of the seed's small energy reserves.

## Plant Spacing
- Broadcast method: no defined spacing; aim for an even, moderately thin stand across the field to reduce lodging.
- Row planting: rows 20-30 cm apart, thinned to a few centimetres between plants within the row once seedlings are established.
- Row planting consistently gives better light penetration, easier weeding, and reduced lodging compared with broadcasting.

## Water Requirements
- Total seasonal water requirement: 450-650 mm.
- Most sensitive to excess moisture at germination and the early seedling stage; brief waterlogging later in the season is tolerated better than by most cereals.
- Moderate drought tolerance during vegetative growth due to a fibrous, relatively efficient root system.
- Moisture stress during heading and grain filling reduces both yield and grain size.

## Fertiliser Recommendations
- General guideline: 40-60 kg N/ha and 30-40 kg P2O5/ha, adjusted to soil fertility and yield target.
- Split nitrogen application: roughly half at planting/early establishment and half at tillering.
- Avoid excessive nitrogen, which promotes tall, weak stems and dramatically increases lodging risk, a leading cause of harvest loss in teff.
- Apply well-decomposed manure or compost where available to improve soil structure, particularly on light sandy soils.
- See `fertilisers/fertiliser_basics.md` for general application methods and timing principles.

## Weed Management
- Weed competition is a major yield constraint because young teff seedlings are small and slow to establish ground cover.
- Two hand weedings are typical: the first 3-4 weeks after emergence, the second before heading.
- Row planting makes inter-row hoeing and hand weeding considerably easier than in broadcast fields.
- Grass weeds are particularly problematic because they closely resemble teff seedlings, making early identification and removal difficult.
- See `weeds/general_weed_management.md` for general principles.

## Pest Management
- Teff has relatively few serious insect pests compared with other cereals.
- Key pests: teff shoot fly (damages seedlings), African armyworm, aphids, and grasshoppers.
- Bird damage (notably by quelea and other grain-eating birds) can be significant at the milk-to-dough grain stage; scaring devices and timely harvest help reduce losses.
- Scout fields regularly during establishment and grain filling for early signs of infestation.

## Disease Management
- Key diseases: teff rust, damping-off at the seedling stage, and head smudge/leaf blight complexes under humid conditions.
- Use certified, disease-free seed and resistant varieties where available.
- Rotate with legumes or other non-grass crops to reduce disease carryover in the soil.
- Avoid excessively dense stands, which raise humidity within the canopy and favour fungal disease development.

## Growth Stages
1. Germination and emergence (5-10 days after planting).
2. Seedling and tillering stage (10-40 days).
3. Stem elongation and booting (40-60 days).
4. Heading and flowering (60-75 days).
5. Grain filling and physiological maturity (75-120 days, depending on variety and altitude).

## Harvest Indicators
- The whole field turns from green to golden yellow or straw colour as grains harden.
- Grains become firm and resistant to crushing between the fingers.
- Harvest when 80-90% of panicles have changed colour; delaying harvest significantly increases lodging and shattering losses, since mature teff stems are thin and easily bent by wind or rain.
- Because lodging risk rises sharply after full ripening, many farmers cut slightly before complete field-wide maturity and finish drying the crop in stooks.

## Post-Harvest Handling
- Cut plants close to the ground using a sickle and bind into small sheaves or stooks to continue drying in the field for several days.
- Traditional threshing is done by driving livestock over the drying stalks on a prepared threshing floor, or by mechanical thresher where available.
- Because the grain is extremely small, threshing floors must be clean and well-prepared to avoid contaminating the grain with sand and soil, a recognised food-safety and quality issue for teff.
- Winnow thoroughly to remove chaff, dust, and soil particles before storage or milling.

## Storage
- Dry grain to 10-12% moisture content before storage to prevent mould.
- Store in clean, dry sacks or containers in a cool, well-ventilated area protected from moisture and rodents.
- The small, hard grain is less prone to weevil damage than maize or sorghum, but storage moths and rodents can still cause losses if grain is not properly dried and sealed.
- Sieve stored grain periodically to check for and remove any sand or soil contamination.
- See `storage/grain_storage.md` for general grain storage guidance.

## Expected Yield
- Traditional broadcast systems with minimal inputs: 0.6-1.2 tonnes/ha.
- Improved row-planted systems with recommended seed rate and fertiliser: 1.5-2.5 tonnes/ha.
- Research-station and best-practice on-farm trials: 3-4+ tonnes/ha.
- The large gap between traditional and improved yields is driven mainly by seeding method (broadcast versus row) and seed rate control.

## Common Mistakes
- Broadcasting seed at very high rates, leading to overcrowded, weak, lodging-prone stands.
- Sowing too deep, which prevents the small seed from emerging.
- Delaying harvest past full maturity, resulting in major losses from lodging and grain shattering.
- Threshing on a poorly prepared floor, contaminating the grain with sand and soil.
- Applying excessive nitrogen fertiliser, which worsens lodging.

## Best Farming Practices
- Use row planting with a calibrated, reduced seed rate mixed with sand or ash for even distribution.
- Select improved, lodging-tolerant varieties suited to local conditions.
- Weed early and thoroughly during the critical establishment period.
- Harvest promptly at physiological maturity to minimise lodging and shattering losses.
- Prepare a clean threshing floor to protect grain quality and food safety.
- Rotate teff with legumes such as faba bean or chickpea where feasible, to maintain soil fertility and interrupt disease and weed cycles associated with continuous cereal cropping.
- Keep detailed records of planting date, seed rate, and weather conditions from season to season, since fine-tuning seed rate and timing to local rainfall patterns is one of the most effective ways smallholders can raise teff yields over time.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Why is teff normally broadcast rather than planted in rows?**
A: Broadcasting is the traditional method because the seed is extremely small and difficult to meter precisely by hand. Row planting with a reduced, calibrated seed rate consistently produces higher yields and less lodging, and is increasingly recommended by agricultural extension programmes.

**Q: Why does my teff crop fall over (lodge) before harvest?**
A: Lodging is usually caused by overly dense stands from broadcasting at high seed rates, excessive nitrogen fertiliser, or wind and rain acting on the crop's naturally thin stems near maturity. Row planting at recommended seed rates and balanced fertilisation reduce this risk.

**Q: Can teff be grown outside Ethiopia and Eritrea?**
A: Yes. Teff adapts to a wide range of soils and moderate rainfall conditions and is increasingly grown in parts of Southern Africa, Kenya, and beyond as a specialty grain or forage crop, though yields and agronomic knowledge are less established outside its traditional highland range.

**Q: Why does teff grain sometimes contain sand or soil?**
A: This usually results from threshing on an unprepared, bare threshing floor. Preparing a clean, hard floor (or using a tarpaulin or mechanical thresher) and thorough winnowing significantly reduces this contamination.

**Q: Is teff drought tolerant?**
A: Teff is more drought tolerant than many cereals once established, and can also tolerate brief waterlogging better than most grains, which is why it is grown on vertisol soils that flood periodically in Ethiopia. Moisture stress during heading and grain filling, however, still reduces yield and grain size.

**Q: What is the difference between white and brown/red teff varieties?**
A: Teff varieties differ mainly in grain colour (white, mixed, or brown/red) and this is largely a varietal and market-preference trait rather than an agronomic one. White teff is generally preferred and commands stronger demand in Ethiopian urban markets, while brown and mixed types are often grown for home consumption or in areas with fewer marketing options; agronomic management is similar across colour types, though maturity length and lodging tolerance vary by variety.
