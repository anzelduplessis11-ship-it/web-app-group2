# Cassava

## Overview
Cassava is a major root crop staple in Africa, valued for its drought tolerance, ability to grow on poor soils, and flexible harvest timing. It is used for food (flour, fufu, gari, tapioca), animal feed, and industrial starch production. It is propagated vegetatively from stem cuttings rather than seed.

## Scientific Name
*Manihot esculenta* Crantz

## Suitable Climate
- Grows well in temperatures of 25-29°C.
- Tolerates annual rainfall from 750 mm to over 2,000 mm; highly drought-tolerant once established.
- Sensitive to frost and prolonged cold below 10°C.
- Thrives from sea level up to about 1,500 m altitude.

## Suitable Soil
- Performs well on light, sandy loam to sandy clay loam soils with good drainage.
- Tolerates poor, acidic soils (pH 4.5-8.0) better than most staple crops.
- Poorly drained, waterlogged soils cause root rot and should be avoided.

## Soil Preparation
- Plough and harrow to loosen soil for root development, particularly on heavier soils.
- Make ridges or mounds 20-30 cm high on heavy or poorly drained soils to improve root formation and simplify harvesting.
- Minimal tillage is possible on light sandy soils.

## Planting Season by African Region
| Region | Typical Planting Window | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| West Africa | March-May (start of rains) | Can be planted through the wet season in humid zones |
| East Africa | March-May and Sept-Nov (bimodal areas) | Planting timed to rainfall onset |
| Southern Africa | Nov-Dec (start of rains) | Avoid planting into cold, dry winter months |
| Central Africa | Year-round in humid equatorial zones | Rainfall is generally well distributed |

## Seed or Seedling Requirements
- Propagated from stem cuttings ("setts") of healthy, disease-free mother plants aged 8-18 months.
- Cuttings should be 20-30 cm long with 5-7 nodes.
- Select cuttings from the middle/lower part of the stem, avoiding overly woody or overly green sections.
- Treat cuttings with fungicide/insecticide dip where cassava mosaic disease or mealybug pressure is high, and use certified disease-free planting material where available.

## Plant Spacing
- Standard spacing: 1 m x 1 m, giving approximately 10,000 plants/ha.
- Wider spacing (1 m x 1.2 m or more) for vigorous branching varieties or intercropping.
- Plant cuttings at a 45-degree angle or vertically, with two-thirds of the cutting buried in the soil.

## Water Requirements
- Cassava is highly drought-tolerant once established (after 2-3 months).
- Establishment phase (first 2-3 months) benefits from consistent soil moisture for good sprouting and root initiation.
- Supplemental irrigation is rarely used commercially but can improve yield in prolonged dry seasons.

## Fertiliser Recommendations
- Cassava can produce reasonable yields on low-fertility soils, but responds well to balanced fertilisation on depleted soils.
- General guideline: 40-80 kg N/ha, 20-40 kg P2O5/ha, 60-100 kg K2O/ha, split with basal application at planting and topdress at 2-3 months.
- Potassium is particularly important for root bulking; deficiency reduces root yield and quality.
- Organic manure or compost application improves soil structure, especially on sandy soils.

## Weed Management
- Weed control is most critical in the first 3-4 months before canopy closure, after which cassava suppresses weeds itself.
- Weed at least twice: around 4-6 weeks and 10-12 weeks after planting.
- Mulching between rows can help suppress weeds and conserve moisture in drier zones.

## Pest Management
- Key pests: cassava mealybug, cassava green mite, whiteflies (which also transmit disease), and termites.
- Use resistant varieties, biological control agents (e.g., parasitic wasps for mealybug), and field sanitation.
- Avoid moving planting material from infested fields to clean fields.

## Disease Management
- Key diseases: cassava mosaic disease, cassava brown streak disease, bacterial blight, and root rots.
- Use certified, disease-free planting material as the primary control method, since many cassava diseases are propagated through infected cuttings.
- Rogue and destroy visibly infected plants promptly.
- See `diseases/cassava_mosaic_disease.md` for detailed guidance.

## Growth Stages
1. Sprouting and establishment (0-1 month).
2. Vegetative growth and canopy development (1-4 months).
3. Root bulking (4-8 months), the main period of storage root development.
4. Maturity and root filling completion (8-18 months depending on variety).

## Harvest Indicators
- Most varieties mature between 8 and 18 months after planting, depending on variety and use (fresh consumption vs. processing).
- Yellowing and dropping of lower leaves can indicate approaching maturity, though this is not a definitive sign.
- Test-dig a few plants to check root size and starch content before full harvest.
- Cassava can be left in the ground beyond maturity as a form of in-field storage, but prolonged delay increases fibre content and disease/pest risk.

## Post-Harvest Handling
- Harvest carefully to avoid bruising or breaking roots, which accelerates spoilage.
- Fresh cassava roots deteriorate within 2-4 days after harvest (physiological post-harvest deterioration) and should be processed or consumed quickly, or stored using specific methods.
- Process into more stable forms (gari, flour, chips) if immediate sale or consumption is not possible.

## Storage
- Fresh roots: store in cool, humid, shaded conditions or bury in moist sand/soil clamps to extend shelf life to 1-2 weeks.
- Dried products (chips, flour): store in dry, well-ventilated, pest-proof containers or bags.
- See `storage/perishable_storage.md` for further guidance on root and tuber storage.

## Expected Yield
- Traditional/low-input systems: 5-10 tonnes/ha of fresh roots.
- Improved management with good varieties and fertilisation: 15-25 tonnes/ha.
- High-input commercial systems: 25-40+ tonnes/ha.

## Common Mistakes
- Using planting material from disease-infected fields.
- Harvesting too early, before adequate starch accumulation.
- Delaying processing after harvest, leading to root spoilage.
- Neglecting weed control in the critical early establishment period.
- Continuous cassava monocropping without rotation, depleting soil potassium.

## Best Farming Practices
- Source certified, disease-free planting material.
- Rotate cassava with legumes or cereals to manage soil fertility and disease pressure.
- Weed early and thoroughly before canopy closure.
- Harvest at appropriate maturity and process or sell promptly due to rapid post-harvest deterioration.
- Practice field sanitation by removing and destroying infected plant material.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Why do my cassava leaves show yellow mosaic patterns?**
A: This is a classic symptom of cassava mosaic disease, a viral disease spread by whiteflies and infected cuttings. Affected plants should be removed and destroyed, and future planting material sourced from certified disease-free stock.

**Q: How long can I leave cassava in the ground before harvesting?**
A: Many varieties can remain in the ground for several months past minimum maturity, which is a useful in-field storage strategy, but prolonged delay increases fibrous root content and vulnerability to pests and disease.

**Q: Why does my harvested cassava spoil within a few days?**
A: Fresh cassava roots undergo rapid physiological post-harvest deterioration once harvested. Processing into gari, flour, or chips shortly after harvest, or using cool, humid storage methods, helps extend usability.
