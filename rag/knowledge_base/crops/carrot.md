# Carrot

## Overview
Carrot is a cool-season root vegetable grown for its swollen, edible taproot. It is a valuable cash and nutrition crop for African smallholders because it stores and transports relatively well, commands steady urban market demand, and is rich in beta-carotene (provitamin A), fibre, and potassium. Carrot production in Africa is concentrated in cool highland zones (for example the Kenyan and Ethiopian highlands, the Cameroon highlands, and cooler parts of Southern Africa) and in the cool dry season of lowland and Sahelian areas.

The defining agronomic challenge of carrot is producing long, straight, smooth roots. Root quality depends almost entirely on soil physical condition and even moisture. Because the marketable product is a taproot that grows straight down, carrot must be direct-seeded where it will mature; it is never transplanted, since disturbing the young taproot causes forking and stunting. Carrot rewards careful seedbed preparation, patient thinning, and disciplined irrigation more than heavy fertiliser inputs. Getting the physical seedbed right is the single most important decision a carrot grower makes.

## Scientific Name
*Daucus carota* L. subsp. *sativus*. Family: Apiaceae (Umbelliferae), the same family as parsley, coriander, celery, and parsnip. This family relationship matters for rotation planning, because these crops share pests such as the carrot rust fly.

## Suitable Climate
- Carrot is a cool-season crop. The optimum air temperature for growth and good root colour is roughly 16-21 °C.
- Roots develop best when temperatures stay between about 15 and 24 °C. Germination occurs from about 7 °C upward but is fastest and most even around 20-25 °C soil temperature.
- Temperatures consistently above 28-30 °C produce pale, short, poorly coloured roots with a coarse texture and a stronger, sometimes bitter or turpentine-like flavour. High soil temperature also favours forking.
- Cool temperatures below about 10 °C for extended periods, especially in biennial types after a cold spell, can trigger premature bolting (flowering), which makes roots woody and unmarketable.
- Carrot tolerates light frost and its flavour can even improve slightly with cool nights, but hard frost damages exposed shoulders and foliage.
- In hot African lowlands, grow carrot in the cool dry season; in highland zones it can be grown for much of the year. Altitudes above roughly 1,500 m in equatorial regions give the most reliable results.

## Suitable Soil
- The ideal soil is a deep, loose, well-drained, stone-free sandy loam or loam with abundant organic matter that is already well decomposed.
- Deep soil (at least 30 cm of loose, workable soil, ideally more) allows roots to elongate straight without hitting a hard pan.
- Stones, clods, buried crop residues, and compacted layers cause forking, splitting, and misshapen roots and must be removed or broken up.
- Heavy clay soils are poor for carrots: they compact, crust, waterlog, and physically obstruct the taproot, giving short, forked, or stubby roots. If only clay is available, grow shorter stump-rooted (Chantenay- or Nantes-type short) varieties on raised, well-tilled beds.
- Preferred soil pH is 6.0-6.8. Carrot is moderately sensitive to acidity; very acid soils reduce yield and nutrient availability. See `soil/soil_testing_and_fertility.md` and `soil/soil_types_africa.md`.
- Carrot is moderately sensitive to salinity, so use good-quality irrigation water where possible.

## Soil Preparation
1. Choose a plot that has not grown carrot or other Apiaceae (parsley, celery, coriander, parsnip) for at least 2-3 years, to break carrot rust fly and nematode cycles.
2. Clear all weeds, crop residues, and roots. Remove stones and break up clods; the finer and more uniform the tilth, the straighter the roots.
3. Cultivate deeply, to at least 25-30 cm, using a fork, hoe, or draught/tractor implement, and break any compacted subsurface pan.
4. Incorporate only WELL-ROTTED compost or manure during land preparation, ideally applied to the PREVIOUS crop. Never apply fresh manure to carrots: fresh or partly decomposed manure causes forked, hairy, and split roots and encourages excessive leaf growth. See `fertilisers/organic_fertilisers.md`.
5. Rake the seedbed to a fine, crumbly surface free of large aggregates. A fine tilth improves seed-to-soil contact and even germination of the small seed.
6. Form raised beds 15-20 cm high where drainage is poor or soil is heavy; raised beds also deepen the effective loose rooting zone.
7. Level the bed surface so irrigation water spreads evenly and does not pool, which prevents patchy germination.

## Planting Season by African Region

| Region | Typical Planting Window | Notes |
| --- | --- | --- |
| West Africa | October to February (cool dry season) | Confined mainly to cooler highland areas (for example the Cameroon and Nigerian plateaus) and to the harmattan cool season; hot humid lowlands give poor roots. |
| East Africa | Year-round in highlands; main rains and short rains in cooler zones | Kenyan and Ethiopian highlands above ~1,500 m allow near-continuous staggered planting; time crops to avoid the hottest months. |
| Southern Africa | February to May (autumn) and again in cool season under irrigation | Grown as a winter/cool-season crop; avoid mid-summer heat in low-lying areas. Frost-prone highveld plots should protect emerging seedlings. |
| Central Africa | Cooler months and higher-altitude sites | Restricted to highland and montane areas (for example eastern DRC, Rwanda, Burundi highlands); hot humid basin lowlands are unsuitable. |
| North Africa | September to November (autumn) and February to March (spring) | Mediterranean climate favours autumn and spring sowings under irrigation; avoid summer heat and winter hard frost. |

Stagger sowings every 2-3 weeks for a continuous supply and to spread market risk. Confirm local timing with an agricultural extension officer, as microclimate and altitude strongly affect the window.

## Seed or Seedling Requirements
- Carrot is ALWAYS direct-seeded in the final position and NEVER transplanted. Transplanting damages the young taproot and causes forking, stunting, and Y-shaped or twisted roots.
- Seed is very small (about 700-1,000 seeds per gram). Sow thinly to reduce later thinning work and competition.
- Typical seed rate is about 3-5 kg/ha for row sowing, higher for broadcasting; smallholder plots use roughly 4-6 g per 10 m of row.
- Sow at 1-2 cm depth into moist soil; deeper sowing delays or prevents emergence.
- Germination is slow, usually 10-21 days, and slower in cold soil. Keep the surface continuously moist during this period; surface crusting after heavy rain or hard irrigation is a major cause of poor stands.
- Mixing seed with dry sand or fine compost helps spread the tiny seed evenly. A light mulch of fine grass or straw keeps the surface moist and reduces crusting, but must be removed or thinned once seedlings emerge.
- Use certified, fresh seed of an adapted variety. Carrot seed loses viability quickly, typically within 1-3 years, so avoid old seed.
- Choose variety type to match soil depth: long Imperator types need deep loose soil; Nantes and Chantenay (shorter, stump-rooted) types tolerate shallower or heavier soils.

## Plant Spacing
- Sow in rows 20-30 cm apart to allow weeding and airflow.
- After emergence, thin seedlings to a final in-row spacing of 3-8 cm (commonly about 5 cm) depending on the root size wanted; wider spacing gives larger roots, closer spacing gives more but smaller roots.
- Thinning is essential. Crowded carrots produce thin, competing, misshapen roots. Thin in two stages: first when seedlings have 2-3 true leaves, then a second time 2-3 weeks later.
- Thin in the late afternoon or evening and firm and water the soil afterwards, because the crushed foliage of pulled seedlings releases an odour that attracts carrot rust fly. Remove thinnings from the field.
- Final target plant population is roughly 500,000-1,000,000 plants/ha depending on spacing and market root size.

## Water Requirements
- Carrot needs a steady, even moisture supply throughout the season; total seasonal water use is roughly 350-550 mm depending on climate and season length.
- The most critical rule is CONSISTENCY. Irregular watering (drying out then heavy watering or rain) causes the roots to crack and split, and alternating stress and growth causes rough, ringed, or misshapen roots.
- Keep the seedbed continuously moist during the long germination period; never let the surface dry and crust while seeds are germinating.
- Once established, apply water to maintain even soil moisture in the top 30 cm without waterlogging. Waterlogging causes root rots, cavity spot, and forking.
- Drip irrigation or well-managed furrow irrigation gives the most uniform moisture and keeps foliage dry, reducing leaf blights. Overhead irrigation wets foliage and increases disease; if used, water early in the day so leaves dry quickly. See `irrigation/irrigation_methods.md` and `irrigation/irrigation_scheduling.md`.
- Reduce irrigation slightly in the final 1-2 weeks before harvest to firm the roots and reduce splitting, but do not let the crop wilt.

## Fertiliser Recommendations
- Base fertility on a soil test where possible; see `soil/soil_testing_and_fertility.md` and `fertilisers/fertiliser_basics.md`.
- Carrot needs moderate nitrogen, high potassium, and adequate phosphorus. Excess nitrogen causes lush tops, hairy and forked roots, and poor colour and storage, so do not over-apply N.
- Incorporate well-decomposed compost or manure (about 15-25 t/ha) during land preparation or, better, apply it to the previous crop. Never use fresh manure on carrots.
- Typical guideline rates (confirm locally): nitrogen about 60-100 kg N/ha, phosphorus about 40-60 kg P2O5/ha, potassium about 100-150 kg K2O/ha. Potassium strongly improves root quality, colour, and sweetness.
- Apply all phosphorus and potassium and about one third of the nitrogen at or before sowing, worked into the seedbed.
- Side-dress the remaining nitrogen in split doses about 4-6 weeks after emergence, when roots begin to swell. Avoid late heavy nitrogen.
- Boron deficiency causes cracked, cavity-spotted roots; a small boron application may help on deficient sandy soils but boron is toxic in excess, so apply only on confirmed deficiency and on extension advice. See `fertilisers/nutrient_deficiencies_guide.md`.

## Weed Management
- Carrots are weak early competitors because they germinate slowly and the seedlings are small and delicate, so early weed control is critical.
- Weed early and often by hand or shallow hoeing; keep cultivation shallow to avoid damaging the developing taproot and bringing up new weed seed.
- Because carrot emergence is slow, a "stale seedbed" technique works well: prepare the bed, irrigate to germinate weeds, then kill the flush of weed seedlings by shallow hoeing before sowing carrot.
- The classic marker-crop trick is to sow a few fast-germinating radish seeds along the carrot rows; the radishes mark the rows within days so you can begin weeding before slow carrots emerge.
- A thin layer of fine organic mulch between rows suppresses weeds and conserves moisture once seedlings are established.
- Follow integrated weed management and avoid heavy reliance on herbicides; if any herbicide is used it must be one registered for carrots. Confirm registered products and rates with a qualified agricultural extension officer. See `weeds/general_weed_management.md`.

## Pest Management
Follow integrated pest management (IPM): rotate crops, keep fields clean, monitor regularly, encourage natural enemies, and use chemicals only as a last resort and only registered, approved products confirmed with a qualified agricultural extension officer. See `emergency_guides/pest_outbreak_emergency_guide.md`.

- Carrot rust fly (*Psila rosae*): the most serious specialist pest. Larvae tunnel into roots, leaving rusty-brown scarring and making roots unmarketable. Manage by rotation away from Apiaceae, by not leaving thinnings or damaged roots in the field, by removing thinnings and watering after thinning to mask the attractant odour, by using floating row covers/fine netting where affordable, by timing sowings to avoid main fly flights, and by prompt harvest of mature roots.
- Root-knot nematodes (*Meloidogyne* spp.): cause forking, galling, and stubby, hairy, distorted roots, worst in sandy soils. Manage with rotation (avoid host crops, use resistant or poor-host rotation crops such as some cereals and marigold cover crops), organic matter, and good soil health rather than chemicals. Solarisation of beds can reduce populations.
- Aphids (see `pests/aphids.md`): cluster on foliage, stunt plants, and transmit viruses such as carrot motley dwarf. Encourage ladybirds, lacewings, and parasitic wasps; use soap or neem sprays for small outbreaks.
- Cutworms: caterpillars that cut off seedlings at soil level at night. Manage with clean, weed-free seedbeds, hand-collection at night, and protective collars for high-value beds.
- Other pests include leafhoppers (vectors of aster yellows), thrips (see `pests/thrips.md`), and whitefly (see `pests/whitefly.md`) in some areas.
- Where chemical control is justified, use active-ingredient classes appropriate to the pest (for example a registered product for soil-dwelling larvae) strictly according to the label and pre-harvest interval, and only after confirming local registration with an extension officer.

## Disease Management
- Alternaria leaf blight (*Alternaria dauci*) and Cercospora leaf spot (*Cercospora carotae*): the most common foliar diseases, causing dark leaf spots, yellowing, and death of foliage, which weakens roots and hampers mechanical or hand harvest. Manage with certified disease-free seed, rotation, wider spacing and dry foliage (drip or furrow rather than overhead irrigation), destruction of infected residues, and, where justified, protective sprays such as copper-based fungicides applied preventively.
- Powdery mildew (*Erysiphe heraclei*): white powdery growth on foliage in dry, warm conditions; manage with resistant varieties, airflow, and appropriate registered fungicides where severe.
- Cavity spot (*Pythium* spp.): sunken elliptical lesions on the root surface, worst in wet, cool, waterlogged soils. Manage with good drainage, avoiding waterlogging, balanced calcium and boron nutrition, and rotation.
- Sclerotinia rot / white mould (*Sclerotinia sclerotiorum*): watery soft rot with white cottony growth, especially in humid dense stands and in storage. Manage with rotation, good airflow, avoiding dense canopies and injury, and careful curing and cool storage.
- Bacterial soft rot and other storage rots enter through wounds and bruises; minimise injury at harvest and store cool.
- General disease control: rotate at least 2-3 years away from Apiaceae, use clean seed, keep foliage dry, remove and destroy infected debris, and manage soil moisture. For any fungicide, use only locally registered products and rates confirmed with a qualified agricultural extension officer, respecting pre-harvest intervals. See `emergency_guides/crop_emergency_guide.md`.

## Growth Stages
1. Germination and emergence (about 7-21 days after sowing): slow and moisture-dependent; keep the surface moist and crust-free.
2. Seedling establishment (2-4 weeks): first true leaves develop; the crop is very weak against weeds and needs the first thinning.
3. Vegetative leaf growth (4-8 weeks): the fern-like canopy expands and builds the photosynthetic capacity that will fill the root.
4. Root enlargement / bulking (8-14 weeks): the taproot swells rapidly; this is the most water- and potassium-sensitive stage and the period of second thinning and side-dressing.
5. Maturity (roughly 90-140 days after sowing depending on variety and temperature): roots reach full size, colour, and sweetness.
6. Bolting (undesirable): if exposed to cold stress or in long-day heat, plants may send up a flower stalk, making roots woody and unmarketable; harvest before this in warm conditions and use bolt-resistant varieties.

## Harvest Indicators
- Harvest is judged mainly by ROOT SHOULDER DIAMETER at the crown, visible at the soil surface. Most market carrots are pulled when shoulders reach about 2-4 cm across, depending on the target market.
- Days to maturity are roughly 90-140 depending on variety and temperature; baby carrots are lifted earlier.
- Roots should be firm, well coloured, and of the length and shape typical of the variety; sample-lift a few roots to check.
- Harvest before roots become oversized, as over-mature roots crack, become woody, pithy, or bitter and store poorly.
- In warm climates harvest promptly at maturity to avoid heat-related quality loss and bolting.
- Lift when soil is moist but not waterlogged; loosen soil beside the row with a fork before pulling to avoid snapping roots. Handle gently to prevent breakage and skinning.
- Harvest in the cool of the morning to reduce field heat and wilting. See `harvesting/harvesting_best_practices.md`.

## Post-Harvest Handling
- Remove the tops (foliage) soon after harvest, cutting about 1-2 cm above the crown. Attached tops continue to draw moisture from the root, causing rapid wilting and softening.
- Wash off soil in clean water to improve appearance and market grade, then allow surface moisture to drain; avoid leaving roots wet and warm, which encourages rot.
- Cool the roots as soon as possible to remove field heat; carrots are best kept cold and moist.
- Grade by size, straightness, colour, and freedom from forking, cracking, greening, and rust-fly damage. Good grading raises price and reduces rejection. See `marketplace/product_grading_and_quality.md`.
- Handle gently: bruises, cuts, and skinning are entry points for rots and cause rapid deterioration.
- Discard forked, split, green-shouldered, or damaged roots from the main pack; these can be sold as a lower grade or used at home.
- Pack in ventilated crates or bags that avoid crushing; avoid long periods in the sun during transport to market.

## Storage
- Carrots store best in COOL, MOIST conditions: about 0-4 °C with 90-95% relative humidity. Under these conditions good roots can keep for several weeks to a few months.
- Without refrigeration, use evaporative cooling structures, cool shaded pits, or clamps: layer topped roots in moist sand or cool soil in a shaded, ventilated place to slow moisture loss and keep them firm.
- The main causes of storage loss are wilting (low humidity), rotting (warmth, wetness, and wounds), and sprouting or greening (light and warmth). Keep roots covered, cool, and out of direct light.
- Do not store carrots near ethylene-producing fruit such as bananas, apples, or ripening tomatoes; ethylene induces bitterness in carrots.
- Sort out any rotting or damaged roots before and during storage to prevent spread.
- For short farm-gate holding, keep topped, washed, drained roots in shade under damp cloth or in moist sand and move to market quickly. See `storage/perishable_storage.md`.

## Expected Yield
- Smallholder / low-input systems: roughly 8-20 t/ha, often limited by poor seedbed preparation, uneven germination, weed competition, and irregular watering.
- Improved smallholder systems (good seedbed, thinning, even irrigation, balanced fertiliser): roughly 20-35 t/ha.
- High-input / commercial systems (precision sowing, drip irrigation, full nutrition and pest management, adapted varieties): roughly 35-60 t/ha or more.
- Yields vary widely by variety, altitude, season, soil depth, and management. A large share of the harvest can be downgraded by forking, cracking, or rust-fly damage, so quality management strongly affects marketable (not just total) yield.

## Common Mistakes
- Transplanting seedlings instead of direct-seeding, causing forked and stunted roots.
- Applying fresh or poorly rotted manure, which causes forking, hairy roots, and excessive leaf growth.
- Sowing into stony, cloddy, compacted, or shallow soil, giving short, split, or forked roots.
- Failing to thin, or thinning too late, so roots stay thin, crowded, and misshapen.
- Irregular watering, causing splitting, cracking, and rough roots.
- Over-applying nitrogen, giving lush tops but pale, hairy, poorly stored roots.
- Growing in hot conditions, producing pale, short, strongly flavoured roots and risking bolting.
- Leaving thinnings and damaged roots in the field, attracting carrot rust fly.
- Harvesting too late, so roots become woody, cracked, or bitter.
- Leaving tops on after harvest, causing rapid wilting and softening.

## Best Farming Practices
- Prepare a deep, fine, stone-free seedbed and, where soils are heavy or shallow, use raised beds and shorter-rooted varieties.
- Direct-seed thinly with fresh certified seed of an adapted variety, and keep the surface moist and crust-free until emergence.
- Thin promptly in two stages, remove thinnings, and water after thinning to reduce rust-fly attraction.
- Irrigate consistently to maintain even moisture, preferring drip or furrow irrigation to keep foliage dry.
- Base fertiliser on a soil test, favour potassium, avoid excess nitrogen, and use only well-rotted organic matter.
- Rotate at least 2-3 years away from carrot and other Apiaceae to break pest and disease cycles.
- Practise IPM: monitor regularly, encourage natural enemies, use physical barriers such as netting, and use only registered chemicals confirmed with an extension officer.
- Harvest at the correct shoulder diameter, top and cool roots promptly, grade carefully, and store cool and moist.
- Adopt climate-smart practices: mulching, organic matter build-up, and soil conservation to protect the fragile seedbed. See `soil/soil_conservation_and_erosion.md`.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Why are my carrots forked, twisted, or split into several roots?**
A: Forking is caused by physical obstruction and disturbance of the taproot: stones, clods, compacted or shallow soil, fresh manure, root-knot nematodes, or transplanting. Prepare a deep, loose, stone-free seedbed, use only well-rotted organic matter, and always direct-seed rather than transplant.

**Q: Can I raise carrots in a nursery and transplant them like tomatoes?**
A: No. Carrots must be direct-seeded where they will grow. Transplanting damages the taproot and produces forked, stunted, and poorly shaped roots. Sow thinly into a fine seedbed and thin the seedlings instead.

**Q: Why are my carrot roots pale, short, and strongly flavoured?**
A: This is usually caused by high temperatures. Carrot is a cool-season crop; above about 28-30 °C roots become pale, short, coarse, and bitter or turpentine-flavoured. Grow carrots in the cool season, in highlands, or under cooler conditions, and choose heat-tolerant varieties.

**Q: What causes carrot roots to crack or split?**
A: Splitting is caused mainly by irregular watering, where the soil dries out and is then suddenly wetted by heavy irrigation or rain, and by leaving roots in the ground too long. Keep soil moisture even throughout the season and harvest at the correct size.

**Q: Why is germination so slow and patchy?**
A: Carrot seed is small and naturally slow, taking 10-21 days, and germinates poorly if the surface dries out or forms a crust. Sow into moist soil at 1-2 cm, keep the surface continuously moist, use a light mulch to prevent crusting, and use fresh viable seed.

**Q: How do I know when carrots are ready to harvest?**
A: Judge by the root shoulder diameter at the crown, typically about 2-4 cm for market carrots, and by the variety's days to maturity (about 90-140 days). Sample-lift a few roots to check size, colour, and firmness, and harvest before roots become oversized and woody.

**Q: How can I control carrot rust fly without strong chemicals?**
A: Rotate away from carrots and related crops, remove and destroy thinnings and damaged roots, water and firm the soil after thinning to mask the attractant odour, cover beds with fine netting where affordable, time sowings to avoid main fly flights, and harvest mature roots promptly.

**Q: How should I store carrots without a refrigerator?**
A: Remove the tops, keep roots cool and moist, and store them layered in damp sand in a shaded, ventilated pit or clamp, or use an evaporative cooling structure. Keep them out of light and away from ethylene-producing fruit, and sort out any rotting roots. See `storage/perishable_storage.md`.
