# Yam (Dioscorea spp.)

## Overview

Yam is a starchy tuber crop of major economic, food-security and cultural importance across the West African "yam belt" that stretches from Côte d'Ivoire through Ghana, Togo, Benin and Nigeria into Cameroon. This zone produces the large majority of the world's yams, with Nigeria alone accounting for well over half of global output. Beyond West Africa, yams are grown in Central Africa, parts of East Africa (Ethiopia, Uganda, Tanzania) and the humid coastal belts wherever a long, warm, moist growing season is available.

Yam is distinct from cassava, sweet potato and cocoyam: it produces one or several large underground tubers on an annual vine that must climb a support. The crop is prized as a "food that fills the barn," a store of wealth, and a centrepiece of festivals such as the New Yam Festival. It is dense in carbohydrate energy, supplies useful potassium, vitamin C and dietary fibre, and its tubers store for months when handled well, making yam a strategic hedge against seasonal hunger.

For smallholders, yam is both a subsistence staple and a high-value cash crop. However, it is labour-intensive (mounding, staking, weeding), demands large quantities of planting material (seed yam), and is vulnerable to nematodes, storage rots and rapid soil fertility decline. This document covers agronomy, integrated pest and disease management, and post-harvest handling suited to low-input African farming systems. For soil fertility fundamentals see `soil/soil_testing_and_fertility.md`, and for rotation partners see `crops/cassava.md` and `crops/maize.md`.

## Scientific Name

Yam belongs to the genus *Dioscorea* in the family Dioscoreaceae. Several species are cultivated in Africa:

- ***Dioscorea rotundata*** (white Guinea yam) — the dominant West African species, the standard for pounded yam (fufu).
- ***Dioscorea alata*** (water yam or greater yam) — Asian in origin, high-yielding, widely grown, tolerant of poorer soils.
- ***Dioscorea cayenensis*** (yellow Guinea yam) — closely related to *D. rotundata*, yellow-fleshed.
- ***Dioscorea dumetorum*** (trifoliate or bitter yam) — nutritious but must be processed to remove bitter alkaloids.
- ***Dioscorea esculenta*** (lesser yam) and ***Dioscorea bulbifera*** (aerial/potato yam) — minor but locally important.

Yams are monocotyledonous climbing vines. Most cultivated species are dioecious (separate male and female plants), and many rarely flower, so propagation is almost entirely vegetative via tubers rather than true seed.

## Suitable Climate

Yam is a crop of the warm, humid and sub-humid tropics. Key climatic requirements:

- **Temperature:** Optimum growth occurs between 25 and 30 degrees Celsius. Growth slows sharply below about 20 degrees Celsius, and frost is lethal. Yam is not suited to cool highland or temperate zones.
- **Rainfall:** A well-distributed 1,000–1,500 mm over a growing season of 7–10 months is ideal. Yam needs abundant moisture during vine growth and tuber bulking, followed by a drier period to promote maturation and skin setting before harvest.
- **Growing season length:** Most yams require a long season (about 180–300 days depending on species and variety), which is why planting is timed to the onset of rains in most regions.
- **Photoperiod:** Yam is broadly a short-day plant for tuber initiation; shortening days later in the season help trigger tuber bulking.
- **Sunlight:** Full sun is required for the climbing canopy; this is why staking is critical, as shaded vines yield poorly.

Yam tolerates the high humidity of forest and forest-savanna transition zones but is sensitive to prolonged drought and to waterlogging. In drier savanna areas it can still succeed where rainfall is concentrated and supplementary moisture or residual soil water is available. See `guides/climate_smart_agriculture.md` for adapting to increasingly erratic rainfall.

## Suitable Soil

Yam performs best on deep, fertile, well-drained soils that allow the tuber to expand freely:

- **Texture:** Deep sandy loams and friable loams are ideal. Heavy clays restrict tuber expansion, cause misshapen tubers and increase rot risk; very sandy soils drain and leach too fast without organic matter.
- **Depth:** A loose, stone-free profile of at least 30–45 cm lets tubers grow long and straight. Shallow or compacted soils give forked, stubby tubers.
- **Drainage:** Good drainage is essential. Waterlogging quickly causes tuber rot; this is a major reason yam is grown on mounds or ridges.
- **pH:** A slightly acidic to neutral range of about 5.5–6.5 is preferred. Strongly acidic soils may need liming, guided by a soil test.
- **Fertility and organic matter:** Yam is a heavy feeder that draws down soil nutrients rapidly. It traditionally opens the rotation on freshly cleared or long-fallowed land rich in organic matter. High organic matter improves structure, water holding and nutrient supply.

Confirm soil suitability with a simple soil test before investing in seed yam; see `soil/soil_testing_and_fertility.md`.

## Soil Preparation

Land preparation for yam is among the most labour-intensive of any African crop because tubers need loose, deep, well-aerated soil.

1. **Land clearing:** Clear the field of bush, weeds and crop residues at the end of the previous season or during the dry season. Traditionally yam follows a fallow or is the first crop after clearing forest or savanna. Avoid burning where possible, as it destroys organic matter and soil life; instead slash and compost or mulch.
2. **Mounds or ridges:** Yam is planted on mounds or ridges to give the tuber deep loose soil and to shed excess water.
   - **Mounds:** Large mounds 0.3–1.0 m high are typical, spaced roughly 1 m apart. Larger mounds suit deep-rooting varieties and wetter soils.
   - **Ridges:** In mechanised or larger plots, tied ridges 0.3–0.5 m high can be used and are quicker to make.
3. **Incorporate organic matter:** Work well-rotted manure, compost or fallow vegetation into the mound as it is built. This is the single most effective low-input step for good yields.
4. **Timing:** Prepare mounds toward the end of the dry season so they are ready for planting at the first reliable rains. In some systems mounds are made and planted in the dry season with sprouting delayed until rains arrive.

Deep, loose soil directly determines tuber size, shape and marketability, so time and care spent on soil preparation pay off strongly.

## Planting Season by African Region

Yam is planted to make maximum use of the rainy season. Early ("first season") planting during or just before the early rains generally gives higher yields than late planting. Windows below are indicative and vary with local rainfall onset, variety and altitude.

| Region | Typical Planting Window | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| West Africa – forest zone (southern Nigeria, Ghana, Côte d'Ivoire, Benin) | November–March (dry-season/early planting) into April | Dry-season planting of pre-sprouted setts is common; tubers sprout with first rains. Long season maximises bulking. |
| West Africa – savanna/transition zone | March–May, at onset of single rains | Later planting than forest zone; align tightly with reliable rain onset to avoid drought stress at establishment. |
| Central Africa (Cameroon, DRC, CAR) | February–April | Bimodal rainfall in parts allows some flexibility; plant with the main rains. |
| East Africa (Uganda, Tanzania, western Kenya, Ethiopia) | February–April (long rains) | Grown mainly in warm, humid lowland and mid-altitude areas; avoid cool highlands. |
| Sahelian fringe / drier margins | May–June with rains | Only where rainfall and residual moisture allow a long enough season; risk of drought is high. |
| Southern Africa (warm low-veld areas) | September–November (spring, warming soils) | Marginal; needs warm frost-free season and irrigation or reliable summer rain. |

Planting early and staking promptly are the two decisions with the greatest influence on final yield.

## Seed or Seedling Requirements

Yam is propagated vegetatively, and planting material ("seed yam") is the largest single cost and the main constraint for smallholders.

- **Seed yam (whole small tubers):** Whole tubers of about 200–350 g give the most reliable, vigorous plants. Farmers dedicate a portion of the harvest, or a second "milking" harvest, to producing seed-sized tubers.
- **Setts (cut pieces):** Larger ware tubers are cut into setts of about 100–300 g, each carrying a piece of skin (periderm) that regenerates sprouts. Head (proximal) setts sprout fastest; middle and tail setts are slower but usable.
- **Sett treatment:** After cutting, dip or dust cut surfaces in wood ash (a low-input option) and allow them to cure in shade for a day or two to suberise before planting. This reduces rot. Where available, treatment with approved fungicide/insecticide combinations is used to protect against rots and beetles — always confirm locally registered, approved products and rates with a qualified agricultural extension officer.
- **Minisett technique:** To multiply scarce material, small setts of about 25–50 g are raised in nurseries or seedbeds to produce seed yams; this greatly increases the multiplication ratio and is a recommended low-cost improvement.
- **Seed rate:** Because setts are large, planting material is heavy — often on the order of 1.5–3 tonnes per hectare of seed yam depending on sett size and spacing.
- **Health and selection:** Select seed only from healthy, vigorous plants and clean, blemish-free tubers. Reject material showing dry rot, nematode galling, cracks or beetle damage, as pests and diseases carry over on planting material. Use clean, sharp, disinfected knives when cutting setts to avoid spreading rot.

## Plant Spacing

Spacing depends on mound size, variety vigour and whether tubers are grown for ware (eating) or seed.

- **Ware yam (large tubers):** Roughly 1 m x 1 m, giving about 10,000 plants per hectare (one plant per mound). Wider spacing produces larger individual tubers.
- **Closer spacing (0.5–1 m):** Used for smaller-tubered types, water yam, or seed-yam production where many medium tubers are wanted rather than a few large ones; plant populations of 12,000–20,000 per hectare are possible.
- **Staking layout:** Arrange rows so vines can be trained onto individual stakes or a trellis without excessive shading. Adequate spacing keeps the canopy well lit and airy, reducing foliar disease.

Match spacing to the target market: fewer, larger tubers for premium ware sales; more, medium tubers for seed and for household use.

## Water Requirements

- **Total need:** About 1,000–1,500 mm of well-distributed water across the season. Most smallholder yam is rain-fed.
- **Critical periods:** Moisture is most important during the vegetative/vine growth and the tuber-bulking phases (roughly the middle third to two-thirds of the season). Water stress during bulking sharply reduces tuber size.
- **Establishment:** Adequate soil moisture is needed for sprouting and early root growth; dry-season-planted setts wait in the mound until rains trigger emergence.
- **Maturation:** A drier spell in the last weeks helps tubers mature, set their skins and store better. Excess late rain can encourage rot and re-sprouting.
- **Drainage over irrigation:** Because yam is far more sensitive to waterlogging than to short dry spells, mounds/ridges and good drainage matter more than irrigation in most systems. Where irrigation is available (rare for smallholders), supplement only during bulking-phase drought and never let the mound become saturated.
- **Mulching:** A grass or leaf mulch on mounds conserves moisture, moderates soil temperature and suppresses weeds — a valuable low-input practice. See `guides/water_management_and_irrigation.md`.

## Fertiliser Recommendations

Yam is a heavy feeder that depletes soil nutrients quickly, yet it responds moderately (not dramatically) to mineral fertiliser and is traditionally grown on inherently fertile land.

- **Start with a soil test:** Base rates on soil test results and local extension guidance rather than blanket application; see `soil/soil_testing_and_fertility.md`.
- **Organic matter first:** Incorporating well-rotted manure, compost or green manure (for example on the order of 5–10 tonnes per hectare of compost/manure) into mounds is the foundation of yam nutrition. It supplies balanced nutrients, improves tuber shape and sustains soil health.
- **Potassium (K):** Yam has a high potassium demand for tuber bulking and quality. Potassium-rich amendments (including wood ash as a low-input source) are valuable.
- **Nitrogen (N):** Moderate nitrogen supports vine and canopy growth, but excess N promotes leafy growth at the expense of tubers and can worsen storage rot. Apply modestly and split.
- **Phosphorus (P):** Supports root and early establishment; supply according to soil test.
- **Indicative mineral guidance:** Where compound fertilisers are used, a balanced NPK with adequate potassium is typical, applied a few weeks after emergence and again at early bulking. Exact product grades and rates vary by locality, soil and season — confirm with a qualified agricultural extension officer. Avoid placing fertiliser in direct contact with the sett.
- **Sustainable rotation:** Rotate yam as the first crop after a legume fallow or intercrop with legumes to build nitrogen naturally. Never invent or rely on a fixed price for inputs; costs vary widely by locality and season.

## Weed Management

Weeds compete strongly during the first 8–12 weeks before the yam canopy closes and can drastically cut yields if uncontrolled.

- **Timing:** Keep the crop weed-free during the critical early period. Two to three manual weedings (hand hoe) during the first three months are typical.
- **Mulching:** A thick organic mulch on and around mounds suppresses weeds, conserves moisture and adds organic matter — the preferred low-input, climate-smart method.
- **Staking and canopy closure:** Prompt staking lets the vine canopy develop and shade out later weeds.
- **Intercropping:** Traditional intercrops (maize, vegetables, legumes, melon) established early can provide ground cover that limits weeds while adding food and income; avoid over-crowding that shades the yam.
- **Herbicides:** Chemical control is possible using registered pre-emergence and post-emergence herbicide classes, but weed control in smallholder yam is predominantly manual and cultural. If herbicides are considered, confirm locally registered, approved products, timing and rates with a qualified agricultural extension officer, and follow all label and safety precautions. Integrated weed management combining mulch, timely weeding and good spacing is the sustainable approach.

## Pest Management

Manage pests through Integrated Pest Management (IPM): clean planting material, resistant/tolerant varieties, field sanitation, rotation, and chemical control only as a confirmed last resort using locally registered products. See `pests/integrated_pest_management.md`.

- **Yam nematodes** (yam nematode *Scutellonema bradys*; root-knot nematodes *Meloidogyne* spp.): The most damaging pests. They cause dry rot and cracking of tubers in the field and in store, and galling of roots. IPM: plant clean, nematode-free seed; rotate with non-hosts (cereals, some legumes); fallow; use tolerant varieties; solarise or hot-water-treat seed setts where feasible; and remove/destroy infested tubers. Cross-reference `pests/nematodes.md`.
- **Yam beetles** (*Heteroligus* spp.): Adults tunnel into tubers, creating holes that admit rot and downgrade quality. IPM: early planting to escape peak beetle activity, deep mound soil, field sanitation, destroying breeding sites near streams, and treating seed setts. Where insecticides are justified, use registered products from appropriate active-ingredient classes and confirm choice with an extension officer.
- **Mealybugs and scale insects:** Attack tubers especially in storage, weakening seed yams. IPM: inspect and clean seed, discard infested tubers, and keep stores hygienic.
- **Termites:** Can damage setts and weak plants, particularly in drier areas. Manage with good soil moisture/organic matter, healthy vigorous plants and destruction of nearby mounds where problematic.
- **Scutch/scale on vines and general defoliators:** Usually minor; monitor and hand-pick.
- **Rodents and larger vertebrates:** Damage tubers in field and store; use trapping, fencing and clean surroundings.

Always scout regularly, keep records, and preserve natural enemies by avoiding unnecessary broad-spectrum spraying. Any pesticide use must follow the label, use protective equipment, observe pre-harvest intervals, and be verified with a qualified agricultural extension officer.

## Disease Management

- **Yam anthracnose** (*Colletotrichum gloeosporioides*): The most serious foliar disease, especially of water yam (*D. alata*), causing dark leaf and stem lesions, blight and dieback in humid conditions. Management: grow tolerant/resistant varieties (a priority for *D. alata*), use clean planting material, ensure good spacing and staking for air movement, avoid excess nitrogen, and rotate. Where fungicides are warranted, appropriate classes are used protectively; confirm registered products and timing with an extension officer.
- **Yam mosaic and other viruses** (yam mosaic virus, badnaviruses): Cause mosaic, leaf distortion and yield decline, and are carried in seed tubers. Management: use virus-tested/clean seed, select from healthy plants, rogue out symptomatic plants, and control insect vectors (aphids, mealybugs). See `pests/aphids.md`.
- **Dry rot complex** (nematode- and fungus-associated, e.g. *Fusarium*, plus *Scutellonema*): Major cause of field and storage tuber loss. Management overlaps with nematode control: clean seed, curing after harvest, careful handling to avoid wounds, and hygienic storage.
- **Wet/soft rots** (bacterial and fungal, e.g. *Erwinia/Pectobacterium*, *Botryodiplodia/Lasiodiplodia*, *Penicillium*, *Aspergillus*): Cause soft, wet or mouldy decay in store, entering through wounds and bruises. Management: minimise wounding at harvest, cure tubers, store only sound tubers in a cool, dry, ventilated store, and inspect regularly to remove rotting tubers.
- **Leaf spots and blights:** Usually secondary; managed by sanitation, spacing and resistant varieties.

The strongest disease controls for yam are clean, healthy planting material, resistant varieties, field sanitation, and careful wound-free post-harvest handling — not fungicides. See `guides/plant_disease_management.md`.

## Growth Stages

1. **Dormancy and sprouting (0–6+ weeks):** After planting, setts break dormancy and produce sprouts and adventitious roots. Dormancy length varies by species and how recently tubers were harvested.
2. **Emergence and early vegetative (weeks 4–10):** The vine emerges and must be trained onto a stake. Root system and canopy establish. This is the critical weed-control and staking window.
3. **Vine growth / canopy development (roughly months 2–4):** Rapid climbing growth builds the photosynthetic canopy. Full sun on the canopy is essential.
4. **Tuber initiation (around months 3–4):** The plant begins forming the new tuber(s), aided by shortening days. Adequate moisture and potassium matter here.
5. **Tuber bulking (roughly months 4–7):** The main dry-matter accumulation phase; the single most yield-determining period. Maintain moisture, weed control and canopy health.
6. **Maturation and senescence (months 7–10):** Vines yellow and die back; tubers reach full size and set their skins. A drier spell aids maturation and storability.
7. **Harvest readiness:** Signalled by canopy senescence and skin set (see Harvest Indicators).

Total season length ranges from about 6 to 10 months depending on species (water yam and Guinea yams differ) and variety.

## Harvest Indicators

- **Vine senescence:** Yellowing and drying of the leaves and vines is the clearest sign that tubers have matured, typically 7–10 months after planting.
- **Skin set:** Mature tubers have a firm, well-set skin (periderm) that resists rubbing off — important for storage.
- **Single vs double harvest:**
  - *Single harvest:* Lift the whole tuber once at full maturity for maximum size and best storage.
  - *Double harvest ("milking"):* In some Guinea yam systems, a first harvest removes part of the tuber mid-season while leaving the head attached; the plant regrows a second, smaller tuber often used as seed yam. This spreads labour and supplies planting material but can stress the plant.
- **Timing for market vs seed:** Harvest ware tubers at full maturity for eating quality; seed yams may be lifted slightly differently to favour medium sizes.
- **Careful lifting:** Dig carefully with a flat blade or digging stick well away from the tuber to avoid cutting or bruising it, as wounds are the main entry point for storage rot. Harvest in dry conditions where possible.

## Post-Harvest Handling

Post-harvest losses in yam are high (frequently 20–40 percent or more) and are driven mainly by rots, sprouting, weight loss and pest damage. Careful handling greatly extends storage life.

1. **Avoid wounding:** Handle tubers gently; do not throw or drop them. Cuts and bruises are the primary route for rot pathogens.
2. **Curing:** Cure freshly harvested tubers for about 1–2 weeks in a warm, humid, shaded, well-ventilated place (or heaped and covered with dry grass). Curing heals minor wounds by forming a corky layer (wound periderm), markedly reducing storage rot.
3. **Cleaning and sorting:** Remove adhering soil gently (do not wash if the tubers are to be stored long-term). Sort out damaged, diseased, cut or nematode-galled tubers for immediate use — never store them with sound tubers.
4. **Grading:** Separate ware tubers, seed yams and damaged tubers. Keep seed yams from healthy plants only.
5. **Transport:** Move tubers in padded, ventilated containers; avoid heaping heavy loads that crush lower tubers.

Good post-harvest hygiene at this stage determines how well the crop stores and its final market value. See `guides/post_harvest_handling.md`.

## Storage

Yam tubers, unlike most roots, can be stored for several months if kept cool, dry, dark and ventilated and if sprouting and rot are controlled.

- **Yam barn (traditional):** Tubers are tied individually to a vertical wooden frame under a shade tree or thatch roof. This keeps them off the ground, well ventilated, shaded and easy to inspect — an effective, low-cost method for several months of storage.
- **Platform / raised store:** Tubers laid in a single layer on a slatted platform in a shaded, airy structure; turn and inspect regularly.
- **Conditions:** Aim for cool (ideally around 12–15 degrees Celsius where achievable), dry, well-ventilated and dark conditions. Darkness and cool temperatures slow sprouting; ventilation removes heat and moisture that drive rot.
- **Regular inspection:** Check tubers at least weekly. Remove any that show soft rot, dry rot, sprouting or beetle/mealybug attack before they infect neighbours. Rub off sprouts to reduce weight loss and shrivelling.
- **Sprout suppression:** Cool, dark storage is the main low-input sprout control. Chemical sprout suppressants exist but are generally not appropriate for smallholder food yam; consult an extension officer before considering any treatment.
- **Seed yam storage:** Store seed yams especially carefully, as their health determines next season's crop; keep them separate and well ventilated.

Even with good management, expect some loss over a long storage season; sell or process surplus before quality declines.

## Expected Yield

- **Smallholder rain-fed, low input:** Commonly about 8–15 tonnes of fresh tubers per hectare.
- **Improved varieties with good agronomy (fertile soil, clean seed, staking, timely weeding):** About 20–30 tonnes per hectare.
- **Well-managed / high-potential conditions:** 30–40+ tonnes per hectare is achievable for responsive varieties on fertile, well-drained land.

Yields are strongly influenced by soil fertility and depth, quality of planting material, staking, timely early weeding, moisture during bulking, and pest/nematode pressure. Because yam draws heavily on soil fertility, yields typically decline on continuously cropped land — another reason to rotate and fallow. All figures are agronomic ranges that vary by locality, season, species and variety; they are not guarantees, and market prices likewise vary widely and are not stated here.

## Common Mistakes

- **Using poor or infected planting material:** Planting rotten, nematode-galled or virus-carrying setts spreads problems and caps yield. Always select clean seed from healthy plants.
- **Planting too late:** Late planting shortens the bulking window and reduces yields; align planting with the earliest reliable rains.
- **Failing to stake or late staking:** Unstaked, sprawling vines are shaded and yield far less. Stake promptly after emergence.
- **Shallow or compacted mounds:** Restrict tuber growth and cause forked, small or misshapen tubers.
- **Neglecting early weeding:** Weed competition in the first three months causes large yield losses.
- **Over-applying nitrogen:** Promotes vines over tubers and worsens storage rot.
- **Continuous cropping on the same land:** Depletes fertility and builds up nematodes and diseases; rotate and fallow.
- **Careless harvesting and no curing:** Wounded, uncured tubers rot rapidly in store.
- **Storing damaged with sound tubers:** One rotting tuber infects its neighbours; sort rigorously.
- **Relying on chemicals over IPM:** Ignoring clean seed, sanitation and resistant varieties in favour of sprays is costly and ineffective for yam's main problems.

## Best Farming Practices

- **Open the rotation with yam:** Grow yam first after a legume fallow or on freshly fertile land, then follow with cereals, legumes or cassava. See `crops/maize.md` and `crops/cassava.md`.
- **Invest in clean seed:** Use the minisett technique to multiply healthy planting material and steadily improve seed quality.
- **Build soil with organic matter:** Incorporate compost, manure and green manures into mounds; keep potassium supply adequate.
- **Prepare deep, loose mounds/ridges** and ensure good drainage.
- **Plant early and stake promptly** to maximise the bulking season and canopy light capture.
- **Mulch** to conserve moisture, suppress weeds and moderate soil temperature.
- **Weed early and often** during the critical first three months.
- **Practise IPM:** resistant varieties (especially anthracnose-tolerant *D. alata*), clean seed, sanitation, rotation, scouting, and chemical control only as a verified last resort with extension guidance.
- **Cure and store carefully** in a ventilated yam barn; inspect regularly and remove rotting tubers.
- **Keep records** of varieties, planting dates, inputs, pests and yields to improve each season.
- **Confirm all agrochemical decisions** (product class, registration, dose, safety interval) with a qualified agricultural extension officer before use.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Why must yam vines be staked?**
A: Yam is a climbing vine that needs full sunlight on its leaves to build the large tubers. Staking lifts the canopy into the light, improves air circulation (reducing anthracnose and other foliar diseases), and can substantially increase yield compared with letting vines sprawl on the ground.

**Q: How much planting material do I need per hectare?**
A: Because setts are large (about 100–350 g each) and populations are around 10,000 plants per hectare, seed requirements are heavy — roughly 1.5–3 tonnes per hectare. The minisett technique lets you multiply scarce, healthy seed yam efficiently to reduce this burden over time.

**Q: Why do my tubers rot so quickly in storage?**
A: The main causes are wounds from careless harvesting, failure to cure the tubers, storing damaged or diseased tubers alongside sound ones, and poor ventilation. Harvest gently, cure for 1–2 weeks, sort out any damaged tubers, and store in a cool, dry, ventilated, shaded structure with weekly inspection.

**Q: What is the difference between white Guinea yam and water yam?**
A: White Guinea yam (*Dioscorea rotundata*) is the West African favourite for pounded yam and generally stores and cooks well. Water yam (*Dioscorea alata*) is higher-yielding and more tolerant of poorer soils but is more susceptible to anthracnose, so choosing anthracnose-tolerant varieties is important.

**Q: Can I grow yam every year on the same plot?**
A: It is not advisable. Yam is a heavy feeder that quickly depletes soil fertility, and continuous cropping builds up nematodes and diseases. Rotate yam with cereals and legumes and include a fallow or green-manure phase to restore fertility and break pest cycles.

**Q: What is the most damaging pest of yam?**
A: Nematodes — especially the yam nematode (*Scutellonema bradys*) and root-knot nematodes — are the most damaging, causing dry rot and cracking in the field and in storage. The best defence is clean, nematode-free planting material combined with rotation, fallowing and tolerant varieties. Confirm any nematicide use with an extension officer.

**Q: When should I harvest my yam?**
A: Harvest when the vines and leaves have yellowed and dried and the tuber skin is firm and set, typically 7–10 months after planting. Lift carefully in dry conditions to avoid cutting the tubers. Some Guinea yam systems use a mid-season "milking" harvest followed by a second smaller tuber for seed.

**Q: Which fertiliser should I use and how much?**
A: Start with organic matter (compost or manure) worked into the mounds, ensure adequate potassium, and apply nitrogen only modestly to avoid excess vine growth. Base any mineral fertiliser rate on a soil test and local recommendations; specific product grades, rates and prices vary by locality and season, so confirm them with a qualified agricultural extension officer. See `soil/soil_testing_and_fertility.md`.
