# Maize (Corn)

## Overview
Maize is the most widely cultivated cereal crop in Africa, grown both as a staple food crop and as a cash crop. It is used for human consumption (flour, porridge, roasted or boiled cobs), animal feed, and industrial processing. Maize is highly responsive to good management, meaning yields vary enormously between poorly managed and well-managed fields.

## Scientific Name
*Zea mays* L.

## Suitable Climate
- Optimal growing temperature: 18-32°C.
- Requires 500-1200 mm of well-distributed rainfall per season, or supplemental irrigation.
- Sensitive to frost at all growth stages.
- Grows from sea level up to approximately 2,400 m altitude, with different maturity varieties suited to different altitude bands.
- Drought stress during flowering (tasseling and silking) causes the greatest yield loss.

## Suitable Soil
- Deep, well-drained loam or sandy loam soils are ideal.
- Tolerates a wide pH range of 5.5-7.5; optimal pH is 6.0-6.8.
- Poorly drained or waterlogged soils reduce yield significantly.
- Moderate tolerance to low fertility, but responds strongly to fertile soils high in organic matter.

## Soil Preparation
- Plough to a depth of 20-30 cm to break compaction and allow root penetration.
- Harrow to create a fine, level seedbed.
- Incorporate crop residues or organic manure at least 2-3 weeks before planting to allow decomposition.
- On sloped land, use contour ploughing or tied ridges to reduce soil erosion.
- Conduct a soil test where possible before the season to guide lime and fertiliser decisions.

## Planting Season by African Region
| Region | Typical Planting Window | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| West Africa (Sahel/Guinea savanna) | May-June (start of rains) | Second planting possible in some bimodal zones (Aug-Sept) |
| East Africa (highlands) | March-April (long rains); Oct-Nov (short rains) | Depends on bimodal rainfall pattern |
| Southern Africa | Oct-Dec (start of summer rains) | Adjust with onset of rains; avoid late planting after mid-Dec |
| Central Africa | Sept-Nov and March-April in bimodal zones | Two cropping seasons possible in humid zones |
| North Africa | Under irrigation: April-June (summer maize) | Rainfed maize is limited due to arid climate |

## Seed or Seedling Requirements
- Use certified seed of a variety adapted to local altitude, rainfall, and disease pressure (open-pollinated variety or hybrid).
- Seed rate: approximately 20-25 kg/ha for normal spacing; adjust for seed size and germination rate.
- Test germination rate before planting if seed is farm-saved or older than one season.
- Treat seed with a recommended fungicide/insecticide seed dressing where soil pests or seed-borne diseases are a known risk.

## Plant Spacing
- Standard spacing: 75 cm between rows, 25-30 cm between plants (1 plant/station), giving approximately 44,000-53,000 plants/ha.
- For two plants per station, widen in-row spacing to 50 cm.
- Denser spacing can be used for silage maize; wider spacing for drought-prone areas to reduce competition for water.

## Water Requirements
- Total seasonal water requirement: 500-800 mm depending on climate and variety.
- Critical water-sensitive stages: germination, knee-high stage, tasseling/silking, and grain-filling.
- Avoid water stress particularly during the two weeks before and after silking, as this is the most yield-determining period.

## Fertiliser Recommendations
- Basal application at planting: a compound fertiliser supplying phosphorus and nitrogen (e.g., NPK 10-20-10 or similar), guided by local soil test recommendations.
- Topdress with nitrogen (e.g., urea or ammonium nitrate) in two splits: at knee-high stage and at tasseling.
- General guideline in absence of soil test: 60-120 kg N/ha, 30-60 kg P2O5/ha, 30-60 kg K2O/ha, adjusted for yield target and soil fertility.
- Apply organic manure or compost (5-10 tonnes/ha) where available to improve soil structure and nutrient supply over time.
- See `fertilisers/fertiliser_basics.md` for application methods and timing principles.

## Weed Management
- First weeding: 2-3 weeks after emergence, before weeds compete with young maize.
- Second weeding: 5-6 weeks after emergence, before canopy closure.
- Hand hoeing, mechanical cultivation, or approved pre-emergence and post-emergence herbicides can be used.
- Striga (witchweed) is a major parasitic weed problem in some regions; see `weeds/striga.md`.
- Maintain weed-free conditions especially during the first 6 weeks (critical weed-free period).

## Pest Management
- Key pests: fall armyworm, stalk borers, termites, and storage weevils.
- Scout fields weekly, especially during vegetative stages.
- See `pests/fall_armyworm.md` for detailed management guidance.
- Use integrated pest management combining scouting, biological control, cultural practices, and chemical control only when thresholds are exceeded.

## Disease Management
- Key diseases: maize streak virus, grey leaf spot, maize lethal necrosis, common rust, and ear rots.
- Use resistant/tolerant varieties where available.
- Practice crop rotation with non-cereal crops to break disease cycles.
- Avoid dense planting in high-humidity areas to reduce foliar disease pressure.
- See `diseases/maize_streak_virus.md` for details on a major regional disease.

## Growth Stages
1. Germination and emergence (0-10 days after planting).
2. Vegetative stage (V1 to VT): leaf development through tasseling, approximately 10-65 days.
3. Reproductive stage: silking, pollination, blister, milk, dough, and dent stages, approximately 65-100 days.
4. Physiological maturity: black layer formation at the kernel base, marking the end of dry matter accumulation.

## Harvest Indicators
- Husks turn from green to pale brown/straw colour and dry out.
- Kernels form a black layer at the point of attachment (physiological maturity).
- Grain moisture content at physiological maturity is typically 30-35%; harvest for storage when moisture drops to 20-25% in the field, then dry further.
- For fresh/green maize (roasting or boiling), harvest at the milk stage, 18-24 days after silking.

## Post-Harvest Handling
- Harvest promptly at maturity to reduce field losses from birds, rodents, and weathering.
- Dry cobs or shelled grain to 12-13% moisture content before storage to prevent mould and insect damage.
- Shell maize only after adequate drying to reduce grain damage and mould risk.
- Sort out damaged, mouldy, or insect-infested grain before storage.

## Storage
- Store in a cool, dry, well-ventilated area protected from rain and rodents.
- Use hermetic storage bags (e.g., triple-layer bags) or metal silos to protect against storage weevils and grain borer without chemical treatment.
- If using chemical grain protectants, follow label instructions and locally approved products only.
- Monitor stored grain monthly for moisture, insects, and mould.
- See `storage/grain_storage.md` for full storage guidance.

## Expected Yield
- Smallholder rainfed systems with basic management: 1-3 tonnes/ha.
- Improved management with certified seed, adequate fertiliser, and good weed/pest control: 4-8 tonnes/ha.
- Irrigated, high-input commercial systems: 8-12+ tonnes/ha.
- Actual yield depends heavily on rainfall distribution, variety, soil fertility, and pest/disease pressure.

## Common Mistakes
- Planting uncertified, low-quality, or old seed with poor germination.
- Late weeding, allowing weeds to compete during the critical early growth period.
- Applying all nitrogen fertiliser at planting instead of splitting applications, leading to nutrient loss and lodging.
- Delayed harvesting, increasing losses to pests, weather, and grain quality decline.
- Storing grain before it is properly dried, leading to mould and aflatoxin risk.

## Best Farming Practices
- Rotate maize with legumes (beans, soybean, groundnut) to improve soil nitrogen and break pest/disease cycles.
- Use certified seed adapted to local conditions.
- Apply fertiliser based on soil test results where possible.
- Practice timely weeding and pest scouting.
- Intercrop with legumes where land and light conditions allow, to improve total land productivity and soil health.
- Dry grain adequately before storage to reduce aflatoxin contamination risk.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Why are my maize plants yellowing from the bottom leaves upward?**
A: This is commonly a sign of nitrogen deficiency, especially if it occurs during the vegetative stage. Confirm with a soil test if possible and consider a nitrogen topdress application.

**Q: Can I plant maize twice in the same field every year without rotation?**
A: Continuous maize monocropping increases the risk of disease build-up (e.g., grey leaf spot), soil nutrient depletion, and pest pressure such as stalk borers. Rotation with legumes is recommended.

**Q: How do I know if my maize is ready to harvest?**
A: Check for the black layer at the base of kernels and dry, pale brown husks. Grain that is still milky or doughy is not yet at physiological maturity.

**Q: What causes poorly filled cobs with gaps in kernel rows?**
A: This is often caused by poor pollination due to drought stress, heat stress, or nutrient deficiency (especially nitrogen and boron) during the tasseling/silking period.
