# Lablab Bean (Dolichos)

## Overview
Lablab bean, also widely known as Dolichos bean, hyacinth bean, or njahi in parts of East Africa, is a highly versatile legume grown across the continent for food, livestock fodder, and soil improvement. It is a vigorous, climbing (or in some varieties, more bushy) annual or short-lived perennial that produces immature green pods eaten as a vegetable, mature dry beans used much like other pulses, and edible young leaves, while also serving as one of the most valuable forage and green manure legumes available to African smallholders. As a legume, lablab fixes substantial atmospheric nitrogen and produces a large volume of leafy biomass, making it an excellent cover crop, relay crop, or component of agroforestry and mixed crop-livestock systems. It tolerates a wide range of soils, including poor and moderately acidic soils, and is considerably more drought-tolerant once established than most common beans. Because of its vigorous climbing habit, lablab benefits from support, such as stakes, trellises, or being grown alongside a taller companion crop like maize or sorghum. Its main management considerations are providing adequate support for climbing types, timing forage or green manure cutting correctly to maximise nutrient benefit, and managing the hard seed coat that can cause uneven germination if seed is not treated before sowing.

## Scientific Name
*Lablab purpureus* (L.) Sweet, synonym *Dolichos lablab* L., family Fabaceae.

## Suitable Climate
- Optimal growing temperature: approximately 18-30°C; the crop tolerates a wide temperature range and grows from lowland tropical to cooler highland conditions.
- Grows well with approximately 400-700 mm of rainfall per season and is notably drought-tolerant once its deep root system is established.
- Sensitive to frost, though hardier to cool conditions than many tropical legumes.
- Adapted to a wide altitude range, from sea level to approximately 2,000 m.
- Tolerates brief periods of waterlogging better than most grain legumes, though prolonged waterlogging still reduces vigour and increases root disease risk.

## Suitable Soil
- Grows on a wide range of soil types, from sandy to clay soils, including soils considered too poor for many other legumes.
- Tolerates a pH range of approximately 4.5-7.5, including moderately acidic soils where many legumes perform poorly.
- Shows moderate tolerance of salinity compared with many legumes.
- Does not perform well under prolonged waterlogging, although it tolerates occasional wet conditions better than most beans.

## Soil Preparation
- A moderate, well-prepared seedbed is sufficient; heavy tillage is not required, and minimum tillage can be used, particularly when lablab is grown as a cover crop or relay crop.
- Clear residues and weeds ahead of planting to reduce early competition with the relatively slow initial growth phase.
- Where lablab is grown as a green manure or forage crop, broadcasting onto a lightly cultivated surface is a common, low-input establishment method.
- Incorporate organic matter on very poor soils to support establishment, although the crop itself is often used specifically to build soil fertility for a following crop.

## Planting Season by African Region
| Region | Typical Planting Window | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| East Africa (Kenya, Uganda, Tanzania - major production and forage use) | Mar-May and Oct-Dec (bimodal rains) | Widely grown as njahi/dolichos for grain and as forage/cover crop |
| West Africa | Jun-Aug (onset of rains) | Often grown as forage or cover crop alongside cereals |
| Southern Africa | Nov-Jan (summer rains) | Commonly used as a forage legume and soil-improving relay crop |
| Central Africa | Bimodal windows (Sept-Nov and Mar-Apr) | Suited to intercropping and agroforestry systems |
| North Africa | Limited commercial cultivation; where grown, under irrigation in warm months | Confirm local variety adaptation before large-scale planting |

Lablab is also frequently planted as a relay or cover crop after a main food crop is harvested, taking advantage of residual soil moisture, rather than strictly following a single seasonal window.

## Seed or Seedling Requirements
- Lablab is direct-seeded; transplanting is not standard practice.
- Seed rate: approximately 20-40 kg/ha for sole cropping aimed at grain or vegetable pod production; lower rates of approximately 15-20 kg/ha are used when broadcast as a cover crop or forage stand.
- The seed coat is hard and can cause slow, uneven germination; scarifying (lightly nicking or abrading) the seed coat, or soaking seed briefly before sowing, improves and speeds germination.
- Sow at a depth of approximately 3-5 cm into moist soil.
- Inoculating seed with appropriate rhizobia can improve nodulation and nitrogen fixation where lablab, or compatible legumes, has not been grown recently on the site.

## Plant Spacing
- Climbing types intended for grain or vegetable pod production and grown with support: approximately 60-90 cm between rows and 20-30 cm between plants.
- Where grown along a support crop such as maize or sorghum, plant lablab within or between the cereal rows at a reduced density appropriate to intercropping.
- Bush-type or more compact varieties can be planted more closely than climbing types.
- For forage or green manure/cover crop use, broadcast or drill densely to maximise ground cover and biomass rather than following wide row spacing.

## Water Requirements
- Total seasonal water requirement: approximately 400-700 mm depending on climate, variety, and end use.
- The crop is deep-rooted and notably drought-tolerant once established, making it valuable in variable-rainfall environments.
- The most water-sensitive stages are establishment and flowering; moisture stress at these stages reduces vigour and pod or forage yield, though the crop typically recovers well from short dry spells.
- Excessive waterlogging, particularly at establishment, increases the risk of root rot and should be avoided.

## Fertiliser Recommendations
- As a legume, lablab fixes substantial atmospheric nitrogen and generally requires little or no nitrogen fertiliser.
- A modest phosphorus application can support root establishment and nodulation on deficient soils.
- Because lablab is frequently grown specifically to improve soil fertility for a following crop, heavy fertiliser input on the lablab crop itself is often unnecessary and works against its role as a low-cost soil-building legume.
- Where soils are known to be severely deficient in specific nutrients, apply based on a soil test; see `soil/soil_testing_and_fertility.md` and `fertilisers/fertiliser_basics.md`.
- Organic manure or compost can be beneficial on very poor soils at establishment but is not usually essential given the crop's own soil-improving role.

## Weed Management
- Early weeding is important, as young lablab seedlings are relatively slow to establish and can be outcompeted by fast-growing weeds.
- A first weeding at approximately 2-3 weeks after emergence is usually sufficient to give the crop a competitive advantage.
- Once established, lablab's vigorous, spreading vine growth forms a dense canopy that suppresses most later-emerging weeds very effectively, making it a useful "smother crop" in rotations.
- See `weeds/general_weed_management.md` for general integrated weed control principles.

## Pest Management
- **Pod borers**: caterpillars that bore into developing pods, reducing seed quality and yield; monitor from flowering onward and remove and destroy visibly infested pods.
- **Aphids**: cluster on shoot tips and pods, reducing vigour and potentially transmitting viruses; see `pests/aphids.md`.
- **Bean fly**: can attack young seedlings at the base of the stem, particularly under stress; ensure good early growing conditions to help seedlings outgrow damage.
- **Storage bruchids**: attack dried seed in storage, similar to other grain legumes, and require prompt drying and protected storage to control.
- Use integrated pest management, combining regular scouting with cultural controls, reserving chemical control for confirmed, significant infestations and confirming any product with a qualified agricultural extension officer.

## Disease Management
- **Leaf spot diseases** (including *Cercospora* and *Phaeoisariopsis* species): cause brown or dark leaf spotting, generally more significant under humid, high-rainfall conditions.
- **Bacterial blight**: can cause water-soaked lesions on leaves and pods in wet conditions.
- **Root rots**: more likely on waterlogged or poorly drained soils, particularly at establishment.
- Viral mosaic diseases occur but are generally less severe in lablab than in some other legumes.
- Manage disease risk through adequate spacing for airflow, crop rotation, avoidance of waterlogged sites, and removal of severely infected plant material; consult a qualified agricultural extension officer for unusual or severe outbreaks.

## Growth Stages
1. Germination and emergence (approximately 5-10 days after planting, longer if seed is not scarified).
2. Vigorous vegetative and vining growth, requiring support for climbing types (approximately 20-60 days).
3. Flowering, producing distinctive purple or white flower spikes (approximately 45-90 days after planting).
4. Pod development and maturity: for fresh pod harvest, pods are picked young and tender; for dry grain, the crop is left to mature fully, typically reaching maturity at 120-200 days depending on variety, with some long-season or perennial-type landraces taking considerably longer.
5. For forage or green manure use, the crop is managed by repeated cutting at flowering or early podding rather than being taken through to full seed maturity.

## Harvest Indicators
- For fresh vegetable use, pick pods while young, tender, and still flexible, before the seeds inside harden.
- For dry grain, wait until pods have dried, turned brown, and rattle when shaken, indicating the seed inside has hardened.
- For forage, cut at approximately 50% flowering to balance biomass yield with feed quality, as nutritive value declines once the crop becomes more woody and fibrous.
- For green manure or cover crop use, slash and incorporate the crop at flowering or early podding to capture the maximum nitrogen and biomass benefit before stems become woody and slow to decompose.

## Post-Harvest Handling
- For dry grain, thresh only after pods are fully dry, then winnow to remove pod husks and debris, similar to handling for cowpea and common bean.
- Sort out discoloured, shrivelled, or insect-damaged seed before storage.
- For fresh pods, handle gently to avoid bruising and market promptly, as they are more perishable than dry grain.
- For forage, wilt cut material briefly before feeding or ensiling where appropriate, following standard forage-handling practice; see `livestock/goats_sheep_and_cattle.md` for feeding guidance.

## Storage
- Store well-dried grain in a cool, dry, well-ventilated place protected from moisture, rodents, and insects, as with other grain legumes.
- Storage bruchids can attack dried lablab seed; hermetic (airtight) storage is effective at controlling them without chemicals.
- Seed retained for the next season's planting should be stored separately, kept dry, and ideally scarified again shortly before the next sowing to ensure good germination.
- See `storage/grain_storage.md` for further guidance on grain storage principles.

## Expected Yield
- Dry grain: approximately 0.5-1.5 tonnes/ha under smallholder rainfed management, depending on variety and season.
- Fresh green pods: approximately 3-6 tonnes/ha where grown primarily as a vegetable crop.
- Forage/fodder: approximately 4-8 tonnes/ha of dry matter per season under good management, making it one of the more productive forage legumes available to smallholders.
- Green manure biomass contribution can be substantial, meaningfully improving soil organic matter and nitrogen supply for a following crop.

## Common Mistakes
- Failing to provide stakes, a trellis, or a support crop for climbing varieties, resulting in tangled, poorly ventilated growth with higher disease risk and difficult harvesting.
- Delaying incorporation of the crop as green manure until stems become woody, which slows decomposition and reduces the nitrogen benefit to the following crop.
- Sowing untreated, hard-coated seed without scarifying or soaking it first, leading to slow and uneven germination.
- Overgrazing or cutting forage stands too low or too frequently, weakening regrowth potential and shortening the productive life of the stand.

## Best Farming Practices
- Grow climbing types alongside a support crop such as maize or sorghum, or provide stakes or a trellis, to keep pods and leaves off the ground and improve airflow.
- Use lablab deliberately within rotations to build soil fertility ahead of a nutrient-demanding crop, timing incorporation at flowering for the best nitrogen benefit.
- Combine dual-purpose use where practical, taking a partial forage cut before allowing remaining growth to mature for grain.
- Scarify or soak seed before sowing to ensure even, timely germination.
- Integrate lablab into mixed crop-livestock systems as a high-value forage legume, coordinating cutting schedules with livestock feeding needs; see `livestock/goats_sheep_and_cattle.md`.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Do I need to provide support for my lablab plants?**
A: For climbing varieties, yes. Lablab is a vigorous vine that grows much better, stays cleaner, and suffers less disease when grown up stakes, a trellis, or a tall companion crop such as maize or sorghum, rather than sprawling on the ground.

**Q: My lablab seed germinated slowly and unevenly. Why?**
A: Lablab seed has a hard seed coat that can slow and stagger germination. Scarifying the seed coat lightly or soaking seed for a few hours before sowing improves both the speed and uniformity of germination.

**Q: When should I cut lablab if I am using it as green manure?**
A: Cut and incorporate the crop at flowering or early pod set. At this stage the plant has accumulated substantial nitrogen-rich biomass but has not yet become woody, so it breaks down more readily in the soil and delivers the greatest fertility benefit to the following crop.

**Q: Can I use lablab for both animal feed and grain from the same planting?**
A: Yes, in many systems a partial forage cut is taken at around 50% flowering, after which the plant is allowed to regrow and set pods for grain harvest later, giving a dual-purpose return, provided the cutting is not so severe that it prevents adequate regrowth.

**Q: Does lablab need much fertiliser to grow well?**
A: Generally no. As a legume it fixes much of its own nitrogen and is often deliberately used to improve soil fertility for a following crop. A modest phosphorus application can help on very poor soils, but heavy fertiliser use is usually unnecessary.

**Q: Is lablab suitable for drought-prone areas?**
A: Yes. Once established, its deep root system makes it considerably more drought-tolerant than many common beans, and it is a widely used option for building resilience into mixed cropping and forage systems in variable-rainfall environments.
