# Cotton

## Overview
Cotton is a major fibre and cash crop across Africa, grown for the lint (fibre) that is ginned from seed cotton and spun into textiles, and for cottonseed, which yields cooking oil and livestock feed cake. It is central to the rural economy in West and Central Africa (the "Cotton Four" of Benin, Burkina Faso, Mali, and Chad, plus Côte d'Ivoire and Cameroon), East Africa (Tanzania, Uganda), and Southern Africa (Zambia, Zimbabwe, Mozambique). Cotton is an annual (grown as such by smallholders) warm-season crop, typically maturing in about 150-180 days, and it is often produced under contract or outgrower schemes linked to ginneries. Because cotton is sold by both weight and fibre quality, clean picking free of contamination, correct grading, and careful handling directly affect the price farmers receive. Cotton is also pest-intensive, so integrated pest management is essential to control costs, protect health and the environment, and sustain yields. For grading and quality see `marketplace/product_grading_and_quality.md`, and for value addition see `pricing/price_negotiation_and_value_addition.md`.

## Scientific Name
*Gossypium hirsutum* L. (upland cotton, the most widely grown), family Malvaceae. Some regions also grow *Gossypium barbadense* (extra-long-staple/Egyptian-type cotton).

## Suitable Climate
- Cotton is a warm-season crop needing temperatures of approximately 21-35°C and a long, frost-free growing season of about 150-180 days.
- It requires moderate, well-distributed rainfall of roughly 500-1,200 mm during growth, with a drier period at maturity for clean boll opening.
- It grows best at low to mid altitudes with abundant sunshine.
- Cotton tolerates heat well but is sensitive to prolonged waterlogging and to frost.
- Rain or high humidity during boll opening and picking stains and weathers the lint, reducing grade; a dry harvest period is important.
- Drought during flowering and boll filling sharply cuts yield; see `weather/drought_and_flood_management.md`.

## Suitable Soil
- Deep, well-drained, medium-textured soils (sandy loams to clay loams) with good moisture retention are ideal.
- A pH range of approximately 5.8-8.0 is tolerated, with near-neutral soils preferred.
- Cotton has a deep taproot and benefits from deep soils that store moisture for the long season.
- Avoid waterlogged, shallow, or highly saline soils, though cotton is more salt-tolerant than many crops.
- Test soil to guide fertiliser and correct constraints; see `soil/soil_testing_and_fertility.md`.

## Soil Preparation
- Prepare a deep, well-tilled, weed-free seedbed with a fine, level tilth for uniform planting depth and emergence.
- Deep ploughing or ripping breaks compaction and helps the taproot access stored moisture.
- Incorporate crop residues and well-decomposed organic matter to improve structure and water retention; see `fertilisers/organic_fertilisers.md`.
- On slopes and in erosion-prone areas, use contour ridging or tied ridges to conserve moisture and reduce runoff; see `soil/soil_conservation_and_erosion.md`.
- In dryland areas, tied ridges or basins help capture rainfall during the season.

## Planting Season by African Region
| Region | Typical Planting Window | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| West Africa (Mali, Burkina Faso, Benin) | May-July (onset of rains) | Main rainfed cotton belt; plant with first reliable rains |
| Central Africa (Cameroon, Chad, CAR) | May-July | Rainfed; avoid late planting that pushes harvest into rains |
| East Africa (Tanzania, Uganda) | Nov-Dec and March-May | Depends on unimodal or bimodal rainfall zone |
| Southern Africa (Zambia, Zimbabwe, Mozambique) | Nov-Dec (start of rains) | Plant early to finish before season end |
| Irrigated schemes (e.g. Sudan, Egypt) | Feb-April | Long season managed under irrigation |

## Seed or Seedling Requirements
- Cotton is direct-seeded; use certified, treated seed of a recommended variety adapted to local conditions and the ginnery's fibre requirements.
- Delinted, graded, and dressed seed improves germination and early protection against soil-borne pests and diseases.
- Seed rate is approximately 15-30 kg/ha depending on seed size, spacing, and whether sowing is by hand or planter.
- Sow 2-4 seeds per hole (thinning later to the strongest plant) or use a planter for even stands at the correct depth of about 3-5 cm.
- Confirm seed source and any seed treatment products with a qualified agricultural extension officer or the outgrower scheme; some regions use conventional and others biotech (insect-resistant) varieties subject to national regulation; see `regulations/agricultural_regulations_overview.md`.

## Plant Spacing
- Common spacing is approximately 75-100 cm between rows and 20-30 cm within rows, giving roughly 40,000-70,000 plants per hectare depending on variety and rainfall.
- Closer within-row spacing suits lower-rainfall areas and shorter varieties; wider spacing suits vigorous varieties and higher rainfall.
- Thin to one healthy plant per stand two to three weeks after emergence to achieve the target population.
- Uniform, adequate plant stands help canopy closure, weed suppression, and even boll development.
- Avoid overly dense stands, which encourage excessive vegetative growth, poor boll set, and higher pest and disease pressure.

## Water Requirements
- Total seasonal water requirement is approximately 700-1,300 mm depending on climate, variety, and season length.
- Critical water-sensitive stages are flowering (squaring and bloom) and boll development; stress at these stages causes shedding of squares and bolls and small bolls.
- Early growth needs adequate but not excessive moisture; late-season moisture stress can be tolerated and aids maturity.
- A drier period at boll opening and picking is important for clean, high-grade lint.
- Under irrigation, schedule to meet peak demand at flowering and boll fill while avoiding waterlogging; see `irrigation/irrigation_scheduling.md`.

## Fertiliser Recommendations
- Base fertiliser on soil analysis and expected yield; see `soil/soil_testing_and_fertility.md` and `fertilisers/fertiliser_basics.md`.
- Cotton responds strongly to nitrogen, but excess nitrogen causes rank vegetative growth, delayed maturity, boll shedding, and higher pest pressure, so match rates to the crop.
- Apply phosphorus and any needed potassium at or before planting; split nitrogen between early growth and the start of flowering.
- Potassium is important for boll filling and fibre quality, especially on potassium-deficient soils.
- Incorporate organic matter and, where deficient, correct micronutrients such as boron and zinc, which affect boll set; see `fertilisers/nutrient_deficiencies_guide.md`.
- Avoid late, heavy nitrogen, which delays maturity and increases the harvest running into the wet season.

## Weed Management
- Cotton is a poor early competitor; keep it weed-free for the first six to eight weeks, which is the most critical period.
- First weeding around 2-3 weeks after emergence, followed by further weedings before canopy closure.
- Combine timely hand or hoe weeding, inter-row cultivation, and, where used, locally registered pre- or post-emergence herbicides.
- Once the canopy closes, cotton shades out many later weeds, reducing weeding needs.
- Confirm registered herbicides and follow label safety directions; see `weeds/general_weed_management.md` and `regulations/pesticide_safety_and_handling.md`.

## Pest Management
- Cotton is attacked by many pests, including bollworms (American/African bollworm and spiny/pink bollworms), sucking pests (aphids, jassids/leafhoppers, whiteflies, cotton stainers), thrips, and mites; see `pests/aphids.md`, `pests/thrips.md`, and `pests/whitefly.md`.
- Bollworms feed inside bolls and are the most damaging; sucking pests reduce vigour and, in the case of stainers and whiteflies, downgrade lint through staining and sticky honeydew.
- Integrated pest management is essential: scout weekly, use economic thresholds, conserve natural enemies, plant on time to escape peak pest pressure, and destroy crop residues after harvest to break pest cycles.
- Rotate insecticide active-ingredient classes to slow resistance, and spray only when scouting exceeds thresholds rather than on a fixed calendar.
- Where insect-resistant (biotech) varieties are grown, follow refuge and stewardship rules to protect their durability.
- Confirm recommended active-ingredient classes and locally registered products with a qualified agricultural extension officer, and observe all safety precautions and pre-harvest intervals; cotton is a heavily sprayed crop, so protective equipment and safe handling are vital; see `regulations/pesticide_safety_and_handling.md`.

## Disease Management
- Key diseases include bacterial blight (angular leaf spot and boll rot), fusarium and verticillium wilts (soil-borne), and seedling damping-off, plus boll rots in wet conditions.
- Use certified, treated seed of resistant or tolerant varieties as the primary defence.
- Practise crop rotation with non-host crops (such as cereals or legumes) to reduce soil-borne wilts and bacterial blight carry-over.
- Destroy and remove infected crop residues after harvest, and avoid working in the field when foliage is wet to limit bacterial spread.
- Ensure good drainage and balanced nutrition to reduce stress and boll rots.
- For severe or unfamiliar outbreaks, consult a qualified agricultural extension officer.

## Growth Stages
1. Germination and emergence (0-10 days after sowing).
2. Seedling and early vegetative growth: establishment of leaves and the taproot (approximately 10-40 days).
3. Squaring: flower buds ("squares") form (approximately 35-60 days).
4. Flowering (bloom): creamy-white flowers open and turn pink/red before falling; pollination sets bolls (approximately 55-100 days).
5. Boll development and filling: bolls enlarge and fibre and seed develop (approximately 80-140 days).
6. Boll opening and maturity: mature bolls split open to reveal fluffy lint, typically from about 140-180 days depending on variety and conditions.

## Harvest Indicators
- Harvest (pick) seed cotton when bolls have fully opened and the lint is fluffy, dry, and white; pick in dry weather.
- Multiple picking rounds are needed as bolls open over several weeks; pick the lower, earlier-opening bolls first.
- Do not pick unopened or partly open bolls, and avoid picking when lint is wet with dew or rain, which lowers grade and encourages mould.
- Timely picking of clean, fully open lint before weathering, staining, or contamination protects grade and price.

## Post-Harvest Handling
- Keep seed cotton clean and free of contamination: leaves, trash, soil, string, hair, and especially plastic and polypropylene fibres, which are serious contaminants that downgrade or reject lint.
- Use clean cotton or natural-fibre bags and containers for picking and storage, never synthetic sacks that shed fibres, and keep picked cotton away from oil, grease, and coloured materials.
- Sort or grade seed cotton by cleanliness and colour; separate stained, weathered, or trash-laden cotton from clean white cotton, as ginneries pay by grade.
- Keep seed cotton dry before delivery to the ginnery; damp cotton loses weight quality and can heat and discolour.
- Deliver promptly to the ginnery, where lint is separated from seed; clean handling protects fibre grade and value; see `marketplace/product_grading_and_quality.md`.

## Storage
- Store seed cotton in a clean, dry, well-ventilated space protected from rain, damp, and rodents, on raised platforms and away from contaminants.
- Ensure cotton is dry before storage; damp cotton heats, discolours, loses grade, and can even self-heat.
- Avoid mixing grades; keep clean white cotton separate from stained or trashy cotton.
- Protect from fire risk and keep away from fuels, oils, and coloured or synthetic materials.
- For cottonseed retained for oil, feed, or planting, dry and store it in cool, dry, pest-free conditions; see `storage/grain_storage.md`.

## Expected Yield
- Smallholder rainfed yields of seed cotton commonly range from approximately 600-1,500 kg per hectare.
- Well-managed rainfed or irrigated crops with good varieties, nutrition, and pest control can yield approximately 2,000-3,500 kg of seed cotton per hectare or more.
- Ginning outturn (lint fraction) is typically around 35-42% of seed cotton weight, the remainder being seed.
- Actual yield depends on variety, rainfall, soil fertility, and especially the effectiveness of pest and weed management.

## Common Mistakes
- Contaminating seed cotton with plastic, synthetic-bag fibres, trash, or damp, which sharply downgrades lint.
- Calendar spraying instead of scouting, which wastes money, harms beneficial insects, and drives insecticide resistance.
- Applying excess nitrogen, causing rank growth, boll shedding, delayed maturity, and higher pest pressure.
- Late planting that pushes boll opening and picking into wet weather, staining lint.
- Weeding too late, allowing weeds to suppress the poor-competing young crop.
- Failing to destroy crop residues after harvest, allowing bollworms and diseases to carry over.

## Best Farming Practices
- Plant certified, recommended varieties on time into a clean, well-prepared seedbed at the correct population.
- Scout weekly and follow IPM with economic thresholds, natural-enemy conservation, and rotation of insecticide classes; see `sustainability/sustainable_farming_practices.md`.
- Weed early to protect the critical first six to eight weeks, then let the canopy suppress later weeds.
- Match nitrogen to the crop and supply phosphorus and potassium based on soil analysis.
- Pick clean, dry, fully open cotton in rounds, keep it free of contamination, and grade before delivery.
- Destroy crop residues after harvest and rotate with non-host crops to break pest and disease cycles.
- Keep records and use group marketing or contract terms to strengthen prices; see `pricing/price_negotiation_and_value_addition.md`.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Why is my cotton being downgraded even though it looks white?**
A: The most common cause is contamination with foreign matter, especially plastic and polypropylene fibres from synthetic sacks, plus trash, soil, and staining. Use only clean cotton or natural-fibre bags, keep cotton off the ground and away from synthetics, and sort by cleanliness. See `marketplace/product_grading_and_quality.md`.

**Q: When should I spray for bollworms?**
A: Spray based on scouting and economic thresholds, not a fixed calendar. Scout weekly, count pests and beneficial insects, and treat only when pest numbers exceed the threshold. Rotate insecticide classes to slow resistance and always use registered products with protective equipment.

**Q: My cotton grew very tall and leafy but set few bolls. Why?**
A: This is usually caused by too much nitrogen and/or excess moisture, which promote vegetative growth at the expense of bolls (rank growth). Match nitrogen to the crop's needs based on soil analysis, avoid late heavy nitrogen, and maintain the correct plant population.

**Q: How much rain does cotton need and when?**
A: Cotton needs about 500-1,200 mm during growth, with the most critical demand at flowering and boll filling. It benefits from a drier period at boll opening and picking. Both drought at flowering and rain at harvest reduce yield and grade.

**Q: Should I grow insect-resistant (biotech) cotton?**
A: Where such varieties are legally available and part of your scheme, they can reduce bollworm sprays, but you must follow refuge planting and stewardship rules to keep them effective. Availability depends on national regulation; confirm with your extension officer and outgrower scheme.

**Q: Can I reuse seed from my harvest for planting?**
A: For most improved and especially hybrid or biotech varieties, saving seed is not recommended (and may not be permitted), as performance and any built-in traits are not reliably passed on. Use certified, treated seed each season from an approved source.

**Q: Why does rain during picking matter so much?**
A: Rain and high humidity at boll opening weather and stain the exposed lint, encourage mould, and lower grade and price. Plant on time so bolls open in a drier period, and pick clean, dry cotton promptly.

**Q: What can I do with the cottonseed after ginning?**
A: Cottonseed is valuable: it is pressed for cooking oil, and the remaining cake is a protein-rich livestock feed. Some seed is retained (treated) for planting. Selling or using cottonseed and cake is an important source of additional value. See `pricing/price_negotiation_and_value_addition.md`.
