# Papaya / Pawpaw

## Overview
Papaya, widely known as pawpaw across much of Africa, is a fast-growing, short-lived fruit crop that begins bearing within the first year of planting, making it one of the quickest fruit enterprises available to smallholders. A single plant can fruit almost continuously for two to four years before productivity declines. Papaya suits homestead plots, market gardens, and intercropping systems, and it provides both a cash crop and a highly nutritious household food rich in vitamins A and C. The fruit sells fresh for eating; unripe green fruit is used as a vegetable and as a source of the enzyme papain. Because the plant grows quickly and yields early, it offers rapid returns and quick recovery after drought or disease losses. The main challenges are its extreme sensitivity to waterlogging and root/collar rot, the complication of separate male and female (and hermaphrodite) plants affecting fruit set, high vulnerability to viral diseases such as papaya ringspot virus, and the delicacy of the ripe fruit, which bruises and spoils rapidly. Success depends on well-drained soil, good-quality seed of known sex expression, disciplined disease and pest management, and careful post-harvest handling.

## Scientific Name
*Carica papaya* L. (family Caricaceae).

## Suitable Climate
- Papaya is a warm-climate crop, best at 21-33°C. Growth slows below 15°C, and frost kills the plant.
- It grows from sea level to about 1,600 m in the tropics; at higher, cooler altitudes fruit ripens slowly and quality declines.
- Rainfall of 1,000-2,000 mm well distributed is ideal, but consistent soil moisture with excellent drainage matters more than total rainfall. Dry spells require irrigation.
- Papaya is very sensitive to strong wind (the tall, top-heavy plant snaps or lodges) and to waterlogging; both are common causes of plant loss.
- High humidity favours fungal diseases such as anthracnose and powdery mildew.

## Suitable Soil
- Papaya demands deep, light, freely draining soils; sandy loam and loam are ideal. Even short periods of waterlogging cause root and collar rot and rapid plant death.
- Optimal pH is 6.0-6.5. It tolerates a moderate range but not saline or sodic soils.
- Rich organic matter improves growth and water-holding while maintaining drainage.
- Because the plant has a soft stem and shallow roots, soil that drains freely and does not crust is essential. See `soil/soil_types_africa.md`.

## Soil Preparation
- Select a well-drained, sheltered site; avoid low, water-collecting ground and frost pockets.
- Prepare planting holes about 45 x 45 x 45 cm, mixing topsoil with 5-10 kg well-decomposed compost or manure and a phosphorus source.
- On heavy or wet soils, plant on raised ridges or mounds 30-40 cm high to prevent waterlogging around the stem base.
- Establish windbreaks on exposed sites. On slopes, plant on the contour with conservation measures; see `soil/soil_conservation_and_erosion.md`.

## Planting Season by African Region

## Seed or Seedling Requirements
- Use fresh, good-quality seed from a reputable source or from high-quality ripe fruit of a known variety. Certified seed of improved varieties gives better uniformity and disease tolerance.
- Sex expression matters: papaya plants may be male, female, or hermaphrodite. Male plants do not fruit. Growers commonly raise several seedlings per hole and remove excess males once flowering reveals sex, keeping females (which need a pollinator nearby) or self-fruitful hermaphrodites.
- "Gynodioecious" varieties producing mainly female and hermaphrodite plants simplify management and are preferred where available.
- Raise seedlings in nursery bags or trays for 4-6 weeks until 15-20 cm tall, then transplant carefully; papaya dislikes root disturbance, so use containerised seedlings.
- Seed rate is low; a small quantity raises many seedlings. Harden seedlings before transplanting.

## Plant Spacing
- Although grown as a tree-like crop, papaya is spaced like a tall annual because of its short life and continuous fruiting.
- Standard spacing: 2 x 2 m to 2.5 x 2.5 m (about 1,600-2,500 plants/ha).
- Closer spacing (1.8 x 1.8 m) increases early yield per hectare but raises disease risk and shading; wider spacing improves airflow and fruit size.
- Where sex is unknown, plant two to three seedlings per station and thin to one productive female or hermaphrodite after flowering, keeping about one male per 20-25 females if using dioecious types requiring pollination.

## Water Requirements
- Papaya needs consistent moisture for continuous flowering and fruiting but must never be waterlogged; the balance is critical.
- Water regularly through dry spells; water stress causes flower and fruit drop and small fruit, while excess water causes root and collar rot.
- Apply water to the root zone, keeping the stem base relatively dry to prevent collar rot. Basin or drip irrigation around, but not against, the stem is ideal.
- Mulching conserves moisture; keep mulch away from the stem base. See `irrigation/irrigation_methods.md` and `irrigation/irrigation_scheduling.md`.

## Fertiliser Recommendations
- Papaya is a fast, heavy feeder because it grows and fruits continuously. Feed little and often.
- Apply nitrogen, phosphorus, and potassium in monthly or six-weekly small doses once established, increasing potassium as fruiting intensifies to support fruit size and sweetness.
- Incorporate generous compost or well-decomposed manure at planting and top up periodically to maintain organic matter.
- Watch for boron deficiency (causes lumpy, distorted fruit and gummy patches) and zinc/magnesium deficiencies; correct with targeted supply. See `fertilisers/nutrient_deficiencies_guide.md` and `fertilisers/organic_fertilisers.md`.
- Guide rates with a soil test where possible; see `soil/soil_testing_and_fertility.md` and `fertilisers/fertiliser_basics.md`.

## Weed Management
- Keep a weed-free circle around each plant during establishment to reduce competition; papaya's shallow roots compete poorly.
- Mulch around plants to suppress weeds and conserve moisture, keeping mulch clear of the stem base.
- Weed shallowly by hand or hoe; avoid deep cultivation that damages shallow roots.
- A low cover crop or intercrop between rows in early growth protects soil. See `weeds/general_weed_management.md`.

## Pest Management
- Fruit flies attack ripening fruit; manage with sanitation, baits, traps, and harvesting at the colour-break stage. See `pests/fruit_flies.md`.
- Papaya mealybug can heavily infest leaves, stems, and fruit, causing distortion and sooty mould; conserve and, where available, use biological control agents, and remove heavily infested material.
- Aphids and whiteflies are important mainly as virus vectors; monitor and manage to slow virus spread. See `pests/aphids.md` and `pests/whitefly.md`.
- Red spider mites cause leaf stippling in hot, dry conditions.
- Follow integrated pest management: scout regularly, conserve natural enemies, keep the crop vigorous, and use only locally registered products confirmed with a qualified agricultural extension officer, following `regulations/pesticide_safety_and_handling.md`.

## Disease Management
- Papaya ringspot virus (spread by aphids) is the most damaging disease worldwide, causing ringspots on fruit, mosaic and distortion of leaves, and severe yield loss. Manage with tolerant varieties where available, rogueing (removing) infected plants early, controlling aphids, and isolating new plantings from old infected ones.
- Collar rot and root rot (*Phytophthora*, *Pythium*) kill plants rapidly in wet soil; prevent with excellent drainage, mounding, and keeping the stem base dry.
- Anthracnose causes sunken spots on ripening fruit developing in storage; manage with sanitation, airflow, and careful post-harvest handling.
- Powdery mildew and damping-off in the nursery are managed with spacing, airflow, and clean nursery practice.
- Prevention centres on drainage, clean planting material, vector control, and sanitation. Confirm any product with an extension officer.

## Growth Stages
Papaya is fast-cycling; the "establishment to bearing" sequence occurs within the first year:
1. **Nursery (Weeks 0-6):** Seed germinates and seedlings grow to transplant size.
2. **Establishment (Months 0-3 after transplant):** Rapid vegetative growth; the single stem thickens and gains height.
3. **Flowering and sex determination (about Months 3-5):** First flowers appear, revealing plant sex; remove excess males.
4. **Fruit set and development (Months 5-9):** Fruit forms on the stem below the growing crown and enlarges over several months.
5. **Continuous bearing (Months 9 onward, for 2-4 years):** The plant flowers and fruits continuously up the stem while growing taller, giving a near-continuous harvest.
6. **Decline:** After two to four years plants become too tall to harvest and yields fall; plantings are then replaced, often in a rolling programme so new plants are always coming into bearing.

## Harvest Indicators
- Fruit is ready at the colour-break stage, when the skin begins to change from green to yellow (usually one to two yellow streaks) for market and transport, or fuller yellow for immediate local ripe sale.
- Latex in the fruit turns watery rather than milky as maturity approaches.
- For papain (enzyme) production, green mature fruit is tapped for latex before ripening.
- Harvest by cutting or twisting the fruit gently with a short stalk; support the fruit to prevent it dropping and bruising.

## Post-Harvest Handling
- Papaya is very perishable and bruises easily; handle with great care and keep out of the sun in the field.
- Harvest at colour-break for markets needing shelf life; fully ripe fruit must be sold or consumed within a couple of days.
- Wash gently and allow surface latex to dry; sort and grade by size, ripeness, and freedom from blemish, fruit fly damage, and disease. See `marketplace/product_grading_and_quality.md`.
- Pack in single layers in padded, ventilated crates to prevent compression bruising. See `harvesting/harvesting_best_practices.md`.

## Storage
- Ripe papaya lasts only a few days at ambient tropical temperature. Cool storage around 10-13°C at 85-90% relative humidity extends life to one to three weeks for colour-break fruit.
- Do not store below about 10°C; papaya suffers chilling injury (pitting, blotchy skin, failure to ripen).
- Papaya is climacteric and ripens with ethylene; controlled warm ripening gives even market fruit.
- Where cold storage is unavailable, sell quickly, ripen in small batches, or process surplus into juice, dried fruit, jam, or (from green fruit) papain and vegetable products. See `storage/perishable_storage.md`.

## Expected Yield
- A single well-managed plant can produce 30-60 fruit per year, roughly 20-60 kg, over its productive life.
- Smallholder plantings: 20-40 tonnes/ha per year.
- Well-managed plantings with good varieties, nutrition, and disease control: 40-80 tonnes/ha per year at peak, though ringspot virus commonly cuts this sharply.
- Yields decline in the third and fourth years, which is why rolling replanting is used.

## Common Mistakes
- Planting on heavy or poorly drained soil, causing collar and root rot and sudden plant death.
- Keeping too many male plants, which take space and water but bear no fruit.
- Ignoring aphids and infected plants, letting papaya ringspot virus spread through the plot.
- Piling mulch or soil against the stem base, encouraging collar rot.
- Over- or under-watering, causing root rot or heavy fruit and flower drop.
- Harvesting too ripe for transport, or handling roughly, leading to heavy bruising and spoilage.

## Best Farming Practices
- Plant good-quality seed of tolerant, gynodioecious varieties on well-drained soil or mounds, in sheltered sites.
- Manage plant sex early, keeping productive females/hermaphrodites and enough pollinators.
- Control aphids and rogue virus-infected plants promptly to slow ringspot spread.
- Feed little and often, keep the stem base dry, and manage water for steady moisture without waterlogging.
- Replant on a rolling schedule so young plants continually replace ageing ones, and handle fruit gently at harvest. See `sustainability/sustainable_farming_practices.md` and `weather/climate_smart_agriculture.md`.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: My papaya plant is big and healthy but produces no fruit. Why?**
A: It is probably a male plant, which flowers but does not set fruit (male flowers hang on long, thin stalks). Remove excess males and keep female or hermaphrodite plants, ensuring a pollinator is nearby if using female-only types.

**Q: My papaya suddenly wilted and fell over at the base. What happened?**
A: This is typically collar or root rot from waterlogged soil or water and mulch against the stem base. Improve drainage, plant on mounds, keep the stem base dry, and never let water stand around the plant.

**Q: My papaya leaves are mottled and distorted and the fruit has rings. Is it curable?**
A: This is papaya ringspot virus, spread by aphids. There is no cure. Remove and destroy infected plants early, control aphids, plant tolerant varieties, and keep new plantings away from old infected ones.

**Q: How soon will I get fruit from papaya?**
A: Papaya is fast: plants usually flower within 3-5 months of transplanting and produce ripe fruit within about 9-11 months, then fruit continuously for two to four years.

**Q: When should I pick papaya for the market?**
A: Pick at colour-break, when one or two yellow streaks appear on the green skin, for firmness and shelf life during transport. Fully ripe fruit is only for immediate local sale.

**Q: Why is my papaya fruit lumpy and distorted with gummy patches?**
A: This is often boron deficiency, sometimes combined with poor pollination or cool weather. Correct boron nutrition (carefully, as excess is toxic) and ensure good pollination.

**Q: How long does a papaya plant keep producing?**
A: Productively about two to four years. After that the plant is too tall to harvest and yields fall, so growers replant on a rolling basis to keep young plants coming into bearing.

**Q: Can I grow papaya at high altitude?**
A: Only up to about 1,600 m in the tropics. Above that, cool temperatures slow ripening and reduce fruit quality and sweetness. Frost will kill the plant outright.
