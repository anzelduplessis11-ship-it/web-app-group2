# Sesame (Simsim)

## Overview
Sesame, also widely known as simsim in East Africa, is an oilseed crop of major economic importance in Sudan, Ethiopia, Nigeria, Tanzania, and Uganda, among other African producers. It is grown for edible oil, tahini, confectionery, and as a valuable export crop that earns foreign exchange for several African economies. Sesame is notably drought-tolerant and well suited to marginal, semi-arid land where many other cash crops fail, making it an important option for diversifying smallholder income in dry regions. The crop has an indeterminate flowering habit, extremely small seed, and pods that shatter readily at maturity, all of which make timely harvest and careful post-harvest handling especially important for minimising losses.

## Scientific Name
*Sesamum indicum* L.

## Suitable Climate
- Warm-season crop; optimal growing temperature: 25-30°C.
- Requires a long, frost-free growing period; frost kills the crop at any stage.
- Rainfall requirement: 500-650 mm over the growing season for most varieties, though short-duration varieties can produce a crop with as little as 300 mm.
- Deep-rooted and drought-tolerant once established, making it well suited to semi-arid areas with erratic rainfall.
- High humidity and heavy rain near maturity increase the risk of pod and seed rot and encourage premature shattering.

## Suitable Soil
- Prefers well-drained sandy loam to loam soils.
- Optimal pH range: 5.5-8.0, a fairly wide tolerance.
- Very poor tolerance for waterlogging, even for short periods, which quickly causes root rot and plant death.
- Performs reasonably on soils of moderate to low fertility, though yield responds positively to improved soil fertility and organic matter.

## Soil Preparation
- Prepare a fine, well-drained seedbed, since the seed is very small and germinates poorly if sown into cloddy or crusted soil.
- Plough to a depth of 15-20 cm and harrow to break clods.
- In higher-rainfall areas, form ridges or raised beds to improve drainage and protect the crop from waterlogging.
- Remove crop residues that could interfere with even seed placement and early seedling emergence.

## Planting Season by African Region
| Region | Typical Planting Window | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| West Africa (Nigeria, Burkina Faso, Niger) | Onset of rains (May-July) | Time planting to avoid excess rain at the harvest stage |
| East Africa (Ethiopia, Tanzania, Uganda) | Main rains (Apr-Jun) or short rains (Sept-Oct) in bimodal zones | Choose the season with a drier finish to reduce shattering and pod rot |
| Horn of Africa / Sudan | Rainy season (Jun-Jul) | Sudan is among the world's largest sesame exporters |
| Southern Africa (Mozambique, Zambia, Malawi, Zimbabwe) | Start of summer rains (Nov-Dec) | Emerging production areas with expanding smallholder interest |

## Seed or Seedling Requirements
- Direct-seeded; the extremely small seed makes precise sowing difficult, similar to the challenge faced with teff.
- Seed rate: approximately 3-5 kg/ha.
- Mix seed with dry sand or fine soil before broadcasting or row planting to achieve a more even distribution and avoid overly dense patches.
- Use certified, high-quality seed of a variety suited to local shattering resistance and maturity length, since shattering tolerance varies significantly between varieties.
- Sow at a shallow depth of 1-2 cm; germination typically occurs within 3-5 days under warm, moist conditions.

## Plant Spacing
- Rows: 45-60 cm apart.
- Within-row spacing: 10-15 cm after thinning excess seedlings.
- Target plant population: approximately 150,000-220,000 plants/ha, adjusted for variety branching habit (branched types need more space than single-stem types).

## Water Requirements
- Total water requirement: 400-650 mm over the growing season.
- Most critical stages for water availability: germination and flowering/pod set.
- Deep-rooted and relatively drought-tolerant during vegetative growth once established.
- Waterlogging at any growth stage is far more damaging than moisture deficit and should be avoided through good drainage and appropriate planting timing.

## Fertiliser Recommendations
- Sesame has modest nutrient requirements compared with many other cash crops.
- General guideline: 20-40 kg N/ha, 20-30 kg P2O5/ha, and 0-20 kg K2O/ha depending on soil fertility.
- Apply phosphorus and potassium basally at planting; split nitrogen between planting and early vegetative growth.
- Avoid excessive nitrogen, which promotes vigorous vegetative growth at the expense of seed yield, delays maturity, and can increase lodging.
- See `fertilisers/fertiliser_basics.md` for general application guidance.

## Weed Management
- Sesame seedlings grow slowly at first and compete poorly with weeds, making the first 4-6 weeks after emergence a critical weed-free period.
- Two hand weedings are typically needed: shortly after emergence and again before flowering begins.
- Avoid weed competition especially in the run-up to flowering, when weed pressure has the greatest impact on final yield.
- See `weeds/general_weed_management.md` for general principles.

## Pest Management
- Key pests: sesame gall midge, leaf webworm, aphids, whiteflies, and pod-feeding bugs.
- Aphids and whiteflies are also of concern as vectors of viral and phytoplasma diseases.
- Scout fields regularly during flowering and pod development, when pest pressure has the greatest effect on final seed yield.
- See `pests/aphids.md` and `pests/whitefly.md` for related management guidance.

## Disease Management
- Key diseases: Fusarium wilt, phyllody (a phytoplasma disease spread by leafhoppers, causing flowers to become leaf-like and sterile), bacterial leaf spot, powdery mildew, and charcoal rot.
- Use resistant or tolerant varieties where available.
- Treat seed with an appropriate fungicide before planting where seed-borne disease is a known risk.
- Rotate with cereals or other non-host crops; avoid continuous sesame cropping on the same field, which allows soil-borne diseases to build up.

## Growth Stages
1. Germination and emergence (3-7 days after planting).
2. Vegetative growth (2-5 weeks).
3. Flowering, which occurs over an extended period from the lower to upper nodes of the plant (approximately weeks 5-8).
4. Capsule (pod) development and seed filling (weeks 8-12).
5. Physiological maturity (90-120 days after planting, depending on variety).

## Harvest Indicators
- Lower leaves turn yellow and begin to drop as the plant approaches maturity.
- Capsules change colour from green to yellow-brown and begin to dry from the base of the plant upward.
- Harvest when approximately 50-75% of capsules on the plant have changed colour, rather than waiting for full field-wide dryness, since fully dry capsules shatter and shed seed rapidly.
- Because flowering and pod set are indeterminate (occurring continuously over several weeks), the plant will have capsules at multiple stages of maturity at harvest time, requiring a judgement call on optimal timing.

## Post-Harvest Handling
- Cut whole plants near the base and bundle into stooks or sheaves, standing them upright in the field to finish drying and ripening evenly for 5-10 days.
- Thresh by inverting and beating the dried plants over a clean cloth or tarpaulin to catch the shattering seed and minimise loss and soil contamination.
- Clean and winnow the threshed seed to remove chaff, dust, and plant debris.
- Handle harvested material gently and promptly, since delayed threshing after full drying substantially increases shattering losses.

## Storage
- Dry seed to 6-7% moisture content before storage; sesame's high oil content makes it prone to rancidity if stored too moist or in warm conditions.
- Store in a cool, dry, well-ventilated location away from direct sunlight and heat sources to preserve oil quality and flavour.
- Use clean, breathable bags and avoid conditions that allow moisture pickup, which accelerates spoilage and mould growth.
- Periodically inspect stored seed for signs of insect activity, mould, or off-odours indicating oil rancidity.

## Expected Yield
- Smallholder traditional systems: 300-500 kg/ha.
- Improved management with better varieties, timely weeding, and careful harvest timing: 700-1,200 kg/ha.
- Research and best-practice on-farm conditions: 1,500+ kg/ha.
- Yield is heavily influenced by harvest timing, since both early and late harvest reduce recoverable seed, the latter through shattering.

## Common Mistakes
- Delaying harvest until capsules are fully dry, resulting in major shattering losses in the field.
- Planting too densely, causing lodging and poor pod fill due to excessive competition for light and nutrients.
- Applying excessive nitrogen fertiliser, which favours vegetative growth over seed yield.
- Threshing directly on bare ground, contaminating the seed with soil and stones and lowering quality.
- Continuous sesame cropping without rotation, allowing soil-borne diseases such as Fusarium wilt to build up.

## Best Farming Practices
- Harvest and stook plants before full capsule dehiscence to minimise shattering losses.
- Select shatter-tolerant or resistant varieties where available.
- Rotate sesame with cereals or legumes to break disease cycles and maintain soil health.
- Dry harvested seed thoroughly and store in cool, dry conditions to protect oil quality.
- Thresh on a clean surface (tarpaulin or prepared floor) rather than bare soil to maintain seed cleanliness and marketability.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Why do I lose so much sesame seed at harvest?**
A: Sesame capsules shatter (split open and release seed) readily once fully dry. Most field losses occur from harvesting too late, after capsules have already begun to split, or from delayed threshing after cutting. Harvesting when 50-75% of capsules have changed colour and threshing promptly on a clean surface reduces these losses.

**Q: Why is sesame considered a good crop for dry areas?**
A: Sesame has a deep root system and can produce an economic yield with as little as 300-500 mm of rainfall once established, making it more drought-tolerant than many other oilseed and cash crops. It performs particularly well on marginal land unsuitable for more water-demanding crops.

**Q: Can sesame be grown continuously on the same field every season?**
A: Continuous sesame cropping is not recommended, as it allows soil-borne diseases such as Fusarium wilt to build up over time. Rotating with cereals or legumes for at least one to two seasons helps break disease cycles and maintain yields.

**Q: Why does my sesame oil taste rancid after storage?**
A: Rancidity usually results from storing seed at too high a moisture content or in warm, humid conditions, both of which accelerate oxidation of the oil. Drying seed to 6-7% moisture and storing in a cool, dry, well-ventilated place preserves oil quality for much longer.

**Q: How do I know when to plant sesame relative to the rains?**
A: Sesame should be planted at the onset of reliable rains but timed so that harvest falls during a drier period, since heavy rain near maturity increases pod rot and shattering losses. In bimodal rainfall regions, choosing the season with a drier finish is usually preferable to the season with a wetter finish.
