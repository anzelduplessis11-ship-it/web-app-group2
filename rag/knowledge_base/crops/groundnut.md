# Groundnut (Peanut)

## Overview
Groundnut is an important legume grown across Africa for both food and income. The seeds (kernels) are rich in protein and oil, eaten roasted or boiled, ground into paste, or pressed for cooking oil, while the haulms (vines) make valuable livestock fodder. As a legume, groundnut fixes atmospheric nitrogen, improving soil fertility and making it an excellent rotation crop with cereals such as maize, sorghum, and millet. Yields respond well to good seed quality, correct spacing, and careful harvesting and drying, which is also critical for avoiding aflatoxin contamination.

## Scientific Name
*Arachis hypogaea* L.

## Suitable Climate
- Optimal growing temperature: 25-30°C.
- Requires approximately 500-1000 mm of well-distributed rainfall per season.
- Sensitive to frost; grown in the warm season.
- Best suited to low and mid-altitude areas, generally below approximately 1,500 m.
- A dry period at the end of the season aids maturation and safe drying; heavy rain near harvest raises the risk of mould and aflatoxin.

## Suitable Soil
- Well-drained, light-textured sandy loam soils are ideal, as the crop forms its pods underground ("pegging") and needs loose soil.
- Tolerates a pH range of approximately 5.5-7.0; slightly acidic to neutral soils are suitable.
- Calcium availability is important for pod filling; deficient soils may produce empty or poorly filled pods.
- Heavy clay and waterlogged soils hinder pegging and pod development and should be avoided.

## Soil Preparation
- Prepare a loose, fine, well-drained seedbed to allow easy pegging and pod formation.
- Plough and harrow to remove clods and create a level surface.
- Incorporate crop residues or well-decomposed organic matter ahead of planting to improve soil structure.
- Avoid compacted soil, which restricts peg penetration and reduces pod set.
- On sloped land, use contour cultivation to reduce erosion.

## Planting Season by African Region
| Region | Typical Planting Window | Notes |

| West Africa (Sahel/savanna) | May-July (onset of rains) | A major traditional crop in the semi-arid savanna belt |
| East Africa | March-April (long rains); Oct-Nov (short rains) | Depends on bimodal rainfall pattern |
| Southern Africa | Nov-Dec (start of summer rains) | Avoid late planting that pushes harvest into the wet season |
| Central Africa | Two windows in bimodal zones (Sept-Nov and Feb-April) | Ensure a drier spell for harvest and drying |

## Seed or Seedling Requirements
- Use certified or good-quality shelled seed of a variety adapted to local rainfall and season length.
- Shell pods shortly before planting to preserve germination and viability; avoid using cracked or shrivelled kernels.
- Seed rate: approximately 60-100 kg/ha of shelled seed, depending on seed size and spacing.
- Test germination before planting if seed is farm-saved; discard damaged or discoloured seed, which may carry disease or reduce stands.
- A seed dressing against soil-borne fungi may be beneficial where damping-off is common; confirm locally registered products with an extension officer.

## Plant Spacing
- Common spacing: 30-45 cm between rows and 10-15 cm between plants, giving a relatively dense stand.
- Denser, well-managed stands help suppress weeds and can improve yield where moisture is adequate.
- Uniform, adequate plant populations are important, as gaps reduce total pod yield.

## Water Requirements
- Total seasonal water requirement: approximately 450-700 mm depending on climate and variety.
- Critical water-sensitive stages: flowering, pegging, and pod filling.
- Water stress during flowering and pegging sharply reduces pod set and yield.
- Excessive moisture near harvest promotes pod diseases and aflatoxin risk; a drier finish is preferable.

## Fertiliser Recommendations
- As a legume, groundnut fixes much of its own nitrogen and generally needs little nitrogen fertiliser.
- Phosphorus supports root growth, nodulation, and pod filling; a basal phosphorus application benefits deficient soils.
- Calcium is important for pod filling; on deficient soils, a calcium source (such as gypsum) applied at flowering can improve kernel filling.
- Where soils are known to be low in the relevant nutrients, apply based on a soil test; see `soil/soil_testing_and_fertility.md` and `fertilisers/fertiliser_basics.md`.
- Inoculating seed with appropriate rhizobia can improve nitrogen fixation on soils where groundnut has not been grown recently.

## Weed Management
- First weeding: 2-3 weeks after emergence, before weeds compete with young plants.
- Second weeding: around 5-6 weeks after emergence, and complete weeding before pegging begins.
- Avoid disturbing the soil once pegging has started, as cultivation can damage developing pegs and pods.
- Hand weeding, shallow hoeing, or an approved pre-emergence herbicide can be used; confirm registered products locally.
- See `weeds/general_weed_management.md` for integrated weed control principles.

## Pest Management
- Key pests: aphids (which can also transmit viruses), thrips, leaf-feeding caterpillars, and termites and other soil pests that attack pods.
- Scout regularly during vegetative and flowering stages; see `pests/aphids.md` for aphid management.
- Termite damage to pods is a common cause of loss in drier areas; timely harvesting reduces exposure.
- Use integrated pest management combining scouting, cultural practices, and chemical control only when thresholds are exceeded, using locally registered products.

## Disease Management
- Key diseases: early and late leaf spot, groundnut rosette (a virus spread by aphids), and rusts.
- Use resistant or tolerant varieties where available, and healthy, disease-free seed.
- Practice crop rotation with non-legume crops (such as cereals) to reduce build-up of leaf spot and other soil-borne diseases.
- Control aphids to help limit the spread of rosette virus.
- For suspected severe or unfamiliar disease outbreaks, consult a qualified agricultural extension officer.

## Growth Stages
1. Germination and emergence (0-10 days after planting).
2. Vegetative growth: development of leaves and branches (approximately 10-40 days).
3. Flowering and pegging: yellow flowers appear and pegs grow down into the soil (approximately 30-70 days).
4. Pod filling and maturity: kernels fill and pods mature underground, typically reaching maturity 90-150 days after planting depending on variety.

## Harvest Indicators
- The inside of mature pod shells darkens (showing dark veining), and kernels fill the shells and develop their variety colour.
- Leaves may yellow and some drop as the crop matures.
- Test-lift a few plants and open pods to check that most have reached maturity before harvesting the whole field.
- Because pods mature underground and not all at once, aim to harvest when the majority of pods are mature to balance yield and quality.

## Post-Harvest Handling
- Lift plants carefully, ideally when soil is slightly moist, to avoid leaving pods buried in the ground.
- Strip or leave pods on the plant to dry, and dry them promptly and thoroughly, as slow drying greatly increases the risk of mould and aflatoxin.
- Dry pods until kernels are hard and rattle in the shell; avoid drying directly on bare soil, which can contaminate pods.
- Sort out damaged, discoloured, shrivelled, or mouldy pods and kernels, as these are most likely to carry aflatoxin.

## Storage
- Store well-dried pods or kernels in a cool, dry, well-ventilated place protected from moisture, rodents, and insects.
- Storing under-dried groundnuts is a major cause of mould growth and aflatoxin contamination, which is a serious food safety hazard.
- Use clean, dry bags or containers and keep them off the floor and away from damp walls.
- Monitor stored groundnuts regularly for signs of moisture, insects, or mould, and remove affected stock.
- If aflatoxin contamination is suspected, do not consume or sell the affected stock and consult a qualified agricultural or food safety authority; see `storage/grain_storage.md`.

## Expected Yield
- Smallholder rainfed systems with basic management: approximately 0.5-1.5 tonnes/ha of unshelled pods.
- Improved management with good seed, spacing, and agronomy: approximately 2-3 tonnes/ha of pods.
- Higher yields are achievable under favourable conditions with adequate calcium, phosphorus, and pest/disease control.
- Actual yield depends heavily on variety, rainfall distribution, soil, and management.

## Common Mistakes
- Planting cracked, shrivelled, or old seed with poor germination.
- Cultivating or weeding aggressively after pegging has begun, damaging developing pods.
- Harvesting too early (immature, poorly filled pods) or too late (pods lost in the soil or over-dry and shattering).
- Drying groundnuts too slowly or on bare, damp ground, greatly increasing aflatoxin risk.
- Storing under-dried nuts, leading to mould and aflatoxin contamination.

## Best Farming Practices
- Rotate groundnut with cereals such as maize, sorghum, or millet to benefit from its nitrogen fixation and break disease cycles.
- Use good-quality, well-adapted seed and shell it only shortly before planting.
- Ensure adequate calcium and phosphorus on deficient soils to support pod filling.
- Harvest at the correct maturity and dry pods rapidly and thoroughly to protect quality and food safety.
- Practice careful sorting and clean storage to minimise aflatoxin risk; see `sustainability/sustainable_farming_practices.md`.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Why are many of my groundnut pods empty or poorly filled?**
A: Poor pod filling is often linked to calcium deficiency, moisture stress during pegging and pod filling, or poor pegging into compacted soil. Ensure adequate calcium on deficient soils and avoid soil compaction and water stress at those stages.

**Q: How do I reduce the risk of aflatoxin in my groundnuts?**
A: Harvest at proper maturity, avoid leaving mature pods in wet soil, dry pods rapidly and thoroughly (not on bare damp ground), sort out damaged and mouldy nuts, and store well-dried nuts in a cool, dry, airy place. If contamination is suspected, do not consume or sell it and consult a qualified authority.

**Q: Do I need to add nitrogen fertiliser to my groundnuts?**
A: Generally no, because groundnut fixes much of its own nitrogen as a legume. Focus instead on phosphorus and calcium on deficient soils, and consider rhizobium inoculation where groundnut has not been grown for some time.

**Q: Why are my groundnut leaves developing dark spots?**
A: Dark leaf spots commonly indicate early or late leaf spot disease. Use tolerant varieties and rotation, remove heavily infected residues, and consult an extension officer if the disease is severe or spreading rapidly.
