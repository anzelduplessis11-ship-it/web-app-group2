# Eggplant

## Overview
Eggplant (aubergine or brinjal) is a warm-season vegetable grown widely across Africa for fresh market sale, valued for its long harvesting period and steady demand. Alongside the common purple aubergine, Africa is home to important indigenous eggplants, the scarlet or bitter "African eggplant" and the "gboma" type, which are grown for both their fruit and, in some cases, their leaves and are prized in local diets and markets. Eggplant belongs to the Solanaceae family and is a close relative of tomato, chilli, and sweet pepper, so it shares many of the same pests and diseases and must be managed within the same rotation planning (see `crops/tomato.md`, `crops/chilli_pepper.md`, and `crops/sweet_pepper.md`). It is a robust, deep-rooted crop that, once established, can crop over several months, giving repeated pickings of glossy, immature fruit. The plants become heavy with fruit and benefit from staking. Eggplant rewards good drainage, balanced nutrition, disciplined pest control (especially against the fruit and shoot borer), and frequent harvesting of tender fruit at the right stage.

## Scientific Name
*Solanum melongena* L. (common eggplant, aubergine, brinjal). Two indigenous African species are also widely grown and are important traditional vegetables: *Solanum aethiopicum* L. (scarlet or "African eggplant", including bitter-fruited and leafy types) and *Solanum macrocarpon* L. (gboma eggplant, grown for both fruit and leaves). All are Solanaceae relatives of tomato (*Solanum lycopersicum*), chilli, and sweet pepper (*Capsicum* species).

## Suitable Climate
- Optimal growing temperature: 22-30°C during the day and 18-24°C at night; growth slows below about 18°C and fruit set is poor below 15°C or in cold nights.
- Warm-season crop that is very sensitive to frost; it is more heat-tolerant than tomato and sweet pepper but still suffers flower drop in extreme heat above about 35°C.
- Requires approximately 600-1000 mm of water over its long growing and harvesting cycle, through rainfall and/or irrigation.
- Tolerates a range of tropical and subtropical conditions and cropping over long warm periods; indigenous African eggplants are often well adapted to local heat and stress.
- High humidity and prolonged leaf wetness increase fungal and bacterial disease pressure, so humid lowland zones need extra vigilance and good drainage.

## Suitable Soil
- Deep, well-drained, fertile sandy loam to loam soils rich in organic matter are ideal, and the deep root system benefits from a deep soil.
- Optimal pH: 5.5-6.8; correct strongly acid soils with lime based on a soil test.
- Excellent drainage is essential because eggplant is highly susceptible to bacterial wilt and other soil-borne diseases in waterlogged soils.
- Avoid heavy, poorly drained clays; where soils are heavy, grow on raised beds or ridges.
- See `soil/soil_types_africa.md` and `soil/soil_testing_and_fertility.md` for matching the crop to local soils and testing guidance.

## Soil Preparation
- Plough and harrow to a fine, level tilth to a depth of 25-30 cm to accommodate the deep root system and help establishment.
- Incorporate 15-25 tonnes/ha of well-decomposed manure or compost several weeks before transplanting, as eggplant is a heavy feeder over its long season.
- Form raised beds or ridges in heavy soils and high-rainfall areas to ensure drainage and reduce bacterial wilt risk.
- Remove and destroy residues of previous solanaceous crops (tomato, pepper, eggplant, potato) to reduce carry-over of shared pests and diseases.
- Avoid fields recently cropped to solanaceous plants or with a history of bacterial wilt; rotate with cereals or legumes.
- Conduct a soil test where possible to guide lime and fertiliser use; see `soil/soil_testing_and_fertility.md`.

## Seed or Seedling Requirements
- Eggplant is **raised from seed in a nursery and then transplanted**, giving uniform, vigorous plants and good early management.
- Use certified, disease-free seed of a variety suited to the target market (common purple aubergine or an indigenous African eggplant type) and to local disease pressure; where bacterial wilt is common, choose tolerant varieties or grafted plants.
- Seed rate: approximately 200-400 g/ha for nursery raising, depending on variety and germination.
- **Nursery management:** Sow in clean, well-drained beds or trays with fresh or sterilised media to prevent damping-off; keep moist but not waterlogged, shade lightly until germination, then expose gradually to full sun. Eggplant seed can germinate slowly, so keep beds warm and evenly moist.
- Seedlings are ready to transplant at about 5-7 weeks old with 4-6 true leaves and 12-15 cm height.
- Harden seedlings for about a week before transplanting by reducing watering and increasing sun exposure.
- Transplant in the cool of late afternoon or on an overcast day and water in well to reduce shock.

## Plant Spacing
- Standard spacing: 60-90 cm between rows and 45-60 cm between plants; eggplant plants grow large and bushy and need more room than pepper.
- Wider spacing improves airflow to reduce disease and gives the heavy, spreading plants room to develop and be harvested.
- Vigorous varieties and fertile soils warrant the wider end of the range.
- Overcrowding raises humidity and disease pressure and makes staking, spraying, and harvesting difficult.

## Water Requirements
- Eggplant needs consistent, even soil moisture over its long season; both drought and waterlogging reduce yield and quality.
- The most water-sensitive stages are establishment, flowering, and continuous fruit development during the long harvest period.
- Water stress during fruiting causes flower drop, small or bitter fruit, and can contribute to blossom-end rot.
- Because harvesting continues for months, maintain steady irrigation throughout the picking period rather than tapering off early.
- Drip or furrow irrigation is preferred over overhead watering, which wets foliage and fruit and spreads disease; see `irrigation/irrigation_methods.md` and `irrigation/irrigation_scheduling.md`.

## Fertiliser Recommendations
- Basal application at transplanting: a compound fertiliser supplying phosphorus for root establishment, plus incorporated organic matter.
- Topdress with nitrogen and potassium in several splits through the long season, increasing potassium during flowering and fruiting to support the heavy, extended fruit load.
- General guideline in the absence of a soil test: approximately 100-150 kg N/ha, 50-100 kg P2O5/ha, and 100-150 kg K2O/ha, split over the season and adjusted to soil fertility; the long harvest period means nutrition must be replenished with regular top-dressing.
- Ensure adequate calcium and even moisture to prevent blossom-end rot, which is mainly a calcium-transport problem driven by uneven watering.
- Excess nitrogen produces lush leaves with poor fruit set and greater disease susceptibility, so keep nutrition balanced.
- See `fertilisers/fertiliser_basics.md`, `fertilisers/organic_fertilisers.md`, and `fertilisers/nutrient_deficiencies_guide.md`.

## Weed Management
- Keep the crop weed-free during the first 6-8 weeks after transplanting, when eggplant competes poorly with weeds.
- Weed by hand or with shallow hoeing to avoid damaging surface roots; take care as the stems and leaves of some types carry spines.
- Mulching between rows suppresses weeds, conserves moisture, and reduces soil splash that spreads soil-borne disease onto lower leaves and fruit.
- Weeds host aphids, whiteflies, mites, and viruses, so weed control is also pest and disease management.
- See `weeds/general_weed_management.md` for integrated weed control principles.

## Pest Management
Eggplant is attacked by several serious pests; use integrated pest management (IPM) combining resistant varieties, sanitation, scouting, and targeted control.
- **Eggplant fruit and shoot borer:** The most damaging pest; caterpillars bore into shoot tips, causing wilting "dead hearts", and into fruit, making them rot and become unmarketable. Manage by regularly cutting off and destroying wilting shoots and bored fruit, using pheromone traps for monitoring and mass trapping, growing tolerant varieties, and rotating away from solanaceous crops. Avoid routine calendar spraying, which harms natural enemies and drives resistance.
- **Aphids:** Suck sap and transmit viruses, clustering on shoot tips and leaf undersides. See `pests/aphids.md`.
- **Whitefly:** Sucks sap, causes sooty mould from honeydew, and transmits viruses; a major concern in warm areas. See `pests/whitefly.md`.
- **Red spider mites:** Cause stippling, bronzing, and webbing on leaf undersides in hot dry conditions; maintain plant moisture and conserve predatory mites.
- **Flea beetles:** Small beetles that chew many tiny "shot holes" in leaves, especially on young plants and seedlings; healthy vigorous seedlings and, where needed, targeted control help the crop grow past the damage.
- **IPM practices:** Scout at least weekly, focusing on shoot tips and leaf undersides; remove and destroy borer-infested shoots and fruit; rotate away from solanaceous crops; remove weeds and volunteers; and conserve natural enemies.
- Where chemical control is necessary, use only locally registered products, refer to active-ingredient classes rather than brand names, rotate classes to slow resistance, observe pre-harvest intervals, and confirm the product and dose with a qualified agricultural extension officer. See `regulations/pesticide_safety_and_handling.md` and `emergency_guides/pest_outbreak_emergency_guide.md`.

## Disease Management
- **Bacterial wilt:** The most serious disease of eggplant in many African areas, causing sudden wilting of green plants in warm, wet, waterlogged soils. Manage with resistant varieties or grafting onto resistant rootstocks, good drainage, raised beds, rotation away from solanaceous crops for at least 3-4 seasons, and removal of infected plants. See `diseases/tomato_bacterial_wilt.md`.
- **Fusarium and Verticillium wilts:** Soil-borne fungal vascular diseases causing yellowing, wilting, and browning of the internal stem; manage with resistant varieties, rotation, good drainage, and field sanitation, as there is no cure once plants are infected.
- **Phomopsis blight:** A fungal disease causing leaf spots, stem cankers, and soft, rotting lesions on fruit, worsened by warm humid conditions; use clean seed, adequate spacing, crop rotation, and removal of infected fruit and residues.
- **Damping-off in the nursery:** A fungal complex that rots seedlings at the soil line; prevent with clean media, good drainage, and avoiding overwatering and dense sowing.
- **Viral diseases:** Viruses spread by aphids and whiteflies cause mosaic, leaf distortion, and stunting; there is no cure, so use clean seed, control the insect vectors, and remove infected plants.
- **Blossom-end rot:** A dark, sunken patch at the blossom end of the fruit caused by calcium failing to reach the fruit due to uneven watering; maintain even soil moisture and mulch.
- Cross-reference late blight of solanaceous crops in `diseases/tomato_late_blight.md`, and use `emergency_guides/crop_emergency_guide.md` for severe or unclear outbreaks.
- For any chemical disease control, confirm locally registered, approved products and correct dosages with a qualified agricultural extension officer, and rely primarily on resistant varieties, grafting, sanitation, rotation, and drainage.

## Growth Stages
1. Nursery/seedling stage (0-5-7 weeks before transplanting).
2. Establishment after transplanting (1-3 weeks) as roots recover and growth resumes.
3. Vegetative growth (3-7 weeks after transplanting), building a large bushy frame of stems and leaves.
4. Flowering and fruit set, beginning roughly 7-10 weeks after transplanting and continuing over a long period.
5. Continuous fruit development and harvesting, with the plant flowering and fruiting simultaneously for several months, giving repeated pickings.

## Harvest Indicators
- Harvest fruit **glossy and immature**, before the skin dulls and the seeds harden; tender, shiny fruit are highest quality and most marketable.
- A ripe-for-harvest fruit has bright, glossy skin, is firm, and springs back slightly when pressed; a dull skin and hard, brown seeds indicate the fruit is over-mature, seedy, and bitter.
- First harvest typically begins about 10-14 weeks after transplanting, depending on variety and climate.
- Cut fruit from the plant with a knife or secateurs, leaving a short stalk and the green cap (calyx) attached, rather than pulling, which damages the plant.
- Pick frequently, every 3-7 days, because regular harvesting of young fruit keeps the plant productive and prevents fruit from becoming over-mature; the harvest period can extend for several months.
- Some indigenous African eggplant types are harvested at specific colour stages preferred locally; harvest to match the market.

## Post-Harvest Handling
- Harvest in the cool of the morning and keep fruit shaded to reduce field heat and moisture loss.
- Handle gently, as the fruit bruise and the skin scars easily; use clean ventilated crates rather than tightly packed sacks.
- Sort and grade by size, shape, colour, glossiness, and freedom from bruising, borer holes, and disease; keep the calyx attached, as buyers use it to judge freshness.
- Remove any diseased, borer-damaged, dull, or over-mature fruit before packing.
- Because eggplant loses moisture quickly and wilts, market it promptly and keep it cool and shaded during transport.
- See `harvesting/harvesting_best_practices.md`, `marketplace/product_grading_and_quality.md`, and `marketplace/buyer_preferences_and_seasonal_demand.md`.

## Storage
- Eggplant is perishable and does not store long; hold at about 10-12°C and 90-95% relative humidity for short-term storage of about 1-2 weeks.
- Temperatures below about 10°C cause chilling injury (pitting, surface scald, and browning), so do not store too cold.
- Fruit lose moisture and become dull and shrivelled quickly, so keep them cool, shaded, and humid and market them soon after harvest.
- If refrigeration is unavailable, keep fruit in a cool, shaded, ventilated place and sell quickly rather than holding.
- See `storage/perishable_storage.md` for general guidance on storing perishable produce.

## Expected Yield
- Smallholder open-field systems with basic management: approximately 10-20 tonnes/ha.
- Improved management with quality seedlings, balanced fertiliser, irrigation, staking, and good pest/disease control: approximately 25-40 tonnes/ha.
- High-input commercial systems: 40-70+ tonnes/ha over the full harvest period.
- Indigenous African eggplant yields vary by type and market; leafy types are also harvested for leaves as an additional product.
- Actual yield depends strongly on variety, plant population, water and nutrient management, harvest frequency, and losses to the fruit and shoot borer and to bacterial wilt.

## Common Mistakes
- Planting on poorly drained soil or after solanaceous crops, leading to severe bacterial wilt losses.
- Ignoring the fruit and shoot borer until fruit damage is widespread, instead of routinely removing infested shoots and fruit and monitoring with traps.
- Harvesting fruit too late, when the skin is dull and seeds are hard, giving seedy, bitter, low-value fruit.
- Harvesting too infrequently, so fruit over-mature on the plant and the plant slows its production.
- Overhead irrigation and overcrowding, which raise humidity and drive fungal and bacterial diseases.
- Applying excess nitrogen, producing leafy plants with poor fruit set.
- Failing to stake heavy plants, so branches break or fruit lie on the soil and rot.

## Best Farming Practices
- Choose varieties suited to the target market (including well-adapted indigenous African eggplants) and to local disease pressure, using certified, disease-free seed or grafted plants where bacterial wilt is severe.
- Raise strong, uniform seedlings in a clean nursery and harden them before transplanting.
- Plant on well-drained raised beds and rotate away from solanaceous crops for at least 3-4 seasons.
- Maintain steady soil moisture with drip or furrow irrigation throughout the long harvest period, and mulch to conserve water and suppress weeds.
- Stake the heavy plants and scout weekly, promptly removing borer-infested shoots and fruit as the core of IPM.
- Harvest glossy immature fruit frequently, handle gently, and grade carefully for the fresh market, using registered pesticides only when justified and confirmed with an extension officer.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: My eggplant fruit have holes and rot inside, and some shoot tips are wilting. What is the cause?**
A: This is almost certainly the eggplant fruit and shoot borer, whose caterpillars bore into shoot tips (causing wilting "dead hearts") and into fruit. The most effective response is to cut off and destroy affected shoots and fruit regularly, use pheromone traps to monitor and trap the moths, grow tolerant varieties, and rotate away from solanaceous crops. Confirm any pesticide choice and timing with an extension officer.

**Q: When should I pick eggplant fruit?**
A: Harvest while the fruit are still glossy, firm, and immature, with soft seeds inside. Once the skin becomes dull and the seeds turn hard and brown, the fruit is over-mature, seedy, and bitter and fetches a lower price. Pick frequently, every 3-7 days, to keep the plants productive.

**Q: My eggplant plants wilted suddenly even though the soil was moist. Why?**
A: Sudden wilting of green plants in warm, wet soils is typically bacterial wilt, a soil-borne disease with no chemical cure and a major problem for eggplant. Remove and destroy affected plants, improve drainage with raised beds, avoid replanting solanaceous crops there for several seasons, and use resistant varieties or grafted plants. See `diseases/tomato_bacterial_wilt.md`.

**Q: What is African eggplant and how does it differ from the common purple aubergine?**
A: African eggplant refers mainly to *Solanum aethiopicum* (scarlet or bitter eggplant) and *Solanum macrocarpon* (gboma), indigenous species grown for their fruit and, in some types, their leaves. They are important traditional vegetables, often well adapted to local heat and stress, and are managed much like common aubergine (*Solanum melongena*) but with variety-specific colour and harvest preferences set by local markets.

**Q: Why are my eggplant fruits bitter?**
A: Bitterness increases when fruit are harvested over-mature (dull skin, hard seeds) or when plants are stressed by drought or heat during fruiting. Harvest young, glossy fruit and keep soil moisture even; some indigenous types are naturally more bitter and are grown deliberately for that flavour.

**Q: Do eggplant plants need staking?**
A: Yes, staking is recommended because eggplant plants grow large and become heavy with fruit, so branches can break or bend to the ground where fruit rot. Support keeps fruit clean and off the soil, improves airflow to reduce disease, and makes harvesting easier.

**Q: Can I grow eggplant right after tomato or pepper in the same field?**
A: No. Eggplant, tomato, chilli, and sweet pepper are all Solanaceae and share pests and diseases such as bacterial wilt, wilts, aphids, whiteflies, and viruses, which build up with continuous solanaceous cropping. Rotate the whole family with cereals or legumes for at least 3-4 seasons. See `crops/tomato.md`, `crops/chilli_pepper.md`, and `crops/sweet_pepper.md`.

**Q: Why do my seedlings collapse and die at the soil line in the nursery?**
A: This is damping-off, a fungal disease of overcrowded, overwatered, poorly drained nurseries. Prevent it by using clean or sterilised media, sowing thinly, ensuring good drainage and airflow, and avoiding overwatering. Do not transplant weak or affected seedlings into the field.
