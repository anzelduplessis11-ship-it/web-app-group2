# Coffee

## Overview
Coffee is one of Africa's most important export and cash crops, providing income to millions of smallholder households across the highlands of East, Central, and West Africa. Two species dominate cultivation: arabica coffee, prized for its fine flavour and grown at higher, cooler altitudes, and robusta coffee, which is hardier, higher-yielding, more disease-tolerant, and suited to warmer lowlands. Coffee is a perennial shrub or small tree that begins bearing two to four years after planting and can remain productive for 20 to 40 years with good management. Because the crop is sold into quality-sensitive international and specialty markets, careful attention to variety selection, shade management, harvesting only ripe cherries, and correct processing directly determines the price a farmer receives. Coffee farming rewards patience, consistent husbandry, and post-harvest discipline more than almost any other smallholder crop, and it integrates well with agroforestry and climate-smart approaches. For guidance on grading and buyer expectations see `marketplace/product_grading_and_quality.md`, and for turning cherry into higher-value products see `pricing/price_negotiation_and_value_addition.md`.

## Scientific Name
*Coffea arabica* L. (arabica) and *Coffea canephora* Pierre ex A. Froehner (robusta), family Rubiaceae.

## Suitable Climate
- Arabica prefers cool, stable temperatures of approximately 15-24°C and grows best at altitudes of roughly 1,400-2,100 m in tropical highlands.
- Robusta tolerates warmer conditions of approximately 22-30°C and is grown at lower altitudes, typically below 1,200 m.
- Well-distributed annual rainfall of about 1,200-2,000 mm is ideal, with a distinct dry spell to trigger uniform flowering.
- A short dry period of two to four months encourages synchronised flowering and even ripening.
- Both species are sensitive to frost, prolonged drought, strong winds, and extreme heat; rising temperatures from climate change are pushing arabica production to higher elevations.
- Moderate shade buffers temperature extremes and is increasingly recommended as a climate adaptation; see `weather/climate_smart_agriculture.md`.

## Suitable Soil
- Deep, well-drained, fertile loams rich in organic matter are ideal; coffee roots dislike waterlogging.
- A slightly acidic pH of approximately 5.0-6.5 suits both species; strongly acidic or alkaline soils reduce nutrient availability.
- Volcanic and forest-derived soils common in the East African highlands are particularly well suited.
- Good depth (at least 1 m) allows strong root development and drought resilience.
- Test soil before establishment to correct acidity and nutrient gaps; see `soil/soil_testing_and_fertility.md`.

## Soil Preparation
- Clear the land while retaining or planting suitable shade trees, which protect young coffee and improve long-term sustainability.
- Dig planting holes well in advance, typically 60 cm wide and 60 cm deep, separating topsoil from subsoil.
- Mix topsoil with well-decomposed manure or compost and return it to the hole before planting.
- On slopes, establish contour rows, terraces, or grass strips to control erosion; see `soil/soil_conservation_and_erosion.md`.
- Prepare planting holes and shade at least one to two months before transplanting so soil settles and organic matter integrates.

## Planting Season by African Region
| Region | Typical Planting Window | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| East Africa (Ethiopia, Kenya, Uganda, Tanzania) | March-May (long rains); Oct-Nov (short rains) | Transplant seedlings at the start of reliable rains |
| Central Africa (DRC, Rwanda, Burundi) | Sept-Nov and Feb-April | Bimodal rainfall gives two establishment windows |
| West Africa (Côte d'Ivoire, Cameroon – mainly robusta) | April-June (onset of main rains) | Robusta suited to warmer, lower zones |
| Southern Africa (Zambia, Malawi highlands) | Nov-Dec (start of rains) | Limited to cooler high-altitude niches |
| Highland niches generally | Beginning of the main wet season | Establish when soil moisture is reliable for several months |

## Seed or Seedling Requirements
- Coffee is normally established from nursery-raised seedlings rather than direct seeding.
- Use seed or clonal material of improved, locally recommended varieties selected for disease resistance (for example resistance to coffee leaf rust and coffee berry disease) and cup quality.
- Raise seedlings in a shaded nursery for six to twelve months until they reach the four-to-eight leaf-pair stage and are hardened off before transplanting.
- Robusta is often propagated clonally from cuttings of superior mother plants to preserve yield and quality traits.
- Approximately 1,300-2,500 seedlings are needed per hectare depending on spacing; raise 10-15% extra for gap-filling.
- Source planting material from certified nurseries or registered multiplication programmes to avoid introducing disease.

## Plant Spacing
- Arabica is commonly spaced at approximately 2 m between rows and 1.5-2 m within rows, giving about 2,500-3,300 plants per hectare depending on variety vigour and pruning system.
- Robusta, being larger, is spaced wider at approximately 3 m by 3 m, giving roughly 1,100 plants per hectare.
- Hedgerow and higher-density systems are used with dwarf/compact varieties and intensive pruning.
- Establishment years (years 1-3): plants grow vegetatively; intercrop with legumes or short-term food crops to use space and generate income while the canopy fills.
- Bearing years (year 3-4 onward): the canopy closes, competition rises, and pruning replaces intercropping to manage the frame.
- Maintain permanent shade trees at a wider spacing overhead.

## Water Requirements
- Coffee needs reliable moisture, roughly 1,200-2,000 mm annually, ideally from well-distributed rainfall.
- A defined dry period is beneficial to induce uniform flowering; the first rains after this dry spell trigger the main flowering flush.
- Critical moisture-sensitive stages are flowering, fruit set, and berry expansion; stress at these times causes flower abortion and small beans.
- In drier robusta zones or during establishment, supplementary irrigation improves survival and yield; see `irrigation/irrigation_scheduling.md`.
- Mulching conserves soil moisture, moderates temperature, and suppresses weeds.

## Fertiliser Recommendations
- Base all fertiliser decisions on soil and, where possible, leaf analysis; see `soil/soil_testing_and_fertility.md` and `fertilisers/fertiliser_basics.md`.
- Coffee is a heavy feeder of nitrogen and potassium during berry development; phosphorus supports root and early growth.
- Split nitrogen and potassium into two or more applications timed to flowering and berry filling for efficient uptake.
- Apply well-decomposed manure or compost annually to sustain organic matter and micronutrients; see `fertilisers/organic_fertilisers.md`.
- Return coffee pulp and husks as compost to recycle nutrients rather than discarding them.
- Correct micronutrient deficiencies (for example zinc and boron), which are common on old coffee soils; see `fertilisers/nutrient_deficiencies_guide.md`.
- Maintain soil pH with lime where soils have become strongly acidic.

## Weed Management
- Keep the area under the canopy (the "collar" around each stem) weed-free, especially during establishment, to reduce competition for water and nutrients.
- Use mulch from prunings, shade-tree leaf litter, or cut grass to suppress weeds and conserve moisture.
- Slash or mow inter-rows rather than bare-cultivating on slopes to protect the soil and reduce erosion.
- Cover crops and legumes between young trees smother weeds and add nitrogen.
- Where herbicides are used, apply only locally registered products following label safety guidance, and avoid spraying onto coffee foliage; see `weeds/general_weed_management.md` and `regulations/pesticide_safety_and_handling.md`.

## Pest Management
- Key pests include the coffee berry borer (a small beetle that tunnels into beans), antestia bugs, leaf miners, mealybugs, scales, and root-feeding nematodes.
- Practise integrated pest management (IPM): regular scouting, sanitation, biological control, and chemical control only when monitoring shows it is justified.
- The coffee berry borer is best managed by frequent, complete harvesting of ripe cherries, prompt removal of leftover and fallen berries after harvest, and the use of baited traps.
- Encourage natural enemies and birds by maintaining shade and habitat diversity.
- Antestia bug can cause "potato taste" defects, damaging cup quality; monitor and manage populations through pruning for an open canopy and targeted control.
- When chemical control is needed, use active-ingredient classes recommended for coffee and confirm locally registered products with a qualified agricultural extension officer; observe pre-harvest intervals and safety precautions in `regulations/pesticide_safety_and_handling.md`.

## Disease Management
- Major diseases include coffee leaf rust (orange powdery spots on leaf undersides), coffee berry disease (dark sunken lesions on green berries, a serious arabica disease), coffee wilt disease (tracheomycosis, affecting robusta in Central Africa), and root-rot fungi.
- Plant resistant or tolerant varieties as the first line of defence, especially against leaf rust and coffee berry disease.
- Prune to open the canopy for airflow and light, which lowers humidity and disease pressure, and remove diseased branches and berries.
- Maintain balanced nutrition and appropriate shade, as stressed trees are more susceptible.
- Uproot and safely destroy plants affected by coffee wilt disease, and disinfect tools to prevent spread.
- Where protective fungicide sprays are justified, use recommended active-ingredient classes and confirm registered products locally; time applications to the wet season and vulnerable berry stages.

## Growth Stages
For this perennial crop, growth is described across establishment and bearing years:
1. Nursery stage (0-12 months): seed germinates and seedlings are raised under shade until ready to transplant.
2. Establishment (years 1-2 after transplanting): vegetative growth of the main stem and primary branches; no meaningful yield; focus on shade, weeding, and framing.
3. Early bearing (years 3-4): first flowering and cherry harvests, increasing each year.
4. Full bearing (years 5-15+): peak, relatively stable production with good management, pruning, and nutrition.
5. Flowering-to-harvest cycle within each season: white fragrant flowers open after the dry-season break, followed by green berry set, berry expansion, and ripening to red (or yellow, depending on variety). Arabica cherries mature in roughly 7-9 months; robusta in about 9-11 months.
6. Decline and rejuvenation: aging or overgrown trees are rejuvenated by stumping or cyclic pruning to restore productivity rather than replanting the whole field at once.

## Harvest Indicators
- Harvest only fully ripe cherries, which are bright red (or fully yellow in yellow-fruited varieties); ripe cherries are firm and detach easily.
- Selective hand-picking of ripe cherries, repeated every one to two weeks over several rounds, gives the best quality and price.
- Avoid picking green (unripe) or overripe/dried (black) cherries together with ripe ones, as they lower grade and cup quality.
- Ripening is often uneven within and between trees, especially for arabica, requiring multiple picking rounds through the season.

## Post-Harvest Handling
- Process cherries promptly, ideally the same day, to avoid fermentation defects.
- Two main methods are used: the dry (natural) method, in which whole cherries are dried, and the wet (washed) method, in which cherries are pulped, fermented to remove mucilage, washed, and then dried.
- The washed method generally produces cleaner, higher-value arabica but requires clean water and careful management; the dry method suits robusta and water-scarce areas.
- Float cherries in water before processing to remove floaters (immature, insect-damaged, or diseased), improving quality.
- Dry parchment or cherry slowly and evenly on raised beds or clean tarpaulins, turning regularly, until the moisture content reaches approximately 10-12%.
- Avoid drying on bare soil, which causes contamination and off-flavours, and protect drying coffee from rain and dew.
- Careful, uniform drying prevents mould (including the risk of ochratoxin) and preserves flavour and grade; see `storage/perishable_storage.md`.

## Storage
- Store dried parchment or green beans in clean, dry, well-ventilated stores away from moisture, strong odours, agrochemicals, and fuel, which coffee readily absorbs.
- Use clean jute or breathable bags stacked on pallets off the floor and away from damp walls.
- Maintain moisture content near 10-12%; higher moisture risks mould, ochratoxin formation, and loss of grade.
- Hull parchment into green bean close to the time of sale to preserve quality and freshness.
- Monitor stored coffee for pests, moisture, and musty smells, and keep records of lots for traceability; see `storage/grain_storage.md`.

## Expected Yield
- Smallholder farms with basic management typically yield approximately 300-800 kg of clean (green) coffee per hectare.
- Well-managed farms with good varieties, nutrition, pruning, and pest control can yield approximately 1,000-2,000 kg of green coffee per hectare.
- Robusta generally out-yields arabica per plant but may fetch a lower unit price.
- Yields fluctuate year to year and often follow a biennial pattern of high and low bearing; good pruning and nutrition moderate this.
- Actual yield depends heavily on variety, altitude, rainfall, tree age, and management.

## Common Mistakes
- Neglecting pruning, leading to overgrown, unproductive, disease-prone trees and pronounced biennial bearing.
- Strip-picking or mixing green and overripe cherries with ripe ones, which sharply reduces grade and price.
- Drying cherries on bare ground or too quickly, causing contamination, mould, and off-flavours.
- Establishing coffee without shade or erosion control on steep land, reducing resilience to heat and drought.
- Ignoring the coffee berry borer by leaving unharvested and fallen cherries in the field, allowing pest carry-over.
- Applying fertiliser or lime without a soil test, wasting money and risking imbalance.

## Best Farming Practices
- Choose disease-resistant, locally recommended varieties suited to your altitude and market.
- Manage moderate shade and diversify with trees for climate resilience, soil protection, and additional products; see `sustainability/sustainable_farming_practices.md`.
- Prune annually to maintain an open, manageable frame, renew bearing wood, and improve airflow.
- Harvest only ripe cherries in multiple rounds and process the same day for top quality.
- Recycle pulp and husks as compost, and feed the crop based on soil and leaf analysis.
- Practise strict field sanitation for berry borer control and adopt IPM before reaching for chemicals.
- Keep records of inputs, yields, and lots to support traceability, certification, and stronger price negotiation; see `pricing/price_negotiation_and_value_addition.md`.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Should I grow arabica or robusta?**
A: Match the species to your altitude and climate. Arabica suits cool highlands (roughly above 1,400 m) and fetches higher specialty prices but is more disease-prone. Robusta suits warmer lowlands, is hardier and higher-yielding, and is better for water-scarce or hotter zones. Consult your extension officer on locally recommended varieties.

**Q: How long before my coffee starts producing?**
A: Coffee begins light bearing about two to four years after transplanting and reaches full production around year five. It can remain productive for 20-40 years with good pruning, nutrition, and pest management.

**Q: Why is my coffee price low even though my yield is good?**
A: Price depends heavily on quality. Mixing unripe or overripe cherries, drying on bare ground, over-drying or under-drying, and mould all lower your grade. Selective picking of ripe cherries, clean same-day processing, and even drying to correct moisture raise your grade and price. See `marketplace/product_grading_and_quality.md`.

**Q: What causes orange powder on the underside of my coffee leaves?**
A: That is coffee leaf rust, a major fungal disease that causes defoliation and yield loss. Use resistant varieties, prune for airflow, maintain balanced nutrition, and apply recommended protective fungicides only where justified, confirming registered products with your extension officer.

**Q: How do I control the coffee berry borer?**
A: The most effective measures are cultural: harvest all ripe cherries frequently and completely, collect and destroy leftover and fallen berries after harvest to break the pest's cycle, and use baited traps. Chemical control is a last resort using registered products applied at the correct timing.

**Q: Is shade good or bad for coffee?**
A: Moderate shade is generally beneficial for smallholders, especially as the climate warms. It buffers temperature, conserves moisture, reduces stress, supports beneficial insects and birds, and provides extra products from shade trees. Excessive dense shade, however, can reduce yield and raise some disease risks, so shade should be managed and thinned.

**Q: Can I add value to my coffee before selling?**
A: Yes. Careful selective picking, proper washing or natural processing, and correct drying and sorting significantly raise grade and price. Group marketing, certification, traceability, and even small-scale hulling or roasting can capture more value. See `pricing/price_negotiation_and_value_addition.md`.

**Q: Should I replace old coffee trees or rejuvenate them?**
A: Aging or overgrown trees can often be rejuvenated through stumping or cyclic pruning to renew bearing wood, which is cheaper and faster than full replanting. Replace only when trees are diseased, unproductive despite renewal, or of poor varieties, and stagger replanting to keep income flowing.
