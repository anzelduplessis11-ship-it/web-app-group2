# Irish Potato

## Overview
Irish potato (also called round potato or, simply, potato) is an important food and cash crop in the cooler highland regions of Africa. It produces edible tubers that are boiled, fried, roasted, or mashed, and it matures relatively quickly, making it valuable for both household food security and income. Potato is a management-intensive crop that is highly sensitive to the quality of planting material and to certain diseases, so success depends strongly on using clean seed tubers and good field hygiene.

## Scientific Name
*Solanum tuberosum* L.

## Suitable Climate
- Optimal growing temperature: 15-20°C; tuber formation is poor above approximately 25°C.
- Requires 500-700 mm of well-distributed water over the growing cycle, through rainfall and/or irrigation.
- Best suited to cool highlands, typically 1,500-3,000 m altitude in tropical Africa.
- Very sensitive to frost, which can kill foliage; also sensitive to prolonged heat, which reduces tuber set.
- High humidity with cool, wet conditions strongly favours late blight, the most damaging potato disease.

## Suitable Soil
- Deep, loose, well-drained sandy loam or loam soils are ideal, as loose soil allows tubers to expand.
- Optimal pH: 5.0-6.5; slightly acidic soils help suppress common scab.
- Avoid heavy, poorly drained soils, which cause tuber rot and misshapen tubers.
- Responds well to soils rich in organic matter, which improve moisture retention and structure.

## Soil Preparation
- Plough and harrow to produce a deep, loose, fine seedbed to a depth of 25-30 cm.
- Incorporate well-decomposed manure or compost before planting to improve structure and fertility.
- Form ridges or plan for earthing-up (hilling), as tubers must be well covered with soil.
- On sloped land, ridge along contours to reduce soil erosion.
- Avoid fields recently cropped with potato or other solanaceous crops (tomato, pepper, eggplant) to reduce disease carry-over; conduct a soil test where possible (see `soil/soil_testing_and_fertility.md`).

## Planting Season by African Region
| Region | Typical Planting Window | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| West Africa (highland/plateau areas) | Cooler months and under irrigation (roughly Oct-Feb in higher zones) | Limited to cooler highland pockets; lowlands are generally too hot |
| East Africa (highlands) | Long rains (Feb-Apr) and short rains (Sept-Nov) | Major potato-growing region; two seasons in bimodal areas |
| Southern Africa | Cool-season planting; irrigated winter crops and spring plantings | Frost is a risk in mid-winter at high altitude; time to avoid frost at emergence |
| Central Africa (highlands) | Onset of rains in bimodal zones (Feb-Mar and Sept-Oct) | High-altitude areas favour good tuber set |

## Seed or Seedling Requirements
- Potato is planted from **seed tubers**, not botanical seed; the quality of seed tubers is the single most important factor in yield.
- Use certified, disease-free seed tubers of a variety adapted to local altitude, market, and disease pressure (including late blight tolerance where available).
- Prefer well-sprouted ("chitted") seed tubers roughly the size of a hen's egg; large tubers can be cut into pieces, each with at least two healthy sprouts (eyes), and cut surfaces allowed to heal before planting.
- Seed rate: approximately 1.5-2.5 tonnes/ha depending on tuber size and spacing.
- Avoid using ware potatoes (table potatoes) as seed, as they often carry diseases and give poor, uneven crops.
- Green-sprout (chit) seed tubers in a cool, well-lit place for a few weeks before planting to encourage strong, short sprouts.

## Plant Spacing
- Standard spacing: approximately 75 cm between ridges/rows and 25-30 cm between plants, giving roughly 44,000-53,000 plants/ha.
- Closer in-row spacing produces more but smaller tubers (useful for seed production); wider spacing produces fewer, larger tubers (useful for ware/table market).
- Plant seed tubers 10-15 cm deep, with sprouts facing upward, on well-formed ridges.

## Water Requirements
- Requires consistent soil moisture; both drought and waterlogging reduce yield and tuber quality.
- Critical water-sensitive stages are tuber initiation (around flowering) and tuber bulking.
- Water stress during tuber bulking reduces size, while erratic watering can cause growth cracks and misshapen or hollow tubers.
- Where irrigation is used, avoid waterlogging, which promotes tuber rots; see `irrigation/irrigation_methods.md`.

## Fertiliser Recommendations
- Basal application at planting: a compound fertiliser supplying phosphorus, nitrogen, and potassium, placed in the planting furrow and covered before the seed tuber to avoid direct contact.
- Topdress with nitrogen at earthing-up (hilling) to support foliage and tuber growth.
- Potato has a high potassium demand; ensure adequate potassium for good tuber quality and storage.
- General guideline in the absence of a soil test: balanced N, P, and K with emphasis on phosphorus at planting and potassium for tuber quality, adjusted to yield target and soil fertility.
- Apply well-decomposed manure or compost to improve soil structure and moisture retention.
- See `fertilisers/fertiliser_basics.md` for application methods and timing principles.

## Weed Management
- Keep the crop weed-free during the first 4-6 weeks, before the canopy closes.
- Earthing-up (hilling) around 3-5 weeks after emergence controls weeds, covers developing tubers to prevent greening, and improves drainage; a second earthing-up may be done later.
- Hand weeding and hilling with a hoe are the most common methods; avoid deep cultivation that damages tubers.
- See `weeds/general_weed_management.md` for integrated weed control principles.

## Pest Management
- Key pests: potato tuber moth, aphids (which transmit viruses), cutworms, and, in some regions, potato cyst nematodes.
- Use clean, certified seed tubers to avoid introducing tuber moth and nematodes.
- Practice deep and thorough earthing-up to keep tubers covered, as exposed tubers are more prone to tuber moth attack.
- Rotate with non-solanaceous crops and remove volunteer potato plants and crop debris.
- Where chemical control is needed, use only locally registered products and confirm active-ingredient choices with an extension officer.
- See `pests/aphids.md` for guidance on aphids as virus vectors.

## Disease Management
- Key diseases: late blight (the most destructive), bacterial wilt, early blight, common scab, and various viruses.
- Late blight can destroy a crop within days in cool, wet weather; use tolerant varieties, ensure good airflow, and apply preventive control measures early where blight pressure is high.
- Use certified, disease-free seed tubers and practice rotation of at least 2-3 seasons away from solanaceous crops to manage bacterial wilt and other soil-borne diseases.
- Remove and destroy diseased plants and volunteer potatoes; do not compost visibly diseased material.
- Suspected bacterial wilt or severe blight outbreaks warrant prompt consultation with a qualified extension officer; see `emergency_guides/crop_emergency_guide.md`.

## Growth Stages
1. Sprout development and emergence (0-2 weeks after planting).
2. Vegetative growth: leaf and stem development (2-5 weeks).
3. Tuber initiation: tubers begin to form, usually around flowering (approximately 5-7 weeks).
4. Tuber bulking: rapid tuber growth and dry matter accumulation (7-12 weeks).
5. Maturation: foliage yellows and dies back, and tuber skins set, marking readiness for harvest.

## Harvest Indicators
- Foliage naturally yellows and dies back (senescence), signalling that tubers have matured.
- Tuber skin "sets" (does not rub off easily when pressed with the thumb), which is important for storage.
- For ware (table) potatoes, allow full maturity and skin set; for early or fresh market ("new potatoes"), harvest earlier while skins are still thin.
- Cut and remove haulms (foliage) a couple of weeks before harvest where late blight is present, to prevent spores reaching the tubers.
- Total time from planting to harvest is typically 3-4.5 months depending on variety and climate.

## Post-Harvest Handling
- Harvest in dry conditions and avoid bruising or cutting tubers, as wounds allow rot organisms to enter.
- Cure freshly harvested tubers in a cool, dark, well-ventilated place for one to two weeks to allow skins to toughen and minor wounds to heal.
- Sort out green, damaged, rotting, or diseased tubers before storage; green tubers contain bitter compounds and should not be eaten.
- Keep seed tubers separate from ware tubers and handle them especially carefully.

## Storage
- Store tubers in a cool, dark, well-ventilated place; darkness prevents greening and light exposure of ware tubers.
- Ware potatoes for eating store best at cool temperatures (roughly 7-10°C) with good ventilation; very cold storage can make tubers taste sweet.
- Seed tubers can be stored in diffused (indirect) light to encourage short, sturdy green sprouts for the next planting.
- Inspect stored tubers regularly and remove any that are rotting to prevent spread.
- See `storage/perishable_storage.md` for general guidance on storing perishable produce.

## Expected Yield
- Smallholder systems with basic management and uncertain seed quality: approximately 5-15 tonnes/ha.
- Improved management with certified seed, adequate fertiliser, and good blight control: approximately 20-30 tonnes/ha.
- High-input commercial systems: 30-40+ tonnes/ha.
- Actual yield depends heavily on seed quality, variety, altitude, water, and especially late blight management.

## Common Mistakes
- Using ware potatoes or informally saved tubers as seed, spreading disease and giving weak, uneven crops.
- Planting in fields recently cropped with potato or other solanaceous crops, increasing bacterial wilt and blight pressure.
- Inadequate earthing-up, leading to greened tubers and higher tuber moth damage.
- Delaying late blight control until symptoms are widespread, by which time the crop can be lost.
- Storing bruised, wet, or diseased tubers, leading to rapid rot in storage.

## Best Farming Practices
- Use certified, disease-free, well-sprouted seed tubers of an adapted variety.
- Rotate potato with non-solanaceous crops (e.g., cereals, legumes) for at least 2-3 seasons.
- Earth up (hill) thoroughly and in good time to cover tubers and control weeds.
- Manage late blight proactively in cool, wet conditions with tolerant varieties and timely, locally approved control measures.
- Cure and sort tubers carefully before storage, keeping seed and ware potatoes separate.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Why are some of my potato tubers turning green, and are they safe to eat?**
A: Greening is caused by exposure to light, either in the field (from tubers not being covered) or during storage. Green tubers develop bitter compounds and should not be eaten. Prevent greening by earthing-up well in the field and storing tubers in the dark.

**Q: Can I save tubers from this year's harvest to plant next season?**
A: You can, but there is a real risk of carrying over diseases and viruses that reduce yield over time. If you do save seed, select healthy, medium-sized tubers only from disease-free plants, store them in diffused light, and consider refreshing with certified seed periodically. Certified seed tubers generally give the most reliable results.

**Q: My potato plants collapsed suddenly and the cut stem oozes a cloudy liquid. What is this?**
A: A sudden wilt with cloudy ooze from the cut stem can indicate bacterial wilt, a serious soil-borne disease. Avoid replanting potato or other solanaceous crops in that field for several seasons, do not use tubers from affected plants as seed, and consult a qualified extension officer for confirmation and guidance.

**Q: How do I reduce the risk of late blight?**
A: Choose blight-tolerant varieties where available, ensure good airflow through proper spacing, avoid prolonged leaf wetness, and act early with locally approved preventive measures when cool, wet weather sets in. Removing haulms before harvest during blight outbreaks also helps protect the tubers.
