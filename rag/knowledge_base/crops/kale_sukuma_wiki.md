# Kale (Sukuma Wiki)

## Overview
Kale, widely known in East Africa as sukuma wiki, is one of the most important leafy vegetables grown by smallholder farmers in Kenya, Tanzania, and Uganda, and it is increasingly common across the rest of the continent. The Swahili name sukuma wiki means "push the week", reflecting its role as an affordable, dependable green that helps households stretch food supplies between paydays. Unlike its close relative cabbage, kale is a non-heading brassica: it produces an upright stem bearing a rosette of broad, often curled or flat leaves that are harvested repeatedly over several months rather than as a single head. This "cut-and-come-again" habit, in which the lower and outer leaves are picked while the growing point keeps producing new foliage, makes kale a continuous source of income and nutrition from a single planting.

Kale is hardy, fast-establishing, and adaptable across altitudes and seasons, from cool highlands to warm lowlands under irrigation. It is a heavy nitrogen feeder because leaf production must be sustained over a long picking period. As a member of the cabbage family, kale shares the full brassica pest and disease complex, so its management overlaps substantially with cabbage; see `crops/cabbage.md`. Careful rotation away from other brassicas, sound soil fertility, steady moisture, and integrated pest management are the foundations of a productive kale crop.

## Scientific Name
*Brassica oleracea* var. *acephala* (Acephala Group). The varietal name *acephala* means "without a head", distinguishing kale from heading cabbage (*Brassica oleracea* var. *capitata*). Many local sukuma wiki types are open-pollinated collards within this same group; curly-leaf kales and thousand-headed types also belong here.

## Suitable Climate
- Optimal growing temperature: 15-25°C; kale grows actively across a wider temperature band than cabbage and is more tolerant of warmth.
- Grows well from cool highlands (above 1,500 m) to warm mid-altitude and lowland areas when moisture is adequate.
- Tolerates light frost; brief cold can even improve leaf flavour, but hard freezes damage foliage.
- Sustained heat above roughly 30°C reduces leaf quality, encourages bitterness and toughness, and speeds up bolting (premature flowering).
- Requires roughly 500-700 mm of water spread evenly across a long harvest cycle, from rainfall and/or irrigation.
- Performs best under mild, consistent conditions; it can be grown year-round in cool highland zones and during cooler seasons or under irrigation in warmer areas.

## Suitable Soil
- Prefers fertile, well-drained loam or sandy loam soils that are rich in organic matter and retain moisture without waterlogging.
- Optimal pH: 6.0-7.0; slightly limed soils in the upper part of this range help suppress clubroot on acidic land.
- Tolerates a range of soil textures but produces poorly in shallow, infertile, or waterlogged soils.
- Deep, well-structured soils support the long root system needed to sustain many months of leaf harvest.
- See `soil/soil_types_africa.md` for matching kale to local soils and `soil/soil_testing_and_fertility.md` for testing before planting.

## Soil Preparation
- Plough and harrow to a fine, level tilth suitable for small transplants or direct-seeded rows.
- Incorporate well-decomposed manure or compost (15-25 tonnes/ha) before planting to build fertility, improve structure, and support long-term leaf production.
- Form raised beds or ridges in heavy soils and high-rainfall areas to improve drainage and reduce root disease.
- Avoid fields recently cropped with other brassicas (cabbage, rape, cauliflower, broccoli, mustard) to reduce carry-over of clubroot, black rot, and diamondback moth.
- Where possible, conduct a soil test to guide lime and fertiliser decisions; see `soil/soil_testing_and_fertility.md`.

## Seed or Seedling Requirements
- Kale can be **raised in a nursery and transplanted** (the most common commercial method) **or direct-seeded** in the field where labour or seedling supply is limited.
- Use certified or good-quality open-pollinated seed of a locally adapted variety; many smallholders save seed from open-pollinated sukuma wiki types.
- **Nursery raising:** Sow seed thinly in well-prepared, fertile nursery beds or trays; keep beds moist and lightly shaded until germination, then harden off gradually in full sun. Seedlings are ready to transplant at about 3-5 weeks, when they have 4-6 true leaves and a sturdy stem.
- Seed rate: approximately 250-500 g/ha for nursery raising; higher for direct seeding, followed by thinning to the target spacing.
- Harden seedlings by reducing watering and increasing sun exposure for several days before transplanting.
- Transplant in the cool of late afternoon or on a cloudy day and water in well to reduce transplant shock.
- Direct-seeded stands should be thinned early to avoid crowding, which encourages weak plants and disease.

## Plant Spacing
- Standard spacing: approximately 45-60 cm between rows and 30-45 cm between plants.
- Closer spacing gives more but smaller plants and higher early leaf yield per unit area; wider spacing gives larger, longer-lived plants that are easier to manage and pick.
- Plant seedlings to the base of the first true leaves and firm the soil around the stem for good anchorage during the long harvest period.
- Adequate spacing improves airflow, which lowers foliar disease pressure and eases repeated picking.

## Water Requirements
- Requires consistent, even soil moisture throughout the long harvest cycle to sustain steady leaf production.
- Critical water-sensitive periods are establishment after transplanting or thinning, and the intervals immediately after each harvest, when the plant is pushing out new leaves.
- Moisture stress makes leaves tough, small, and bitter and can trigger premature bolting; alternating drought and heavy watering reduces quality.
- Drip or furrow irrigation is preferred over overhead watering, which prolongs leaf wetness and encourages black rot and downy mildew; see `irrigation/irrigation_methods.md`.
- For guidance on timing and amounts across the season, see `irrigation/irrigation_scheduling.md`.

## Fertiliser Recommendations
- Kale is a **heavy nitrogen feeder** because leaves are removed continuously; nitrogen must be replenished regularly to keep new foliage tender and green.
- Basal application at transplanting: a compound fertiliser supplying phosphorus and nitrogen to support root and early leaf establishment.
- Topdress with nitrogen in repeated small splits through the harvest period, for example after every one or two picking rounds, to sustain leaf regrowth.
- Ensure adequate potassium for plant vigour and disease resistance, and adequate calcium and magnesium to prevent leaf disorders and yellowing.
- Well-decomposed manure or compost applied at land preparation, supplemented with liquid organic feeds during cropping, supports both fertility and soil structure; see `fertilisers/organic_fertilisers.md`.
- In the absence of a soil test, follow balanced nutrition with a strong, repeated nitrogen supply adjusted to soil fertility and observed leaf colour; pale, yellowing older leaves usually indicate nitrogen shortage. See `fertilisers/fertiliser_basics.md` and `fertilisers/nutrient_deficiencies_guide.md`.

## Weed Management
- Keep the crop weed-free during the first several weeks after transplanting or thinning, before the canopy develops.
- Weed by hand or with shallow cultivation to avoid damaging the shallow feeder roots.
- Mulching between rows suppresses weeds, conserves moisture, and keeps soil temperatures steady.
- Control brassica-family weeds (wild mustard and related plants) promptly, as they host shared pests and diseases.
- See `weeds/general_weed_management.md` for integrated weed control principles.

## Pest Management
- Key pests: **diamondback moth** (the most damaging), cabbage aphid and other aphids, whitefly, cabbage sawfly and loopers, cutworms, and flea beetles.
- Diamondback moth larvae chew holes in leaves and quickly develop resistance to chemicals; rely on integrated pest management combining regular scouting, biological control (for example naturally occurring parasitic wasps and *Bacillus thuringiensis*-based products), field sanitation, and rotation of active-ingredient classes.
- Aphids cluster on leaf undersides and shoot tips, sucking sap and transmitting viruses; conserve natural enemies such as ladybird beetles and hoverflies, and see `pests/aphids.md`.
- Whitefly also sucks sap and vectors viruses; see `pests/whitefly.md`. Thrips can occasionally scar leaves; see `pests/thrips.md`.
- Cutworms sever young transplants at soil level at night; flea beetles pit the leaves of young plants. Both are worst at establishment.
- Scout at least twice weekly, focusing on leaf undersides, the growing point, and newly expanded leaves.
- Because kale leaves are eaten, observe pre-harvest intervals strictly and prefer low-toxicity options on a crop that is harvested continuously. Use only locally registered products and confirm active-ingredient choices, dosages, and safe pre-harvest intervals with a qualified agricultural extension officer.
- For severe infestations, see `emergency_guides/pest_outbreak_emergency_guide.md`.

## Disease Management
- Key diseases: **black rot** (caused by *Xanthomonas campestris*), downy mildew, Alternaria leaf spot, damping-off in the nursery, clubroot, and Fusarium wilt (yellows).
- Black rot produces V-shaped yellow lesions at the leaf margins with blackened veins; it spreads in warm, wet, overcrowded conditions. Use clean seed, avoid overhead irrigation, and remove infected leaves and debris.
- Downy mildew and Alternaria leaf spot cause discoloured, spotted foliage in humid weather; improve airflow through spacing and manage overhead moisture.
- Clubroot swells and distorts roots, stunting plants; it is very persistent in acidic soils. Manage by liming acidic land, improving drainage, and practising strict rotation.
- Damping-off kills seedlings in the nursery; sow thinly, avoid overwatering, and use well-drained media.
- **Rotate away from all brassicas for at least 2-3 seasons**, use clean planting material, and maintain field sanitation to break disease cycles.
- Where copper-based fungicides or other products are considered, confirm locally registered, approved products and dosages with a qualified agricultural extension officer, and respect pre-harvest intervals on this frequently picked crop. See `emergency_guides/crop_emergency_guide.md` for severe or unclear situations.

## Growth Stages
1. Nursery/seedling stage (0-3-5 weeks before transplanting), or germination and thinning for direct-seeded crops.
2. Establishment after transplanting or thinning (1-2 weeks), when roots take hold.
3. Vegetative growth: rapid production of the leaf rosette and stem elongation.
4. First harvest maturity: the plant carries enough leaves that lower and outer leaves can be picked (about 4-8 weeks after transplanting).
5. Repeated harvest (cut-and-come-again) phase: continuous leaf picking and regrowth sustained over several months with good feeding and watering.
6. Decline/bolting: with age, heat, or stress the plant eventually elongates and flowers, and leaf quality falls, signalling the end of the productive cycle.

## Harvest Indicators
- First harvest is possible once plants are well established and carry a good number of fully expanded leaves, typically 4-8 weeks after transplanting.
- Harvest the **lower and outer mature leaves** first, snapping or cutting them off cleanly at the stem while leaving the growing point and younger inner leaves to continue producing.
- Pick leaves while they are still tender and dark green; over-mature leaves become tough, fibrous, and bitter.
- Under good management, plants can be picked every 1-2 weeks over several months.
- Harvest in the cool part of the day to preserve freshness and reduce wilting.

## Post-Harvest Handling
- Harvest into the cool early morning or late afternoon to reduce field heat and slow wilting.
- Handle leaves gently to avoid bruising, tearing, and crushing, which accelerate yellowing and rot.
- Kale leaves lose water and wilt quickly; keep them shaded and, where possible, sprinkle lightly or cover with a damp cloth to maintain freshness.
- Sort out damaged, yellowed, insect-eaten, or diseased leaves and tie tender leaves into uniform bunches for market.
- Wash with clean, safe water if leaves will be sold ready to cook, and avoid contamination from soil, manure, or dirty water. Kale is eaten cooked, which reduces some risks, but clean handling still protects food safety and market appeal.
- See `harvesting/harvesting_best_practices.md` for general harvest hygiene.

## Storage
- Kale is highly perishable and stores far less well than heading cabbage; it is best sold and consumed within 1-3 days of picking under ambient tropical conditions.
- Cool, humid conditions extend freshness; leaves keep for several days to about a week in cold storage but wilt rapidly in heat and sun.
- Keep bunches out of direct sun, in shade with good airflow, and lightly moistened to reduce moisture loss.
- Do not pack leaves tightly in sealed sacks, which causes heating and rapid rot; use ventilated crates or loose bundles.
- Because of short shelf life, stagger plantings and pick to order rather than storing large quantities. See `storage/perishable_storage.md` for general guidance on storing perishable produce.

## Expected Yield
- Yield is measured as total leaf harvest over the whole cropping period rather than a single cut.
- Smallholder systems with basic management: approximately 10-20 tonnes/ha over the season.
- Improved management with quality seedlings, adequate repeated nitrogen, good water, and effective pest control: approximately 20-40 tonnes/ha.
- High-input systems with intensive fertigation and pest management: 40-60+ tonnes/ha over an extended picking period.
- Actual yield depends heavily on variety, plant population, soil fertility, the length of the harvest period, and pest/disease control.

## Common Mistakes
- Continuous brassica cropping without rotation, allowing clubroot, black rot, and diamondback moth to build up.
- Under-feeding nitrogen during the long harvest period, resulting in pale, small, tough leaves and declining yield.
- Irregular watering, which makes leaves bitter and fibrous and triggers premature bolting.
- Stripping too many leaves at once or removing the growing point, which exhausts the plant and shortens its productive life.
- Delaying diamondback moth control or relying on a single chemical, allowing resistant populations to develop.
- Harvesting over-mature leaves that are tough and unmarketable, and holding cut leaves too long in the heat so they wilt and yellow.

## Best Farming Practices
- Choose locally adapted varieties and stagger plantings so leaves are available and marketable throughout the year.
- Raise strong, uniform seedlings or thin direct-seeded stands early, and space plants for good airflow.
- Feed nitrogen in repeated small splits and maintain steady soil moisture to sustain tender leaf regrowth.
- Practise cut-and-come-again harvesting: pick only lower and outer mature leaves and always leave the growing point and enough foliage to power regrowth.
- Use integrated pest management for the brassica pest complex, rotating active-ingredient classes and conserving natural enemies; prefer low-toxicity options and respect pre-harvest intervals on this continuously picked crop.
- Rotate away from all brassicas for at least 2-3 seasons and maintain strict field sanitation to break pest and disease cycles.
- Handle and cool harvested leaves quickly, and sell promptly because kale does not store well.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: How is kale (sukuma wiki) different from cabbage if they are the same species?**
A: Kale and cabbage are both forms of *Brassica oleracea*, but kale is a non-heading type grown for repeated leaf harvests, while cabbage forms a single dense head harvested once. Kale tolerates warmth better and produces continuously over months. They share the same pest and disease complex, so much of the management is similar; see `crops/cabbage.md`.

**Q: How often can I harvest leaves from one kale plant?**
A: Under good feeding and watering, you can pick the lower and outer mature leaves every 1-2 weeks over several months. Always leave the growing point and enough younger leaves to sustain the plant, and never strip it bare, or regrowth will slow and the plant will decline.

**Q: Why are my kale leaves small, tough, and bitter?**
A: This usually points to nitrogen shortage, heat stress, irregular watering, or leaving leaves on the plant too long. Feed nitrogen in repeated splits, keep soil moisture even, grow in cooler seasons or shade in hot areas, and pick leaves while they are still young and tender.

**Q: My kale leaves are full of small holes and I see little green caterpillars. What are they?**
A: These are most likely diamondback moth larvae or related brassica caterpillars, the most damaging kale pests. Scout leaf undersides and growing points at least twice weekly, remove caterpillars by hand in small plots, use biological options such as *Bacillus thuringiensis*, and rotate active-ingredient classes to avoid resistance. Confirm registered products, dosages, and pre-harvest intervals with an extension officer.

**Q: Can I grow kale in the same plot every season since it grows so well?**
A: No. Continuous brassica cropping builds up clubroot, black rot, and diamondback moth. Rotate away from all brassicas (cabbage, rape, cauliflower, mustard) for at least 2-3 seasons, and follow with unrelated crops such as legumes or cereals to break the pest and disease cycles.

**Q: The edges of my kale leaves are turning yellow with black veins. What is wrong?**
A: These are classic symptoms of black rot, a bacterial disease caused by *Xanthomonas* that spreads in warm, wet, crowded conditions. Use clean seed, avoid overhead watering, improve spacing and airflow, remove and destroy affected leaves and debris, and rotate away from brassicas. See `emergency_guides/crop_emergency_guide.md` for severe cases.

**Q: Is it safe to eat sukuma wiki that had insect damage or was sprayed?**
A: Kale is normally eaten cooked, which reduces some risks, but leaves should still be washed in clean, safe water and any diseased or heavily damaged leaves discarded. If any product was applied, strictly observe the pre-harvest interval before picking and eating, and confirm safe intervals with a qualified extension officer.

**Q: How should I sell kale so it stays fresh and fetches a good price?**
A: Harvest in the cool of the day, keep leaves shaded and lightly moist, and tie tender, dark-green leaves into clean, uniform bunches. Sort out yellowed or damaged leaves. Because kale wilts quickly, pick to order and sell within a day or two rather than storing large quantities; see `marketplace/product_grading_and_quality.md`.
