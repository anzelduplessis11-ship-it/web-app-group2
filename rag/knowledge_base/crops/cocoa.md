# Cocoa

## Overview
Cocoa is a perennial tropical tree grown for its seeds (beans), which are fermented, dried, and processed into cocoa and chocolate. West Africa dominates world production, with Côte d'Ivoire and Ghana together supplying the majority of global cocoa, alongside significant output from Nigeria, Cameroon, and growing production in parts of Central and East Africa. Cocoa is the backbone of millions of smallholder livelihoods. It is an understorey forest species that grows best under partial shade, begins bearing three to five years after planting, and can remain productive for several decades. Because cocoa is sold on quality-graded international markets, correct harvesting of ripe pods, proper fermentation, and thorough drying are decisive for flavour, bean grade, and price. Poor fermentation or drying, or contamination with mould and off-flavours, downgrades otherwise good beans. Cocoa integrates well with agroforestry and climate-smart shade systems. For grading and quality standards see `marketplace/product_grading_and_quality.md`, and for value addition see `pricing/price_negotiation_and_value_addition.md`.

## Scientific Name
*Theobroma cacao* L., family Malvaceae. Major cultivated groups include Forastero (hardy, high-yielding, bulk cocoa), Criollo (fine-flavour but delicate and lower-yielding), and Trinitario (hybrids combining traits).

## Suitable Climate
- Cocoa is a lowland humid-tropics crop, growing best at temperatures of approximately 21-32°C with high humidity.
- It requires abundant, well-distributed rainfall of about 1,250-2,500 mm per year, with no long dry season.
- It is grown at low to mid altitudes, typically below 800 m, in the equatorial belt.
- Cocoa is highly sensitive to drought, strong winds, frost, and prolonged dry spells; young trees especially need protection.
- Partial shade, particularly during establishment, is essential to protect trees from heat and moisture stress; see `weather/climate_smart_agriculture.md`.
- Deep, consistent humidity favours flowering and pod development throughout much of the year.

## Suitable Soil
- Deep, well-drained, fertile loams rich in organic matter are ideal; cocoa roots are very sensitive to waterlogging.
- A pH range of approximately 5.0-7.0 is suitable, with slightly acidic to neutral soils preferred.
- Soils should be at least 1-1.5 m deep to allow strong rooting and drought buffering.
- Free drainage is essential; avoid low-lying, flood-prone, or heavy waterlogged sites.
- Test soil before establishment to identify acidity and nutrient constraints; see `soil/soil_testing_and_fertility.md`.

## Soil Preparation
- Establish cocoa under managed shade, retaining selected forest trees or planting temporary shade (such as bananas/plantains) and permanent shade trees.
- Clear undergrowth and mark planting positions along lines; on slopes, follow the contour and install erosion controls; see `soil/soil_conservation_and_erosion.md`.
- Dig planting holes about 60 cm wide and deep, mixing topsoil with well-decomposed manure or compost.
- Provide temporary shade for young seedlings using plantain, cassava, or other cover before permanent shade establishes.
- Prepare land and shade in advance so transplanting coincides with reliable rains.

## Planting Season by African Region
| Region | Typical Planting Window | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| West Africa (Côte d'Ivoire, Ghana, Nigeria) | April-June (main rains) | Transplant at onset of reliable rains; provide temporary shade |
| Central Africa (Cameroon, Gabon, DRC) | March-May and Sept-Nov | Bimodal rainfall allows two windows |
| East Africa (Uganda, Tanzania lowlands) | March-May (long rains) | Limited to warm, humid lowland niches |
| Humid equatorial zones generally | Start of a reliable wet period | Avoid establishing before a dry season |
| Irrigated/riverine sites | Onset of rains, supplemented by irrigation | Young trees need continuous moisture |

## Seed or Seedling Requirements
- Cocoa is established from nursery-raised seedlings or, increasingly, from grafted/clonal plants of improved varieties.
- Use seed from recommended hybrid pods or clonal material selected for yield, disease resistance (for example to black pod and swollen shoot), and bean quality.
- Raise seedlings in polybags in a shaded nursery for approximately 4-6 months until they have several true leaves and are hardened off.
- Grafting or budding of high-performing clones onto rootstocks is used to propagate elite material and rehabilitate old farms.
- Approximately 1,000-1,300 plants are needed per hectare depending on spacing; raise extra for gap-filling.
- Obtain planting material from certified nurseries or registered programmes to avoid spreading disease, especially cocoa swollen shoot virus.

## Plant Spacing
- Common spacing is approximately 3 m by 3 m, giving about 1,100 trees per hectare, with shade trees at wider intervals overhead.
- Some systems use around 2.5-3.5 m spacing depending on variety vigour and shade level.
- Establishment years (years 1-3): trees grow vegetatively under temporary and permanent shade; intercrop with food crops (plantain, cassava, cocoyam) to use space and earn income.
- Bearing years (year 3-5 onward): the canopy closes, intercropping is phased out, and management shifts to pruning, sanitation, and harvesting.
- Maintain permanent shade trees to provide moderate shade over the mature cocoa canopy.

## Water Requirements
- Cocoa needs high, evenly distributed moisture, about 1,250-2,500 mm per year, with no prolonged dry spell.
- Young trees are extremely vulnerable to drought and must be protected with shade and mulch during establishment.
- Critical moisture-sensitive periods are flowering, pod set, and pod filling; stress causes flower and cherelle (young pod) abortion.
- In areas with a marked dry season, mulching and, where feasible, supplementary irrigation improve survival and yield; see `irrigation/irrigation_scheduling.md`.
- Good drainage is equally vital, as waterlogging causes root death and disease.

## Fertiliser Recommendations
- Base fertiliser decisions on soil and, where possible, leaf analysis; see `soil/soil_testing_and_fertility.md` and `fertilisers/fertiliser_basics.md`.
- Cocoa removes significant potassium and nitrogen in harvested pods; phosphorus supports root and early growth.
- Apply fertilisers in splits during the rains for efficient uptake, and keep them clear of the trunk base.
- Return pod husks (composted) and prunings to the soil to recycle nutrients, especially potassium; see `fertilisers/organic_fertilisers.md`.
- Maintain organic matter with mulch and shade-tree leaf litter, which is central to cocoa nutrition on many smallholder farms.
- Correct micronutrient deficiencies (for example zinc) where diagnosed; see `fertilisers/nutrient_deficiencies_guide.md`.

## Weed Management
- Keep young cocoa weed-free, as weeds compete strongly with slow-growing seedlings during establishment.
- Once the canopy and shade close, weed pressure falls sharply because the shaded understorey suppresses most weeds.
- Use mulch from prunings, husks, and leaf litter to smother weeds, conserve moisture, and build soil.
- Slash or hand-weed rather than bare-cultivating to protect shallow roots and soil structure.
- Where herbicides are used, apply only locally registered products carefully, avoiding contact with cocoa stems and drift; see `weeds/general_weed_management.md` and `regulations/pesticide_safety_and_handling.md`.

## Pest Management
- Key pests include cocoa mirids/capsids (sap-sucking bugs that damage shoots and pods and open the way for disease), pod-boring insects, mealybugs (which transmit swollen shoot virus), stem borers, and rodents that gnaw pods.
- Practise integrated pest management: regular scouting, canopy and shade management, sanitation, and targeted control only when justified.
- Maintain moderate, even shade and an open canopy through pruning to reduce capsid and disease pressure.
- Remove and destroy diseased and damaged pods promptly (sanitation harvesting) to break pest and disease cycles.
- Control ants and mealybugs to limit spread of swollen shoot virus.
- When chemical control is necessary, use recommended active-ingredient classes and confirm locally registered products with a qualified agricultural extension officer; observe pre-harvest intervals; see `regulations/pesticide_safety_and_handling.md`.

## Disease Management
- Major diseases include black pod disease (a fungal/oomycete disease causing black, rotting pods, worst in wet conditions), cocoa swollen shoot virus disease (spread by mealybugs, causing swellings, leaf symptoms, dieback, and yield collapse), and various stem and root diseases.
- Plant tolerant or resistant varieties where available and use clean, certified planting material.
- Practise strict sanitation: regularly remove and destroy diseased pods and prune for airflow to lower humidity around pods.
- For swollen shoot virus, promptly uproot and destroy infected trees and control mealybug vectors; replant with clean material.
- Where protective fungicide sprays against black pod are justified in wet seasons, use recommended active-ingredient classes, confirm registered products locally, and time applications to protect developing pods.
- Improve drainage and shade balance, as excessive shade and humidity worsen black pod.

## Growth Stages
For this perennial tree, growth is described across establishment and bearing years:
1. Nursery stage (0-6 months): seed germinates or grafts establish; seedlings raised under shade.
2. Establishment (years 1-3): vegetative growth of the stem to the "jorquette" (branching point) and canopy formation under shade; no meaningful yield.
3. Early bearing (years 3-5): first flowering and pods, increasing yearly; flowers form directly on the trunk and older branches (cauliflory).
4. Full bearing (year 5-25+): steady production with two or more main harvest peaks per year in many areas, following the flowering flushes.
5. Pod development cycle: pollinated flowers set small "cherelles" (many of which naturally abort), and surviving pods take approximately 5-6 months to ripen.
6. Decline and rehabilitation: aging or diseased farms are rehabilitated through pruning, gap-filling, grafting, or staged replanting with improved clones.

## Harvest Indicators
- Harvest pods when fully ripe: ripe pods change colour (green pods turn yellow; red/purple pods turn orange-yellow) and sound hollow when tapped.
- Harvest ripe pods regularly, roughly every one to three weeks during peak season, to avoid over-ripening and losses.
- Cut pods carefully from the tree with a knife or pruning tool without damaging the "cushions" on the trunk where future flowers form.
- Do not harvest unripe pods (poor bean quality and fermentation) or leave over-ripe/diseased pods on the tree (disease reservoir).

## Post-Harvest Handling
- Break pods and extract the wet beans with their pulp within a few days of harvest; discard diseased or germinated beans.
- Ferment the beans (a critical step for flavour) in heaps covered with banana leaves, or in fermentation boxes, for approximately 5-7 days, turning them to ensure even fermentation.
- Proper fermentation develops chocolate flavour precursors and reduces bitterness and astringency; under- or over-fermentation lowers grade and price.
- After fermentation, dry the beans slowly and evenly, ideally on raised platforms or mats, turning regularly, until moisture reaches approximately 7-7.5%.
- Avoid drying on bare soil, over roads, or over smoky fires, which cause contamination and smoky off-flavours that downgrade beans.
- Sort out flat, broken, mouldy, or germinated beans; well-fermented, well-dried, clean beans command the best price; see `marketplace/product_grading_and_quality.md`.

## Storage
- Store dried beans (approximately 7-7.5% moisture) in clean, dry, well-ventilated stores in breathable jute bags on pallets off the floor and away from damp walls.
- Keep beans away from moisture, strong odours, fuel, and agrochemicals, which cocoa readily absorbs, causing taint and rejection.
- Under-dried beans develop mould and off-flavours, including the risk of ochratoxin; ensure thorough drying before storage.
- Monitor stored beans for moisture, insect pests (such as storage moths and beetles), and mould, and treat or remove affected stock.
- Store for the shortest practical time and maintain lot records for traceability; see `storage/grain_storage.md`.

## Expected Yield
- Smallholder farms with basic management commonly yield approximately 300-600 kg of dried beans per hectare.
- Well-managed farms with good varieties, shade, nutrition, and pest/disease control can yield approximately 800-1,500 kg or more per hectare.
- Yields typically rise from early bearing to a peak in the middle years and decline as trees age unless rehabilitated.
- Actual yield depends on variety, shade, soil fertility, rainfall, tree age, and the intensity of black pod and swollen shoot management.

## Common Mistakes
- Harvesting unripe or diseased pods together with ripe ones, reducing bean quality and grade.
- Poor or skipped fermentation, producing purple, bitter, low-value beans.
- Drying too fast, on bare ground, or over smoky fires, causing contamination and off-flavours.
- Leaving diseased pods and debris in the field, allowing black pod and pests to build up.
- Ignoring swollen shoot virus, letting infected trees and mealybugs spread the disease across the farm.
- Establishing cocoa without adequate shade and drainage, leading to stress and heavy losses.

## Best Farming Practices
- Use improved, disease-tolerant planting material from certified sources, and manage moderate, even shade.
- Prune regularly to maintain an open canopy, improve airflow, and ease harvesting and spraying.
- Practise sanitation harvesting: remove and destroy diseased and over-ripe pods to control black pod and capsids.
- Harvest only ripe pods, ferment fully (5-7 days), and dry slowly and cleanly to correct moisture.
- Recycle pod husks and prunings as compost/mulch to sustain fertility, and feed based on soil analysis.
- Adopt IPM and minimise pesticide use; control mealybugs and remove swollen-shoot-infected trees promptly.
- Keep records and pursue group marketing, certification, and traceability to improve prices; see `pricing/price_negotiation_and_value_addition.md` and `sustainability/sustainable_farming_practices.md`.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Why do my beans turn out bitter and purple instead of brown?**
A: Purple, bitter beans usually indicate poor or insufficient fermentation. Ferment wet beans for about 5-7 days in heaps or boxes, turning them for even fermentation, before drying. Proper fermentation develops chocolate flavour and raises grade and price.

**Q: How long before cocoa starts producing pods?**
A: Cocoa begins bearing about three to five years after transplanting and reaches full production around year five to seven. With good management it remains productive for several decades, and old farms can be rehabilitated rather than fully replanted.

**Q: What is causing black, rotting pods on my trees?**
A: This is black pod disease, favoured by wet, humid, heavily shaded conditions. Manage it by pruning for airflow, balancing shade, removing and destroying infected pods promptly, improving drainage, and using recommended protective fungicides where justified.

**Q: My trees show swellings and abnormal leaves and are declining. What is it?**
A: These are symptoms of cocoa swollen shoot virus disease, spread by mealybugs. There is no cure for infected trees; uproot and destroy them, control mealybugs, and replant with clean, certified material. Report suspected outbreaks to your extension officer.

**Q: Do I need shade trees for cocoa?**
A: Yes, especially during establishment and increasingly as a climate adaptation. Moderate, even shade protects young trees from heat and drought, supports beneficial insects, and provides extra products. Too much dense shade, however, lowers yield and worsens black pod, so shade must be managed.

**Q: Why are many young pods (cherelles) shrivelling and dying?**
A: Some cherelle wilt is natural, as the tree sheds pods it cannot support. Excessive loss can also result from moisture stress, poor nutrition, or capsid/disease damage. Maintain moisture, balanced nutrition, and pest control to keep more pods to maturity.

**Q: How should I dry my beans to get the best price?**
A: Dry fermented beans slowly and evenly on raised platforms or clean mats, turning regularly, to about 7-7.5% moisture. Avoid bare soil, roadsides, and smoky fires, which cause contamination and off-flavours. Clean, well-fermented, well-dried beans earn the highest grade. See `marketplace/product_grading_and_quality.md`.

**Q: Can I add value to my cocoa?**
A: Careful fermentation, drying, and sorting to premium grade is the most important value addition for most smallholders. Group marketing, certification (such as sustainability or fine-flavour schemes), and traceability can raise prices further. See `pricing/price_negotiation_and_value_addition.md`.
