# Green Gram / Mung Bean (Vigna radiata)

## Overview
Green gram, widely known as mung bean, is a short-duration grain legume valued across Africa for its fast maturity, drought escape, and high-quality protein. It is grown chiefly for its small green (sometimes yellow or black) dry seeds, which are eaten boiled, sprouted, milled into flour, or split into dhal. In many African markets green gram fetches a comparatively good price and is favoured by traders because clean, uniform grain stores and transports well. Because it matures in roughly 60-90 days, it fits neatly into short rainy seasons, residual soil moisture windows after a cereal, and as a relay or catch crop between main crops.

As a legume, green gram fixes atmospheric nitrogen through root-nodule bacteria, improving soil fertility and making it an excellent rotation and intercropping partner for cereals such as maize, sorghum, and pearl millet. It is well suited to smallholder, low-input systems in semi-arid zones: it needs little fertiliser, tolerates heat, and can produce a useful harvest on modest rainfall. Its main weaknesses are sensitivity to waterlogging, susceptibility to several insect pests and diseases during flowering and podding, and heavy losses to storage bruchid weevils if grain is not protected. Green gram is closely related to cowpea (`crops/cowpea.md`) and black gram, and many of the same agronomic and storage principles apply.

## Scientific Name
*Vigna radiata* (L.) R. Wilczek. Family Fabaceae (Leguminosae), subfamily Faboideae. It is commonly called green gram, mung bean, moong, or golden gram. It is closely related to black gram (*Vigna mungo*) and cowpea (*Vigna unguiculata*).

## Suitable Climate
- Warm-season crop with an optimal temperature range of approximately 25-35 degrees Celsius; it tolerates heat well but is sensitive to frost.
- Grows on approximately 300-700 mm of well-distributed rainfall per season; because of its short cycle it can escape drought that would damage longer-duration crops.
- Best adapted to low and mid-altitude areas, generally below about 1,500-1,800 m; growth slows and maturity is delayed at higher, cooler elevations.
- Requires a warm, relatively dry ripening period; continuous rain or high humidity during flowering and pod maturity encourages disease and pre-harvest sprouting.
- Photoperiod-sensitive in some varieties; choose locally adapted varieties so flowering coincides with adequate moisture. See `weather/climate_smart_agriculture.md`.

## Suitable Soil
- Performs best on well-drained sandy loams to loams with good structure and moderate fertility.
- Tolerates a soil pH of approximately 6.0-7.5; slightly acidic to neutral soils are ideal. Strongly acidic soils (below about pH 5.5) reduce nodulation and yield.
- Does not tolerate waterlogging or heavy, poorly drained clays, which cause root rots, poor nodulation, and yellowing.
- Tolerates moderately low fertility better than many crops because of nitrogen fixation, but responds well to improved soil organic matter.
- Salinity and hardpans reduce root development; avoid compacted or saline patches. See `soil/soil_types_africa.md` and `soil/soil_testing_and_fertility.md`.

## Soil Preparation
- Prepare a firm, reasonably fine, level seedbed for good seed-to-soil contact and even emergence; green gram seed is small and should not be buried too deeply.
- One or two light cultivations are usually enough; excessive tillage wastes moisture in semi-arid zones. Minimum tillage or planting into residue conserves soil water and protects structure.
- Incorporate well-rotted manure, compost, or crop residues ahead of planting to improve infiltration and moisture retention; see `fertilisers/organic_fertilisers.md`.
- On sloping land, cultivate along the contour and maintain field bunds or strips to reduce erosion and conserve moisture; see `soil/soil_conservation_and_erosion.md`.
- Ensure good drainage: form shallow furrows or raised beds in areas prone to short spells of waterlogging.
- Where feasible, base any fertiliser decisions on a soil test; see `soil/soil_testing_and_fertility.md`.

## Seed or Seedling Requirements
- Green gram is direct-seeded; it is not transplanted.
- Use certified or good-quality seed of a locally adapted variety. Where available, choose improved varieties with uniform, synchronous maturity, disease tolerance (especially to yellow mosaic and powdery mildew), and bold, marketable grain.
- Typical seed rate for a sole crop: approximately 12-25 kg/ha, adjusted for seed size, spacing, and germination. Use lower rates when intercropping.
- Test germination before planting if seed is farm-saved; discard shrivelled, discoloured, cracked, or weevil-holed seed, which carries storage insects and germinates poorly.
- Where green gram has not been grown recently, inoculating seed with the appropriate rhizobia (cowpea/Vigna group) can markedly improve nodulation and yield on responsive soils.
- A seed dressing against soil-borne fungi and early insect pests can help where these are a known problem; confirm locally registered, approved products and rates with a qualified agricultural extension officer.

## Plant Spacing
- Common sole-crop spacing: approximately 30-45 cm between rows and 10-15 cm between plants within the row, giving roughly 250,000-400,000 plants/ha.
- Erect, bushy varieties can be spaced more closely; spreading types need slightly wider spacing.
- Sowing depth: approximately 2-4 cm. Shallow planting into moist soil gives quick, even emergence; deeper sowing delays and weakens seedlings.
- When intercropped with cereals such as maize, sorghum, or millet, green gram is planted between or within the cereal rows at reduced density to limit competition for light and water.
- Row planting (rather than broadcasting) is strongly recommended because it eases weeding, scouting, spraying, and harvesting.

## Water Requirements
- Total seasonal water requirement is approximately 300-500 mm, depending on climate, variety, and cycle length.
- The most water-sensitive stages are germination/establishment, flowering, and pod filling; stress at flowering and pod fill causes flower drop and shrivelled grain, sharply reducing yield.
- Green gram tolerates short dry spells during vegetative growth but should not be stressed at flowering.
- Avoid excess water, especially late in the season: waterlogging and high humidity during ripening promote root rot, foliar disease, pod rot, and pre-harvest sprouting.
- In irrigated systems, apply light, frequent irrigation rather than heavy flooding; withhold water as pods mature to hasten even drying. See `irrigation/irrigation_scheduling.md` and `irrigation/irrigation_methods.md`.

## Fertiliser Recommendations
- As a legume, green gram meets much of its nitrogen need through fixation and usually requires little or no nitrogen fertiliser. A small "starter" dose of nitrogen may help early growth on very poor soils or where nodulation is slow.
- Phosphorus is the most important nutrient for green gram: it supports root growth, nodulation, flowering, and pod set. A basal phosphorus application benefits phosphorus-deficient soils.
- Potassium and sulphur may be needed where soils are deficient; apply based on a soil test rather than routinely.
- Rhizobium inoculation is a low-cost way to boost nitrogen fixation where green gram or related Vigna legumes have not been grown recently.
- Well-rotted manure or compost improves fertility, structure, and moisture-holding capacity over time and is valuable where available.
- Base rates on a soil test and confirm locally suitable products with an extension officer. See `soil/soil_testing_and_fertility.md`, `fertilisers/fertiliser_basics.md`, `fertilisers/nutrient_deficiencies_guide.md`, and `fertilisers/organic_fertilisers.md`.

## Weed Management
- Green gram competes poorly with weeds in the first 3-4 weeks; this early period is the critical weed-free window that most affects yield.
- First weeding: about 2-3 weeks after emergence. Second weeding: about 4-5 weeks after emergence, before the canopy closes.
- Once a good crop closes its canopy it suppresses later weeds, reducing the need for further weeding.
- Hand hoeing and hand pulling are the most common smallholder methods; weed shallowly to avoid damaging the crop's shallow roots.
- Clean, weed-free seed and row planting make weeding faster and reduce carry-over of weed seed.
- Where herbicides are used, only pre-emergence or selective products registered for green gram should be applied, strictly at label rates; confirm choices with an extension officer. Watch for the parasitic weed Striga in cereal rotations. See `weeds/general_weed_management.md` and `weeds/striga.md`.

## Pest Management
- Key field pests include aphids (which also transmit viruses), whiteflies (vectors of yellow mosaic virus), thrips, jassids/leafhoppers, pod-sucking bugs, pod borers (including legume pod borer and the African bollworm complex), and bean flies/stem maggots that attack seedlings.
- The most damaging storage pest is the bruchid weevil (pulse beetle), which often begins infesting pods in the field and can destroy stored grain within weeks.
- Practise integrated pest management (IPM): start with clean seed and resistant varieties; scout the crop at least weekly, especially from flowering through pod fill; encourage natural enemies; and use rotation and field hygiene to break pest cycles.
- Cultural controls include timely planting to escape peak pest periods, removing crop residues and volunteers, and avoiding continuous legume cropping on the same land.
- Apply chemical control only when scouting shows pests exceeding economic thresholds, choosing an active-ingredient class appropriate to the pest (for example, insecticidal soaps or botanical products such as neem for aphids and thrips; targeted insecticides for pod borers). Rotate chemical classes to slow resistance, protect pollinators by not spraying open flowers midday, and always confirm locally registered, approved products, rates, and pre-harvest intervals with a qualified agricultural extension officer.
- See `pests/aphids.md`, `pests/whitefly.md`, `pests/thrips.md`, `pests/african_bollworm.md`, and for storage pests `pests/grain_weevils_and_borers.md` and `storage/grain_storage.md`.

## Disease Management
- Key diseases include mungbean yellow mosaic virus (whitefly-transmitted, one of the most damaging), powdery mildew (favoured by cool, dry, cloudy conditions late in the season), Cercospora leaf spot, anthracnose, bacterial leaf spot, root rots and damping-off (in waterlogged or over-wet soils), and pod/seed rots in humid conditions.
- Use resistant or tolerant varieties where available and always plant clean, certified, disease-free seed.
- Control whiteflies and aphids to limit the spread of viral diseases, since vector management is the main defence against yellow mosaic.
- Rotate with non-legume crops (cereals) for at least one to two seasons, ensure good drainage and spacing for airflow, and remove and destroy infected residues.
- For fungal diseases such as powdery mildew and leaf spots, protectant or systemic fungicide classes (for example, sulphur-based products or triazole fungicides) may be used when disease pressure is high; confirm locally registered, approved products and dosages with a qualified agricultural extension officer, and prioritise cultural and varietal control first.
- For suspected severe or unfamiliar outbreaks, consult an extension officer promptly. See `troubleshooting/crop_troubleshooting_guide.md` and the related legume disease reference `diseases/bean_anthracnose.md`.

## Growth Stages
1. **Germination and emergence (0-7 days after planting):** seed imbibes water and the seedling emerges; even soil moisture is essential for a uniform stand.
2. **Vegetative growth (about 7-30 days):** leaves, branches, and root nodules develop; nitrogen fixation becomes established. Keep this early period weed-free.
3. **Flowering (about 30-50 days):** small yellow flowers appear and set pods; this is a critical, moisture- and pest-sensitive stage. Avoid water stress and protect flowers and young pods from insects.
4. **Pod development and filling (about 45-70 days):** pods elongate and seeds fill; adequate moisture and pest control determine grain size and quality.
5. **Maturity and drying (about 60-90 days after planting):** pods change from green to black or brown, seeds harden, and the crop dries down, ready for harvest. Maturity is often uneven, so pods ripen in flushes.

## Harvest Indicators
- Pods change colour from green to their mature black or brown and become dry and papery; seeds inside are hard and rattle.
- Because pods mature in flushes and can shatter when over-dry, growers commonly make two to three pickings, harvesting mature pods and returning as more ripen.
- Alternatively, where varieties have synchronous maturity, the whole crop is cut or pulled once most pods (roughly 80-90%) have turned dark and dried.
- Harvest in the cooler morning hours to reduce shattering losses, but ensure the crop is dry, not wet with dew, before threshing.
- Do not delay harvest of fully mature pods: over-ripe pods shatter and scatter grain, and field bruchid infestation increases.

## Post-Harvest Handling
- Harvest mature pods promptly to reduce shattering losses and to limit storage bruchid infestation that begins in the field.
- Dry harvested pods or plants further on a clean tarpaulin, mat, or raised drying surface (not bare soil, which contaminates grain and risks aflatoxin-producing moulds and stones); see `storage/aflatoxin_prevention.md`.
- Thresh only after adequate drying, by gentle beating in bags or with sticks, to avoid cracking the seed coat; damaged seed loses market value and stores poorly.
- Winnow to remove chaff, dust, and pod debris, then dry the clean grain to a safe moisture level of approximately 12-13% (grain is hard and cannot be dented by a fingernail).
- Sort and grade to remove immature, shrivelled, discoloured, mouldy, or insect-holed seed, and stones or foreign matter, as clean uniform grain earns a better price. See `harvesting/harvesting_best_practices.md` and `marketplace/product_grading_and_quality.md`.

## Storage
- Store only thoroughly dried, clean, well-sorted grain in a cool, dry, well-ventilated place protected from moisture, rodents, and insects.
- The bruchid weevil (pulse beetle) is the main storage threat and can rapidly destroy grain. Hermetic (airtight) storage bags or sealed containers are highly effective and chemical-free: they deprive the insects of oxygen and stop reproduction.
- Traditional low-cost protectants such as thoroughly mixed ash or food-grade inert dusts, and solar or sun heating of grain before sealing, can help reduce weevil damage in small quantities.
- If chemical grain protectants are used, apply only locally approved products at label rates and observe safety and pre-consumption intervals; confirm with an extension officer. See `regulations/pesticide_safety_and_handling.md`.
- Store bags off the floor on pallets and away from walls; inspect stored grain regularly for insects, heating, or moisture, and remove affected stock immediately.
- See `storage/grain_storage.md` for full guidance.

## Expected Yield
- Smallholder rainfed systems with basic management: approximately 0.3-0.8 tonne/ha of dry grain.
- Improved management with good seed, correct spacing, timely weeding, and effective pest control: approximately 1.0-1.8 tonnes/ha.
- Under favourable conditions with well-adapted improved varieties and strong field and storage pest management, yields of about 2.0 tonnes/ha or more are achievable.
- Actual yield depends heavily on variety, rainfall distribution, insect and disease pressure (especially yellow mosaic virus and pod borers), and post-harvest losses to bruchids. Values vary widely by locality, season, and variety.

## Common Mistakes
- Neglecting whitefly and aphid control, allowing yellow mosaic and other viruses to spread and cut yields severely.
- Failing to protect flowers and pods from insect pests during the critical flowering-to-pod-fill window.
- Planting on poorly drained land or over-irrigating, causing root rot, poor nodulation, and yellowing.
- Applying nitrogen fertiliser unnecessarily and skipping phosphorus, wasting inputs since the crop fixes its own nitrogen but responds to phosphorus.
- Storing grain without protection against the bruchid weevil, leading to rapid destruction of the harvest.
- Delaying harvest of mature pods, increasing shattering losses and field carry-over of storage insects.
- Broadcasting seed instead of row planting, making weeding, scouting, spraying, and harvesting harder.

## Best Farming Practices
- Rotate and intercrop green gram with cereals such as maize, sorghum, and pearl millet to exploit its nitrogen fixation, spread risk, and improve total land productivity; see `sustainability/sustainable_farming_practices.md`.
- Use good-quality, well-adapted, disease-tolerant seed and consider rhizobium inoculation where the crop has not been grown recently.
- Plant in rows at the correct depth into moist soil for even establishment, and keep the crop weed-free for the first month.
- Scout weekly from flowering onward and use IPM, applying only locally registered products when thresholds are exceeded.
- Manage soil and water with climate-smart practices: conserve moisture, ensure drainage, maintain organic matter, and cultivate on the contour on slopes.
- Harvest promptly, dry grain thoroughly on a clean surface, grade for market, and use hermetic storage to control bruchids without chemicals.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Why are my green gram leaves turning yellow with a mosaic pattern, and the plants stunted?**
A: This is typical of mungbean yellow mosaic virus, spread by whiteflies. There is no cure once a plant is infected. Prevent it by planting resistant or tolerant varieties, using clean seed, and controlling whiteflies early. Remove severely infected plants and see `pests/whitefly.md`.

**Q: Do I need to apply nitrogen fertiliser to green gram?**
A: Generally no. As a legume, green gram fixes most of its own nitrogen through root nodules. Focus on phosphorus where soils are deficient, and consider rhizobium inoculation where green gram has not been grown recently. Adding nitrogen is usually an unnecessary expense.

**Q: Weevils keep destroying my stored green gram. How can I protect it?**
A: The bruchid (pulse) beetle often starts infesting pods in the field and multiplies rapidly in storage. Harvest promptly, dry grain thoroughly, sort out damaged seed, and store in hermetic (airtight) bags or sealed containers, which suffocate the insects and are very effective without chemicals. See `storage/grain_storage.md`.

**Q: My plants flowered well but set very few pods. What went wrong?**
A: Poor pod set is commonly caused by water stress at flowering, high heat, or insect pests such as thrips and pod-sucking bugs damaging flowers and young pods. Ensure adequate moisture at flowering, scout regularly, and manage pests using IPM with locally approved products.

**Q: Can I grow green gram after harvesting my maize?**
A: Yes. Because green gram matures in about 60-90 days, it fits well as a rotation or relay crop after or between cereals, using residual soil moisture. This also breaks pest and disease cycles and improves soil nitrogen for the next cereal crop.

**Q: How do I know when green gram is ready to harvest?**
A: The crop is ready when pods turn from green to black or brown, become dry and papery, and the seeds are hard. Because pods ripen in flushes, you may need two or three pickings, or harvest the whole crop once most pods have dried. Harvest before pods shatter.

**Q: What causes a white powdery coating on the leaves late in the season?**
A: This is powdery mildew, favoured by cool, cloudy, dry conditions during late growth. Use tolerant varieties, ensure good spacing for airflow, and if pressure is high a sulphur-based or triazole fungicide class may be used. Confirm locally registered products and dosages with an extension officer.

**Q: Is green gram profitable for smallholders and easy to sell?**
A: Green gram is often a good cash legume because it matures quickly, needs few inputs, and is in steady demand, with clean uniform grain preferred by buyers. Prices vary by locality, season, and grain quality, so grade well and check current market demand; see `marketplace/buyer_preferences_and_seasonal_demand.md`.
