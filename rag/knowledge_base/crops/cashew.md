# Cashew

## Overview
Cashew is a hardy, drought-tolerant tree crop grown widely across the coastal and savanna belts of West Africa, with major production in Côte d'Ivoire, Nigeria, Benin, Guinea-Bissau, Ghana, and Tanzania and Mozambique in East and Southern Africa. It is valued both for its kernel, which supplies a large export market, and for the cashew apple, a fleshy fruit used locally for juice, wine, and animal feed, though the apple is far less commercialised than the nut. Cashew tolerates poor, sandy, and marginal soils where few other cash crops thrive, and requires relatively low input once established, making it an important source of income for smallholders on land unsuited to more demanding crops. Trees begin bearing within three to four years of grafted or improved seedling planting and can remain productive for 25-30 years or more. The main production challenges are powdery mildew, which can devastate flowering flushes in humid seasons, inconsistent quality of unimproved seedling orchards, and the need for careful drying and storage to prevent kernel spoilage and aflatoxin contamination. Renewed investment in grafted planting material, orchard rejuvenation, and improved on-farm processing is steadily raising both yields and farm-gate value across the West African cashew belt.

## Scientific Name
*Anacardium occidentale* L. (family Anacardiaceae).

## Suitable Climate
- Cashew thrives in warm tropical lowland climates from sea level up to about 600-800 m altitude; higher elevations delay flowering and reduce yield.
- Optimal mean temperatures are 20-30°C; the tree tolerates temperatures up to 40°C but is sensitive to cold and cannot withstand frost.
- Cashew requires a distinct dry season of four to six months for good flowering and fruit set; it performs poorly where rainfall is continuous or very high year-round, since humid conditions during flowering favour powdery mildew and anthracnose.
- Annual rainfall of 800-1,500 mm is ideal; the tree is notably drought tolerant once established, making it well suited to semi-arid savanna margins.
- Strong coastal winds are generally well tolerated, and cashew is often used as a windbreak and soil-stabilising tree on sandy coastal soils.

## Suitable Soil
- Cashew is remarkably adaptable and grows well on poor, sandy, lateritic, and even degraded soils where many other crops fail.
- It performs best on deep, well-drained sandy loam soils; optimal pH is 5.0-6.5, though the tree tolerates a wider range.
- Good drainage is essential; cashew does not tolerate waterlogged or heavy clay soils, which promote root rot.
- Its deep taproot system allows it to access moisture in the dry season even on poor soils, contributing to its drought resilience. See `soil/soil_types_africa.md`.

## Soil Preparation
- Clear the site of woody vegetation, stumps, and termite mounds before planting; cashew is often planted on land being reclaimed from bush fallow.
- Dig planting holes approximately 60 x 60 x 60 cm, incorporating well-decomposed manure or compost into the backfilled topsoil, particularly on very poor sandy sites.
- On sloping or erosion-prone land, establish contour planting lines or bunds to conserve soil and moisture. See `soil/soil_conservation_and_erosion.md`.
- Deep ripping or subsoiling on compacted or lateritic soils before planting improves early root penetration.

## Seed or Seedling Requirements
- Grafted seedlings from proven high-yielding, disease-tolerant clones are strongly preferred over raising trees directly from seed nuts, as grafted trees bear earlier, more uniformly, and with better nut quality.
- Where seed nuts are used (common in traditional smallholdings), select large, healthy nuts from high-yielding mother trees and float-test in water or brine to discard poor-quality (floating) nuts before nursery sowing.
- Raise seedlings in the nursery for eight to twelve weeks, hardening them under reduced shade before transplanting.
- Choose planting material adapted to local rainfall patterns and resistant to powdery mildew where this disease is a known local problem.

## Plant Spacing
- Standard spacing for traditional cashew orchards is 8 x 8 m to 10 x 10 m (100-156 trees/ha).
- Improved, more intensively managed orchards with pruning and canopy management may use 7 x 7 m (about 200 trees/ha).
- Wider spacing (12 x 12 m) suits very poor, low-fertility soils where tree vigour and canopy spread will be limited.
- Adequate spacing supports airflow through the canopy, which reduces powdery mildew pressure and eases harvesting of fallen nuts.

## Water Requirements
- Established cashew trees are highly drought tolerant and are typically grown without irrigation under rainfed conditions across the savanna and coastal belts.
- Young trees (first one to two years) benefit from supplemental watering during prolonged dry spells to ensure establishment, roughly 10-20 litres per tree per week if rainfall is inadequate.
- A well-defined dry season is beneficial for flower induction; excessive irrigation or rainfall during the flowering period increases disease pressure and reduces fruit set.
- Mature orchards rarely require irrigation except in unusually severe or prolonged droughts affecting flowering and nut development.

## Fertiliser Recommendations
- Young trees benefit from modest nitrogen and phosphorus applications in the first two to three years to support establishment and canopy development.
- Bearing trees respond well to balanced nitrogen, phosphorus, and potassium applications, with potassium particularly supporting nut fill; apply in split doses at the start and end of the rains.
- Apply 10-20 kg of well-rotted manure or compost per tree annually where available, especially on the poor sandy soils typical of cashew-growing areas.
- Because cashew is often grown on nutrient-poor soils, periodic soil testing helps identify limiting nutrients, particularly potassium and micronutrients such as zinc and boron. See `fertilisers/nutrient_deficiencies_guide.md`, `fertilisers/organic_fertilisers.md`, and `soil/soil_testing_and_fertility.md`.

## Weed Management
- Maintain a weed-free basin around young trees for the first two to three years to reduce competition for scarce moisture and nutrients.
- Mulching with crop residue, grass, or leaf litter conserves soil moisture, particularly valuable on the sandy soils where cashew is commonly grown, and suppresses weeds.
- Slashing rather than ploughing between mature tree rows limits erosion and preserves surface feeder roots.
- Intercropping cashew with groundnut, cowpea, or cassava during the first two to three establishment years is common practice and helps offset the low income during this period while keeping the orchard floor managed. See `weeds/general_weed_management.md`.

## Pest Management
- Cashew helopeltis (mosquito bug) and other sap-sucking bugs damage young shoots, flowers, and developing nuts, causing blackened lesions and dieback; monitor at flowering and manage through sanitation and, where necessary, targeted spraying.
- Coreid bugs and stink bugs damage nuts and apples during development, reducing marketable yield.
- Termites can attack young trees and root systems, especially on poor sandy soils; protect young trees with appropriate soil treatment at planting. See `pests/termites.md`.
- Cashew stem and root borers weaken trees over time; remove and destroy heavily infested wood.
- Practise integrated pest management: scout regularly from flowering through nut development, protect natural predators, and use locally registered products only where thresholds justify it, following `regulations/pesticide_safety_and_handling.md`.

## Disease Management
- Powdery mildew (*Oidium anacardii*) is the most damaging cashew disease, attacking flower panicles and young nuts in cool, humid conditions and causing severe flower and nut drop; manage through resistant/tolerant planting material, canopy pruning for airflow, and protectant fungicide sprays timed at flowering where mildew pressure is historically high.
- Anthracnose causes leaf, twig, and inflorescence blight in humid conditions; sanitation and pruning for airflow reduce severity.
- Dieback and gummosis, often associated with fungal infection following insect or mechanical injury, can be managed by pruning out affected branches and avoiding wounds during field operations.
- Root rot occurs on poorly drained sites; avoid waterlogged soils and ensure good site drainage.
- Maintain orchard sanitation, prune for an open canopy, and consult an extension officer to confirm locally registered fungicides and correct timing.

## Growth Stages
Cashew develops from establishment through to long-term bearing over several decades:
1. **Establishment (Year 1-2):** Root and canopy development; formative pruning to build a strong, low-branching framework; protection from termites, livestock, and drought stress.
2. **Juvenile (Year 2-3):** Rapid vegetative growth; light early flowering may occur but should not be allowed to over-stress the young tree.
3. **Early bearing (Year 3-5):** First commercial harvests, with yields increasing each year as canopy size grows.
4. **Full bearing (Year 6-15 onward):** Peak, relatively stable annual production through an annual cycle of dry-season flowering, nut and apple development, and harvest.
5. **Decline/rejuvenation:** Yields decline in very old orchards (25-30 years plus); rejuvenation pruning or replanting with improved grafted material restores productivity.

## Harvest Indicators
- The nut matures before the cashew apple fully ripens; ripe nuts turn grey-brown and detach easily, typically falling to the ground with the attached apple.
- Fully ripe apples show characteristic yellow, red, or orange colouring depending on variety and separate readily from the nut, which can then be twisted or cut off.
- Harvest by collecting fallen nuts and apples daily during the peak season to reduce losses to rot, pests, and livestock.
- Nuts collected should be sun-dried briefly before storage or transport for processing to reduce moisture content immediately after harvest.

## Post-Harvest Handling
- Separate the nut from the apple promptly; the apple is highly perishable and should be consumed, processed into juice or wine, or discarded quickly, while the nut can be dried and stored.
- Sun-dry raw nuts on clean mats or raised racks for two to three days, turning regularly, until the nuts rattle when shaken and reach a safe moisture content for storage.
- Sort nuts to remove immature, damaged, or diseased nuts, and grade by size where required by buyers. See `marketplace/product_grading_and_quality.md`.
- Avoid drying nuts directly on bare ground, which introduces soil moisture, contamination, and stones. See `harvesting/harvesting_best_practices.md`.

## Storage
- Store dried raw cashew nuts in clean, dry, well-ventilated jute or breathable sacks, off the ground on pallets, in a covered, rodent-proof store.
- Maintain low moisture content (below about 10%) to prevent mould growth and aflatoxin contamination during storage; periodically check stored nuts for moisture uptake, especially in humid seasons. See `storage/aflatoxin_prevention.md`.
- Avoid storing nuts in direct contact with damp floors or walls, and inspect regularly for pest infestation and mould.
- Processed kernels are far more susceptible to rancidity and pest damage than raw nut-in-shell and should be stored under cooler, moisture-controlled conditions and sold or used promptly. See `storage/perishable_storage.md`.

## Expected Yield
- Yield rises with tree age: young bearing trees (3-5 years) may produce 1-3 kg of raw nuts per tree, while mature trees (10 years and older) can yield 8-15 kg or more per tree per year under good management.
- On a per-hectare basis, traditional unimproved smallholder orchards often yield only 300-500 kg of raw nuts per hectare, while well-managed orchards with improved grafted material can achieve 1-2 tonnes per hectare.
- Kernel recovery from raw nut-in-shell is typically around 20-25% by weight.
- Poor planting material, powdery mildew outbreaks, and delayed or inadequate drying are the most common reasons actual yields and quality fall short of potential.

## Common Mistakes
- Relying entirely on unselected seed-nut seedlings, resulting in highly variable tree vigour, yield, and nut quality across the orchard.
- Neglecting powdery mildew control during flowering, leading to severe flower and nut drop in humid seasons.
- Leaving fallen nuts and apples uncollected for extended periods, increasing losses to rot, insects, and rodents.
- Drying nuts insufficiently or on bare, damp ground before storage, causing mould and aflatoxin risk.
- Allowing orchards to become old and unpruned without rejuvenation, resulting in declining yields over time.
- Ignoring soil fertility management on the poor sandy soils typical of cashew belts, limiting long-term productivity.

## Best Farming Practices
- Establish orchards with grafted, high-yielding, disease-tolerant planting material suited to local rainfall and disease pressure.
- Prune young trees to a strong, open, low-branching framework and maintain airflow through the canopy in mature trees to reduce powdery mildew.
- Collect fallen nuts and apples daily during peak harvest and dry nuts promptly and thoroughly before storage.
- Intercrop with legumes or food crops during the establishment years to generate early income and improve soil fertility.
- Maintain basic soil fertility through organic matter and balanced fertiliser use, guided by soil testing where possible.
- Rejuvenate or replant ageing, low-yielding orchards with improved grafted material to sustain long-term productivity. See `sustainability/sustainable_farming_practices.md`.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: How long does a cashew tree take to start bearing nuts?**
A: Grafted trees typically begin bearing within three to four years, with commercially significant yields by year five to six. Seedling trees grown from seed nuts take longer, generally five to six years, and give more variable results.

**Q: Why are my cashew flowers and young nuts turning black and dropping?**
A: This is a typical symptom of powdery mildew, which thrives in cool, humid conditions during flowering. Improve airflow through pruning, use tolerant planting material where available, and apply a protectant fungicide programme timed to flowering if the disease is a recurring problem in your area.

**Q: Can cashew be grown on poor, sandy soil?**
A: Yes. Cashew is one of the most soil-tolerant tree crops and performs well on poor, sandy, and lateritic soils that are unsuitable for many other cash crops, provided drainage is adequate and some organic matter and fertiliser are supplied.

**Q: What should I do with the cashew apple after removing the nut?**
A: The apple is highly perishable and should be used quickly, either consumed fresh, processed into juice, jam, or wine, or fed to livestock, since it spoils within a day or two of harvest and is not typically stored.

**Q: Why do stored cashew nuts sometimes develop mould?**
A: Mould usually results from inadequate drying before storage or storage in humid, poorly ventilated conditions. Nuts should be sun-dried until they rattle when shaken and stored in breathable sacks off the ground in a dry, covered store.

**Q: Does cashew need irrigation?**
A: Established cashew is drought tolerant and is usually grown as a rainfed crop. Only young trees during establishment, or mature trees in unusually severe droughts affecting flowering and nut fill, typically need supplemental watering.
