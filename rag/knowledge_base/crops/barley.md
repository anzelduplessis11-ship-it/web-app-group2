# Barley

## Overview
Barley is an ancient cool-season cereal grown across the African highlands and Mediterranean fringe, valued for its hardiness, short growing season, and ability to yield on marginal soils where wheat struggles. In Ethiopia, where barley has been cultivated for thousands of years, it is a highland staple used for foods such as roasted grain, porridge, flatbreads, and traditional beverages, and Ethiopia holds one of the world's richest reserves of barley diversity. Barley is also important in the highlands of Eritrea, Kenya, and Tanzania, and across North Africa (Morocco, Algeria, Tunisia, Egypt) as both a food and animal-feed crop and, increasingly, as a malting crop for the brewing industry. Barley tolerates cold, drought, and salinity better than most cereals, matures quickly, and fits well into rotations. It is broadly grown as two types: food/feed barley and malting barley, the latter requiring specific grain-quality standards. Farmers also distinguish two-row and six-row barley by the number of grain rows on the head. Main production challenges include acidic highland soils, frost, foliar diseases such as scald and net blotch, and meeting the quality specifications demanded by maltsters.

## Scientific Name
*Hordeum vulgare* L., family Poaceae (Gramineae). Two-row barley is sometimes referred to as *Hordeum vulgare* subsp. *distichon* and six-row barley as subsp. *vulgare*.

## Suitable Climate
- Barley is a cool-season crop; optimal growing temperatures are approximately 12-25°C.
- More cold- and frost-tolerant than wheat during vegetative growth, but flowering remains frost-sensitive.
- Grown in tropical Africa mainly at high altitude, typically 2,000-3,500 m in the Ethiopian and East African highlands, and in the cool season at lower elevations in North Africa.
- Requires approximately 300-500 mm of water over the season; it is notably more drought-tolerant than wheat and can be grown on drier and shorter-season sites.
- Matures faster than wheat (often 90-130 days), allowing it to escape late-season drought and to be grown at the highest, coldest cropping altitudes.
- Sensitive to prolonged waterlogging and to very hot, dry conditions during grain filling, which shrivel the grain.

## Suitable Soil
- Performs best on well-drained loam to clay-loam soils but tolerates poorer, shallower, and stonier soils than most cereals.
- Optimal pH is approximately 6.0-7.8; barley is the most salt-tolerant of the common cereals and does relatively well on alkaline and mildly saline soils.
- Barley is moderately sensitive to strong acidity and aluminium toxicity, which are common in highland soils and reduce yields; liming helps on acidic sites.
- Does not tolerate waterlogging; heavy, poorly drained soils cause root disease and yellowing.
- Fertile, moisture-retentive soils give the best grain quality for both food and malting.
- Test soil to guide lime and fertiliser use; see `soil/soil_testing_and_fertility.md`.

## Soil Preparation
- Prepare a fine, firm, level seedbed for uniform shallow seeding, as barley grain is small and needs good soil contact.
- One ploughing followed by harrowing is usually sufficient; barley establishes well on moderately prepared land.
- On acidic highland soils, incorporate agricultural lime several weeks before sowing based on a soil test to reduce aluminium toxicity and improve nutrient availability.
- On heavy soils prone to waterlogging, use raised beds or ensure surface drainage.
- Incorporate crop residues, compost, or well-rotted manure to build organic matter and moisture-holding capacity; see `fertilisers/organic_fertilisers.md`.
- In dry areas, conserve moisture by minimising the delay between final tillage and sowing and by keeping the seedbed weed-free.

## Seed or Seedling Requirements
- Barley is direct-sown from seed and is not transplanted.
- Use certified or good-quality seed of a variety matched to your purpose (food, feed, or malting) and to local disease pressure; malting requires specific approved varieties to meet buyer specifications.
- Seed rate: approximately 80-125 kg/ha for drilled or broadcast sowing, adjusted for seed size, germination, and seedbed quality.
- Target roughly 200-300 plants per square metre.
- Sow 3-5 cm deep into moist soil; barley emerges vigorously but still suffers from too-deep placement.
- Treat seed against seed-borne smuts and seedling diseases where these are common; confirm locally registered, approved seed dressings and rates with a qualified agricultural extension officer.
- For malting barley, protect grain quality from the seed stage onward, as damaged or diseased grain fails malting standards.

## Plant Spacing
- Barley is drilled or broadcast rather than planted at fixed spacing.
- Drilled rows are typically 15-25 cm apart, with seed distributed continuously within the row.
- Narrow rows and even seed spread improve early ground cover and weed suppression.
- When broadcasting, distribute seed evenly and lightly harrow to cover to a consistent depth.
- A uniform, competitive stand matters more than exact geometry.

## Water Requirements
- Total seasonal water requirement is approximately 300-500 mm.
- The most sensitive stages are tillering, stem elongation, heading, and grain filling.
- Barley is more drought-tolerant than wheat, but moisture stress at flowering reduces grain set and stress during filling shrivels the grain and lowers malting quality.
- Under irrigation, water at establishment, tillering, booting, and grain filling; avoid waterlogging, which barley tolerates poorly.
- Excess late moisture and high humidity encourage foliar diseases and can cause lodging.
- Match variety maturity to expected season length so grain filling completes before terminal drought.

## Fertiliser Recommendations
- Base rates on a soil test; see `soil/soil_testing_and_fertility.md` and `fertilisers/fertiliser_basics.md`.
- Nitrogen: typically 40-90 kg N/ha. For food and feed barley, moderate-to-good nitrogen raises yield and protein. For malting barley, deliberately limit and time nitrogen carefully, because excess nitrogen raises grain protein above malting specifications and reduces malt quality.
- Phosphorus: apply the full requirement (commonly 15-30 kg P/ha) as a basal dressing at sowing to support rooting and tillering, especially in cool soils.
- Potassium where soils are deficient; many highland soils supply enough, but confirm by testing.
- On acidic highland soils, address associated micronutrient issues and aluminium toxicity through liming and targeted amendments.
- Split nitrogen (part at sowing, part at tillering) to improve efficiency; avoid heavy single doses that cause lodging.
- Incorporate manure or compost for long-term fertility; see `fertilisers/organic_fertilisers.md`.
- After a legume crop in rotation, reduce nitrogen accordingly.

## Weed Management
- Weeds compete most in the first 3-6 weeks; barley's vigorous early growth makes it more competitive than many cereals, but early weeding still protects yield and quality.
- Common weeds include wild oats, ryegrass, and various broadleaf species; wild oats are especially troublesome and hard to distinguish from the crop when young.
- A dense, uniform, well-fertilised stand and narrow rows suppress weeds effectively.
- Hand weeding or hoeing at 25-35 days after emergence is effective on smallholdings.
- Use clean seed and crop rotation to reduce weed carry-over, especially wild oats.
- Selective grass and broadleaf herbicides are used in larger systems; correct identification, timing, and mode-of-action rotation are essential. Confirm locally registered, approved products and rates with a qualified agricultural extension officer.
- See `weeds/general_weed_management.md`.

## Pest Management
- Key field pests include aphids (which transmit barley yellow dwarf virus, a major yield reducer), armyworms, cutworms, and, in drier areas, termites.
- Barley yellow dwarf virus causes yellowing/reddening and stunting; managing aphid vectors and using tolerant varieties reduces its impact. See `pests/aphids.md`.
- Birds such as quelea can damage ripening crops and require scaring and coordinated control.
- Storage pests include grain weevils, the lesser grain borer, and grain moths; see the Storage section and `storage/grain_storage.md`.
- Practise integrated pest management (IPM): scout regularly, conserve natural enemies, use tolerant varieties, and time planting to avoid peak pest pressure.
- Where chemical control is justified, use the appropriate active-ingredient class only when scouting thresholds are exceeded, and confirm locally registered, approved products and dosages with a qualified agricultural extension officer.

## Disease Management
- Major foliar diseases include scald, net blotch, spot blotch, barley leaf rust, and powdery mildew; these are favoured by cool, humid highland conditions.
- Seed-borne and head diseases include loose and covered smut and Fusarium head blight.
- Barley yellow dwarf virus (aphid-transmitted) is an important viral disease.
- The most effective controls are growing resistant or tolerant varieties, using clean certified/treated seed, rotating with non-cereal crops, and removing or ploughing down infected residues.
- Avoid excessive nitrogen and overly dense canopies that trap humidity.
- Triazole and other fungicide classes manage foliar diseases when they appear early on a susceptible crop, timed around flag-leaf to heading. Confirm locally registered, approved fungicides, timing, and dosages with a qualified agricultural extension officer, and rotate fungicide groups to slow resistance.
- For malting barley, disease control is doubly important because infected grain is downgraded or rejected.

## Growth Stages
1. Germination and emergence (0-10 days after sowing).
2. Tillering (approximately 12-35 days): side shoots form; barley tillers freely.
3. Stem elongation/jointing (approximately 35-55 days): stems lengthen and the head develops within.
4. Booting and heading (approximately 50-70 days): the head emerges from the flag-leaf sheath; barley heads carry long awns.
5. Flowering (approximately 60-80 days): pollination occurs, often within or just as the head emerges.
6. Grain filling (milk to dough, approximately 75-105 days).
7. Physiological maturity and ripening (approximately 90-130 days depending on variety and altitude).

## Harvest Indicators
- The crop turns golden and the awned heads dry and bend over.
- Grains harden and cannot be dented with a thumbnail; straw becomes brittle.
- Harvest for storage when grain moisture falls to approximately 13-14% or lower.
- For malting barley, harvest at the right maturity and avoid pre-harvest sprouting, weathering, and mechanical damage, all of which reduce germination and malting quality.
- Harvest promptly, as ripe barley heads shatter and lodge readily and the brittle awns and straw increase shattering losses.

## Post-Harvest Handling
- Cut or combine when grain is dry; smallholders cut with sickles, bundle, and stack to complete drying before threshing.
- Thresh by machine, animal treading, or beating, then winnow to remove awns, chaff, and straw.
- Dry grain on clean tarpaulins or floors (never bare soil) to approximately 12-13% moisture for safe storage.
- Handle malting barley especially gently to preserve grain germination capacity; avoid cracking and overheating.
- Clean and grade grain, removing broken, shrivelled, discoloured, and foreign matter; maltsters and feed buyers reward clean, uniform grain.
- Barley straw is useful livestock fodder and bedding.

## Storage
- Store thoroughly dried grain (approximately 12-13% moisture) in a cool, dry, well-ventilated, rodent-proof store.
- Hermetic (airtight) bags or sealed silos control storage insects without chemicals and, importantly for malting barley, preserve grain germination.
- Keep malting barley cool and dry to maintain viability; overheated or damp grain loses germination and is rejected for malting.
- Inspect stored grain regularly for insects, heating, and moisture, and act quickly if problems arise.
- Use only locally approved grain protectants if needed, and confirm with a qualified agricultural extension officer.
- See `storage/grain_storage.md`.

## Expected Yield
- Smallholder rainfed highland systems with basic management: approximately 1-2.5 tonnes/ha.
- Improved varieties with adequate fertiliser and disease control: approximately 2.5-4 tonnes/ha.
- Irrigated or high-input malting production: approximately 4-6 tonnes/ha.
- Yield and quality depend on variety, altitude, disease control, water supply, soil acidity management, and timeliness; malting contracts also require meeting protein and germination standards.

## Common Mistakes
- Over-applying nitrogen to malting barley, pushing grain protein above specification and causing rejection.
- Ignoring soil acidity on highland soils, so aluminium toxicity limits yield.
- Growing susceptible varieties in humid highlands and losing yield to scald and net blotch.
- Delaying harvest so heads shatter and lodge, or allowing pre-harvest sprouting that ruins malting quality.
- Storing damp or overheated grain, losing germination (critical for malting) and encouraging insects and mould.
- Using dirty, wild-oat-contaminated seed, spreading a difficult weed.

## Best Farming Practices
- Match variety and management to end use: limit and carefully time nitrogen for malting barley; use food/feed varieties where quality premiums do not apply.
- Lime acidic highland soils based on a soil test and choose acid-tolerant varieties where relevant.
- Sow certified, disease-resistant seed at the correct rate and shallow depth for a dense, competitive stand.
- Rotate barley with legumes (faba bean, field pea, lentil, chickpea) and oilseeds to break disease and weed cycles and improve soil nitrogen; see `crops/chickpea.md`.
- Scout for aphids and foliar diseases and act early using IPM, reserving chemicals for justified situations confirmed with extension advice.
- Harvest promptly, dry and handle grain gently (especially for malting), and store cool, dry, and sealed.
- Adopt climate-smart practices such as residue retention and moisture conservation; see `sustainability/sustainable_farming_practices.md`.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: How is barley different from wheat, and why would I grow it?**
A: Barley is hardier than wheat: it tolerates cold, drought, salinity, and poorer soils better, matures faster, and can be grown at higher, drier, and more marginal sites. It is used for food, animal feed, and malting for brewing. Wheat generally makes better bread and can yield more on good land, but barley is more reliable on tough sites.

**Q: What is malting barley and why does nitrogen matter so much for it?**
A: Malting barley is grown to specific quality standards for brewing. Maltsters need moderate, uniform grain protein and high germination. Too much nitrogen raises grain protein above specification and can cause rejection, so nitrogen is limited and carefully timed. Food and feed barley are less strict, and moderate-to-higher nitrogen can raise their protein and yield.

**Q: Can barley grow where my wheat keeps failing from drought or salty soil?**
A: Often yes. Barley is the most drought- and salt-tolerant common cereal and matures quickly, so it frequently succeeds on dry, saline, or marginal land where wheat fails. Choose a short-season, adapted variety and manage soil acidity if your highland soils are acidic.

**Q: My barley leaves have oval, bleached lesions with brown margins. What is it?**
A: That description fits scald, a common cool, humid highland disease, though net blotch and other leaf diseases look similar. Grow resistant varieties, use clean treated seed, rotate with non-cereals, avoid excess nitrogen, and apply an appropriate fungicide only if needed and confirmed with an extension officer.

**Q: How much seed do I need per hectare?**
A: Roughly 80-125 kg/ha of good-quality seed for drilled or broadcast sowing, aiming for about 200-300 plants per square metre. Adjust upward for larger seed, lower germination, or rough seedbeds.

**Q: When should I harvest, and how do I keep the grain fit for malting?**
A: Harvest when the crop is golden, the straw is brittle, and grain is hard, with moisture around 13-14% or lower. For malting, harvest promptly to avoid sprouting and weathering, handle grain gently to protect germination, and store it cool and dry. Damp or overheated grain loses germination and is rejected.

**Q: What is a good rotation with barley?**
A: Legumes such as faba bean, field pea, lentil, or chickpea, and oilseeds, are excellent. They break barley's disease and weed cycles (including wild oats and leaf diseases) and add nitrogen, reducing fertiliser needs for the following barley crop.
