# Macadamia

## Overview
Macadamia is a high-value evergreen nut tree increasingly planted across the cooler highland zones of East and Southern Africa, notably Kenya, Malawi, South Africa, Zimbabwe, Tanzania, and Mozambique. It is a long-lived perennial that can remain productive for 40-60 years or more once established, making it an attractive long-term investment for smallholders and commercial growers alike. The nut is valued for its kernel, which commands strong demand in export confectionery, snack, and cosmetic-oil markets, giving growers a crop with strong foreign-exchange earning potential. Macadamia is often intercropped with coffee, tea, or annual food crops during the four-to-eight-year establishment period, allowing farmers to earn income from the land while trees mature. The main constraints are the long wait to significant bearing, sensitivity to frost and strong wind while young, susceptibility to stink bugs and husk spot disease, and the need for precise post-harvest drying and processing, since poorly dried kernels quickly turn rancid or develop mould. Grafted, clonally propagated planting material, careful site selection at appropriate altitude, and disciplined post-harvest handling are what separate profitable macadamia enterprises from disappointing ones.

## Scientific Name
*Macadamia integrifolia* Maiden & Betche and *Macadamia tetraphylla* L.A.S. Johnson (family Proteaceae). Most commercial orchards use grafted interspecific hybrid cultivars selected for yield, kernel recovery, and disease tolerance.

## Suitable Climate
- Macadamia is a subtropical highland tree. In equatorial Africa it performs best at altitudes of roughly 1,000-1,900 m, where elevation supplies the cool nights the tree needs; in higher-latitude areas such as South Africa's Mpumalanga and Limpopo provinces, lower altitudes with naturally cooler winters are suitable.
- Optimum mean temperatures are 16-25°C. A cool period with night temperatures below about 18°C for several weeks is important to trigger flower bud initiation; continuously warm, frost-free lowland tropics give poor flowering.
- Annual rainfall of 1,000-2,500 mm, well distributed, supports good growth; prolonged drought during flowering, nut set, or kernel fill sharply reduces yield and kernel quality.
- Young trees are frost-sensitive and can be killed or severely set back; mature trees tolerate only light, brief frost.
- Strong or persistent wind damages the brittle canopy, dislodges immature nuts, and reduces pollination activity by insects; windbreaks are strongly recommended on exposed sites.

## Suitable Soil
- Deep (over 1 m), well-drained, friable soils are essential; red volcanic loams typical of the East African highlands are excellent.
- Optimal soil pH is 5.0-6.5 (slightly acidic); alkaline soils induce iron and manganese deficiency.
- Macadamia is highly intolerant of waterlogging and compacted subsoils, which quickly lead to root rot and tree decline.
- Soils with high clay content and poor internal drainage should be avoided or must be improved with raised planting mounds and drainage channels.
- See `soil/soil_types_africa.md` for regional soil-suitability guidance.

## Soil Preparation
- Select sites with natural slope or install drainage to avoid waterlogging; on flat, heavy soils, plant on raised mounds 30-50 cm high.
- Dig planting holes at least 60 x 60 x 60 cm, mix excavated topsoil with 15-20 kg of well-decomposed compost or manure plus a phosphorus source, and refill before planting.
- On sloping highland terrain, construct contour terraces or bench terraces before planting to control erosion and retain moisture; see `soil/soil_conservation_and_erosion.md`.
- Test soil pH and correct strongly acidic or alkaline sites ahead of planting; see `soil/soil_testing_and_fertility.md`.

## Seed or Seedling Requirements
- Always use grafted clonal seedlings from a reputable nursery; seedling (ungrafted) trees are highly variable, slow to bear, and unreliable in yield and kernel quality.
- Select cultivars proven in the target altitude band and matched to local pollinator requirements; some cultivars benefit from cross-pollination with a compatible variety planted nearby.
- Check that the graft union is well healed, the rootstock is vigorous and disease-free, and the scion shows healthy, pest-free foliage.
- Harden nursery plants under gradually reduced shade for two to three weeks before transplanting to the field.

## Plant Spacing
- Standard spacing is 8 x 8 m to 10 x 10 m (100-156 trees/ha), depending on cultivar vigour and soil fertility.
- Closer spacing (7 x 7 m or 6 x 8 m) is used on less fertile sites or where interplanting with a compatible pollinator cultivar in alternating rows.
- Wider spacing (10 x 10 m or more) suits highly vigorous cultivars on fertile soil.
- Adequate spacing ensures good light penetration and airflow, which reduces husk spot and anthracnose pressure and eases harvesting.

## Water Requirements
- Young trees need regular watering during establishment, especially through the first two dry seasons; drip irrigation delivering 20-40 litres per tree per week during dry spells is typical.
- Water stress during flowering, nut set, and kernel fill (roughly six to seven months after flowering) causes heavy nut drop and poor kernel filling ("empty" or shrivelled kernels).
- Mature trees are moderately drought tolerant but benefit greatly from supplemental irrigation in low-rainfall seasons to sustain yield and kernel oil content.
- Avoid waterlogging around the root zone at any growth stage; ensure irrigation systems do not pond water at the trunk base. See `irrigation/irrigation_methods.md` and `irrigation/irrigation_scheduling.md`.

## Fertiliser Recommendations
- Young trees require light, frequent nitrogen, phosphorus, and potassium applications split into three to four doses per year, increasing gradually as the tree ages.
- Bearing trees need balanced nitrogen and potassium, with potassium particularly important for nut fill and kernel quality; apply in split doses at the start of the rains and after harvest.
- Apply 15-25 kg of well-rotted manure or compost per bearing tree annually to sustain organic matter and micronutrient supply.
- Macadamia is prone to boron, zinc, and manganese deficiencies on some soils, which cause poor nut set, corky tissue, and reduced kernel recovery; correct through foliar or soil-applied micronutrients guided by leaf or soil analysis. See `fertilisers/nutrient_deficiencies_guide.md` and `fertilisers/organic_fertilisers.md`.
- Confirm rates with a soil or leaf test where available; see `soil/soil_testing_and_fertility.md`.

## Weed Management
- Maintain a weed-free basin of at least 1-1.5 m radius around young trees to reduce competition for water and nutrients.
- Mulch the tree basin with grass, straw, or crop residue to suppress weeds, conserve soil moisture, and moderate soil temperature, keeping mulch clear of the trunk collar.
- Slash inter-row vegetation regularly rather than leaving bare soil, which helps control erosion on sloped orchards and can double as green manure.
- Avoid deep cultivation near trees, as macadamia has a shallow, wide-spreading feeder root system easily damaged by hoeing or ploughing. See `weeds/general_weed_management.md`.

## Pest Management
- Stink bugs (including green vegetable bug and macadamia nut borer) pierce developing nuts, causing internal kernel necrosis and premature drop; monitor from nut set onward and manage with orchard sanitation, trapping, and, where necessary, targeted spraying at economic thresholds.
- Macadamia felted coccid and other scale insects weaken shoots and reduce vigour; encourage natural predators and prune out heavily infested twigs.
- Thrips damage young leaves and flowers, reducing nut set in some seasons; monitor at flowering. See `pests/thrips.md`.
- Fruit-spotting bugs and false codling moth can also damage nuts in some regions; regular field scouting from flowering through nut development is essential.
- Follow integrated pest management: scout weekly during flowering and nut development, protect beneficial insects, and use only locally registered products as a last resort, confirmed with an extension officer and applied per `regulations/pesticide_safety_and_handling.md`.

## Disease Management
- Husk spot (*Pseudocercospora macadamiae*) causes dark lesions on the husk that can lead to premature nut drop and reduced kernel quality; manage with orchard sanitation, adequate tree spacing for airflow, and protectant fungicide programmes where husk spot pressure is high.
- Phytophthora root rot and trunk canker cause wilting, dieback, and tree death, especially on poorly drained soils; prevent through good drainage, avoiding trunk injury, and removing and destroying affected trees.
- Anthracnose can affect husks and stored nuts in humid conditions; sanitation and prompt drying after harvest reduce losses.
- Practise strict field sanitation, remove and destroy fallen diseased husks and nuts, and avoid working in wet orchards to limit disease spread. Consult an extension officer to confirm locally registered fungicides.

## Growth Stages
Macadamia orchards develop over many years from establishment to full, long-term bearing:
1. **Establishment (Year 1-2):** Root and canopy development; formative pruning to establish a strong single leader and well-spaced scaffold branches; protection from frost, wind, and pests.
2. **Juvenile (Year 2-4):** Rapid vegetative growth; some early flowering may occur but nut set is minimal and light cropping should not be allowed to stress the young tree.
3. **Early bearing (Year 4-7):** First commercially meaningful harvests; yields increase steadily each year as the canopy fills out.
4. **Full bearing (Year 8-12 onward):** Trees reach mature canopy size and stable annual production, typically sustained for several decades with proper management.
5. **Mature/renovation phase:** Very old or overcrowded orchards can be rejuvenated through selective pruning or top-working with improved cultivars to restore productivity and manageable tree height.

## Harvest Indicators
- Nuts are harvested when mature and naturally drop to the ground; the husk splits open on ripe nuts, exposing the hard brown shell.
- Regular ground collection (every one to two weeks during the harvest flush) prevents prolonged exposure to moisture, pests, rodents, and mould.
- Nuts should not be knocked or picked green from the tree, as immature nuts have poor kernel recovery and oil content.
- A cross-section test (cutting a sample of nuts) can confirm kernel colour and firmness as an indicator of overall orchard maturity before the main harvest push.

## Post-Harvest Handling
- Dehusk nuts within 24 hours of collection to prevent mould growth, discolouration, and kernel quality loss.
- Sort nuts to remove immature, cracked, insect-damaged, or germinated nuts before drying.
- Dry nut-in-shell progressively, first in the shade or under cover to reduce surface moisture, then with controlled heat, down to a final kernel moisture content of about 1.5%, which is essential to prevent rancidity and mould (including aflatoxin-producing fungi) during storage.
- Handle dried nuts gently to avoid shell cracking, which exposes the kernel to pests and moisture. See `harvesting/harvesting_best_practices.md` and `storage/aflatoxin_prevention.md`.

## Storage
- Store dried nut-in-shell in clean, dry, well-ventilated conditions, ideally in moisture-proof bags or containers, away from direct sunlight and heat.
- Maintain low humidity in storage areas; kernels are high in oil and readily absorb moisture and off-odours, leading to rancidity if stored improperly.
- Cracked, low-moisture kernels can be vacuum-packed or stored under refrigeration for extended shelf life where facilities allow.
- Regularly inspect stored nuts for pest infestation, mould, or moisture damage, and remove affected batches promptly. See `storage/aflatoxin_prevention.md` and `storage/perishable_storage.md`.

## Expected Yield
- Yield builds gradually with tree age: young bearing trees (4-7 years) may produce a few kilograms of nut-in-shell per tree, while mature trees (12 years and older) can yield 30-70 kg of nut-in-shell per tree per year under good management.
- On a per-hectare basis, well-managed mature orchards can produce 3-6 tonnes of nut-in-shell per hectare per year.
- Kernel recovery (the proportion of kernel to nut-in-shell weight) is typically 30-38% for good-quality, well-dried nuts, and is a key determinant of overall value.
- Poor pollination weather, pest damage, drought during nut fill, and delayed post-harvest drying are the main reasons actual yields and quality fall below potential.

## Common Mistakes
- Planting ungrafted seedling trees, resulting in slow, uneven bearing and poor, variable kernel quality.
- Selecting sites at unsuitable altitude or without a cool period, leading to poor flowering.
- Leaving fallen nuts on the ground too long before collection, increasing mould, pest damage, and rodent losses.
- Delaying dehusking and drying after harvest, which causes kernel discolouration, rancidity, and mould contamination.
- Over-cultivating or hoeing close to the trunk, damaging the shallow feeder root system.
- Ignoring stink bug damage until nut drop is already severe.

## Best Farming Practices
- Plant grafted, altitude-appropriate cultivars, including a compatible pollinator variety where recommended, to maximise nut set.
- Maintain strong windbreaks and frost protection during the establishment years.
- Practise integrated pest management with regular scouting for stink bugs and thrips from flowering through nut development.
- Collect fallen nuts frequently and dehusk within 24 hours to protect kernel quality.
- Dry nuts thoroughly and consistently to the recommended low moisture content before storage or sale.
- Maintain soil health through mulching, organic matter additions, and balanced, split fertiliser programmes guided by leaf or soil analysis. See `sustainability/sustainable_farming_practices.md`.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: How long does it take for a macadamia tree to start producing nuts?**
A: Grafted trees typically begin light bearing at 4-5 years and reach commercially significant production by 7-8 years, with full mature yields from around year 10-12 onward. Seedling (ungrafted) trees take considerably longer and give unpredictable results.

**Q: Why are my macadamia kernels going rancid or mouldy in storage?**
A: This is almost always due to insufficient drying before storage or poor storage conditions. Kernels must be dried to about 1.5% moisture and kept in dry, well-ventilated, moisture-proof storage away from heat and humidity.

**Q: Can macadamia be grown in lowland tropical areas?**
A: It is not recommended. Macadamia needs a cool period (night temperatures below about 18°C) to trigger flowering, which is generally only available at higher altitudes (1,000 m and above near the equator) or in higher-latitude subtropical areas with cool winters.

**Q: Why do many of my developing nuts drop before maturing?**
A: Common causes include water stress during nut set and kernel fill, stink bug or nut borer damage, poor pollination, and nutrient imbalances, particularly boron deficiency. Ensuring consistent irrigation and pest monitoring during nut development reduces drop.

**Q: Do I need more than one macadamia variety for good yields?**
A: Many cultivars are partially self-fertile, but cross-pollination between compatible varieties generally improves nut set and yield. Planting a recommended pollinator cultivar nearby, or in alternating rows, is good practice.

**Q: How do I know when to harvest macadamia nuts?**
A: Macadamia nuts mature and drop naturally from the tree when the husk splits; they should be collected from the ground regularly (every one to two weeks) rather than picked green, since immature nuts have poor kernel recovery and quality.
