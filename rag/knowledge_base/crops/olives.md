# Olives

## Overview
Olive is the signature tree crop of Africa's Mediterranean climate zones, dominant across North Africa in Tunisia, Morocco, Algeria, Libya, and Egypt, which together account for a large share of world olive and olive oil production, with smaller commercial and smallholder plantings in the highland Mediterranean-type microclimates of South Africa. It is an extremely long-lived evergreen tree, capable of remaining productive for well over a century, and is prized for both table olive processing and olive oil pressing. Olive is highly drought tolerant once established, making it well suited to the semi-arid Mediterranean margins where many other tree crops struggle, though yield and oil quality respond strongly to supplemental irrigation where water is available. The tree requires a period of winter chill to induce flowering, restricting commercial production to Mediterranean or high-altitude subtropical climates rather than the humid or hot lowland tropics. Alternate (biennial) bearing is a well-known characteristic of olive, with heavy crop years often followed by light ones. Major challenges include olive fruit fly, which can devastate both table and oil quality, verticillium wilt, and the labour intensity of hand harvesting for premium table olives. With good orchard management, olive offers a resilient, long-term crop well matched to the dry Mediterranean and semi-arid highland zones of Africa.

## Scientific Name
*Olea europaea* L. (family Oleaceae).

## Suitable Climate
- Olive requires a Mediterranean-type climate with hot, dry summers and cool, wet winters, or an equivalent highland subtropical climate with a distinct cool season.
- A period of winter chilling (commonly cited as several hundred hours below about 10°C, cultivar dependent) is necessary for proper flower bud differentiation; without adequate chilling, flowering is sparse and irregular.
- Optimum growing-season temperatures are 15-30°C; the tree tolerates summer heat above 35-40°C well when adequately watered, but is sensitive to prolonged extreme heat during flowering, which can cause flower abortion.
- Annual rainfall of 400-700 mm, concentrated in the cooler months, supports rainfed production in traditional Mediterranean groves, though supplemental irrigation substantially improves yield and oil content in drier years.
- Mature trees tolerate occasional light frost and moderate drought once established, but young trees are frost sensitive, and severe or prolonged frost damages even mature wood.

## Suitable Soil
- Olive is highly adaptable and tolerates poor, rocky, calcareous, and shallow soils that are unsuitable for many other fruit crops, which is why it dominates marginal hillside land across North Africa.
- Good drainage is essential; olive is very sensitive to waterlogging, which quickly causes root asphyxiation and root rot.
- Optimal soil pH is 6.5-8.5; the tree tolerates moderately alkaline and calcareous soils better than most fruit trees, an advantage on the limestone-derived soils common in North Africa.
- Deep soils support better long-term vigour and drought resilience, though olive can still produce acceptably on shallow, stony soils with careful management. See `soil/soil_types_africa.md`.

## Soil Preparation
- Clear the site of stones, deep-rooted perennial weeds, and old stumps; on hillside sites, construct terraces or contour planting rows to control erosion, a traditional practice throughout North African olive-growing regions.
- Dig planting holes at least 60 x 60 x 60 cm, or larger on rocky or shallow soils, and backfill with a mix of topsoil and well-decomposed manure or compost.
- Ensure adequate drainage at the planting site; avoid depressions where water can pool during winter rains.
- On sloping land, contour bunds and terracing significantly reduce erosion and improve water infiltration for rainfed groves. See `soil/soil_conservation_and_erosion.md`.

## Seed or Seedling Requirements
- Commercial olive orchards are established using rooted cuttings or grafted plants of named cultivars selected for oil content, table quality, or dual purpose use, propagated true to type.
- Avoid establishing orchards from seed, as seedling olives are highly variable, slow to bear (often 8-12 years or more), and unpredictable in fruit and oil quality.
- Select cultivars matched to the intended product (oil, table olive, or dual purpose), local chilling availability, and, where relevant, resistance or tolerance to verticillium wilt.
- Some cultivars are self-incompatible or show poor self-fruitfulness; plant a compatible pollinator cultivar alongside the main variety to ensure good fruit set.

## Plant Spacing
- Traditional rainfed groves use wide spacing of 8 x 8 m to 10 x 10 m (100-156 trees/ha) to allow trees to access sufficient soil moisture without competition.
- Semi-intensive irrigated orchards commonly use 6 x 6 m or 7 x 5 m spacing (about 280-330 trees/ha).
- High-density, hedgerow-style intensive olive systems (used for mechanical harvesting in some modern operations) use much closer spacing, such as 4 x 1.5 m, but require specific dwarfing cultivars and management.
- Spacing decisions should balance available irrigation water, soil fertility, harvesting method (hand versus mechanical), and cultivar vigour.

## Water Requirements
- Established olive trees are highly drought tolerant and are traditionally grown as a rainfed crop across much of North Africa, but yield and oil content respond strongly to supplemental irrigation, particularly during fruit development.
- Young trees (first two to three years) need regular watering to establish a strong root system, roughly 15-30 litres per tree per week during dry periods.
- The most water-sensitive periods are flowering and fruit development in summer; water stress during these stages reduces fruit set and final fruit and oil yield, though a moderate, well-timed deficit can also concentrate certain oil quality attributes in some production systems.
- Drip irrigation is increasingly used in commercial orchards to improve water-use efficiency in this water-scarce growing region. See `irrigation/irrigation_methods.md` and `irrigation/irrigation_scheduling.md`.

## Fertiliser Recommendations
- Young trees benefit from modest, split nitrogen and phosphorus applications during the establishment years to support root and canopy development.
- Bearing trees require balanced nitrogen and potassium, with potassium particularly important for fruit size and oil accumulation; apply in split doses timed to flowering and post-harvest.
- Apply 15-25 kg of well-decomposed manure or compost per bearing tree annually to maintain soil organic matter, especially valuable on the calcareous, low-organic-matter soils typical of North African olive groves.
- Boron deficiency is a common problem in olive on calcareous soils and causes poor fruit set and fruit deformity; correct through soil or foliar application guided by leaf analysis. See `fertilisers/nutrient_deficiencies_guide.md` and `soil/soil_testing_and_fertility.md`.

## Weed Management
- Maintain a weed-free basin around young trees during establishment to reduce competition for limited soil moisture, which is particularly critical in the semi-arid climates where olive is grown.
- In mature rainfed groves, controlled inter-row vegetation (managed through periodic light tillage or mowing) can help reduce erosion on sloping land while minimising water competition close to the trunk.
- Mulching around young trees conserves soil moisture and suppresses weeds; keep mulch away from direct contact with the trunk. See `weeds/general_weed_management.md`.

## Pest Management
- Olive fruit fly (*Bactrocera oleae*) is the single most economically damaging olive pest, laying eggs in developing fruit; larvae damage the flesh, promote fruit rot, and significantly reduce both table olive and oil quality. Management includes monitoring with traps, protein bait sprays, timely harvest, and orchard sanitation to remove fallen infested fruit.
- Olive moth can damage flowers, fruit, and leaves at different larval generations through the season; monitor and manage with targeted treatment where thresholds are exceeded.
- Black scale and other scale insects can infest branches and leaves, reducing vigour and promoting sooty mould; encourage natural predators and prune out heavily infested wood.
- Olive psyllid can damage new shoots and flower buds in some regions, reducing fruit set.
- Follow integrated pest management: monitor fruit fly populations closely from fruit development through harvest, protect natural enemies, and use locally registered products only where necessary, per `regulations/pesticide_safety_and_handling.md`.

## Disease Management
- Verticillium wilt (*Verticillium dahliae*), a soil-borne fungus, causes sudden wilting and dieback of branches and can kill trees, particularly on land previously used for susceptible crops such as cotton or vegetables; management relies on avoiding infected soil, using tolerant cultivars, and removing and destroying affected wood.
- Olive knot (*Pseudomonas savastanoi*) causes rough, tumour-like galls on branches and twigs, entering through pruning wounds or frost damage; manage by pruning out and destroying galls, disinfecting pruning tools, and avoiding pruning in wet weather.
- Peacock spot (*Venturia oleaginea*, also known as olive leaf spot) causes dark, ringed spots on leaves and premature leaf drop in humid conditions, weakening the tree over time; manage with copper-based protectant treatments and good canopy airflow through pruning.
- Anthracnose (olive fruit rot) can affect ripening fruit in humid or rainy conditions near harvest, reducing oil quality; timely harvest and good orchard sanitation reduce risk.
- Practise sanitation, disinfect pruning tools between trees, and avoid pruning during wet weather to limit disease spread.

## Growth Stages
Olive develops slowly but remains productive over a very long lifespan:
1. **Establishment (Year 1-3):** Root and canopy development; formative pruning to establish a strong, open, vase-shaped or central-leader framework depending on the intended management system.
2. **Juvenile (Year 3-5):** Vegetative growth continues; light early flowering may occur but heavy cropping should not be allowed to stress the young tree.
3. **Early bearing (Year 5-8):** First commercially meaningful harvests; yields increase progressively as canopy size grows.
4. **Full bearing (Year 8-15 onward):** Trees reach mature, though often alternate (biennial), annual production, sustained for many decades to over a century with good management.
5. **Ageing/renovation:** Very old trees can be rejuvenated through heavy renewal pruning, restoring productive wood and manageable canopy size; many North African groves contain productive trees well over 100 years old.

## Harvest Indicators
- Olive maturity and harvest timing depend on the intended product: green olives for table processing are picked while still firm and green to straw-coloured; black table olives and oil olives are picked as the fruit changes colour from green through red to purple-black.
- Oil olives are often harvested at a partial colour-change stage (a mix of green and turning fruit) to balance oil yield against oil quality, since fully black, overripe fruit can give oil with higher acidity and reduced shelf life.
- Fruit firmness, oil content (which increases as fruit matures), and colour development are used together to judge optimal harvest timing.
- Hand harvesting (stripping or careful picking) preserves fruit quality best for table olives; mechanical or manual pole-assisted harvesting is more common for oil olives.

## Post-Harvest Handling
- Process or press fruit as soon as possible after harvest, ideally within 24-48 hours, since delays increase fruit fermentation, oil acidity, and off-flavours.
- Avoid piling harvested fruit in deep heaps for extended periods, as this generates heat and accelerates spoilage; store in shallow, ventilated crates before processing.
- Table olives require a curing process (brining, lye treatment, or natural fermentation, depending on style) to remove natural bitterness before they are edible; follow established, food-safety-appropriate curing protocols.
- Sort fruit to remove damaged, diseased, or fly-infested olives before pressing or curing, since these lower both oil quality and table olive grade. See `harvesting/harvesting_best_practices.md` and `marketplace/product_grading_and_quality.md`.

## Storage
- Fresh olive fruit is highly perishable and should not be stored for extended periods before processing; prompt pressing or curing is standard practice.
- Extracted olive oil should be stored in a cool, dark place in sealed, opaque or dark-glass containers to protect it from light and heat, both of which accelerate oxidation and rancidity.
- Cured table olives, once brined or processed, can be stored for extended periods in their brine or curing solution in sealed containers under cool conditions.
- Maintain strict sanitation during processing and storage to prevent microbial spoilage, particularly for naturally fermented table olive products. See `storage/perishable_storage.md`.

## Expected Yield
- Yield varies considerably with tree age, cultivar, water availability, and management: young bearing trees (5-8 years) may produce 5-15 kg of fruit per tree, while mature, well-managed trees (15 years and older) can yield 40-80 kg or more per tree in a heavy-bearing year.
- On a per-hectare basis, traditional rainfed groves typically yield 1-3 tonnes of fruit per hectare, while well-managed irrigated orchards can achieve 6-12 tonnes per hectare or more.
- Oil recovery from fruit typically ranges from 15-25% by weight, depending on cultivar, maturity at harvest, and extraction efficiency.
- Alternate bearing (a heavy year followed by a light one), fruit fly losses, inadequate winter chilling, and water stress during fruit development are the main reasons actual yields fall short of potential.

## Common Mistakes
- Establishing orchards from seedling trees rather than true-to-type cuttings or grafted plants, resulting in slow, unpredictable bearing and inconsistent oil or table quality.
- Ignoring olive fruit fly monitoring until infestation is severe, resulting in major losses in both oil and table olive quality.
- Planting on land with a recent history of verticillium-susceptible crops without addressing soil-borne inoculum risk.
- Delaying pressing or curing after harvest, leading to increased oil acidity or spoiled table olives.
- Neglecting pruning over many years, leading to dense, shaded canopies with reduced fruiting wood and higher disease pressure.
- Underestimating the effect of alternate bearing and failing to manage crop load through pruning to smooth yields between years.

## Best Farming Practices
- Establish orchards using true-to-type cuttings or grafted plants of cultivars suited to local chilling, water availability, and intended product.
- Prune annually to maintain an open canopy that admits light and air, supports renewal of fruiting wood, and helps manage alternate bearing.
- Practise integrated pest management with vigilant olive fruit fly monitoring and timely intervention from fruit development through harvest.
- Maintain soil health with organic matter additions and balanced, soil-test-guided fertiliser applications, paying particular attention to boron on calcareous soils.
- Use terracing and contour planting on sloping land to conserve water and control erosion.
- Process or cure fruit promptly after harvest to preserve oil and table olive quality. See `sustainability/sustainable_farming_practices.md` and `weather/drought_and_flood_management.md`.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: How long does it take for an olive tree to bear fruit?**
A: Trees propagated from cuttings or grafted plants typically begin bearing in five to eight years, with full mature production reached from around year ten to fifteen onward. Seedling trees take considerably longer, often eight to twelve years or more, and give unpredictable fruit quality.

**Q: Why does my olive tree produce a heavy crop one year and almost nothing the next?**
A: This is alternate (biennial) bearing, a well-documented characteristic of olive. It can be reduced, though not eliminated, through regular pruning to manage crop load, balanced nutrition, and adequate irrigation during fruit development in heavy-bearing years.

**Q: What causes worms or maggots inside my olives?**
A: This is typically olive fruit fly larvae, the most damaging olive pest. Manage with monitoring traps, protein bait sprays, prompt removal of fallen infested fruit, and timely harvest before fly pressure builds excessively.

**Q: Can olive oil be pressed at home from freshly picked fruit?**
A: Fruit should be pressed as soon as possible after harvest, ideally within 24-48 hours, using appropriate pressing equipment, since delays lead to fermentation and increased oil acidity. Small-scale and commercial mills both follow this same principle of minimising the time between harvest and pressing.

**Q: Does olive need a cold winter to produce fruit?**
A: Yes. Olive requires a period of winter chilling to properly differentiate flower buds; without adequate cold exposure, flowering is sparse and irregular, which is why commercial production is concentrated in Mediterranean and cool highland subtropical climates rather than the humid tropics.

**Q: Why are green olives bitter straight off the tree?**
A: Fresh olives, whether green or black, contain a naturally bitter compound (oleuropein) that must be removed through curing, such as brining, lye treatment, or natural fermentation, before the fruit is palatable as a table olive.
