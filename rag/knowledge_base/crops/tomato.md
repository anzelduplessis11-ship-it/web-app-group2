# Tomato

## Overview
Tomato is one of the most widely grown vegetable crops across Africa, produced both by smallholder farmers and commercial growers for fresh market and processing. It is a high-value but management-intensive crop, sensitive to disease, pests, and water stress.

## Scientific Name
*Solanum lycopersicum* L.

## Suitable Climate
- Optimal growing temperature: 20-27°C during the day, 15-20°C at night.
- Poor fruit set occurs above 32°C or below 10°C.
- Requires 400-600 mm of water over the growing cycle, through rainfall and/or irrigation.
- Sensitive to frost.

## Suitable Soil
- Deep, well-drained loam or sandy loam soils rich in organic matter.
- Optimal pH: 6.0-6.8.
- Poor drainage increases risk of root diseases such as bacterial wilt and root rot.

## Soil Preparation
- Plough and harrow to a fine tilth to a depth of 20-30 cm.
- Incorporate well-decomposed manure or compost (10-20 tonnes/ha) before planting.
- Form raised beds or ridges in areas with heavy soil or high rainfall to improve drainage.
- Avoid planting in fields with a recent history of solanaceous crops (tomato, pepper, eggplant, potato) to reduce disease carryover.

## Planting Season by African Region
| Region | Typical Planting Window | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| West Africa | Start of dry season (Oct-Dec) for open field; year-round under irrigation | Dry season reduces fungal disease pressure |
| East Africa | Two seasons: Mar-Apr and Sept-Oct | Aligns with bimodal rains |
| Southern Africa | Aug-Oct (spring planting) and Jan-Feb (late summer) | Avoid peak frost risk months |
| Central Africa | Year-round in humid zones with disease management | Requires vigilant disease control |

## Seed or Seedling Requirements
- Raise seedlings in a nursery for 3-4 weeks before transplanting (4-6 true leaves stage).
- Use certified disease-free seed of a variety suited to local disease pressure (e.g., bacterial wilt-resistant varieties where relevant).
- Seed rate: approximately 300-400 g/ha for nursery raising.
- Harden seedlings by reducing water and increasing sun exposure for 3-5 days before transplanting.

## Plant Spacing
- Standard spacing: 60-100 cm between rows, 40-50 cm between plants, depending on variety growth habit (determinate vs. indeterminate).
- Indeterminate (staked) varieties: wider spacing and vertical support systems (stakes, trellis).
- Determinate (bush) varieties: closer spacing, no staking required.

## Water Requirements
- Requires consistent soil moisture; avoid both waterlogging and drought stress.
- Critical stages for water stress sensitivity: flowering and fruit development.
- Irregular watering during fruit development causes blossom end rot and fruit cracking.
- Drip irrigation is preferred where available to reduce foliar disease and improve water use efficiency.

## Fertiliser Recommendations
- Basal application: compound fertiliser supplying phosphorus at transplanting to support root establishment.
- Topdress with nitrogen and potassium at 2-3 week intervals through the growing season, increasing potassium during fruiting.
- General guideline: 100-150 kg N/ha, 80-120 kg P2O5/ha, 100-150 kg K2O/ha, split over the season.
- Calcium deficiency contributes to blossom end rot; ensure adequate calcium supply and consistent watering.
- See `fertilisers/fertiliser_basics.md` for detailed application guidance.

## Weed Management
- Weed regularly, especially in the first 6 weeks after transplanting.
- Mulching (organic or plastic) suppresses weeds, conserves moisture, and reduces soil-splash-related disease transmission.

## Pest Management
- Key pests: tomato leaf miner (*Tuta absoluta*), whiteflies, aphids, cutworms, and fruit worms.
- Use pheromone traps for early detection of leaf miner.
- Practice crop rotation and field sanitation to reduce pest carryover.
- See `pests/aphids.md` for related management guidance.

## Disease Management
- Key diseases: late blight, early blight, bacterial wilt, fusarium wilt, and tomato yellow leaf curl virus.
- Use resistant varieties, crop rotation (avoid solanaceous crops for at least 2-3 years), and proper plant spacing for airflow.
- Avoid overhead irrigation late in the day, which prolongs leaf wetness and promotes fungal disease.
- See `diseases/tomato_late_blight.md` for detailed management guidance.

## Growth Stages
1. Seedling/nursery stage (0-4 weeks).
2. Vegetative growth after transplanting (4-8 weeks).
3. Flowering and fruit set (8-12 weeks).
4. Fruit development and ripening (12-16+ weeks, continuing through multiple harvests for indeterminate types).

## Harvest Indicators
- For fresh market: harvest at "breaker" to "pink" stage (colour change beginning) if transport time is long, or fully ripe for local/immediate sale.
- For processing: harvest at full red ripeness for maximum flavour and colour.
- Fruit should detach easily from the vine with a gentle twist when ripe.

## Post-Harvest Handling
- Harvest in cool parts of the day (early morning) to reduce field heat and improve shelf life.
- Handle fruit gently to avoid bruising, which accelerates spoilage and reduces market value.
- Sort by ripeness and quality immediately after harvest; remove damaged or diseased fruit.
- Use ventilated crates rather than sacks to reduce compression damage.

## Storage
- Fresh tomatoes: store at 10-13°C and 85-90% relative humidity for short-term storage (1-2 weeks); temperatures below 10°C cause chilling injury.
- Avoid storing tomatoes with ethylene-sensitive produce, as tomatoes emit ethylene that accelerates ripening of nearby produce.
- For longer-term preservation, process into paste, sauce, or dried products.

## Expected Yield
- Smallholder open-field systems: 10-20 tonnes/ha.
- Improved management with good disease/pest control and irrigation: 25-40 tonnes/ha.
- High-input greenhouse or intensive systems: 60-100+ tonnes/ha.

## Common Mistakes
- Continuous tomato or solanaceous cropping without rotation, leading to disease build-up.
- Overhead irrigation late in the day, promoting fungal disease.
- Inconsistent watering, causing blossom end rot and fruit cracking.
- Delayed pest scouting, allowing leaf miner or whitefly populations to establish before control measures are applied.
- Harvesting and storing overripe or damaged fruit together with healthy fruit, accelerating spoilage of the whole batch.

## Best Farming Practices
- Rotate with non-solanaceous crops for at least 2-3 seasons.
- Use resistant varieties suited to local disease pressure.
- Stake indeterminate varieties to improve airflow and reduce fruit rot.
- Apply mulch to conserve moisture and suppress weeds.
- Monitor fields weekly for early signs of pests and disease.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Why do my tomatoes develop a dark, sunken patch on the bottom of the fruit?**
A: This is typically blossom end rot, caused by calcium deficiency often linked to inconsistent watering rather than soil calcium alone. Maintaining even soil moisture is the primary management step.

**Q: Why are my tomato leaves curling and yellowing?**
A: This can indicate tomato yellow leaf curl virus (spread by whiteflies), nutrient deficiency, or water stress. Inspect for whitefly presence and consult `diseases/` documents or an extension officer if a viral cause is suspected.

**Q: When is the best time to transplant tomato seedlings?**
A: Transplant when seedlings have 4-6 true leaves, typically 3-4 weeks after sowing, and preferably in the late afternoon or on a cloudy day to reduce transplant shock.
