# Bambara Groundnut

## Overview
Bambara groundnut is an indigenous African legume, believed to have originated in West Africa, and remains one of the continent's most resilient food security crops. Like groundnut, it produces underground pods, but it is far more tolerant of drought, heat, and poor, sandy soils, thriving where most other legumes and cereals struggle. The dried seeds are a complete food, providing a good balance of protein, carbohydrate, and fat, and are boiled, roasted, or ground into flour for porridges, snacks, and traditional dishes across West, Central, Southern, and parts of East Africa. Bambara groundnut is often described as an "orphan crop" because it has received comparatively little formal breeding and research investment relative to its importance, yet it remains a vital fallback crop for smallholder households, particularly women farmers, in semi-arid areas where rainfall is unreliable. As a legume, it fixes atmospheric nitrogen and contributes to soil fertility, making it a valuable rotation and intercrop partner with cereals. Its main agronomic challenges are a relatively long growing season for a legume, limited access to good-quality seed through formal channels, and management practices that mirror groundnut but must account for its slower development and generally lower yield potential.

## Scientific Name
*Vigna subterranea* (L.) Verdc. (synonym *Voandzeia subterranea*), family Fabaceae.

## Suitable Climate
- Optimal growing temperature: approximately 20-28°C.
- Requires only approximately 400-700 mm of rainfall per season, making it markedly more drought-tolerant than groundnut and most other grain legumes.
- Sensitive to frost; grown exclusively in the warm season.
- Adapted to low, mid, and some higher-altitude areas, up to approximately 1,600 m in parts of East and Southern Africa.
- Performs reliably on erratic, low, or poorly distributed rainfall that would cause crop failure in less hardy legumes, which is central to its role as a food security crop in marginal areas.
- Like groundnut, benefits from a drier period toward the end of the season to support pod maturation and safe drying.

## Suitable Soil
- Grows best on light, well-drained sandy or sandy loam soils that allow easy pegging (the process by which the fertilised flower pushes into the soil to form an underground pod).
- Notably tolerant of poor, infertile, and marginal soils where groundnut and most other legumes yield poorly.
- Tolerates a pH range of approximately 5.0-6.5.
- Does not tolerate waterlogging or heavy, compacted clay soils, which restrict pegging and pod development and increase the risk of pod rot.

## Soil Preparation
- Prepare a loose, fine, well-drained seedbed to allow easy peg penetration and pod formation, similar to groundnut.
- Plough and harrow to break clods and create a level surface, particularly on soils with any tendency to crust or compact.
- Ridging or mounding can improve drainage and ease of harvest on heavier or poorly drained soils.
- Incorporate crop residues or well-decomposed organic matter ahead of planting on very poor soils to improve structure and moisture retention.
- Avoid leaving the soil compacted at planting, as this restricts pegging and reduces pod set even where the crop otherwise tolerates poor fertility.

## Planting Season by African Region
| Region | Typical Planting Window | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| West Africa (Sahel/savanna - Nigeria, Burkina Faso, Mali, Ghana) | Jun-Jul (onset of rains) | Core production zone; widely grown as a traditional food security crop |
| East Africa | Mar-Apr (long rains); Oct-Nov (short rains) | Grown in drier lowland zones where groundnut performs less reliably |
| Southern Africa | Nov-Dec (start of summer rains) | Plant early enough to allow the crop's longer season to reach maturity before rains end |
| Central Africa | Bimodal zones (Sept-Nov and Mar-Apr) | Choose the window that best avoids harvest falling in heavy rains |
| North Africa | Minimal commercial cultivation; where grown, under irrigation in warm months | Not a traditional crop of this region; confirm local adaptation before large-scale planting |

## Seed or Seedling Requirements
- Bambara groundnut is direct-seeded; it is not transplanted.
- Use fresh, good-quality seed, ideally from the most recent harvest, as viability declines relatively quickly in storage compared with some other grain legumes.
- Seed rate: approximately 40-60 kg/ha of unshelled seed, or roughly 25-35 kg/ha if shelled, depending on seed size and spacing.
- Shell seed shortly before planting where possible to preserve viability, and avoid using shrivelled, cracked, or insect-damaged seed.
- Sow at a depth of approximately 3-5 cm into moist soil.
- Because formal certified seed systems for this crop are limited in most countries, farmers commonly save their own seed; select large, healthy, well-filled seed from healthy plants for this purpose and store it carefully to preserve germination.

## Plant Spacing
- Common spacing: approximately 30-45 cm between rows and 10-20 cm between plants, similar to groundnut.
- Bushy, non-spreading landraces can be spaced slightly closer than more spreading types.
- Uniform, adequate plant populations matter because gaps translate directly into lost pod yield, given the crop's naturally modest yield potential.

## Water Requirements
- Total seasonal water requirement: approximately 350-500 mm, notably lower than groundnut.
- Critical water-sensitive stages are flowering, pegging, and pod filling, although the crop tolerates dry spells at these stages considerably better than most other legumes.
- Extended drought can still reduce pod number and fill, but Bambara groundnut is far more likely than groundnut or common bean to produce at least some harvest under poor rainfall conditions.
- Excess moisture near harvest increases the risk of pod and seed rot and should be avoided.

## Fertiliser Recommendations
- As a legume, Bambara groundnut fixes much of its own nitrogen and shows limited response to nitrogen fertiliser in most trials.
- The crop generally shows modest but sometimes worthwhile response to phosphorus on very poor soils, supporting root development, nodulation, and pod fill.
- A calcium source can help pod filling on markedly calcium-deficient soils, similar to groundnut, though this is less commonly a limiting factor given the marginal soils where the crop is typically grown.
- Traditionally grown with little or no fertiliser input; where inputs are available, base any application on a soil test rather than blanket recommendations. See `soil/soil_testing_and_fertility.md` and `fertilisers/fertiliser_basics.md`.
- Rhizobium inoculation can improve nodulation and nitrogen fixation on soils where the crop, or compatible legumes, has not been grown recently, though inoculant availability for this specific crop may be limited in some areas.

## Weed Management
- First weeding: approximately 2-3 weeks after emergence, when young plants are most vulnerable to weed competition.
- Second weeding: around 5-6 weeks after emergence, and ideally completed before pegging begins.
- Avoid deep cultivation or disturbance once pegging has started, as this damages developing pegs and young pods, directly reducing yield.
- Hand weeding and shallow hoeing are the most common methods on smallholder plots; see `weeds/general_weed_management.md` for general integrated weed control principles.

## Pest Management
- **Aphids**: suck sap from young growth and can transmit viruses; see `pests/aphids.md`.
- **Leaf miners and foliage-feeding beetles**: cause leaf damage that is usually tolerated without major yield loss unless infestations are severe.
- **Termites**: can damage pods underground, particularly in drier areas and on sandy soils; timely harvest reduces exposure to this risk.
- **Storage bruchids** (seed beetles): the most serious pest of this crop overall, attacking dried seed in storage and capable of causing severe losses if seed is not protected.
- Use integrated pest management combining field scouting, timely harvest, and targeted control only when necessary, confirming any locally registered products with a qualified agricultural extension officer.

## Disease Management
- Bambara groundnut generally suffers from fewer serious disease problems than groundnut, which is one of its practical advantages for smallholder farmers with limited access to crop protection inputs.
- **Leaf spot diseases** (including *Cercospora* species): cause brown spotting on leaves, generally of limited economic significance unless severe.
- **Damping-off**: can affect seedlings in cool, wet, poorly drained conditions at establishment.
- Viral diseases are reported but are generally less prevalent and damaging than the rosette virus problem seen in groundnut.
- Manage disease risk through crop rotation with non-legume crops, use of healthy seed, and avoidance of waterlogged conditions; consult a qualified agricultural extension officer for any unusual or severe outbreak.

## Growth Stages
1. Germination and emergence (approximately 5-10 days after planting).
2. Vegetative growth: leaf and branch development (approximately 10-40 days).
3. Flowering and pegging: small, inconspicuous flowers form close to the ground and self-pollinate, and the fertilised peg pushes into the soil to form the pod (approximately 35-70 days).
4. Pod filling and maturity: pods and seeds develop underground, with the crop typically reaching maturity 90-170 days after planting depending on variety, notably longer on average than most groundnut varieties.

## Harvest Indicators
- Leaves yellow and begin to dry, and the plant dies back as the crop approaches maturity.
- Pods and their internal seed harden as maturity is reached.
- Test-lift a sample of plants and open pods to confirm that the majority have reached maturity before harvesting the whole field, since pods form underground and cannot be assessed visually from above ground.
- Because Bambara groundnut has a notably long growing season for a legume, avoid the common mistake of harvesting too early based on the timing used for groundnut or cowpea.

## Post-Harvest Handling
- Lift plants carefully, ideally when the soil is slightly moist, to minimise pods left behind in the ground.
- Separate pods from the vines and dry them promptly and thoroughly in a single layer, avoiding direct contact with bare, damp soil.
- Dry until seeds are hard and rattle within the pod before threshing or shelling.
- Sort out damaged, shrivelled, discoloured, or insect-damaged pods and seeds, as these are the most likely to carry storage pests or spoilage organisms.

## Storage
- Store well-dried seed, whether shelled or unshelled, in a cool, dry, well-ventilated place protected from moisture, rodents, and insects.
- Storage bruchids are the main storage threat; hermetic (airtight) storage bags or containers are effective at controlling them without chemicals.
- Keep different harvest batches and seed intended for planting separate from seed intended for consumption or sale, and label seed stock clearly.
- Monitor stored seed regularly for signs of moisture, insect activity, or mould, and remove affected stock promptly. See `storage/grain_storage.md`.

## Expected Yield
- Smallholder rainfed systems with basic, traditional management: approximately 400-800 kg/ha of dry seed.
- Improved management with good seed, adequate spacing, and timely weeding: approximately 800 kg to 1.5 tonnes/ha.
- Yields are generally lower than groundnut under favourable conditions, but the crop is far more reliable than groundnut under poor rainfall or on infertile soils, which is its main comparative advantage.
- Actual yield depends heavily on variety (landrace), rainfall distribution, soil quality, and weeding timeliness.

## Common Mistakes
- Applying the shorter maturity expectations of groundnut or cowpea to Bambara groundnut and harvesting too early, before pods have properly filled and hardened.
- Planting old, poorly viable farm-saved seed without testing germination first.
- Disturbing the soil through cultivation after pegging has begun, damaging developing pods.
- Underestimating the crop's storage pest risk and failing to use protective storage methods, leading to avoidable losses to bruchids.
- Overlooking the crop in favour of higher-profile legumes, resulting in weak local seed systems and a shortage of good planting material.

## Best Farming Practices
- Use the crop deliberately as a drought-risk management tool on marginal, poor, or sandy land where more demanding legumes are unreliable.
- Select and store good-quality seed carefully each season, given the limited availability of formal certified seed for this crop.
- Rotate or intercrop with cereals to take advantage of nitrogen fixation and diversify household food supply and risk.
- Time harvest based on test-lifting rather than a fixed calendar, given the crop's variable and often longer maturity period.
- Store dried seed in hermetic containers to protect against storage bruchids without relying on chemical treatments. See `sustainability/sustainable_farming_practices.md`.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: How is Bambara groundnut different from ordinary groundnut?**
A: Both form pods underground, but Bambara groundnut is considerably more tolerant of drought, heat, and poor sandy soils, and generally has a longer growing season and lower yield potential than groundnut. It is valued as a reliable fallback food security crop where groundnut and other legumes are more likely to fail.

**Q: Why did my Bambara groundnut pods seem poorly filled when I harvested at the same time as my groundnut?**
A: Bambara groundnut typically needs a longer growing season than groundnut, often 90-170 days compared with groundnut's shorter cycle. Harvesting on the same schedule as groundnut often means harvesting Bambara groundnut before it is fully mature. Test-lift plants and check pod fill before committing to a full harvest.

**Q: Does Bambara groundnut need fertiliser to produce a good yield?**
A: Generally very little. As a legume it fixes much of its own nitrogen, and it is traditionally grown successfully with minimal or no fertiliser input, even on poor soils. A modest phosphorus application can help on very depleted soils, but heavy fertiliser use is not typically necessary or economical for this crop.

**Q: My stored Bambara groundnut seed is full of small holes and beetles. What happened?**
A: This is storage bruchid (seed beetle) infestation, the crop's main storage threat. Dry seed thoroughly before storage, sort out any damaged seed, and store in hermetic (airtight) bags or containers, which control the insects effectively without chemicals.

**Q: Can Bambara groundnut be intercropped with cereals?**
A: Yes. As with other legumes, intercropping or rotating Bambara groundnut with cereals such as maize, sorghum, or millet takes advantage of its nitrogen fixation, improves overall land productivity, and spreads household food security risk across crops with different tolerances to drought.

**Q: Is Bambara groundnut worth growing if it yields less than groundnut?**
A: Often yes, particularly on marginal land or in drought-prone seasons, because its reliability under poor rainfall and low soil fertility can outweigh its lower yield potential under good conditions. Many farmers grow it specifically as insurance against the higher risk of failure in more demanding legumes.
