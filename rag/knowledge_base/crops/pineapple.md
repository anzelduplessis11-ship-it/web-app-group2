# Pineapple (Ananas comosus)

## Overview

Pineapple is a tropical and subtropical perennial fruit crop grown widely across sub-Saharan Africa for fresh consumption, processing (juice, canned slices, dried rings) and, increasingly, export. It is a herbaceous member of the bromeliad family with a rosette of stiff, sword-shaped leaves and a single terminal fruit that develops from the fusion of many small flowers into one compound fruit. For African smallholders, pineapple is attractive because it tolerates relatively poor, acidic soils, withstands short dry spells better than many fruit crops, needs little machinery, and produces a high-value fruit from planting material that farmers can multiply themselves for free. Major smallholder production zones include the coastal and forest belts of West Africa (Nigeria, Ghana, Côte d'Ivoire, Benin), Central and East Africa (Uganda, Kenya, Tanzania, Democratic Republic of Congo), and parts of Southern Africa (eastern South Africa, Mozambique, Malawi, Zimbabwe lowveld).

Pineapple has a distinctive perennial, ratoon-based cycle. A field is established from vegetative suckers or slips, produces a first ("plant crop") fruit roughly 14 to 24 months after planting, and then produces one or more smaller "ratoon crops" from side shoots before the block is replanted, usually after two to four fruiting cycles. This document covers site selection, establishment, crop management, and integrated pest and disease control suited to low-input smallholder systems. For soil fertility fundamentals see `soil/soil_testing_and_fertility.md`, and for general orchard water planning see `water/irrigation_basics.md`.

## Scientific Name

- Botanical name: *Ananas comosus* (L.) Merr.
- Family: Bromeliaceae
- Common groups/cultivar types grown in Africa:
  - **Smooth Cayenne** group: large cylindrical fruit, favoured for canning and juice; the historic backbone of commercial plantations.
  - **Queen** ("Victoria") group: smaller, sweeter, deep-yellow, aromatic fruit with a long shelf life; popular for the fresh market in East and Southern Africa.
  - **MD2 / "Gold" hybrids**: sweet, low-acid, uniform export-grade fruit; increasingly grown by organised smallholder groups linked to exporters.
  - **Spanish** and local landraces: hardy, spiny types used for local fresh sale.

Common names include pineapple (English), ananas (French and many local languages), nanasi (Swahili), and abacaxi (Portuguese).

## Suitable Climate

Pineapple grows best in a warm, frost-free climate.

- **Temperature:** Optimum growth occurs between 22 and 32 °C. Growth slows below about 20 °C and is severely checked below 15 °C. The crop cannot tolerate frost. Very high temperatures above 35 °C combined with strong sun cause leaf and fruit sunscald, especially on exposed fruit.
- **Altitude:** Productive from sea level up to about 1,600 m in equatorial regions. At higher, cooler altitudes fruit take longer to mature and are often more acidic.
- **Rainfall:** An annual rainfall of 1,000 to 1,500 mm, reasonably well distributed, is ideal. Because the plant uses water-conserving Crassulacean Acid Metabolism (CAM) photosynthesis, it survives dry spells well, but prolonged drought reduces fruit size and yield. Areas with 600 to 1,000 mm can succeed with supplementary irrigation or mulching.
- **Sunlight:** Full sun gives the best fruit sugar and colour. Deep shade produces pale, elongated, low-sugar fruit, though light shade can reduce sunscald in very hot lowlands.
- **Wind:** Sheltered sites are preferred; strong, hot winds increase water loss and can topple heavily laden ratoons.

Climate-smart note: pineapple is a comparatively resilient choice for regions facing more erratic rainfall, provided planting is timed to the rains and organic mulch is used to buffer soil moisture.

## Suitable Soil

- **Texture:** Well-drained sandy loams and loams are best. Pineapple is one of the few fruit crops that tolerates light, low-fertility soils, but it will not tolerate waterlogging even briefly, because the shallow roots rot quickly in saturated soil.
- **Drainage:** Excellent drainage is the single most important soil requirement. Avoid heavy clays, low-lying pans, and areas where water sits after rain. On heavier soils, plant on raised beds or ridges.
- **pH:** Pineapple prefers acidic soil, pH 4.5 to 5.5, and tolerates down to about pH 4.0. This is unusually low for a crop and means liming is rarely needed; over-liming above pH 6.5 can trigger iron and zinc deficiency (leaf yellowing). Confirm soil pH with a test before establishing a block; see `soil/soil_testing_and_fertility.md`.
- **Depth and organic matter:** A rooting depth of 30 to 45 cm is sufficient because roots are shallow. Moderate organic matter improves moisture holding and structure.
- **Salinity:** Moderately sensitive to salt; avoid saline soils and saline irrigation water.

## Soil Preparation

1. **Site clearing:** Clear weeds, previous crop residues and woody regrowth. Remove or destroy old pineapple debris, which harbours mealybugs and disease.
2. **Land assessment:** On sloping land, plant across the slope (on the contour) to control erosion. Steep slopes suit pineapple well because they drain freely, but require contour planting and mulch bunds.
3. **Primary tillage:** Loosen soil to 25 to 30 cm by hand hoe, animal traction or a single plough pass. Because roots are shallow, deep or repeated tillage is unnecessary and can waste labour.
4. **Ridging or bedding:** On heavier or flatter soils, form ridges or raised beds 15 to 25 cm high to guarantee drainage. On light, free-draining soils, flat planting is acceptable.
5. **Organic matter:** Incorporate well-rotted manure or compost at roughly 10 to 20 tonnes per hectare (about 1 to 2 kg per planting station) where available. This is a low-cost way to lift yields; see `soil/composting_and_manure.md`.
6. **Mulch readiness:** Gather dry grass, crop residue or leaf litter to mulch after planting; mulch conserves moisture, suppresses weeds and moderates soil temperature.

## Planting Season by African Region

Plant at the onset of the main rains so young suckers establish with reliable moisture. In frost-prone highland areas, avoid planting so late that flowering coincides with cold months.

| Region | Typical Planting Window | Notes |
|--------|------------------------|-------|
| West Africa (Nigeria, Ghana, Côte d'Ivoire, Benin) | March–June (main rains); September–October in bimodal zones | Two rainy seasons in the forest belt allow two planting windows; stagger plantings for year-round supply. |
| East Africa (Uganda, Kenya, Tanzania) | March–May (long rains); October–November (short rains) | Bimodal rainfall; long rains give the strongest establishment. Highland sites mature more slowly. |
| Central Africa (DR Congo, Cameroon) | March–May and September–November | High, well-distributed rainfall permits flexible planting; ensure drainage in the wettest months. |
| Southern Africa (eastern South Africa, Mozambique, Malawi, Zimbabwe lowveld) | October–December (start of summer rains) | Frost-free, warm lowlands only; avoid winter planting in cooler interior areas. |
| Coastal and island zones (Tanzania coast, Madagascar) | Onset of main rains (varies by locality) | Warm, humid conditions favour Queen and MD2 types; watch for high disease pressure. |

Local planting dates vary by locality, season and variety; confirm timing with a nearby agricultural extension officer.

## Seed or Seedling Requirements

Pineapple is propagated vegetatively, not from true seed. Farmers select from four planting-material types:

- **Suckers:** Shoots arising from the leaf axils or base of the mother plant. They are the fastest to fruit (about 12 to 16 months) and are the preferred smallholder material.
- **Slips:** Small shoots that grow just below the fruit on the stalk. They fruit in about 16 to 20 months.
- **Crowns:** The leafy top of the harvested fruit. Cheapest and most abundant, but slowest to fruit (about 20 to 24 months). Useful for expanding area at no cost.
- **Ground/basal suckers and stem sections (setts):** Used for rapid multiplication of scarce cultivars.

Selection and preparation:

- Choose material from healthy, high-yielding, disease-free mother plants only. Never take suckers from mealybug-infested or wilt-affected plants.
- Grade planting material by size so each block matures uniformly; mixing sizes gives an uneven, hard-to-manage harvest.
- Cure suckers and crowns by removing a few lower leaves and letting the cut base dry in shade for 3 to 7 days before planting; this reduces base rot.
- A dip of the base in an approved fungicide/insecticide solution can reduce rot and mealybug carry-over. Use only locally registered products at label rates, and confirm the correct active-ingredient class and dosage with an extension officer.

Material requirement: at typical densities a hectare needs roughly 30,000 to 60,000 planting units, so smallholders usually multiply their own material over one or two seasons before scaling up.

## Plant Spacing

Pineapple is grown at high density in single or double (paired) rows. Because it is a rosette herb rather than a tree, "orchard" spacing is defined by bed layout rather than tree canopy.

- **Single-row systems:** 30 cm between plants within the row and 60 cm between rows gives about 40,000 to 55,000 plants per hectare.
- **Double-row (paired-row) beds:** Two rows spaced 30 to 40 cm apart, plants 25 to 30 cm apart in the row, with a wider 90 cm path between bed pairs. This gives 50,000 to 65,000 plants per hectare and eases weeding, spraying and harvesting.
- **Low-input/manual smallholder plots:** Slightly wider spacing (e.g. 30 × 30 cm within beds) reduces competition and disease when inputs are limited.

Density trade-off: higher density raises total yield per hectare but reduces individual fruit size. Fresh-market growers targeting large fruit use lower density; processing and juice growers use higher density. Establishment-to-bearing timeline: expect the first (plant) crop fruit 14 to 24 months after planting depending on material type and density, followed by ratoon crops from selected suckers.

## Water Requirements

- **Total need:** Roughly 1,000 to 1,500 mm per cycle, ideally spread evenly. Thanks to CAM photosynthesis, pineapple loses relatively little water and tolerates dry spells, but sustained drought shrinks fruit.
- **Critical periods:** Adequate moisture matters most during (1) establishment of young suckers, (2) rapid vegetative growth, and (3) fruit development after flowering. Water stress during fruit sizing causes small, poorly filled fruit.
- **Irrigation methods:** Where rainfall is inadequate, drip irrigation is most efficient and keeps foliage dry, lowering disease risk. Furrow or watering-can irrigation works on small plots. The leaf rosette funnels rain and dew to the plant base, so overhead water is used efficiently.
- **Drainage over irrigation:** In pineapple, avoiding waterlogging is more important than maximising water. Never irrigate to the point of standing water; root and heart rot follow quickly.
- **Mulching:** A thick organic or dry-grass mulch is the cheapest moisture-conservation tool and is strongly recommended for rain-fed smallholders. See `water/irrigation_basics.md`.

## Fertiliser Recommendations

Base all rates on a soil test; the figures below are general ranges that vary by locality, soil and variety. Confirm specific products and rates with an extension officer.

- **Nitrogen (N):** The most yield-determining nutrient. Typical range 8 to 12 g N per plant per cycle, split into 3 to 5 applications during vegetative growth. Excess nitrogen late in the cycle delays maturity and lowers fruit sugar.
- **Potassium (K):** Critical for fruit size, sweetness and acidity balance. Often applied at rates similar to or higher than nitrogen, e.g. 10 to 15 g K₂O per plant, split during growth and early fruit development.
- **Phosphorus (P):** Needed in smaller amounts, e.g. 2 to 4 g P₂O₅ per plant, best applied at or near planting.
- **Application method:** Place fertiliser in the leaf axils or as a band beside the plant base. Foliar sprays of nitrogen and micronutrients are commonly used because the funnel-shaped rosette captures solution efficiently.
- **Micronutrients:** On very acidic or sandy soils watch for iron and zinc deficiency (interveinal yellowing); correct with approved foliar micronutrient sprays.
- **Low-input options:** Well-rotted manure or compost (10 to 20 t/ha), wood ash as a potassium source (apply cautiously as it raises pH), and legume residues can substitute for part of the mineral requirement. See `soil/composting_and_manure.md` and `soil/soil_testing_and_fertility.md`.

Avoid over-liming: pineapple thrives in acid soil, so do not add lime unless a test shows pH below about 4.0.

## Weed Management

Young pineapple is a poor competitor because it grows slowly and roots shallowly, so weed control in the first 6 to 8 months is decisive.

- **Mulching:** A heavy organic or plastic mulch is the most effective, sustainable weed suppressant and doubles as moisture conservation. This is the recommended first line for smallholders.
- **Manual weeding:** Hand-hoe or hand-pull weeds shallowly to avoid damaging surface roots. Weed regularly while the canopy is open; once the leaf canopy closes it shades out most weeds.
- **Cover and intercrop management:** Careful intercropping with short-duration legumes or vegetables in the wide inter-rows during the first months can smother weeds and add income, provided the intercrop is removed before it competes.
- **Herbicides (IPM last resort):** Pre-emergence and selective post-emergence herbicides are used in larger plantings. Use only locally registered products, follow label rates and buffer zones, wear protective equipment, and confirm the correct active-ingredient class and timing with an extension officer. Integrate chemical control with mulch and hand weeding rather than relying on it alone.

## Pest Management

Follow integrated pest management (IPM): use clean planting material, field sanitation, natural enemies and cultural methods first, and reserve chemicals for confirmed thresholds, always using locally registered active-ingredient classes at approved rates confirmed with an extension officer.

- **Mealybugs (*Dysmicoccus* spp.):** The most important pest. They feed on leaf bases and roots and transmit the virus complex causing **mealybug wilt**, the single biggest cause of pineapple crop loss. Mealybugs are tended and spread by ants. Manage by: using clean suckers, controlling ants (they protect mealybugs from predators), encouraging natural enemies (ladybird beetles, parasitic wasps), roguing infested plants, and, where thresholds are exceeded, targeted application of an approved insecticide (e.g. a systemic or contact class advised by an extension officer). See `pests/mealybugs.md` if available.
- **Scale insects:** Similar management to mealybugs; sap-feeders that weaken plants and disfigure fruit.
- **Symphylids and root grubs:** Soil-dwelling pests that damage roots, stunting plants. Manage through crop rotation, fallow, and destroying old pineapple debris that harbours them.
- **Nematodes (root-knot and reniform):** Cause stunting and yield decline, worse in continuous cultivation. Manage with rotation, fallowing, organic matter to boost beneficial soil organisms, and clean planting material. See `pests/nematodes.md` if available.
- **Fruit borers and beetles:** Sporadic; sanitation and prompt harvest reduce damage.
- **Fruit-piercing and sucking pests / birds and rodents:** Protect ripening fruit near harvest; simple netting or covers help on small plots.

Because ants are central to mealybug spread, ant control and habitat for natural predators are the most cost-effective IPM levers on smallholder pineapple.

## Disease Management

- **Mealybug wilt of pineapple:** A virus-associated disease spread by mealybugs; leaves redden, curl downward and the plant collapses. There is no cure once established. Control the mealybug and ant vectors, use clean material, and rogue affected plants (see Pest Management).
- **Heart rot and root rot (*Phytophthora* spp.):** The most damaging fungal-like diseases, favoured by waterlogging and heavy soils. The central leaves pull out easily and the growing point rots. Manage by: planting on raised beds with excellent drainage, avoiding low wet spots, curing suckers before planting, and applying an approved fungicide (e.g. a systemic class effective against oomycetes) only where advised. Drainage is the primary defence.
- **Fusarium disease (fusariosis / "fruitlet core rot" and stem/base rot):** Causes gummosis, base rot and internal fruit rot in some regions. Manage with clean, cured planting material, sanitation and resistant/tolerant cultivars where available.
- **Black rot / water blister (*Chalara / Thielaviopsis*):** A post-harvest fruit rot entering through wounds. Minimise fruit bruising at harvest, keep fruit cool, and handle gently.
- **Bacterial heart rot and fruit collapse:** Occurs in hot, humid zones; sanitation, drainage and careful handling reduce incidence.

General disease principles: choose free-draining sites, use disease-free cured planting material, rotate and fallow blocks between cycles, destroy old crop residue, and disinfect cutting tools between mother plants. Always confirm any registered fungicide product, active-ingredient class and dosage with a qualified extension officer. See `diseases/fungal_diseases_overview.md` if available.

## Growth Stages

Pineapple follows a long, distinct developmental sequence from establishment to bearing and ratooning.

1. **Establishment (0 to 3 months):** Roots and new leaves form from the planted sucker, slip or crown. Weed control and moisture are critical.
2. **Vegetative growth (3 to 10 months):** Rapid leaf production; the plant builds the leaf mass that will determine fruit size. This is the main nitrogen and potassium uptake period.
3. **Floral initiation / forcing (about 10 to 14 months):** The plant naturally initiates a flower when it reaches sufficient size and is triggered by cool nights or shortening days. On managed farms, uniform flowering can be induced ("forcing") using approved plant-growth regulators or ethylene-releasing treatments; confirm any product and method with an extension officer.
4. **Flowering and fruit set (2 to 3 months after initiation):** The red-to-purple inflorescence opens; hundreds of florets fuse into the developing fruit.
5. **Fruit development and maturation (4 to 6 months after flowering):** The fruit enlarges, then sugars rise and shell colour changes from green toward yellow.
6. **Harvest of plant crop:** Roughly 14 to 24 months after planting overall, depending on material type, density and climate.
7. **Ratoon cycles:** After harvest, one or two well-placed suckers are retained per plant to produce a ratoon crop, usually harvested 12 to 15 months later. Fields are typically replanted after two to four fruiting cycles as yield and fruit size decline.

## Harvest Indicators

- **Shell colour:** The base of the fruit turns from green to a golden-yellow; for the fresh export market fruit are often picked at the "colour break" (one-quarter yellow) so they arrive ripe, while local-market fruit are left to colour more fully for sweetness.
- **Aroma:** A sweet, characteristic pineapple smell develops at the base as sugars rise.
- **Eye flattening:** The individual "eyes" (fruitlets) flatten and broaden as the fruit fills.
- **Sound and firmness:** A dull, solid thud when tapped indicates a full fruit; a hollow sound suggests over-maturity.
- **Sugar/acid balance:** Where measurable, soluble solids of about 12 to 16 percent (°Brix) indicate good eating quality; pineapple does not sweeten further after harvest because it has little starch reserve, so it must be picked ripe enough.

Harvest by cutting the stalk with a clean, sharp knife, leaving a short stub. Harvest in the cool of the morning, handle gently, and keep fruit shaded immediately. Because pineapple does not ripen sweeter off the plant, harvest timing directly determines eating quality.

## Post-Harvest Handling

- **Handle gently:** Pineapple bruises easily; bruised tissue invites black rot and reduces market value. Avoid dropping, throwing or overfilling containers.
- **Field shade and prompt movement:** Move harvested fruit out of direct sun quickly; field heat shortens shelf life dramatically.
- **Trimming and cleaning:** Trim the stalk cleanly and remove soil and dead leaf debris. Some markets prefer the crown left on; others (processing) remove it.
- **Sorting and grading:** Grade by size, colour and freedom from defects (sunscald, cracks, rot, insect damage). Uniform grading improves price and buyer trust.
- **Curing cut stalk:** Allowing the cut stalk to dry briefly and, where recommended, treating the cut with an approved post-harvest fungicide reduces stem-end rot; confirm any product with an extension officer.
- **Packing:** Pack in ventilated crates or boxes, ideally single or well-cushioned layers, to prevent compression bruising during transport on rough roads.
- **Cool chain:** Where cooling is available, bring fruit down toward 8 to 13 °C soon after harvest. Do not go below about 7 °C, as pineapple suffers chilling injury (internal browning, dull skin).

For general handling principles see `post_harvest/handling_and_grading.md` if available.

## Storage

- **Shelf life:** Ripe pineapple is perishable, typically keeping 2 to 4 days at ambient tropical temperatures and up to about 2 to 4 weeks under proper cool storage.
- **Optimum storage:** 8 to 13 °C at 85 to 90 percent relative humidity for ripe fruit; mature-green fruit tolerate the lower end better. Below about 7 °C, chilling injury causes internal browning and loss of flavour.
- **Ambient/low-tech storage:** In the absence of cold rooms, keep fruit in a shaded, well-ventilated place off the ground; evaporative cooling structures (charcoal coolers, wet-sand or zeer-type systems) extend life for smallholders. See `post_harvest/low_cost_storage.md` if available.
- **Ethylene sensitivity:** Keep pineapple away from heavy ethylene sources (e.g. ripening bananas) during storage to avoid uneven over-ripening and rot.
- **Value addition:** Because fresh shelf life is short, drying (dried rings), juicing and jam-making are valuable ways for smallholders and cooperatives to reduce losses and capture more value. See `post_harvest/value_addition.md` if available.

## Expected Yield

Yields vary widely with variety, plant density, soil, water and management; treat these as agronomic ranges rather than guarantees.

- **Individual fruit weight:** Queen types about 0.7 to 1.5 kg; Smooth Cayenne and MD2 types about 1.5 to 2.5 kg, occasionally larger.
- **Plant-crop yield:** Well-managed smallholder plots commonly yield 40 to 60 t/ha; low-input rain-fed plots may yield 15 to 30 t/ha; high-density commercial fields can exceed 60 to 80 t/ha.
- **Ratoon crops:** Generally produce smaller fruit and lower total yield than the plant crop, declining with each cycle.
- **Yield drivers:** Plant density, uniform planting-material grading, adequate potassium and nitrogen, good drainage, effective mealybug/wilt control, and timely harvest are the main levers a smallholder can influence.

## Common Mistakes

- **Planting in poorly drained soil:** The leading cause of heart and root rot; always ensure free drainage and use raised beds on heavy soils.
- **Using mixed-size or diseased planting material:** Causes uneven maturity and spreads mealybug wilt and Fusarium. Grade material and take it only from healthy mother plants.
- **Neglecting early weed control:** Slow-growing young pineapple is easily smothered; failing to mulch or weed in the first months sharply cuts yield.
- **Ignoring ants and mealybugs:** Underestimating the ant–mealybug–wilt link leads to sudden, widespread collapse.
- **Over-liming:** Adding lime out of habit raises pH above the crop's acid optimum and induces micronutrient deficiency.
- **Excess or late nitrogen:** Produces lush leaves but delays maturity and yields watery, low-sugar fruit.
- **Harvesting too early:** Pineapple does not sweeten after picking; premature harvest gives permanently sour fruit and lost markets.
- **Rough handling at harvest:** Bruising triggers rot and destroys market value within days.
- **Continuous cropping without rotation:** Builds up nematodes, symphylids and soil disease; rotate or fallow between blocks.

## Best Farming Practices

- **Start with clean, graded, cured planting material** from proven healthy mothers; this single decision shapes uniformity, disease level and yield.
- **Prioritise drainage:** choose free-draining sites, plant on ridges or raised beds on heavier soils, and never allow standing water.
- **Mulch heavily** to conserve moisture, suppress weeds and moderate soil temperature — the highest-value low-cost practice for rain-fed smallholders.
- **Match planting to the rains** using the regional windows above, and stagger plantings for continuous supply and better prices.
- **Feed for fruit quality:** emphasise potassium alongside nitrogen, split applications, and correct micronutrients on acid sands; base rates on a soil test.
- **Practise IPM:** clean material, sanitation, ant and mealybug control, natural enemies, rotation, and chemicals only at confirmed thresholds using locally registered active-ingredient classes at approved rates.
- **Manage the ratoon deliberately:** select one or two strong suckers per plant, and replant blocks after two to four cycles before yield collapses.
- **Handle the harvest with care and speed:** pick at the right ripeness, harvest in the cool, shade fruit immediately, grade well, and move to market or cooling quickly.
- **Add value** through drying or juicing to cut losses from short fresh shelf life.
- **Keep records** of planting dates, material source, inputs and yields per block to improve decisions each cycle.
- Consult a local agricultural extension officer for cultivar choice, forcing methods, and all registered product recommendations. See `soil/soil_testing_and_fertility.md` and `post_harvest/handling_and_grading.md`.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: How long does a pineapple take to fruit after planting?**
A: The first (plant) crop is usually ready 14 to 24 months after planting. Suckers are fastest (about 12 to 16 months to fruit), slips are intermediate, and crowns are slowest (about 20 to 24 months). Grade material by type and size so a block matures together.

**Q: Can I grow pineapple from the crown of a fruit I bought?**
A: Yes. The leafy crown will root and grow, which is a free way to expand your planting. It is the slowest material to fruit, so use crowns to multiply plants and then take faster suckers from those plants for future blocks. Cure the crown base in shade for a few days before planting.

**Q: Why did my pineapples turn out small?**
A: Common causes are very high plant density, water stress during fruit development, low potassium or nitrogen, small or mixed planting material, and mealybug/nematode damage. Fresh-market growers wanting larger fruit should use slightly lower density and ensure adequate potassium and moisture during fruit sizing.

**Q: My plants are collapsing with reddish, curling leaves. What is wrong?**
A: This is the classic sign of mealybug wilt, a virus complex spread by mealybugs that are protected by ants. There is no cure for affected plants. Rogue them, control the ants and mealybugs, use clean planting material next time, and encourage natural predators. Separately, if the central leaves pull out easily and the core is rotten, suspect Phytophthora heart rot from waterlogging.

**Q: Does pineapple get sweeter after I pick it?**
A: No. Pineapple has almost no starch reserve to convert, so it does not become sweeter off the plant; it only softens and eventually rots. Pick it ripe enough for good eating quality, judged by base colour, aroma and flattened eyes.

**Q: Do I need to add lime to my soil for pineapple?**
A: Usually not. Pineapple prefers acidic soil (pH 4.5 to 5.5) and tolerates down to about pH 4.0, which is lower than most crops. Only lime if a soil test shows pH below about 4.0; over-liming causes iron and zinc deficiency and yellow leaves. See `soil/soil_testing_and_fertility.md`.

**Q: How much water does pineapple need, and can it survive dry spells?**
A: It performs best with 1,000 to 1,500 mm well distributed, but its water-conserving CAM photosynthesis lets it survive dry spells better than most fruit crops. Mulch heavily and provide supplementary water during establishment and fruit development. Crucially, never let water stand around the roots, as waterlogging kills faster than drought.

**Q: How many times can I harvest from one planting?**
A: After the plant crop you can keep one or two ratoon crops by retaining strong side suckers, giving roughly two to three harvests over three to four years. Fruit size and yield decline each cycle, so most growers replant the block after two to four cycles with fresh, disease-free material.
