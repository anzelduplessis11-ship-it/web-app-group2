# Avocado

## Overview
Avocado is a high-value perennial fruit tree that has become an important income crop for smallholder farmers across the East African highlands, parts of Southern and Central Africa, and increasingly West Africa. Demand is strong in both domestic urban markets and export markets, particularly for the Hass variety and other quality dessert types. For a smallholder, avocado can transform farm income because a single mature tree can yield hundreds of fruit, and the crop fits well into mixed farming, agroforestry, and coffee-shade systems. It also provides household nutrition, being rich in healthy fats, and the trees offer shade and soil-cover benefits. The main challenges are the tree's sensitivity to waterlogging and root rot, its relatively high value making quality and maturity timing critical, its exacting harvest and post-harvest handling requirements, and the long establishment period before full bearing. Success depends on choosing the right variety for altitude and market, planting on well-drained soil, protecting trees from *Phytophthora* root rot, and harvesting at correct maturity so fruit ripens well without spoiling.

## Scientific Name
*Persea americana* Mill. (family Lauraceae). Three horticultural races are grown: Mexican, Guatemalan, and West Indian, plus hybrids such as Hass (Mexican-Guatemalan).

## Suitable Climate
- Avocado is grown from warm lowlands to cool highlands depending on race and variety. Commercial dessert varieties such as Hass do best at 1,500-2,100 m in the tropics, where temperatures are cool.
- Optimal temperatures are 20-28°C. Prolonged heat above 35°C causes flower and fruit drop; frost damages flowers, young fruit, and shoots, with severity depending on race (Mexican types are the most cold tolerant).
- Rainfall of 1,000-1,600 mm per year, well distributed, is ideal. Dry periods must be bridged with irrigation, especially for young trees and during flowering and fruit set.
- Avocado is very sensitive to strong wind, which causes flower and fruit drop and limb breakage; sheltered sites or windbreaks improve set and yield.
- High humidity during flowering favours diseases such as anthracnose and cercospora.

## Suitable Soil
- Deep (at least 1 m), well-aerated, freely draining soils are essential. Avocado roots need oxygen and die quickly in waterlogged or compacted soil.
- Optimal pH is 5.5-6.5. Highly alkaline soils cause iron and zinc deficiency (leaf yellowing), and saline soils reduce growth.
- Light to medium loams and volcanic soils are excellent. Heavy clays and shallow or hardpan soils are high-risk unless drainage is engineered (mounds, ridges).
- Because *Phytophthora* root rot thrives in wet soil, drainage is the single most important soil requirement. See `soil/soil_types_africa.md`.

## Soil Preparation
- Select a site with natural drainage or slight slope; avoid low-lying, water-collecting spots.
- Dig planting holes about 60 x 60 x 60 cm (larger on heavy soil) at the onset of the rains.
- Mix topsoil with 10-20 kg well-decomposed compost or manure and a phosphorus source; refill the hole and let it settle before planting.
- On heavy or wet soils, plant on raised mounds or ridges 30-50 cm high to lift the root crown above waterlogging.
- Establish windbreaks on exposed sites before or at planting. On slopes, plant on the contour and use soil-conservation structures; see `soil/soil_conservation_and_erosion.md`.

## Planting Season by African Region

| Southern Africa | Warm wet season (Oct-Dec) | Avoid frost pockets; choose cold-tolerant types at higher latitude |
| Central Africa | Onset of main rains (Mar-May or Sept-Oct) | Ensure drainage in high-rainfall zones |

## Seed or Seedling Requirements
- Plant grafted seedlings of a named variety on selected rootstock. Grafted trees come true to type, bear earlier, and can carry root-rot-tolerant rootstocks.
- Choose varieties by market and altitude: Hass and Fuerte for export and premium markets; local green-skin varieties for domestic markets and warmer areas.
- Avocado flowers show a special "A-type" and "B-type" flowering behaviour (protogynous dichogamy) affecting pollination. Planting a mix of A-type and B-type varieties, or ensuring pollinators nearby, improves fruit set.
- Buy healthy plants with a clean, well-healed graft union, straight stem, and no signs of root rot or leaf disease. Harden nursery plants before field planting.

## Plant Spacing
- Orchard spacing depends on variety vigour, rootstock, and pruning plan.
- Standard spacing: 7 x 7 m to 8 x 8 m (about 150-200 trees/ha) for vigorous varieties.
- Semi-intensive with pruning: 6 x 6 m (about 270 trees/ha).
- High-density with strict canopy management: 5 x 5 m or closer, giving earlier per-hectare yields but requiring skilled pruning to prevent shading.
- Interplant A-type and B-type varieties within pollination distance to improve set. Orient and space rows for light penetration and airflow to limit disease.

## Water Requirements
- Avocado needs consistent moisture but must never be waterlogged. The balance between adequate water and good drainage is the central management challenge.
- Young trees require regular watering through dry spells (about 20-40 litres per tree per week depending on climate and soil).
- Critical water-demand periods are flowering, fruit set, and fruit sizing. Water stress at these stages causes heavy fruit drop and small fruit.
- Avoid heavy, infrequent flooding that saturates the root zone; use frequent, moderate applications. Drip or micro-sprinkler irrigation around the root zone is ideal.
- Mulching conserves moisture and keeps surface feeder roots cool and active. See `irrigation/irrigation_methods.md` and `irrigation/irrigation_scheduling.md`.

## Fertiliser Recommendations
- Apply nitrogen, phosphorus, and potassium in small, frequent doses, scaling up with tree age. Young trees need light, regular feeding; bearing trees need more, with higher potassium during fruit development.
- Split fertiliser into two to four applications per year, at the start of rains, at flowering, and after fruit set, spread under the canopy drip line.
- Apply 20-40 kg of well-decomposed manure or compost per bearing tree per year to maintain organic matter.
- Zinc, boron, and iron deficiencies are common, especially on alkaline soils, causing small mottled leaves and poor set; correct with targeted micronutrient supply. See `fertilisers/nutrient_deficiencies_guide.md` and `fertilisers/organic_fertilisers.md`.
- Avocado is sensitive to salinity and to excess chloride; avoid over-fertilising and use a soil test to guide rates. See `soil/soil_testing_and_fertility.md`.

## Weed Management
- Keep the tree basin weed-free during establishment to reduce competition for water and nutrients.
- Mulch heavily under the canopy with organic material; avocado has shallow feeder roots that benefit greatly from mulch for moisture, temperature, and root-rot suppression. Keep mulch away from the trunk.
- Slash or mow between rows; avoid deep cultivation that damages shallow roots and can spread root-rot pathogens.
- A cover crop in the inter-row during early years protects soil and can be managed as green manure. See `weeds/general_weed_management.md`.

## Pest Management
- Avocado generally has fewer serious insect pests than many fruit crops, but key ones include false codling moth (a quarantine pest for export), fruit flies, thrips, and mites.
- Thrips scar young fruit skin, downgrading quality for premium markets; monitor at fruit set. See `pests/thrips.md`.
- Fruit flies attack ripening fruit in warmer areas; use sanitation, baits, and timely harvest. See `pests/fruit_flies.md`.
- Systates weevils and scales can affect young trees and shoots; monitor and manage locally.
- Follow integrated pest management: scout regularly, conserve natural enemies, and use only locally registered products confirmed with a qualified agricultural extension officer, following `regulations/pesticide_safety_and_handling.md`.

## Disease Management
- *Phytophthora* root rot (*Phytophthora cinnamomi*) is the most serious and widespread disease, causing wilting, leaf drop, dieback, and tree death. Manage by planting on well-drained soil or mounds, using tolerant rootstock, avoiding waterlogging, using clean planting material, maintaining organic mulch, and never moving soil or water from infected areas to clean ones.
- Anthracnose (*Colletotrichum*) causes dark, sunken spots on fruit that develop during ripening; manage with orchard sanitation, canopy airflow, and protectant sprays where justified.
- Cercospora spot and scab blemish fruit skin, reducing grade.
- Stem-end rot causes fruit decay from the stalk end after harvest; careful harvesting and cool handling reduce it.
- Prevention centres on drainage, sanitation, resistant/tolerant material, and careful water management. Confirm any fungicide class with an extension officer.

## Growth Stages
For grafted avocado, the orchard develops through establishment to bearing over several years:
1. **Establishment (Year 1-2):** Root and canopy development. Priorities are watering without waterlogging, mulching, weed control, formative pruning, wind protection, and guarding against root rot.
2. **Juvenile/pre-bearing (Year 2-3):** Vigorous canopy growth. Remove early flowers so the tree builds structure.
3. **Early bearing (Year 3-5):** First commercial crops; yields increase each year.
4. **Full bearing (Year 5-8 onward):** Peak, stable production. The annual cycle is flowering (with A/B flower behaviour), fruit set, fruit development over many months (Hass can take 8-14 months from flowering to harvest depending on climate), and harvest.
5. **Mature/renovation:** Old trees can be pruned to control height and stimulate new bearing wood. Alternate bearing can occur and is reduced by good nutrition, water management, and pest control.

## Harvest Indicators
- Avocado does not soften on the tree; it must reach physiological maturity before picking so it will ripen properly after harvest. Picking too early gives fruit that shrivels or ripens unevenly; leaving too long risks over-mature, rancid fruit.
- Maturity indicators: fruit reaching characteristic size and shape for the variety, skin colour or bloom developing (Hass darkens as it ripens after picking), and dry-matter or oil-content testing (commercial packers use minimum dry-matter percentage as the maturity standard).
- Harvest by clipping the fruit with a short stalk (button) attached; do not pull, which tears the skin and invites stem-end rot.

## Post-Harvest Handling
- Handle very gently; avocado bruises internally and shows damage only after ripening, downgrading fruit at market.
- Harvest in cool conditions, keep fruit shaded, and move it out of field heat quickly.
- Clip fruit with a small stalk button; place, do not drop, into padded, ventilated crates.
- Sort and grade by size, maturity, and freedom from blemish, thrips scarring, and disease; remove damaged fruit. See `marketplace/product_grading_and_quality.md` and `harvesting/harvesting_best_practices.md`.
- Rapid cooling extends shelf life significantly for export chains.

## Storage
- Mature, unripe avocado can be held cool to slow ripening: commonly 5-7°C for green-skin types and slightly higher for some varieties, at high humidity, for two to four weeks depending on variety and maturity.
- Avoid chilling injury from storing below the safe temperature for the variety, which causes greying flesh and failure to ripen.
- Avocado is climacteric and ripens in response to ethylene; controlled ethylene ripening in warm rooms gives even market-ready fruit.
- Keep fruit away from strong ethylene sources during storage unless deliberately ripening. Without cold storage, sell mature fruit quickly or ripen in small batches. See `storage/perishable_storage.md`.

## Expected Yield
- Yields rise steeply with age. A young bearing tree may produce a few dozen fruit; a mature Hass or similar tree can yield 150-500 fruit or more per year.
- Smallholder orchards with basic management: 5-10 tonnes/ha.
- Well-managed orchards with good varieties, drainage, nutrition, and pest/disease control: 15-25 tonnes/ha or more at full bearing.
- Root rot, poor pollination weather, wind, and alternate bearing are the main causes of yield shortfall.

## Common Mistakes
- Planting on poorly drained or heavy soil, leading to fatal *Phytophthora* root rot.
- Using ungrafted seedling trees, giving late, variable, low-value fruit.
- Planting a single variety with the wrong flower type nearby, resulting in poor pollination and low set.
- Harvesting immature fruit that will not ripen properly, or over-mature fruit that goes rancid.
- Rough handling that causes hidden bruising, downgrading fruit once it ripens at market.
- Neglecting wind protection, causing heavy flower and fruit drop.

## Best Farming Practices
- Choose grafted, market-matched varieties on tolerant rootstock, and plant a compatible mix of A-type and B-type flowering varieties for pollination.
- Plant only on well-drained soil or raised mounds and manage water to avoid both drought and waterlogging.
- Mulch generously and maintain organic matter to protect shallow roots and suppress root rot.
- Protect trees from wind and monitor for thrips, fruit flies, and root rot, using integrated pest management.
- Harvest at correct maturity (dry-matter standard), handle gently, cool quickly, and grade carefully to protect the premium value. See `sustainability/sustainable_farming_practices.md` and `weather/climate_smart_agriculture.md`.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Why is my avocado tree wilting and dying even though I water it?**
A: The most likely cause is *Phytophthora* root rot, worsened by wet, poorly drained soil. Improve drainage, stop over-watering, mulch with organic matter, and use tolerant rootstock. Do not move soil or water from affected trees to healthy ones.

**Q: My tree flowers heavily but sets few fruit. Why?**
A: Poor pollination is common. Avocado has A-type and B-type flowering, so planting compatible varieties together and supporting pollinators improves set. Cold, wet, or windy weather at flowering, water stress, and boron/zinc deficiency also reduce set.

**Q: How do I know when to pick avocados?**
A: Avocado does not soften on the tree; pick only when physiologically mature. Use variety size and shape, skin colour change, and ideally a dry-matter (oil-content) test. Immature fruit will not ripen properly.

**Q: Why do my avocados rot at the stem end after picking?**
A: This is often stem-end rot, worsened by pulling fruit off (tearing the skin) and by warm handling. Clip fruit with a small stalk button, handle gently, and cool it quickly.

**Q: Can I grow Hass in a hot lowland area?**
A: Hass prefers cool highland conditions (roughly 1,500-2,100 m in the tropics). In hot lowlands, choose West Indian or lowland-adapted green-skin varieties instead.

**Q: How long until an avocado tree produces fruit?**
A: Grafted trees usually bear in 3-4 years and reach full production by about year 5-8. Seedling trees take longer and give variable fruit.

**Q: Do I need more than one avocado tree to get fruit?**
A: A single tree can set some fruit, but yields improve markedly when compatible A-type and B-type flowering varieties are planted together to cross-pollinate.

**Q: How can I reduce fruit drop?**
A: Maintain steady soil moisture through flowering and fruit set, protect trees from wind, ensure balanced nutrition (including boron and zinc), and control thrips and other pests that damage flowers and young fruit.
