# Spinach

## Overview
Spinach is a fast-growing, cool-season leafy vegetable grown for its tender, dark green, nutrient-dense leaves, which are rich in iron, folate, provitamin A, vitamin C, and dietary fibre. It matures quickly, giving smallholders an early return, and many varieties can be harvested repeatedly by the cut-and-come-again method over several weeks.

An important point of clarification for African growers is naming. This document covers TRUE spinach, *Spinacia oleracea*. In much of Africa the word "spinach" is applied loosely in markets and kitchens to other leafy greens, most often Swiss chard (a beet relative sold as "Swiss chard" or, confusingly, as "spinach" or "spinach beet") and to amaranth and other African leafy vegetables. These other greens are more heat-tolerant and, in hot lowlands, are usually the better choice. If you want a warm-season leafy green that behaves like "spinach" but withstands heat, see `crops/amaranth.md`. True spinach is genuinely challenging in hot African lowlands because heat and long days make it bolt (run to seed); it performs best in cool highlands, in the cool dry season, or in cooler months under irrigation.

Because spinach leaves are thin and high in moisture, the crop is highly perishable and must be handled, cooled, and sold quickly. It is also frequently eaten raw or lightly cooked, so food-safety hygiene during growing, washing, and handling is essential.

## Scientific Name
*Spinacia oleracea* L. Family: Amaranthaceae (formerly Chenopodiaceae), which also includes Swiss chard, beetroot, and quinoa. Note that despite sharing the family Amaranthaceae, true spinach is a different genus from amaranth (*Amaranthus* spp.); the two are distinct crops with very different heat tolerance.

## Suitable Climate
- Spinach is a cool-season crop. Optimum growing temperature is roughly 15-20 °C, with good growth in the range of about 10-24 °C.
- Seed germinates best in cool soil, around 10-20 °C. Germination is poor and erratic above about 27-30 °C soil temperature, so hot-season sowing often fails at the seed stage.
- The central climatic problem is BOLTING (running to seed). Spinach bolts in response to heat and, especially, to long days. Once it bolts, the plant sends up a flower stalk, leaves become small, bitter, and tough, and the crop is finished. Warm temperatures and lengthening days together trigger rapid bolting.
- Spinach tolerates light frost and even mild cold improves leaf quality; it is more limited by heat than by cold in African conditions.
- In hot lowland Africa, true spinach is difficult year-round; grow it in the coolest months, at altitude, or under shade netting. In cool highlands it can be grown for much of the year.
- Choose bolt-resistant ("slow-bolting") varieties for marginal or warm conditions.

## Suitable Soil
- Spinach grows best on fertile, well-drained loam or sandy loam high in organic matter, with good moisture-holding capacity.
- It needs a steady supply of nitrogen and moisture for tender, rapid leaf growth, so soils rich in well-decomposed organic matter are ideal.
- Preferred soil pH is 6.0-7.0. Spinach is sensitive to soil acidity; on acid soils below about pH 6.0 growth is stunted and leaves may show deficiency symptoms, so lime acid soils based on a soil test. See `soil/soil_testing_and_fertility.md` and `soil/soil_types_africa.md`.
- Spinach is moderately sensitive to salinity and to waterlogging; ensure good drainage to avoid root and damping-off diseases.
- A fine, firm, weed-free seedbed supports the even germination this small-seeded crop needs.

## Soil Preparation
1. Choose a fertile plot with good drainage that has not grown spinach, Swiss chard, or beet recently, to reduce shared soil diseases.
2. Clear weeds and residues and cultivate to a depth of about 20-30 cm to loosen the soil.
3. Incorporate well-decomposed compost or manure (about 15-30 t/ha) to build organic matter, nitrogen supply, and moisture retention. See `fertilisers/organic_fertilisers.md`.
4. Work the soil to a fine, level tilth so the small seed makes good contact and germinates evenly.
5. Form raised beds where drainage is poor or rainfall is heavy, to prevent waterlogging and damping-off.
6. On acid soils, apply lime well before sowing according to a soil test to reach the target pH.
7. Consider positioning beds where light shade or shade netting can be provided in warm periods to reduce heat stress and bolting.

## Planting Season by African Region

| Region | Typical Planting Window | Notes |
| --- | --- | --- |
| West Africa | November to February (cool dry / harmattan season) | Best confined to the cool season and cooler highland areas; hot humid lowlands cause rapid bolting and poor germination. Amaranth or Swiss chard are often better warm-season substitutes. |
| East Africa | Year-round in cool highlands; cooler months elsewhere | Highlands above ~1,500 m (Kenya, Ethiopia, Rwanda) allow near-continuous staggered sowing; time crops away from the hottest, longest days. |
| Southern Africa | February to August (autumn to winter) under irrigation | Grown mainly as a cool-season crop; frost is tolerated but avoid hot mid-summer plantings in low-lying areas. |
| Central Africa | Cooler months and higher-altitude sites | Practical mainly in highland and montane areas (eastern DRC, Rwanda, Burundi); hot humid basin lowlands cause bolting and disease. |
| North Africa | September to November and February to March | Mediterranean climate suits autumn and early-spring sowings under irrigation; avoid summer heat and long days, and hard winter frost. |

Stagger sowings every 2-3 weeks for continuous supply. Confirm timing locally with an agricultural extension officer, since altitude and microclimate strongly affect bolting.

## Seed or Seedling Requirements
- Spinach can be either direct-seeded or transplanted; direct seeding is most common because the crop is quick and transplanting checks growth.
- Seed rate is roughly 15-30 kg/ha for direct sowing depending on spacing and whether harvested as baby leaf (denser) or full leaf.
- Sow at 1.5-2.5 cm depth into moist soil; soaking seed for a few hours before sowing can speed germination.
- Germination takes about 5-14 days and is poor in warm soil (above ~27 °C); in warm conditions sow in the cool part of the day, keep the surface moist, and consider light shade to cool the soil.
- For transplants, raise seedlings in trays or a nursery bed and transplant at the 3-4 true-leaf stage, watering in well and handling roots gently to minimise transplant shock.
- Use fresh, certified seed of a bolt-resistant variety suited to your season; spinach seed loses viability within a few years.

## Plant Spacing
- For full-leaf spinach, space rows about 20-30 cm apart with plants 7-15 cm apart in the row.
- For baby-leaf or cut-and-come-again production, sow more densely in bands or closely spaced rows.
- Thin direct-seeded stands to the target spacing once seedlings are established; thinnings can be eaten as tender baby leaves.
- Adequate spacing improves airflow, which reduces downy mildew and leaf-spot diseases, and gives larger, cleaner leaves.
- Typical plant population ranges from roughly 300,000 to over 1,000,000 plants/ha depending on leaf-size target and system.

## Water Requirements
- Spinach needs frequent, even moisture for rapid, tender leaf growth; it is shallow-rooted and does not tolerate drought.
- Total seasonal water use is modest because the crop is short (roughly 200-350 mm), but the soil must never dry out, or growth checks, leaves toughen, and bolting is triggered.
- Water lightly and often to keep the topsoil consistently moist without waterlogging; waterlogging causes damping-off and root rots.
- Drip irrigation is ideal because it keeps leaves dry and reduces downy mildew and leaf spots. If overhead irrigation is used, water early in the day so foliage dries quickly. See `irrigation/irrigation_methods.md` and `irrigation/irrigation_scheduling.md`.
- Because leaves are eaten, often raw, use clean irrigation water and avoid contaminated water sources; see the food-safety note under Best Farming Practices.

## Fertiliser Recommendations
- Spinach is a leafy crop with a high nitrogen demand for tender, rapid growth, but nitrogen should be supplied in frequent light doses rather than one heavy application. See `fertilisers/fertiliser_basics.md`.
- Base rates on a soil test; typical guideline rates are nitrogen about 80-120 kg N/ha, phosphorus about 40-60 kg P2O5/ha, and potassium about 60-100 kg K2O/ha (confirm locally).
- Incorporate well-decomposed compost or manure before sowing to supply steady nitrogen and improve soil structure and moisture holding.
- Apply phosphorus and potassium and a portion of nitrogen at sowing, then side-dress or top-dress small amounts of nitrogen every 2-3 weeks and after each cut to keep leaves tender and green.
- Spinach can accumulate nitrate in leaves if heavily over-fertilised with nitrogen, so avoid excessive rates and heavy applications close to harvest.
- Watch for deficiencies: yellowing between veins or pale leaves may indicate nitrogen, magnesium, or manganese shortage, common on acid or poor soils. See `fertilisers/nutrient_deficiencies_guide.md`.

## Weed Management
- Spinach is a poor competitor when young because it is small and shallow-rooted, so early weed control protects both yield and leaf cleanliness.
- Weed by hand or shallow hoeing, keeping cultivation shallow to avoid damaging the shallow spinach roots.
- A stale-seedbed technique (germinating and killing a weed flush before sowing) reduces early weed pressure.
- Light organic mulch between rows suppresses weeds, conserves moisture, and keeps leaves clean and free of soil splash, which also reduces soil-borne disease.
- Follow integrated weed management and minimise herbicide use; any herbicide must be registered for spinach and used strictly per label. Confirm registered products and rates with a qualified agricultural extension officer. See `weeds/general_weed_management.md`.

## Pest Management
Follow integrated pest management (IPM): rotate crops, keep beds clean, monitor regularly, encourage natural enemies, and use chemicals only as a last resort and only registered, approved products confirmed with a qualified agricultural extension officer. Because leaves are eaten, often raw, always respect pre-harvest intervals. See `emergency_guides/pest_outbreak_emergency_guide.md`.

- Aphids (see `pests/aphids.md`): cluster on the undersides of leaves and in growing points, stunt plants, foul leaves with honeydew and sooty mould, and can transmit viruses. Encourage ladybirds, lacewings, and parasitic wasps; wash off or spray small outbreaks with soap or neem solutions.
- Leafminers: fly larvae tunnel between the leaf layers, leaving winding white or brown mines that make leaves unmarketable. Manage by removing and destroying mined leaves, using fine netting, rotating crops, and encouraging parasitic wasps.
- Cutworms: night-feeding caterpillars that cut seedlings at the soil line. Manage with clean, weed-free beds, hand-collection at night, and protective collars for valuable beds.
- Snails and slugs: chew holes in tender leaves, especially in wet, shaded, mulched conditions. Manage by reducing excess mulch and moisture near plants, hand-collecting at night, using barriers or traps, and keeping surroundings clean.
- Other pests include flea beetles (small shot-hole feeding), thrips (see `pests/thrips.md`), and whitefly (see `pests/whitefly.md`) in some areas.
- Where chemical control is justified, choose active-ingredient classes appropriate to the pest (for example a pyrethroid or a biological insecticide such as a *Bacillus thuringiensis* product for caterpillars) and confirm local registration and the pre-harvest interval with an extension officer.

## Disease Management
- Downy mildew (*Peronospora farinosa* f. sp. *spinaciae*, "blue mould"): the most important spinach disease, causing yellow patches on the upper leaf surface with greyish-purple fuzzy growth beneath, worst in cool, humid, wet conditions. Manage with resistant varieties, wider spacing, good airflow, drip rather than overhead irrigation, rotation, and destruction of infected debris; where justified, apply protective fungicides such as copper-based products.
- White rust (*Albugo occidentalis*): white blister-like pustules on the underside of leaves; manage with resistant varieties, rotation, airflow, and dry foliage.
- Leaf spots (Cercospora and Cladosporium): brown to grey spots that reduce leaf quality; manage with clean seed, rotation, sanitation, and dry foliage.
- Damping-off (*Pythium*, *Rhizoctonia*, *Fusarium*): rots seedlings before or after emergence, worst in cold, wet, poorly drained soil sown too densely. Manage with well-drained beds, clean seed, moderate sowing density, and avoiding waterlogging.
- Fusarium wilt (*Fusarium oxysporum* f. sp. *spinaciae*): a soil-borne wilt that yellows and kills plants, worse in warm soils and acid, low-fertility conditions; manage with resistant varieties, long rotations, raised pH, and good soil health.
- General disease control: use certified clean seed and resistant varieties, rotate at least 2-3 years away from spinach, chard, and beet, keep foliage dry, space adequately, and remove infected residues. For any fungicide, use only locally registered products and rates confirmed with a qualified agricultural extension officer, and respect pre-harvest intervals. See `emergency_guides/crop_emergency_guide.md`.

## Growth Stages
1. Germination and emergence (about 5-14 days): cool soil and even moisture are essential; warm soil gives poor stands.
2. Seedling establishment (1-2 weeks after emergence): cotyledons and first true leaves appear; the crop is weak against weeds and needs thinning.
3. Rosette / vegetative leaf growth (2-5 weeks): leaves expand rapidly in a low rosette; this is the main harvestable and most nitrogen- and moisture-demanding phase.
4. Harvest maturity (roughly 30-60 days from sowing depending on variety and temperature): leaves reach usable size for baby-leaf or full-leaf harvest.
5. Bolting (undesirable): triggered by heat and long days, the plant elongates and sends up a flower stalk; leaves turn small, tough, and bitter, ending the harvest. Harvest promptly and use bolt-resistant varieties to delay this.

## Harvest Indicators
- Spinach matures fast, often ready 30-60 days after sowing depending on variety, temperature, and whether harvested as baby leaf or full leaf.
- Harvest baby leaves when they reach about 5-10 cm, and full leaves when they are full-sized, dark green, tender, and turgid but before they toughen.
- Two harvest methods: (1) single harvest by cutting or uprooting the whole plant, or (2) cut-and-come-again, where outer leaves or the whole rosette are cut about 3-5 cm above the crown, allowing regrowth for several further cuts over the following weeks.
- Harvest early in the cool of the morning when leaves are turgid and cool; leaves cut in the heat wilt quickly.
- Harvest before any sign of bolting; once the plant starts to send up a stalk, leaves become bitter and tough.
- Use clean, sharp knives and clean containers to avoid contaminating the edible leaves. See `harvesting/harvesting_best_practices.md`.

## Post-Harvest Handling
- Spinach is highly perishable: thin, moist leaves wilt and yellow within hours in heat. Speed and cooling are essential.
- Harvest into shade, remove field heat quickly, and keep leaves cool and moist from field to market.
- Remove damaged, yellow, diseased, and soil-splashed leaves during sorting.
- Wash gently in clean, safe water to remove soil and reduce contamination, then drain excess water; leaves that stay wet and warm rot quickly, so cool them promptly after washing.
- Grade by leaf size, colour, tenderness, and freedom from pest damage and disease; bunch or bag neatly for market. See `marketplace/product_grading_and_quality.md`.
- Handle gently to avoid bruising and crushing, which cause rapid darkening and decay.
- Move to market or the buyer quickly; short shelf life makes prompt sale critical to reduce losses. See `marketplace/buyer_preferences_and_seasonal_demand.md`.

## Storage
- Spinach stores best cold and moist: about 0-4 °C with 90-95% relative humidity, under which it may keep for only about 5-14 days at best.
- Without refrigeration, keep leaves in shade, cool, and moist using evaporative cooling structures or damp cloth, and sell within a day or two; shelf life is very short at ambient tropical temperatures.
- Keep leaves out of direct sun and wind, which cause rapid wilting.
- Do not store with ethylene-producing fruit such as bananas and ripening tomatoes, which accelerate yellowing.
- Avoid sealing wet, warm leaves in airtight bags, which promotes rotting; cool first, then keep moist and ventilated.
- Because storage life is so short, plan production and harvest timing around a ready market rather than long holding. See `storage/perishable_storage.md`.

## Expected Yield
- Smallholder / low-input systems: roughly 5-12 t/ha of leaves, often limited by heat, bolting, poor germination, and irregular water.
- Improved smallholder systems (bolt-resistant varieties, good nutrition, even irrigation, cut-and-come-again management): roughly 12-25 t/ha over multiple cuts.
- High-input / commercial systems (adapted varieties, drip irrigation, full nutrition, disease control, cool climate): roughly 25-40 t/ha over the season.
- Yields depend heavily on temperature and day length; in hot lowlands true spinach may bolt before reaching good yield, which is why heat-tolerant leafy greens such as amaranth or Swiss chard are often more productive there. See `crops/amaranth.md`.

## Common Mistakes
- Growing true spinach in hot lowland conditions where it bolts before yielding; a heat-tolerant leafy green would perform far better.
- Confusing true spinach with Swiss chard or amaranth and choosing the wrong crop for the season.
- Sowing in warm soil, causing poor, erratic germination.
- Using bolt-susceptible varieties in warm or long-day conditions.
- Letting the soil dry out, which checks growth, toughens leaves, and triggers bolting.
- Over-applying nitrogen, causing soft disease-prone growth and nitrate accumulation.
- Overhead watering in humid conditions, promoting downy mildew and leaf spots.
- Delaying harvest until leaves toughen or the plant bolts.
- Handling and storing leaves warm and dry, causing rapid wilting and losses.
- Washing leaves in unclean water, creating a food-safety hazard for a raw-eaten crop.

## Best Farming Practices
- Confirm you are growing true spinach and match the crop to a cool season, highland site, or shaded/netted bed; in hot lowlands consider amaranth or Swiss chard instead.
- Choose bolt-resistant varieties and sow into cool, moist soil; stagger sowings for continuous supply.
- Build soil organic matter and supply nitrogen in frequent light doses for tender leaves.
- Irrigate little and often to keep soil evenly moist, preferring drip irrigation to keep foliage dry.
- Practise IPM: monitor for aphids, leafminers, and slugs, encourage natural enemies, use netting, and use only registered chemicals confirmed with an extension officer, always respecting pre-harvest intervals.
- Rotate at least 2-3 years away from spinach, chard, and beet to reduce disease.
- Harvest promptly in the cool morning, cool leaves quickly, grade well, and sell fast because the crop is highly perishable.
- Food safety: because spinach is often eaten raw, irrigate and wash with clean, safe water, keep manure well composted and applied well before harvest, harvest into clean containers, and maintain hand and equipment hygiene to reduce contamination risk.
- Adopt climate-smart practices such as shade netting, mulching, and organic matter build-up to buffer heat and moisture stress. See `soil/soil_conservation_and_erosion.md`.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: My "spinach" looks different from pictures of true spinach. Am I growing the right crop?**
A: In many African markets, "spinach" refers to Swiss chard or to amaranth and other African leafy vegetables rather than true spinach (*Spinacia oleracea*). True spinach has smooth or savoyed dark-green leaves in a low rosette and is a cool-season crop. If your green is tall, red-stemmed, or thrives in the heat, it is more likely amaranth or chard. See `crops/amaranth.md`.

**Q: Why does my spinach quickly send up a flower stalk and turn bitter?**
A: This is bolting, triggered by heat and long days. Once spinach bolts, leaves become small, tough, and bitter. Grow it in the cool season or highlands, use bolt-resistant varieties, keep soil consistently moist, and harvest promptly. In hot lowlands, a heat-tolerant leafy green is a better choice.

**Q: Can I grow true spinach in a hot, low-lying area?**
A: It is difficult. Heat causes poor germination and rapid bolting. You can try in the coolest months, at altitude, or under shade netting with bolt-resistant varieties, but for reliable warm-season leafy greens consider amaranth (`crops/amaranth.md`) or Swiss chard instead.

**Q: Why is my spinach seed germinating poorly?**
A: Spinach germinates poorly in warm soil above about 27 °C. Sow into cool, moist soil, soak seed for a few hours before sowing, keep the surface moist, and provide light shade to cool the soil in warm conditions. Use fresh, viable seed.

**Q: How often can I harvest from one planting?**
A: Using the cut-and-come-again method, you can cut the outer leaves or the whole rosette about 3-5 cm above the crown and allow regrowth, taking several cuts over a few weeks. Keep the crop well fed and watered, and stop once the plant bolts or leaves toughen.

**Q: What causes yellow patches with grey fuzz under the leaves?**
A: This is downy mildew, the most common spinach disease, favoured by cool, humid, wet conditions. Use resistant varieties, space plants for airflow, water by drip rather than overhead, rotate crops, and remove infected leaves; apply a registered protective fungicide only if justified and confirmed with an extension officer.

**Q: How do I keep harvested spinach fresh without a refrigerator?**
A: Harvest in the cool morning, keep leaves shaded, cool, and moist, and sell within a day or two, as spinach wilts within hours in heat. Use evaporative cooling or damp cloth, keep it out of sun and wind, and away from ripening fruit. See `storage/perishable_storage.md`.

**Q: Is it safe to eat spinach raw straight from the field?**
A: Only after careful washing with clean, safe water. Because spinach is often eaten raw, use clean irrigation water, apply only well-composted manure well before harvest, harvest into clean containers, and wash leaves thoroughly to reduce the risk of contamination and illness.
