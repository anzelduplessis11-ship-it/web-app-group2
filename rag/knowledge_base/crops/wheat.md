# Wheat

## Overview
Wheat is one of the world's most important cereal grains and a growing staple in several African countries, where it is milled into flour for bread, chapati, mandazi, injera blends, pasta, and other baked and fermented foods. In Africa, wheat is a temperate crop that performs best in cool, high-altitude highlands and in the cooler months of subtropical and Mediterranean climates. Major African producers include Ethiopia, Kenya, Tanzania, South Africa, Egypt, Morocco, Algeria, Tunisia, and Sudan. Wheat is valued by smallholders because it stores well as dry grain, has strong and growing market demand as countries seek to reduce flour imports, and fits well into rotations with legumes and oilseeds. The main challenges for African wheat growers are heat and drought stress in lowland areas, rust diseases (especially stem rust and yellow/stripe rust), and access to quality seed of adapted, disease-resistant varieties. Wheat is broadly divided into bread wheat (*Triticum aestivum*) and durum wheat (*Triticum durum*), the latter used mainly for pasta and semolina and favoured in North Africa and parts of Ethiopia.

## Scientific Name
*Triticum aestivum* L. (bread wheat). Durum or macaroni wheat is *Triticum durum* Desf. (also written *Triticum turgidum* subsp. *durum*). Both belong to the grass family Poaceae (Gramineae).

## Suitable Climate
- Wheat is a cool-season crop; optimal growing temperatures are approximately 15-25°C during the main growth period.
- Grain filling is best at moderate temperatures; sustained temperatures above 30-32°C during flowering and grain filling cause "heat shrivelling," reduced grain weight, and yield loss.
- In tropical Africa, wheat is grown mainly at altitudes above roughly 1,500-2,800 m (highland production, as in Ethiopia and Kenya) or in the cool dry season at lower elevations under irrigation (as in Egypt, Sudan, and North Africa).
- Requires approximately 400-650 mm of well-distributed water over the season; rainfed production depends on reliable cool-season rainfall, while lowland and North African production is largely irrigated.
- Sensitive to frost at flowering, and highly sensitive to hot, dry winds during grain filling.
- Long daylength and cool nights favour high yields; short, cloudy, humid conditions increase rust disease pressure.

## Suitable Soil
- Grows best on deep, well-drained loam to clay-loam soils with good water-holding capacity and organic matter.
- Optimal soil pH is approximately 6.0-7.5; wheat is moderately sensitive to strong soil acidity and aluminium toxicity, which are common in highland soils.
- Tolerates a wide range of soils but performs poorly on waterlogged, shallow, or very sandy soils.
- Saline and sodic soils reduce establishment and yield; durum wheat is slightly more salt-tolerant than bread wheat.
- Fertile vertisols (heavy black cracking clays) are widely used for wheat in Ethiopia and Sudan but require careful drainage management.
- Conduct a soil test to guide lime and fertiliser use; see `soil/soil_testing_and_fertility.md`.

## Soil Preparation
- Prepare a fine, firm, level seedbed to ensure uniform seed placement and good seed-to-soil contact, which is critical for even emergence of this small-seeded cereal.
- Ploughing followed by one or two harrowings is typical; avoid over-tilling, which destroys soil structure and increases erosion risk.
- On heavy vertisols prone to waterlogging, use raised broad beds and furrows (the broad-bed-and-furrow system used in Ethiopia) to drain excess water and improve root health.
- Level the field where irrigation is used so water spreads evenly and no ponding occurs.
- Where soil is acidic (highland areas), incorporate agricultural lime several weeks before planting based on soil-test recommendations to raise pH and reduce aluminium toxicity.
- Incorporate crop residues, compost, or well-rotted manure ahead of planting to build organic matter; see `fertilisers/organic_fertilisers.md`.
- Conserve moisture in rainfed systems with timely, shallow secondary tillage and by minimising the gap between final tillage and sowing.

## Seed or Seedling Requirements
- Wheat is direct-sown from seed; it is not transplanted.
- Use certified or good-quality seed of a variety adapted to your altitude, season, and, critically, carrying resistance to the rust diseases prevalent in your area.
- Seed rate: approximately 100-150 kg/ha for broadcast or drilled sowing, adjusted for seed size, germination percentage, and seedbed conditions; higher rates (up to ~175 kg/ha) are used for broadcasting on rougher seedbeds.
- Target plant population is roughly 200-350 plants per square metre for good tillering and canopy cover.
- Sow seed 3-5 cm deep into moist soil; deeper sowing delays and weakens emergence.
- A fungicidal and insecticidal seed treatment against smuts, seedling blights, and early aphids is beneficial where these are common; confirm locally registered, approved seed dressings and rates with a qualified agricultural extension officer.
- Renew seed regularly, as recycling farm-saved seed for many seasons erodes varietal purity and rust resistance.

## Plant Spacing
- Wheat is usually drilled or broadcast rather than planted at fixed hills.
- Drilled row spacing is typically 15-25 cm between rows, with seed spread continuously within the row.
- Narrower rows and even distribution improve early ground cover, suppress weeds, and can raise yield.
- When broadcasting, aim for even seed distribution and lightly cover the seed by harrowing to achieve consistent depth.
- Uniform stand establishment matters more than exact spacing; gaps and dense patches both reduce yield.

## Water Requirements
- Total seasonal water requirement is approximately 400-650 mm, depending on climate, variety, and season length.
- The most water-sensitive stages are crown root initiation/tillering, stem elongation (booting), heading and flowering, and early grain filling.
- Moisture stress at flowering causes poor grain set ("blanks"), while stress during grain filling reduces grain size and weight.
- Under irrigation, apply water at establishment, tillering, booting, flowering, and grain filling; avoid both drought stress and waterlogging.
- Excess water and high humidity late in the season promote rusts and other foliar diseases and can cause lodging (falling over).
- In rainfed highland systems, match variety maturity to the length of the rainy season so grain filling is not cut short by early drought.

## Fertiliser Recommendations
- Wheat is responsive to nitrogen and phosphorus; base rates on a soil test where possible. See `soil/soil_testing_and_fertility.md` and `fertilisers/fertiliser_basics.md`.
- Nitrogen: typically 60-120 kg N/ha for reasonable yields; split the nitrogen, applying part at or soon after sowing and the remainder at tillering to early stem elongation to improve efficiency and reduce losses.
- Phosphorus: apply the full phosphorus requirement (commonly 20-40 kg P/ha, i.e. roughly 45-90 kg P2O5/ha) as a basal dressing at sowing, as phosphorus is important for root development and tillering, especially in cool soils.
- Potassium is needed where soils are deficient; many African wheat soils supply adequate potassium, but confirm with a soil test.
- On highland acidic soils, sulphur, zinc, and boron deficiencies can occur; correct based on soil analysis.
- Over-application of nitrogen encourages excessive leafiness, lodging, and greater rust susceptibility, so match nitrogen to realistic yield potential and water supply.
- Where legumes such as faba bean, chickpea, or lentil precede wheat in rotation, reduce nitrogen accordingly; see `crops/chickpea.md`.
- Incorporate organic manure or compost to improve long-term fertility; see `fertilisers/organic_fertilisers.md`.

## Weed Management
- Weeds compete most strongly during the first 4-6 weeks; early control protects yield.
- Wild oats, ryegrass, and various broadleaf weeds (such as wild mustard and Ethiopian mustard) are major wheat weeds and can be difficult to distinguish from the crop early on.
- A vigorous, uniform, adequately fertilised stand with narrow rows shades out many weeds.
- Practise crop rotation to break weed cycles, and use clean, weed-free seed to avoid introducing weed seeds such as wild oats.
- Hand weeding or hoeing at around 25-35 days after emergence is effective on smallholdings; two weedings may be needed in weedy fields.
- Selective herbicides (pre-emergence and post-emergence) are widely used in larger wheat systems; grass and broadleaf herbicides differ, so correct identification and timing are essential. Confirm locally registered, approved products and rates with a qualified agricultural extension officer, and rotate herbicide modes of action to prevent resistance.
- See `weeds/general_weed_management.md` for integrated weed control principles.

## Pest Management
- Key field pests include aphids (which also transmit barley yellow dwarf virus), armyworms and African armyworm, cutworms, wheat stem sawfly, termites (in drier areas), and Russian wheat aphid in southern and eastern Africa.
- Birds (such as quelea in the Sahel and East Africa) can cause severe losses at grain filling and require bird-scaring and community coordinated control.
- Storage pests include the grain weevil, lesser grain borer, and Angoumois grain moth; see the Storage section and `storage/grain_storage.md`.
- Practise integrated pest management (IPM): scout fields weekly, conserve natural enemies (ladybird beetles and parasitic wasps that attack aphids), use tolerant varieties, and time planting to escape peak pest periods.
- For aphids, only spray when populations are high and beneficial insects are scarce; see `pests/aphids.md`.
- Where chemical control is justified, use products from the appropriate active-ingredient class only when scouting thresholds are exceeded, and confirm locally registered, approved products and dosages with a qualified agricultural extension officer.

## Disease Management
- The most damaging wheat diseases in Africa are the rusts: stem (black) rust, leaf (brown) rust, and yellow (stripe) rust. Aggressive stem rust races (such as the Ug99 lineage first identified in East Africa) can devastate susceptible varieties.
- Other important diseases include Septoria leaf blotch, Fusarium head blight (scab), powdery mildew, smuts (loose and covered smut), bunt, and take-all root disease.
- The single most important control is growing rust-resistant varieties adapted to your area and replacing them as resistance breaks down; consult extension services for current recommended varieties.
- Use clean, treated, certified seed to control seed-borne smuts and bunt.
- Practise crop rotation with non-cereal crops (legumes, oilseeds) to reduce residue-borne diseases; remove or plough down infected residues.
- Avoid excessive nitrogen and dense canopies that trap humidity and favour foliar disease.
- Triazole and other fungicide classes are used to manage rusts and leaf blotches when disease appears early on a susceptible crop, timed around flag-leaf emergence and heading. Confirm locally registered, approved fungicides, timing, and dosages with a qualified agricultural extension officer, and rotate fungicide groups to slow resistance.
- Report unfamiliar or explosive rust outbreaks promptly, as new races spread rapidly across regions.

## Growth Stages
1. Germination and emergence (0-10 days after sowing): the seedling emerges and the first leaves unfold.
2. Tillering (approximately 15-40 days): the plant produces side shoots (tillers), each capable of bearing a head; tiller number strongly influences yield.
3. Stem elongation/jointing (approximately 40-60 days): stems lengthen and the developing head moves up inside the stem.
4. Booting and heading (approximately 55-75 days): the head swells within the flag-leaf sheath and then emerges.
5. Flowering (anthesis, approximately 65-85 days): pollination occurs; this stage is very sensitive to heat and drought.
6. Grain filling (milk to dough stages, approximately 80-110 days): grains fill with starch, moving from watery (milk) to soft (dough) to hard.
7. Physiological maturity and ripening (approximately 100-140 days depending on variety and altitude): grain hardens and moisture falls toward harvestable levels.

## Harvest Indicators
- The crop turns from green to golden-yellow, and the stems, leaves, and heads dry down.
- Grains become hard and difficult to dent with a thumbnail; they no longer feel doughy.
- At physiological maturity the grain moisture is around 30%, but for direct harvesting and safe storage, wait until grain moisture falls to approximately 13-14%.
- Heads bend over slightly and rattle when shaken once dry.
- Harvest promptly once mature to reduce losses from shattering, lodging, bird damage, and pre-harvest sprouting if late rains occur.

## Post-Harvest Handling
- Harvest by cutting or combining when grain is dry; in smallholder systems, cut with sickles, bundle, and stack or windrow to complete drying before threshing.
- Thresh by machine, animal treading, or beating, then winnow to separate grain from chaff and straw.
- Dry grain further on clean tarpaulins or drying floors (never bare soil) until moisture is approximately 12-13% for safe storage.
- Clean and sort grain to remove chaff, weed seeds, stones, broken grains, and shrivelled kernels, which lower quality and attract storage pests.
- Handle grain gently to avoid cracking, which reduces milling quality and increases susceptibility to storage insects and moulds.
- Wheat straw is valuable as livestock fodder, bedding, mulch, or for soil incorporation.

## Storage
- Store thoroughly dried grain (approximately 12-13% moisture) in a cool, dry, well-ventilated, rodent-proof store.
- Use clean containers, and consider hermetic (airtight) bags or metal/plastic silos, which control storage insects without chemicals by depriving them of oxygen.
- Inspect stored grain regularly for insects, heating, moisture, and mould, and act quickly if problems appear.
- If using grain protectants, apply only locally approved products at label rates; confirm with a qualified agricultural extension officer.
- Keep new grain separate from old stock, and clean the store thoroughly before filling to remove residual insect populations.
- See `storage/grain_storage.md` for detailed guidance.

## Expected Yield
- Smallholder rainfed highland systems with basic management: approximately 1.5-3 tonnes/ha.
- Well-managed rainfed crops with improved, rust-resistant varieties and adequate fertiliser: approximately 3-5 tonnes/ha.
- Irrigated, high-input systems (as in Egypt and parts of southern Africa): approximately 5-7 tonnes/ha, with well-managed fields reaching higher.
- Actual yield depends heavily on variety, disease (especially rust) control, water supply, soil fertility, and timely operations.

## Common Mistakes
- Growing susceptible varieties in rust-prone areas, then losing much of the crop to stem or yellow rust.
- Recycling farm-saved seed for many seasons, eroding varietal purity and disease resistance and spreading smuts.
- Sowing too deep or on a poorly prepared, cloddy seedbed, causing thin, uneven stands.
- Applying all nitrogen at once, or applying excess nitrogen, leading to lodging and heavier disease.
- Poor drainage on heavy vertisols, causing waterlogging and root disease.
- Harvesting late so the crop shatters, lodges, or is stripped by birds, and storing grain that is not dried enough.

## Best Farming Practices
- Choose certified, rust-resistant varieties matched to your altitude and season, and refresh seed regularly.
- Prepare a fine, firm, well-drained seedbed and sow at the correct depth and rate for a uniform, competitive stand.
- Rotate wheat with legumes (chickpea, faba bean, lentil) and oilseeds to break disease and weed cycles and improve nitrogen supply; see `crops/chickpea.md`.
- Split nitrogen applications and match total fertiliser to realistic yield potential and water supply.
- Scout regularly for rusts and aphids and act early using IPM, reserving fungicides and insecticides for when they are justified and confirmed with extension advice.
- Harvest promptly at maturity, dry grain properly, and store in clean, sealed, rodent-proof conditions.
- Adopt climate-smart practices such as residue retention, reduced tillage where appropriate, and moisture conservation; see `sustainability/sustainable_farming_practices.md`.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Why does my wheat get orange or brown powdery pustules on the leaves and stems?**
A: Those are rust diseases (leaf, stem, or yellow rust), the most damaging wheat diseases in Africa. The best protection is growing a resistant variety suited to your area, using clean certified seed, avoiding excess nitrogen, and applying an appropriate fungicide only when needed and confirmed with an extension officer. Report severe outbreaks promptly, as new rust races spread rapidly.

**Q: Can I grow wheat in a hot, low-altitude area?**
A: Wheat is a cool-season crop and struggles with heat, especially during flowering and grain filling. In lowlands it is grown only in the cool dry season, usually under irrigation, and needs heat-tolerant varieties. In tropical Africa, rainfed wheat is best suited to cool highlands above about 1,500 m.

**Q: How much seed do I need per hectare?**
A: Roughly 100-150 kg/ha of good-quality seed for drilled or broadcast sowing, adjusting upward for larger seed, lower germination, or rough seedbeds. Aim for an even, dense stand of about 200-350 plants per square metre.

**Q: Do I need to add nitrogen fertiliser, and how much?**
A: Yes, wheat responds well to nitrogen, commonly 60-120 kg N/ha depending on yield potential and water supply. Split it between sowing and tillering for better efficiency. Base rates on a soil test, and reduce nitrogen after a legume crop. Too much nitrogen causes lodging and more disease.

**Q: When is wheat ready to harvest?**
A: When the crop turns golden, the straw and heads dry down, and the grain is hard and cannot be dented with a thumbnail. For safe storage, grain moisture should be about 13-14% or lower. Harvest promptly to avoid shattering, lodging, and bird losses.

**Q: How do I stop weevils and moths from destroying my stored wheat?**
A: Dry the grain to about 12-13% moisture, clean it well, and store it in a clean, dry, rodent-proof place. Hermetic (airtight) bags or sealed silos control insects without chemicals by removing oxygen. Inspect regularly, and use only locally approved grain protectants if necessary.

**Q: Is durum wheat different from bread wheat?**
A: Yes. Durum (macaroni) wheat, *Triticum durum*, is harder, higher in protein, and used mainly for pasta and semolina; it is important in North Africa and parts of Ethiopia. Bread wheat, *Triticum aestivum*, is used for bread and most baked goods. Durum is slightly more heat- and salt-tolerant but generally lower yielding than bread wheat.

**Q: What is a good crop to rotate with wheat?**
A: Legumes such as chickpea, faba bean, lentil, or field pea, and oilseeds, are excellent rotation partners. They break wheat disease and weed cycles and add nitrogen to the soil, reducing the fertiliser needed for the following wheat crop.
