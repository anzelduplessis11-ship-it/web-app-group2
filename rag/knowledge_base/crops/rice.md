# Rice

## Overview
Rice is an increasingly important staple and cash crop in Africa, grown under rainfed lowland, rainfed upland, and irrigated systems. Demand across the continent has grown faster than local production in many countries, making rice an important crop for both food security and market opportunity.

## Scientific Name
*Oryza sativa* L. (Asian rice, the dominant cultivated species); *Oryza glaberrima* Steud. (African rice, grown in some West African locations)

## Suitable Climate
- Optimal growing temperature: 20-35°C, with 25-30°C ideal for most growth stages.
- Requires 1,000-1,500 mm of water over the growing season for lowland/irrigated systems (through rainfall and/or irrigation); upland rice can be grown with 750-1,200 mm rainfall.
- Sensitive to cold temperatures below 15°C, particularly during flowering.

## Suitable Soil
- Lowland/irrigated rice: performs well on heavier clay or clay loam soils that retain water for flooding.
- Upland rice: performs better on well-drained loam soils.
- Optimal pH: 5.5-6.5, though rice tolerates a wider pH range than most cereals, including some tolerance for waterlogged, poorly aerated soils.

## Soil Preparation
- Lowland/irrigated rice: puddle the field (plough and harrow under flooded conditions) to reduce water percolation and suppress weeds, then level the field for even water distribution.
- Upland rice: plough and harrow as for other cereals to create a fine seedbed.
- Construct or maintain bunds (earth ridges) around lowland plots to retain water.

## Planting Season by African Region
| Region | Typical Planting Window | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| West Africa | May-July (rainfed); year-round under irrigation | Major rice-growing region, especially inland valleys and irrigated schemes |
| East Africa | Mar-May and Sept-Nov | Irrigated schemes allow more flexible timing |
| Southern Africa | Nov-Jan | Limited to irrigated schemes in most areas due to rainfall constraints |
| Central Africa | Year-round in suitable lowland/swamp areas | Rainfall generally well distributed |

## Seed or Seedling Requirements
- Can be established by direct seeding or by transplanting nursery-raised seedlings.
- For transplanting: raise seedlings in a nursery for 21-28 days before transplanting.
- Seed rate: approximately 40-60 kg/ha for transplanting nurseries; 60-100 kg/ha for direct seeding.
- Use certified seed of a variety suited to the water regime (lowland, upland, or irrigated) and local disease pressure.

## Plant Spacing
- Transplanted rice: 20 cm x 20 cm spacing, 2-3 seedlings per hill, giving approximately 250,000 hills/ha.
- Direct-seeded rice: drilled in rows 20-25 cm apart, or broadcast, depending on system and equipment available.

## Water Requirements
- Irrigated/lowland rice: maintain 5-10 cm standing water through most of the growth cycle, particularly during tillering to flowering; drain field 2-3 weeks before harvest.
- Upland rice: relies on rainfall; ensure adequate soil moisture, especially at flowering, the most drought-sensitive stage.
- Alternate wetting and drying (AWD) can be used in irrigated systems to conserve water without significant yield loss.

## Fertiliser Recommendations
- General guideline: 80-120 kg N/ha, 30-60 kg P2O5/ha, 30-60 kg K2O/ha, adjusted for soil fertility and yield target.
- Split nitrogen application: basal at transplanting/sowing, topdress at tillering, and topdress at panicle initiation.
- Apply phosphorus fully at basal, as it is less mobile in flooded soils.
- Zinc deficiency is common in some flooded rice soils; apply zinc sulfate if deficiency symptoms (stunted growth, bronzing of leaves) appear.

## Weed Management
- Weed lowland rice at 2-3 weeks and 5-6 weeks after transplanting/emergence; maintaining standing water helps suppress weeds.
- Upland rice requires more intensive weeding since standing water is not available to suppress weeds; weed at least twice during the first 6 weeks.
- Approved pre-emergence and post-emergence herbicides can supplement hand weeding where available.

## Pest Management
- Key pests: stem borers, rice gall midge, birds (especially quelea), and rodents.
- Use bird-scaring methods and timely harvesting to reduce bird damage.
- Monitor for stem borer damage (deadhearts in vegetative stage, whiteheads at reproductive stage).

## Disease Management
- Key diseases: rice blast, bacterial leaf blight, and sheath blight.
- Use resistant varieties where available.
- Avoid excessive nitrogen application, which increases susceptibility to blast and sheath blight.
- Maintain proper plant spacing for airflow to reduce fungal disease pressure.

## Growth Stages
1. Germination and seedling stage (0-25 days for transplanted systems, including nursery period).
2. Vegetative stage: tillering (25-55 days).
3. Reproductive stage: panicle initiation, booting, heading, and flowering (55-85 days).
4. Ripening stage: milk, dough, and maturity (85-120 days depending on variety).

## Harvest Indicators
- 80-85% of grains on the panicle turn golden yellow/straw coloured.
- Grain moisture content at harvest is typically 20-25%.
- Drain lowland fields 2-3 weeks before expected harvest to allow the field to dry and facilitate harvesting.

## Post-Harvest Handling
- Harvest promptly at maturity to reduce shattering losses and bird/rodent damage.
- Dry paddy (unmilled rice) to 12-14% moisture content before storage or milling.
- Thresh carefully to minimize grain breakage, which reduces milling quality and market value.

## Storage
- Store dried paddy in a cool, dry, well-ventilated area protected from moisture and pests.
- Milled rice has a shorter storage life than paddy due to loss of the protective bran layer; store milled rice in sealed, moisture-proof containers.
- See `storage/grain_storage.md` for further guidance.

## Expected Yield
- Traditional rainfed systems: 1.5-2.5 tonnes/ha (paddy).
- Improved rainfed management: 3-4 tonnes/ha.
- Irrigated systems with good management: 5-7 tonnes/ha.
- High-input irrigated systems: 7-9+ tonnes/ha.

## Common Mistakes
- Excessive nitrogen application, increasing lodging and disease susceptibility.
- Poor water management (either insufficient flooding or failure to drain before harvest).
- Delayed harvesting, increasing shattering losses and grain quality decline.
- Inadequate drying before storage or milling, leading to broken grain and mould.

## Best Farming Practices
- Match variety choice to the water regime available (irrigated, lowland rainfed, or upland).
- Use certified seed and appropriate seedling age at transplanting (21-28 days).
- Practice alternate wetting and drying where water is limited, to conserve water without major yield penalty.
- Drain fields before harvest to ease harvesting and reduce grain moisture.
- Rotate with non-rice crops in upland systems where feasible to manage disease and pest pressure.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Why does my rice show yellow-brown lesions with grey centres on leaves?**
A: This is a common symptom of rice blast disease, which is worsened by excessive nitrogen application and dense planting. Reduce nitrogen rates and consider resistant varieties.

**Q: How much water does rice actually need — does it have to be flooded?**
A: Continuous flooding is traditional for lowland rice and helps suppress weeds, but rice can be grown with alternate wetting and drying or under upland (non-flooded) systems with suitable varieties and adequate rainfall.

**Q: Why is my harvested rice grain breaking a lot during milling?**
A: This is often due to harvesting at too high or too low a moisture content, delayed threshing after harvest, or improper drying (too rapid or uneven). Harvest at 20-25% moisture and dry gradually to 12-14%.
