# Garlic

## Overview
Garlic is a high-value *Allium* crop grown widely across Africa for cooking, flavouring, and its recognised medicinal and preservative properties. It is a close relative of onion and shares many of the same management principles, so growers familiar with onion production can transfer much of that knowledge; see `crops/onion.md` for closely related practices on beds, curing, and *Allium* rotation. Unlike onion, garlic is propagated **vegetatively from individual cloves rather than from seed**, and the size and health of the planting clove strongly determine the size of the bulb harvested. Garlic stores exceptionally well when properly cured, allowing smallholders to sell over many months and to capture higher prices in the off-season. Its main agronomic challenge in Africa is the crop's need for a period of cool temperatures (vernalisation) to form good bulbs, which makes cool highlands and the cool dry season far more suitable than hot, humid tropical lowlands.

## Scientific Name
*Allium sativum* L.

## Suitable Climate
- Optimal growing temperature: 13-24°C during vegetative growth, with cool conditions early in the season being essential for good bulb formation.
- Garlic requires **vernalisation**: exposure to cool temperatures (roughly 0-10°C for several weeks), applied either to the stored cloves before planting or experienced by the young plant in the field, to trigger proper bulbing. Without enough cold, plants produce leaves but few or poorly divided bulbs.
- Cool highlands and the cool dry season are strongly preferred; hot, humid tropical lowlands often give small, single-clove ("round") bulbs and heavy disease pressure.
- A period of longer days and warmer, drying weather at the end of the season helps bulbs mature and cure.
- Requires roughly 400-550 mm of water over the growing cycle, from rainfall and/or irrigation, with dry conditions preferred at maturity.
- Wet, humid weather late in the season increases foliar and bulb diseases and shortens storage life.

## Suitable Soil
- Well-drained, fertile sandy loam to loam soils rich in organic matter are ideal.
- Optimal pH: 6.0-7.0; garlic tolerates slightly alkaline soils better than strongly acidic ones.
- Avoid heavy clay and waterlogged soils, which cause bulb rots, poor clove development, and misshapen bulbs.
- Garlic is shallow-rooted, so a friable, fertile topsoil with steady moisture and good drainage is important.
- Match variety and field choice to local soil conditions; see `soil/soil_types_africa.md` for regional soil guidance and `soil/soil_testing_and_fertility.md` for testing.

## Soil Preparation
- Plough and harrow to a fine, firm tilth, as cloves need good soil contact for uniform sprouting.
- Incorporate well-decomposed manure or compost several weeks before planting; avoid fresh manure, which encourages excessive leaf growth, forked bulbs, and rot organisms. See `fertilisers/organic_fertilisers.md`.
- Form raised beds in heavy soils or high-rainfall areas to improve drainage and reduce bulb rots.
- Remove clods and stones so bulbs can expand evenly and form regular shapes.
- Conduct a soil test where possible to guide fertiliser and lime decisions; see `soil/soil_testing_and_fertility.md`.
- On sloping land, follow contour beds and conservation practices to protect the fertile topsoil; see `soil/soil_conservation_and_erosion.md`.

## Planting Season by African Region
| Region | Typical Planting Window | Notes |

| West Africa | Dry season (Oct-Feb) under irrigation, mainly in cooler highland or Sahelian zones | Hot humid lowlands unsuitable; choose softneck types and cool sites |
| East Africa | Cool highland planting aligned with cooler months (often May-Aug) | High-altitude areas (>1,500 m) give best bulbing; irrigate through dry spells |
| Southern Africa | Autumn to early winter planting (Mar-May) under irrigation | Cool winter growth suits vernalisation; harvest in dry spring weather |
| Central Africa | Drier, cooler windows in highland zones, under irrigation | High humidity raises disease risk; ensure strong drainage and airflow |
| North Africa | Autumn planting (Oct-Nov) for spring/early-summer harvest | Well suited to irrigated cool-season production; both softneck and hardneck grown |

## Seed or Seedling Requirements
- Garlic is propagated **vegetatively from individual cloves**, not from botanical seed; each planted clove grows into one new bulb.
- Use large, healthy, disease-free cloves from firm, well-cured bulbs. Larger cloves generally produce larger bulbs, so grade planting stock and plant only the biggest, soundest cloves; use smaller cloves for eating.
- Source clean planting material from a reputable supplier or from a known healthy crop. Poor-quality or infected cloves are the most common way that stem and bulb nematode, white rot, and basal rot are spread, so **clean seed stock is critical**.
- Separate cloves from the bulb only shortly before planting to avoid drying out, and discard any that are soft, mouldy, damaged, or discoloured at the base.
- **Choosing type:**
  - *Softneck* garlic (does not usually produce a flower stalk) tolerates warmer, subtropical conditions better, is easier to grow across much of Africa, gives more cloves per bulb, and stores the longest, making it the safer choice for most smallholders.
  - *Hardneck* garlic produces a central flower stalk (scape), needs more reliable cold, generally has fewer but larger cloves, and stores for a shorter time; it suits cool highlands.
- Planting rate: roughly 500-1,000 kg of cloves per hectare depending on clove size and spacing, which is a significant cost to plan for.
- Where cloves must be encouraged to bulb in marginally warm areas, pre-chilling planting cloves at around 5-10°C for several weeks before planting can improve bulbing.

## Plant Spacing
- Plant cloves upright with the pointed (growing) end up and the flat basal plate down, at a depth of about 3-5 cm.
- Typical spacing: 25-40 cm between rows and 8-12 cm between cloves within the row.
- Closer spacing raises yield per hectare but tends to give smaller individual bulbs; wider spacing produces larger bulbs favoured by some markets.
- Plant into a firm, moist seedbed and cover cloves gently; planting too deep delays emergence, while planting too shallow exposes bulbs and reduces quality.

## Water Requirements
- Garlic needs consistent, even soil moisture during establishment and active bulb development, but is intolerant of waterlogging.
- Critical water-sensitive stages are sprouting/establishment and the period of rapid bulb enlargement.
- **Stop irrigation as the tops begin to fall over and yellow** near maturity; withholding water at this stage helps bulbs firm up, aids curing, and reduces bulb rots. This mirrors onion management in `crops/onion.md`.
- Prefer drip or furrow irrigation over overhead watering, which wets the foliage and encourages leaf diseases and rust; see `irrigation/irrigation_methods.md`.
- Schedule irrigation to soil moisture and crop stage rather than a fixed calendar; see `irrigation/irrigation_scheduling.md`.

## Fertiliser Recommendations
- Apply a basal dressing at planting supplying phosphorus and some nitrogen to support root and early leaf growth.
- Topdress with nitrogen in one or two splits during early to mid vegetative growth to build a strong leaf canopy, but **stop nitrogen once bulbing begins**, as late nitrogen delays maturity, softens bulbs, and reduces storage life.
- Ensure adequate potassium and phosphorus for firm, well-filled bulbs and good storability; garlic also responds to sulphur, which contributes to pungency and quality.
- Incorporate well-decomposed organic matter to improve soil structure and moisture retention; avoid fresh manure.
- In the absence of a soil test, aim for moderate, balanced nutrition with phosphorus at planting and controlled early nitrogen, adjusted to soil fertility.
- See `fertilisers/fertiliser_basics.md` for application methods and timing, and `fertilisers/nutrient_deficiencies_guide.md` for identifying deficiency symptoms.

## Weed Management
- Keep garlic weed-free, as its sparse, upright foliage competes poorly with weeds, and shallow roots are easily disturbed.
- Weed by hand or with shallow cultivation to avoid damaging shallow roots and developing bulbs.
- Mulching between rows suppresses weeds, conserves moisture, and moderates soil temperature, which suits the crop's preference for cool root conditions.
- The critical period for weed control is the first several weeks after emergence, before the crop establishes a canopy.
- See `weeds/general_weed_management.md` for integrated weed control principles.

## Pest Management
- Practice integrated pest management (IPM): use clean planting material, rotate away from *Allium* crops, scout regularly, encourage natural enemies, and treat chemically only when monitoring shows it is justified.
- Key pests:
  - **Onion thrips** (*Thrips tabaci*) are the most important insect pest, causing silvery streaking and distorted leaves and thriving in hot, dry weather; scout regularly, especially in dry spells. See `pests/thrips.md`.
  - **Onion maggot/fly** larvae tunnel into the base of plants and developing bulbs, causing wilting and rot; crop rotation and field sanitation reduce risk.
  - **Stem and bulb nematode** (*Ditylenchus dipsaci*) is a serious, largely **seed-borne** pest that causes swollen, distorted plants and soft, cracked bulbs, and persists in soil and planting material; the primary control is planting only clean, nematode-free cloves and rotating away from *Allium* crops for several seasons.
  - **Mites** and, in some areas, **aphids** can also affect the crop; see `pests/aphids.md` for related sucking-pest principles.
- Rotate away from onion, garlic, leek, and other *Allium* crops to break pest cycles, and remove and destroy crop residues after harvest.
- Where chemical control is needed, refer to active-ingredient CLASSES rather than brand products, rotate classes to manage resistance, and **always confirm locally registered, approved products and dosages with a qualified agricultural extension officer**.
- For sudden severe infestations, see `emergency_guides/pest_outbreak_emergency_guide.md`.

## Disease Management
- Use certified or known clean cloves, rotate away from *Allium* crops for at least 3-4 seasons, ensure good drainage and airflow, and avoid overhead irrigation as core preventive measures.
- Key diseases:
  - **White rot** (*Sclerotium cepivorum*) is the most serious and persistent garlic disease: it produces small, long-lived resting bodies (sclerotia) that survive in soil for many years, rotting roots and bulbs. Once a field is infested it is very difficult to clear, so prevention is essential: never move soil or planting material from infested fields, plant only clean stock, and use long rotations away from all *Allium* crops.
  - **Basal (Fusarium) rot** causes soft, brown rot at the base plate and is favoured by warm soils and wounded or stressed plants; use clean cloves and avoid damage.
  - **Botrytis neck rot** develops from infected necks and wounds during curing and storage; thorough curing and gentle handling greatly reduce it.
  - **Rust** (*Puccinia* species) produces orange pustules on leaves, worsened by humid, crowded conditions.
  - **Downy mildew** and **purple blotch** cause leaf lesions and dieback in wet, humid weather.
- Rogue out and destroy diseased plants, do not compost infected material, and disinfect tools where soil-borne diseases are present.
- Where fungicides are considered, refer to active-ingredient classes such as copper-based fungicides for foliar diseases, and always confirm registered products and dosages with an extension officer.
- For severe or unclear disease situations, see `emergency_guides/crop_emergency_guide.md`.

## Growth Stages
1. Clove sprouting and emergence (root and shoot growth from the planted clove; about 1-3 weeks).
2. Early vegetative growth (leaf and root development establishing the plant).
3. Active vegetative growth and canopy build-up (main leaf production; cool temperatures during this phase support later bulbing).
4. Bulb initiation, triggered by the combination of prior cold exposure (vernalisation), lengthening days, and warming temperatures.
5. Bulbing and clove division (the bulb enlarges and divides into individual cloves; in hardneck types a scape/flower stalk emerges and is often removed to favour bulb size).
6. Maturation and senescence (tops yellow and fall over, skins dry, bulb firms up, signalling readiness for harvest).

## Harvest Indicators
- Lower leaves yellow and dry while several upper leaves are still green; when roughly one-third to one-half of the leaves have dried, the crop is nearing harvest.
- Bulbs are firm and well divided into cloves, with the wrapper skins beginning to dry.
- In hardneck types, the scape straightens after curling; removing scapes earlier improves bulb size.
- Stop irrigation as tops fall over to firm bulbs and aid curing.
- Do not delay harvest too long, as over-mature bulbs split, wrapper skins break down, and cloves separate, all of which reduce quality and storage life.
- Time from planting to harvest is typically about 4-6 months (roughly 90-150 days) depending on variety, temperature, and altitude.
- Harvest in dry weather by loosening the soil and lifting bulbs carefully to avoid bruising; see `harvesting/harvesting_best_practices.md`.

## Post-Harvest Handling
- Lift bulbs gently and avoid cuts and bruises, which become entry points for storage rots.
- **Curing:** dry the bulbs with tops and roots attached in a shaded, well-ventilated place (or windrowed under dry field conditions) for two to four weeks, until the necks are tight, the outer skins are papery, and the roots are dry and brittle. Keep curing bulbs out of direct sun and rain, and never pile them in closed heaps.
- After curing, trim the roots and cut the tops to a short neck (or braid softneck types), handling bulbs gently.
- Sort out soft, bruised, split, or diseased bulbs and any showing base-plate rot; use these first rather than storing them.
- Grade bulbs by size and quality for market; larger, tight, clean bulbs command better prices. See `marketplace/product_grading_and_quality.md`.
- Curing is the single most important step for achieving garlic's long storage life.

## Storage
- Store only well-cured, sound, dry bulbs in a cool, dry, well-ventilated place; good airflow is essential to prevent rots and sprouting.
- Ideal storage is cool and dry with low humidity; avoid the moderate-cool, damp conditions that encourage sprouting and root regrowth.
- Use nets, slatted crates, braids, or well-ventilated stores rather than sealed sacks or plastic bags that trap moisture.
- Well-cured softneck garlic can store for several months (often 6-8 months or more), while hardneck types store for a shorter period; storage life varies with variety, curing quality, and conditions.
- Inspect stored bulbs regularly and remove any that are soft, sprouting, or rotting.
- Well-stored garlic lets farmers sell into higher off-season prices; see `marketplace/buyer_preferences_and_seasonal_demand.md` and `storage/perishable_storage.md`.

## Expected Yield
- Smallholder systems with basic management and local planting stock: approximately 3-6 tonnes/ha.
- Improved management with quality graded cloves, adequate fertiliser, and good irrigation: approximately 6-12 tonnes/ha.
- High-input systems in suitable cool climates with clean seed stock and full crop management: 12-20+ tonnes/ha.
- Actual yield depends heavily on planting-clove size and health, adequate vernalisation, plant population, water management, and disease control (especially freedom from white rot and nematodes).

## Common Mistakes
- Growing garlic in hot, humid lowlands without enough cool exposure, resulting in poor bulbing or single, undivided "round" bulbs.
- Planting small, weak, or diseased cloves, which produce small bulbs and spread nematode, white rot, and basal rot.
- Reusing planting stock from a field known to have white rot or nematode problems, spreading persistent soil-borne pests.
- Applying nitrogen or irrigation late into bulbing, delaying maturity and reducing storage life.
- Planting cloves upside down or too deep, delaying emergence and reducing bulb quality.
- Skipping or rushing curing, then storing in humid, poorly ventilated conditions, leading to heavy losses from rot and sprouting.
- Rotating garlic with onion or other *Allium* crops instead of away from them, allowing shared pests and diseases to build up.

## Best Farming Practices
- Choose the right type for your climate: softneck for warmer subtropical areas and long storage, hardneck for reliably cool highlands.
- Grow garlic in cool conditions (cool highlands or the cool dry season) so that plants receive the cold exposure needed for good bulbing.
- Plant only large, healthy, clean cloves from a trusted source, and grade planting stock carefully.
- Maintain even soil moisture during bulbing, then stop watering as tops fall over.
- Control nitrogen timing, stopping applications once bulbing begins.
- Cure bulbs thoroughly before storage and handle them gently throughout.
- Rotate away from all *Allium* crops for at least 3-4 seasons, and never introduce planting material or soil from fields affected by white rot or nematodes.
- Practice IPM and confirm any chemical controls with a qualified extension officer.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Can I plant garlic from seed like I do with onion?**
A: No. Garlic is grown vegetatively from individual cloves, not from botanical seed. Each clove you plant grows into one new bulb, so you break a bulb into cloves and plant the largest, healthiest ones. This is a key difference from onion, which is usually raised from seed in a nursery; see `crops/onion.md`.

**Q: My garlic grew well but formed a single round bulb instead of dividing into cloves. Why?**
A: This usually means the plants did not get enough cool exposure (vernalisation) to trigger clove division, which is common in hot lowland conditions. Grow garlic in cooler highlands or the cool dry season, choose a suitable variety, and consider pre-chilling planting cloves at about 5-10°C for several weeks before planting.

**Q: Does clove size really affect my harvest?**
A: Yes. Larger, healthy planting cloves generally produce larger bulbs, while small cloves produce small bulbs. Grade your planting stock and plant only the biggest, soundest cloves, keeping smaller ones for eating.

**Q: Should I grow softneck or hardneck garlic?**
A: For most African smallholders, especially in warmer or subtropical areas, softneck garlic is the safer choice because it tolerates warmth better, gives more cloves per bulb, and stores the longest. Hardneck types need more reliable cold and store for a shorter time, but suit cool highlands and produce a scape that can be harvested and eaten.

**Q: What is white rot and why is everyone so worried about it?**
A: White rot is a soil-borne fungal disease that rots roots and bulbs and produces tiny resting bodies that survive in the soil for many years. Once a field is infested, garlic and other *Allium* crops become very difficult to grow there. The main defence is prevention: plant only clean cloves, never move soil or planting material from infested fields, and use long rotations away from all *Allium* crops.

**Q: When should I stop watering my garlic?**
A: Stop irrigation once the tops begin to yellow and fall over near maturity. Withholding water at this stage helps the bulbs firm up, aids curing, and reduces bulb rots, just as with onion.

**Q: How long can I store garlic, and how do I get it to last?**
A: Well-cured softneck garlic can store for several months, often 6-8 months or more, while hardneck types store for less time. Good storage depends on thorough curing until the necks are tight and skins are papery, gentle handling, sorting out damaged bulbs, and keeping the crop in a cool, dry, well-ventilated place rather than sealed sacks. See `storage/perishable_storage.md`.

**Q: Can I plant garlic straight after an onion crop on the same land?**
A: No. Garlic and onion are both *Allium* crops and share pests and diseases such as thrips, nematodes, and white rot. Rotate away from all *Allium* crops for at least 3-4 seasons to prevent these problems from building up in the soil.
