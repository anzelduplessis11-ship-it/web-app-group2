# Tea

## Overview
Tea is a perennial evergreen shrub grown for its young leaves and buds, which are processed into black, green, or specialty teas. It is a major export and cash crop in the cool, high-rainfall highlands of East and Southern Africa, notably Kenya, Uganda, Tanzania, Rwanda, Burundi, Malawi, and parts of the Democratic Republic of Congo. Africa, led by Kenya, is one of the world's largest exporters of black tea. Tea suits smallholders well because it provides a steady, year-round income from frequent plucking rather than a single seasonal harvest, and a well-managed bush can remain productive for 50 years or more. Success depends on establishing good clonal material, maintaining a level plucking table through skilled pruning and plucking, feeding the crop adequately, and delivering fresh, undamaged leaf quickly to a factory or processing point. Because tea is sold into quality-graded markets, leaf freshness and the "two leaves and a bud" plucking standard directly determine price. For grading expectations see `marketplace/product_grading_and_quality.md`, and for capturing more value see `pricing/price_negotiation_and_value_addition.md`.

## Scientific Name
*Camellia sinensis* (L.) Kuntze, family Theaceae. Two main varieties are grown: var. *sinensis* (small-leaved, cold-tolerant) and var. *assamica* (large-leaved, more vigorous, widely grown in African lowland-highland conditions).

## Suitable Climate
- Tea thrives in warm, humid highlands with temperatures of approximately 18-25°C and no frost.
- It requires high, well-distributed rainfall of about 1,200-2,500 mm per year; dry spells reduce flush and yield.
- Optimal altitude ranges from roughly 1,200 to 2,200 m in the tropics, where cool nights favour quality.
- High humidity and frequent light rainfall or mist support continuous leaf production.
- Tea is sensitive to prolonged drought, frost, hail, and strong sun on young plants; shade during establishment helps.
- Cloudy, cool conditions at high altitude tend to produce higher-quality tea; see `weather/climate_smart_agriculture.md`.

## Suitable Soil
- Deep, well-drained, acidic soils rich in organic matter are essential; tea is an acid-loving (calcifuge) crop.
- The ideal pH is approximately 4.5-5.5; tea will not thrive in neutral or alkaline soils.
- Soils must be free-draining, as tea roots are very intolerant of waterlogging.
- Deep soils (over 1 m) allow strong rooting and drought tolerance.
- Confirm acidity and nutrient status by soil testing before establishment; see `soil/soil_testing_and_fertility.md`.

## Soil Preparation
- Clear land, control perennial weeds thoroughly before planting, and conserve topsoil.
- On the steep slopes typical of tea country, establish contour terraces, ridges, or grass strips to prevent erosion; see `soil/soil_conservation_and_erosion.md`.
- Dig planting holes or continuous furrows and incorporate well-decomposed organic matter.
- Do not add lime; tea requires an acidic soil, and liming can harm the crop.
- Prepare land and establish erosion controls ahead of the rains so planting coincides with reliable moisture.

## Planting Season by African Region
| Region | Typical Planting Window | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Kenya highlands | March-May (long rains) | Main establishment window; steady rainfall aids survival |
| Uganda, Rwanda, Burundi | Sept-Nov and March-May | Bimodal rainfall gives two windows |

## Seed or Seedling Requirements
- Tea is almost always established from vegetatively propagated (clonal) cuttings rather than seed, to preserve yield and quality traits.
- Single-leaf cuttings from selected high-yielding, disease-tolerant clones are rooted in a shaded nursery for about 9-12 months.
- Harden off seedlings gradually before transplanting healthy, well-rooted plants at the start of the rains.
- Approximately 10,000-15,000 plants are required per hectare depending on spacing.
- Raise extra plants for gap-filling, and obtain clonal material from certified or reputable sources to ensure trueness to type and health.

## Plant Spacing
- Common spacing is approximately 1.2 m between rows and 0.6-0.75 m within rows, giving roughly 10,000-13,000 plants per hectare, planted along the contour.
- Closer spacing hastens ground cover and canopy closure, which suppresses weeds and protects the soil.
- Establishment years (years 1-3): the young bushes are trained and frame-formed through formative pruning and pegging or tipping to build a spreading, low frame.
- Bearing years (year 3-4 onward): bushes form a continuous "plucking table"; maintain a level table at a comfortable plucking height through regular plucking and periodic pruning.
- Wider inter-row access paths are left for plucking and field movement in larger fields.

## Water Requirements
- Tea needs consistently high moisture, ideally 1,200-2,500 mm of well-distributed rainfall, because leaf is removed continuously.
- Dry spells cause the bushes to go dormant, sharply reducing flush and yield.
- Critical needs are steady soil moisture during active flushing; prolonged drought delays recovery even after rains return.
- In drier or drought-prone areas, mulching and, where feasible, supplementary irrigation sustain production; see `irrigation/irrigation_methods.md`.
- Good drainage is equally critical, as waterlogging kills tea roots.

## Fertiliser Recommendations
- Tea is a heavy and continuous feeder of nitrogen because leaf (and therefore nutrients) is harvested repeatedly.
- Base rates on soil and leaf analysis and split nitrogen into several applications through the year to match uptake; see `soil/soil_testing_and_fertility.md` and `fertilisers/fertiliser_basics.md`.
- Potassium and phosphorus support root health, bush vigour, and stress tolerance.
- Use fertiliser formulations suited to acid-loving crops; avoid materials that raise soil pH.
- Return prunings to the soil surface as mulch to recycle nutrients and organic matter; see `fertilisers/organic_fertilisers.md`.
- Correct micronutrient deficiencies (for example zinc) where diagnosed; see `fertilisers/nutrient_deficiencies_guide.md`.

## Weed Management
- Keep young tea weed-free during establishment, as weeds compete strongly with slow-growing bushes.
- Once the canopy closes, the dense plucking table shades out most weeds, greatly reducing weeding needs.
- Use mulch from prunings and cut grass to suppress weeds, conserve moisture, and protect the soil.
- Hand weeding and slashing are preferred on slopes; avoid disturbing shallow tea roots.
- Where herbicides are used, apply only locally registered products carefully to avoid contact with tea foliage and drift; see `weeds/general_weed_management.md` and `regulations/pesticide_safety_and_handling.md`.

## Pest Management
- Key pests include tea mosquito bug (helopeltis), thrips, mites (red spider mite), aphids, scale insects, and various leaf-eating caterpillars; termites can attack young plants.
- Practise integrated pest management: regular scouting, healthy vigorous bushes, conservation of natural enemies, and chemical control only when monitoring justifies it.
- Because tea leaf is consumed as a beverage, minimising pesticide use and strictly observing pre-harvest intervals and maximum residue limits is essential for market access.
- Maintain field hygiene, balanced nutrition, and correct plucking to keep bushes vigorous and less pest-prone.
- When chemical control is necessary, use recommended active-ingredient classes and confirm locally registered products with a qualified agricultural extension officer; see `regulations/pesticide_safety_and_handling.md`.

## Disease Management
- Major diseases include blister blight (a leaf disease favoured by cool, wet, cloudy conditions, especially at high altitude), root-rot fungi (such as armillaria and charcoal root rot on old land), and various stem and branch cankers.
- Plant tolerant clones and ensure good drainage and airflow to reduce disease pressure.
- Remove and destroy diseased bushes and, when replanting old tea land, rehabilitate the soil and remove old root debris to limit root-rot carry-over.
- Maintain a healthy plucking table and balanced nutrition, as stressed bushes are more susceptible.
- Where protective fungicides are justified against blister blight in high-risk seasons, use recommended active-ingredient classes, confirm registered products locally, and observe safety intervals.

## Growth Stages
For this long-lived perennial, growth is described across establishment and bearing phases:
1. Nursery stage (0-12 months): cuttings root and develop into transplantable seedlings under shade.
2. Establishment (years 1-3): vegetative growth with formative pruning and framing to build a low, spreading frame and continuous canopy.
3. Onset of plucking (around years 2-4): the plucking table forms and light plucking begins, increasing yearly.
4. Full bearing (year 4 onward): continuous flushing allows regular plucking every 7-14 days depending on climate and growth rate; production can be sustained for decades.
5. Pruning cycle: bushes are pruned back periodically (typically every 3-4 years) to rejuvenate the frame, remove old wood, and keep the table at a manageable height, followed by a recovery period before plucking resumes.
6. Long-term productivity: with good management, tea remains productive for 50+ years, with periodic pruning cycles maintaining vigour.

## Harvest Indicators
- Pluck the tender shoot tips consisting of the bud and the top two young leaves ("two leaves and a bud"), which is the standard for quality.
- Shoots are ready when the terminal bud and two young leaves have expanded above the plucking table; plucking rounds recur every one to two weeks.
- Coarse plucking (taking older, harder leaves) lowers quality and grade, while fine plucking of only tender shoots maximises quality.
- Frequent, timely plucking keeps the table level, stimulates new shoots, and sustains yield and quality.

## Post-Harvest Handling
- Green leaf is highly perishable and must be handled gently and delivered quickly, ideally within a few hours of plucking, to a processing point.
- Avoid compressing, bruising, overheating, or wetting the leaf in the plucking basket or bag, as damage causes premature fermentation and quality loss.
- Keep leaf loose, shaded, and cool during collection and transport; use ventilated baskets rather than tightly packed sacks.
- At the factory, leaf is withered, rolled or cut, oxidised (fermented, for black tea) or steamed/pan-fired (for green tea), dried, and graded.
- For smallholders selling to a factory, timely, careful delivery of fresh, standard-plucked leaf is the main determinant of the price received; see `harvesting/harvesting_best_practices.md`.

## Storage
- Processed (made) tea is dried to low moisture, approximately 3-4%, and must be kept dry to preserve quality.
- Store made tea in clean, dry, airtight or well-sealed containers or foil-lined bags, away from moisture, strong odours, and direct light.
- Tea readily absorbs moisture and odours, so keep it away from spices, fuel, agrochemicals, and damp; contamination ruins flavour and grade.
- Green leaf cannot be stored and must be processed promptly; there is no smallholder storage stage for fresh leaf.
- Keep made tea in a cool, dry store on pallets off the floor, and rotate stock; see `storage/perishable_storage.md`.

## Expected Yield
- Yield is usually measured as made tea (processed) or green leaf (fresh), with roughly 4-4.5 kg of green leaf giving about 1 kg of made tea.
- Smallholder green-leaf yields commonly range from approximately 4,000-8,000 kg per hectare per year, equivalent to roughly 900-1,800 kg of made tea.
- Well-managed farms with good clones, nutrition, and plucking can exceed 10,000-15,000 kg of green leaf per hectare annually.
- Yield is spread across the year through continuous plucking rather than a single harvest.
- Actual yield depends on clone, altitude, rainfall, nutrition, plucking standard, and the pruning cycle stage.

## Common Mistakes
- Planting on poorly drained soils or soils that are not sufficiently acidic, leading to poor growth.
- Liming tea soils, which harms this acid-loving crop.
- Coarse or infrequent plucking, which lowers quality and lets the table grow unmanageably tall.
- Delaying delivery or bruising green leaf, causing quality loss before processing.
- Neglecting formative and cyclic pruning, resulting in tall, woody, low-yielding bushes.
- Applying nitrogen without soil/leaf analysis or in a single large dose, wasting fertiliser and risking imbalance.

## Best Farming Practices
- Establish good clones suited to your altitude and market on well-drained, acidic soils along the contour.
- Build and maintain a level plucking table through skilled formative and cyclic pruning.
- Pluck fine (two leaves and a bud) on regular rounds and deliver fresh, unbruised leaf promptly.
- Feed nitrogen and other nutrients in splits based on soil and leaf analysis, and mulch with prunings.
- Practise IPM and minimise pesticide use to meet residue limits and protect beneficial insects; see `sustainability/sustainable_farming_practices.md`.
- Protect steep land with terraces and grass strips, and conserve soil moisture with mulch.
- Keep records and consider group marketing, certification, and value addition to strengthen prices; see `pricing/price_negotiation_and_value_addition.md`.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: How often can I pluck my tea?**
A: In warm, high-rainfall conditions, mature tea can be plucked every 7-10 days; in cooler or drier periods, plucking rounds may stretch to 14 days or more. Regular, timely plucking of two leaves and a bud sustains both yield and quality.

**Q: Why has my tea stopped growing during the dry season?**
A: Tea goes dormant under moisture stress. Growth resumes once rains return, but prolonged drought delays recovery. Mulching and, where feasible, irrigation help maintain flush. Choosing drought-tolerant clones also helps in drier zones.

**Q: Can I grow tea from seed?**
A: While tea can be grown from seed, smallholders are strongly advised to use clonal (cutting-raised) plants from selected high-yielding, disease-tolerant clones. Clonal material gives uniform bushes and reliable yield and quality; seedlings are variable.

**Q: Why is my tea soil test recommending no lime when other crops need it?**
A: Tea is an acid-loving crop that thrives at a low pH of about 4.5-5.5. Adding lime raises pH and harms tea. This is opposite to the advice for many other crops, so follow tea-specific recommendations.

**Q: What determines the price I get for my green leaf?**
A: Freshness, plucking standard, and cleanliness. Fine-plucked (two leaves and a bud), unbruised, promptly delivered leaf earns the best price and grade. Coarse plucking, bruising, delay, and contamination reduce value. See `marketplace/product_grading_and_quality.md`.

**Q: When and why should I prune my tea?**
A: Prune on a cycle of roughly every 3-4 years to rejuvenate the frame, remove old woody growth, control table height, and stimulate vigorous new shoots. Allow the bush to recover after pruning before resuming full plucking.

**Q: How do I keep pesticide residues low enough for export markets?**
A: Rely on IPM, healthy vigorous bushes, and field hygiene to minimise pests. Use pesticides only when scouting justifies it, choose registered products, and strictly observe pre-harvest intervals and maximum residue limits. Consult your extension officer and factory guidelines.
