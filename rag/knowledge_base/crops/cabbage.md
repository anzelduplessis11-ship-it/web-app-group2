# Cabbage

## Overview
Cabbage is a popular leafy vegetable grown widely across cooler and mid-altitude regions of Africa for both fresh consumption and market sale. It forms a dense head of leaves that stores and transports comparatively well, and it has steady year-round demand in many urban markets. Cabbage is usually raised in a nursery and transplanted to the field. It is a relatively heavy feeder and is attractive to several insect pests, so good soil fertility, water management, and pest control are central to producing firm, marketable heads.

## Scientific Name
*Brassica oleracea* L. var. *capitata*.

## Suitable Climate
- Optimal growing temperature: 15-20°C; cabbage is a cool-season crop and heads best in mild conditions.
- Requires 350-500 mm of water over the growing cycle, through rainfall and/or irrigation.
- High temperatures (above roughly 25-30°C) cause loose heads, poor head formation, and bitterness.
- Grows well in cool highlands and during cooler seasons in lowland areas; tolerates light frost but not hard freezes.
- Consistent, moderate conditions with adequate moisture give the firmest heads.

## Suitable Soil
- Fertile, well-drained loam or sandy loam soils rich in organic matter are ideal.
- Optimal pH: 6.0-6.8; slightly limed soils help suppress clubroot in acidic conditions.
- Tolerates a range of soils but performs poorly in waterlogged ground, which causes root diseases.
- Responds strongly to fertile soils with good moisture-holding capacity.

## Soil Preparation
- Plough and harrow to a fine, level tilth suitable for small transplants.
- Incorporate well-decomposed manure or compost (10-20 tonnes/ha) before planting to build fertility and structure.
- Form raised beds or ridges in heavy soils or high-rainfall areas to improve drainage.
- Avoid fields recently cropped with other brassicas (cabbage, kale, cauliflower, rape) to reduce disease and pest carry-over.
- Conduct a soil test where possible to guide lime and fertiliser decisions; see `soil/soil_testing_and_fertility.md`.

## Planting Season by African Region
| Region | Typical Planting Window | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| West Africa | Cooler dry season (Oct-Feb) under irrigation; cooler highlands | Hot lowland seasons give poor heading |
| East Africa (highlands) | Long rains (Feb-Apr) and short rains (Sept-Nov); year-round at high altitude | Major cabbage-growing region; cool highlands allow continuous production |
| Southern Africa | Autumn to winter under irrigation; spring plantings | Time to avoid the hottest months for good heading |
| Central Africa (highlands) | Bimodal rains (Feb-Mar and Sept-Oct) | Cooler high-altitude sites favour firm heads |
| North Africa | Cool season under irrigation (autumn-winter) | Well suited to irrigated cool-season production |

## Seed or Seedling Requirements
- Cabbage is **raised from seed in a nursery and then transplanted** to the field.
- Use certified seed of a variety suited to the local season and market (early vs. main-season types; head size and storage traits).
- **Nursery raising:** Sow seed thinly in well-prepared, fertile nursery beds or trays; keep beds moist and lightly shaded until germination, then harden off gradually in full sun. Seedlings are usually ready to transplant at about 4-6 weeks, when they have 4-6 true leaves and a sturdy stem.
- Seed rate: approximately 250-400 g/ha of nursery seed to raise enough seedlings for one hectare.
- Harden seedlings by reducing watering and increasing sun exposure for several days before transplanting.
- Transplant in the cool of late afternoon or on a cloudy day and water in well to reduce transplant shock.

## Plant Spacing
- Standard spacing: approximately 45-60 cm between rows and 30-45 cm between plants, depending on variety and target head size.
- Closer spacing produces smaller, more uniform heads (useful for certain markets); wider spacing produces larger heads.
- Plant seedlings to the base of the first true leaves, firming the soil around the stem for good anchorage.

## Water Requirements
- Requires consistent, even soil moisture throughout growth for steady head development.
- Critical water-sensitive stages are establishment after transplanting and active head formation.
- Irregular watering during heading can cause heads to split, and moisture stress reduces head size and quality.
- Drip or furrow irrigation is preferred over overhead watering, which prolongs leaf wetness and encourages disease; see `irrigation/irrigation_methods.md`.

## Fertiliser Recommendations
- Basal application at transplanting: a compound fertiliser supplying phosphorus and nitrogen to support establishment.
- Topdress with nitrogen in two splits, at establishment and again as heads begin to form, since cabbage has a high nitrogen demand for leaf and head growth.
- Ensure adequate potassium for firm heads and adequate calcium to reduce internal disorders such as tip-burn.
- General guideline in the absence of a soil test: balanced nutrition with a strong nitrogen supply, split over the season, adjusted to soil fertility and yield target.
- Apply well-decomposed manure or compost to improve soil structure and nutrient supply.
- See `fertilisers/fertiliser_basics.md` and `fertilisers/organic_fertilisers.md` for application methods and timing principles.

## Weed Management
- Keep the crop weed-free, especially during the first several weeks after transplanting before the canopy closes.
- Weed by hand or with shallow cultivation to avoid damaging the shallow root system.
- Mulching between rows suppresses weeds and conserves moisture.
- Once large outer leaves shade the ground, weed competition is greatly reduced.
- See `weeds/general_weed_management.md` for integrated weed control principles.

## Pest Management
- Key pests: diamondback moth (the most important), cabbage aphids, cabbage webworm/loopers, and cutworms.
- Diamondback moth is prone to developing resistance to chemicals; rely on integrated pest management combining scouting, biological control, cultural practices, and rotating active-ingredient classes.
- Scout regularly, especially on the undersides of leaves and in the developing head.
- Practice field sanitation and rotation away from other brassicas to reduce pest carry-over.
- Where chemical control is needed, use only locally registered products and confirm active-ingredient choices with an extension officer.
- See `pests/aphids.md` for aphid management and `emergency_guides/pest_outbreak_emergency_guide.md` for outbreak situations.

## Disease Management
- Key diseases: black rot (bacterial), clubroot, downy mildew, and damping-off in the nursery.
- Use certified seed, rotate away from brassicas for at least 2-3 seasons, and ensure good drainage and airflow.
- Manage clubroot by liming acidic soils, improving drainage, and strict rotation; it is very persistent in the soil once established.
- Avoid overhead irrigation and overcrowding, which promote foliar and bacterial diseases.
- See `emergency_guides/crop_emergency_guide.md` for guidance on severe or unclear disease situations.

## Growth Stages
1. Nursery/seedling stage (0-4-6 weeks before transplanting).
2. Establishment after transplanting (1-2 weeks).
3. Vegetative rosette stage: production of large frame (wrapper) leaves.
4. Head formation: inner leaves fold and tighten into a head.
5. Maturity: head reaches full size and firmness, marking readiness for harvest.

## Harvest Indicators
- Heads are firm and solid when gently pressed by hand and have reached the size typical for the variety.
- Harvest before heads become over-mature, as delayed harvest leads to splitting and reduced quality.
- Cut the head at the base with a sharp knife, leaving a few wrapper leaves for protection during handling and transport.
- Total time from transplanting to harvest is typically 2.5-4 months depending on variety and temperature.
- Harvest in the cool part of the day to preserve freshness.

## Post-Harvest Handling
- Harvest in the cool early morning to reduce field heat and improve shelf life.
- Handle heads gently to avoid bruising and cracking, which accelerate spoilage.
- Trim off loose or damaged outer leaves, but leave a few tight wrapper leaves to protect the head.
- Sort by size and quality and remove damaged or diseased heads before packing.
- Use ventilated crates rather than compressing heads in sacks.

## Storage
- Fresh cabbage stores best in cool, humid conditions; it keeps for a few weeks under good cool storage and only a short time at ambient tropical temperatures.
- Keep heads out of direct sun and heat, which cause wilting and yellowing of outer leaves.
- Good airflow reduces rot, but very dry conditions cause moisture loss and limp heads.
- Firm, well-wrapped heads store better than loose or damaged ones, so sort carefully before storage.
- See `storage/perishable_storage.md` for general guidance on storing perishable produce.

## Expected Yield
- Smallholder systems with basic management: approximately 20-40 tonnes/ha.
- Improved management with quality seedlings, adequate fertiliser, and good pest control: approximately 40-70 tonnes/ha.
- High-input commercial systems: 70-90+ tonnes/ha.
- Actual yield depends heavily on variety, plant population, soil fertility, water management, and pest/disease control.

## Common Mistakes
- Growing cabbage in hot conditions or seasons, resulting in loose, poorly formed heads.
- Continuous brassica cropping without rotation, leading to clubroot and black rot build-up.
- Under-feeding nitrogen or watering irregularly, leading to small heads or head splitting.
- Delaying diamondback moth control or relying on a single chemical, allowing resistant populations to build up.
- Harvesting late, causing heads to split and lose market value.

## Best Farming Practices
- Grow cabbage in cool seasons or cooler highland sites and choose varieties suited to the season and market.
- Raise strong, uniform seedlings in a well-managed nursery and harden them off before transplanting.
- Maintain steady soil moisture and adequate, well-timed nitrogen for firm heads.
- Use integrated pest management for diamondback moth, rotating active-ingredient classes and using biological controls.
- Rotate away from brassicas for at least 2-3 seasons to reduce disease and pest build-up.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: My cabbages are growing lots of leaves but not forming tight heads. Why?**
A: Loose or absent heads are most often caused by high temperatures, which prevent proper heading, but can also result from excess nitrogen, too-wide spacing, or an unsuitable variety. Grow cabbage in cooler seasons or highland sites, use appropriate spacing, and choose a variety matched to your conditions.

**Q: My cabbage heads are cracking or splitting. What causes this?**
A: Splitting is usually caused by irregular watering (especially heavy watering or rain after a dry spell) or by leaving mature heads in the field too long. Maintain even soil moisture and harvest promptly once heads are firm.

**Q: The leaves of my cabbages are full of small holes and I see little caterpillars. What are they?**
A: These are most likely diamondback moth larvae or related caterpillars, which are major cabbage pests. Scout regularly, remove caterpillars by hand in small plots, use biological controls, and where chemical control is needed, rotate active-ingredient classes to avoid resistance and confirm registered products with an extension officer.

**Q: Should I sow cabbage seed straight into the field?**
A: Most growers raise cabbage seedlings in a nursery for about 4-6 weeks and then transplant, which gives more uniform crops, easier early pest and weed management, and better use of seed. Direct sowing is less common and generally gives less uniform stands.
