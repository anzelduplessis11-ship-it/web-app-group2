# Sweet Pepper

## Overview
Sweet pepper (bell pepper) is a high-value fresh vegetable grown across Africa for local markets, supermarkets, hotels, and export, valued for its mild, sweet flesh and its bright green, red, yellow, or orange fruit. It is a warm-season, frost-sensitive member of the Solanaceae family and a close relative of chilli pepper, tomato, and eggplant, so it shares many pests and diseases and belongs in the same rotation planning (see `crops/chilli_pepper.md`, `crops/tomato.md`, and `crops/eggplant.md`). Compared with chilli, sweet pepper is more demanding: it needs more water and nutrients, is more sensitive to heat and to sunscald on exposed fruit, and produces large, fleshy fruit that command a premium when well graded. Sweet pepper is almost always raised in a nursery and transplanted, benefits from staking or support, and rewards even moisture, balanced nutrition, and careful handling with attractive, marketable fruit. Because the fruit are perishable and highly visual, grading and gentle post-harvest handling strongly affect the price a farmer receives (see `marketplace/product_grading_and_quality.md`).

## Scientific Name
*Capsicum annuum* L. (Grossum Group). Sweet pepper is the mild, blocky-fruited group of the same species that includes many chilli types; the closely related hot peppers include *Capsicum frutescens* and *Capsicum chinense*. It is a Solanaceae relative of tomato (*Solanum lycopersicum*) and eggplant (*Solanum melongena*).

## Suitable Climate
- Optimal growing temperature: 20-27°C during the day and 16-20°C at night; growth and fruit set are poor below about 15°C and above about 30-32°C.
- Warm-season crop that is very sensitive to frost and to cold, chilly nights, which cause poor fruit set and misshapen fruit.
- Requires approximately 600-900 mm of water over the growing cycle, supplied by rainfall and/or irrigation; sweet pepper is less drought-tolerant than chilli and needs steady moisture.
- High temperatures and intense sun expose fruit to sunscald (pale, sunken, papery patches), so a healthy leaf canopy for shading is important.
- Prefers moderate humidity; very high humidity and prolonged leaf wetness increase fungal and bacterial diseases, while hot dry winds increase flower drop.

## Suitable Soil
- Deep, well-drained, fertile sandy loam to loam soils high in organic matter are ideal for the large nutrient and water demand.
- Optimal pH: 5.5-6.8; correct strongly acid soils with lime based on a soil test.
- Excellent drainage is essential because sweet pepper is very susceptible to root and vascular diseases, including bacterial wilt, in waterlogged soils.
- Avoid heavy, poorly drained clays; where soils are heavy, grow on raised beds or ridges.
- See `soil/soil_types_africa.md` and `soil/soil_testing_and_fertility.md` for matching the crop to local soils and testing.

## Soil Preparation
- Plough and harrow to a fine, level tilth to a depth of 25-30 cm to give the fleshy, moderately deep root system room to develop.
- Incorporate 15-25 tonnes/ha of well-decomposed manure or compost several weeks before transplanting, as sweet pepper responds strongly to organic matter.
- Form raised beds or ridges in heavy soils and high-rainfall areas to ensure drainage and reduce bacterial wilt risk.
- Remove and destroy residues of previous solanaceous crops (tomato, pepper, eggplant, potato) to reduce carry-over of shared pests and diseases.
- Avoid fields recently cropped to solanaceous plants or with a history of bacterial wilt; rotate with cereals or legumes.
- Conduct a soil test where possible to guide lime and fertiliser use; see `soil/soil_testing_and_fertility.md`.

## Seed or Seedling Requirements
- Sweet pepper is **raised from seed in a nursery and then transplanted**, giving uniform, vigorous plants and good early management.
- Use certified, disease-free seed of a variety suited to the target market (green versus coloured fruit, blocky bell versus elongated types) and to local disease pressure.
- Seed rate: approximately 200-400 g/ha for nursery raising, depending on variety and germination.
- **Nursery management:** Sow in clean, well-drained beds or trays with fresh or sterilised media to prevent damping-off; keep moist but not waterlogged, shade lightly until germination, then expose gradually to full sun.
- Seedlings are ready to transplant at about 5-7 weeks old with 4-6 true leaves and 10-15 cm height.
- Harden seedlings for about a week before transplanting by reducing watering and increasing sun exposure.
- Transplant in the cool of late afternoon or on an overcast day and water in well to reduce shock.

## Plant Spacing
- Standard spacing: 45-60 cm between rows and 30-45 cm between plants, giving good ground cover and canopy for fruit shading.
- Adequate spacing improves airflow to reduce disease while keeping enough leaf canopy to shade fruit against sunscald.
- Where plants are staked or trellised, slightly closer in-row spacing can be used with wider rows for access.
- Overcrowding raises humidity and disease pressure and makes harvesting and spraying difficult.

## Water Requirements
- Sweet pepper needs consistent, even soil moisture throughout the season and is more sensitive to water stress than chilli.
- The most water-sensitive stages are establishment, flowering, and fruit enlargement; stress at these stages causes flower and fruit drop, small fruit, and blossom-end rot.
- Avoid both drought and waterlogging; fluctuating moisture is a leading cause of blossom-end rot and cracked or misshapen fruit.
- Do not stop watering during fruiting, as the large fleshy fruit need steady moisture to size up.
- Drip or furrow irrigation is strongly preferred over overhead watering, which wets foliage and fruit and spreads disease; see `irrigation/irrigation_methods.md` and `irrigation/irrigation_scheduling.md`.

## Fertiliser Recommendations
- Basal application at transplanting: a compound fertiliser supplying phosphorus for root establishment, plus incorporated organic matter.
- Topdress with nitrogen and potassium in several splits through the season, increasing potassium during flowering and fruiting to support the large fruit load and fruit quality.
- General guideline in the absence of a soil test: approximately 120-180 kg N/ha, 60-100 kg P2O5/ha, and 120-180 kg K2O/ha, split over the season; sweet pepper is a heavier feeder than chilli.
- Ensure adequate calcium and, above all, even moisture to prevent blossom-end rot, which is primarily a calcium-transport problem driven by uneven watering.
- Excess nitrogen causes lush leaves, poor fruit set, and greater disease susceptibility, so keep nutrition balanced.
- See `fertilisers/fertiliser_basics.md`, `fertilisers/organic_fertilisers.md`, and `fertilisers/nutrient_deficiencies_guide.md`.

## Weed Management
- Keep the crop weed-free during the first 6-8 weeks after transplanting, when sweet pepper competes poorly with weeds.
- Weed by hand or with shallow hoeing to protect the shallow feeder roots.
- Mulching between rows suppresses weeds, conserves moisture (helping prevent blossom-end rot), and reduces soil splash that spreads soil-borne disease.
- Weeds host aphids, whiteflies, thrips, and viruses, so weed control is also disease and pest management.
- See `weeds/general_weed_management.md` for integrated weed control principles.

## Pest Management
Sweet pepper is attacked by the same insect and mite pests as chilli; use integrated pest management (IPM) combining resistant varieties, sanitation, scouting, and targeted control.
- **Aphids:** Suck sap and transmit viruses; found on shoot tips and leaf undersides. See `pests/aphids.md`.
- **Whitefly:** Sucks sap, causes sooty mould from honeydew, and transmits viruses; a major concern in warm areas. See `pests/whitefly.md`.
- **Thrips:** Cause silvery scarring and distortion of leaves and flowers, thrive in hot dry weather, and transmit viruses. See `pests/thrips.md`.
- **Fruit borers:** Caterpillars bore into fruit causing rot and unmarketable produce; hand-pick and destroy infested fruit and use pheromone traps where available.
- **Red spider mites:** Cause stippling, bronzing, and webbing in hot dry conditions on leaf undersides; maintain moisture and conserve predatory mites.
- **IPM practices:** Scout at least weekly, especially leaf undersides and shoot tips; rotate away from solanaceous crops; remove weeds and volunteers; conserve natural enemies; and use reflective mulches to deter whitefly and aphids.
- Where chemical control is necessary, use only locally registered products, refer to active-ingredient classes rather than brand names, rotate classes to slow resistance, observe pre-harvest intervals, and confirm the product and dose with a qualified agricultural extension officer. See `regulations/pesticide_safety_and_handling.md` and `emergency_guides/pest_outbreak_emergency_guide.md`.

## Disease Management
- **Bacterial wilt:** A destructive soil-borne disease causing sudden wilting of green plants in warm, wet, waterlogged soils. Manage with resistant varieties, good drainage, raised beds, rotation away from solanaceous crops for at least 3-4 seasons, and removal of infected plants. See `diseases/tomato_bacterial_wilt.md`.
- **Anthracnose and fruit rot:** Fungal disease causing sunken, dark, water-soaked lesions on fruit, worsened by wet humid weather; use clean seed, adequate spacing, timely harvest, and removal of infected fruit.
- **Powdery mildew:** White powdery growth on leaves causing leaf drop; improve airflow and manage with approved fungicides only if severe.
- **Viral diseases:** Viruses spread by aphids and whiteflies cause mosaic, leaf curl, stunting, and distorted fruit; there is no cure, so use virus-free seed, control the insect vectors, remove infected plants, and grow tolerant varieties.
- **Damping-off in the nursery:** A fungal complex that rots seedlings at the soil line; prevent with clean media, good drainage, and avoiding overwatering and dense sowing.
- **Blossom-end rot:** A dark, sunken, leathery patch at the blossom (bottom) end of the fruit caused by calcium failing to reach the fruit, usually due to uneven watering; sweet pepper is especially prone. Maintain even soil moisture, mulch, and avoid excess nitrogen.
- **Sunscald:** Not a disease but a physiological injury where exposed fruit develop pale, sunken, papery patches from direct sun; prevent by keeping a healthy leaf canopy, adequate spacing for shading, and controlling leaf-loss diseases and pests.
- Cross-reference late blight of solanaceous crops in `diseases/tomato_late_blight.md`, and use `emergency_guides/crop_emergency_guide.md` for severe or unclear outbreaks.
- For any chemical disease control, confirm locally registered, approved products and correct dosages with a qualified agricultural extension officer, and rely primarily on resistant varieties, sanitation, rotation, and drainage.

## Growth Stages
1. Nursery/seedling stage (0-5-7 weeks before transplanting).
2. Establishment after transplanting (1-3 weeks) as roots recover and growth resumes.
3. Vegetative growth (3-7 weeks after transplanting), building the frame of stems and leaves.
4. Flowering and fruit set, beginning roughly 6-9 weeks after transplanting and continuing over an extended period.
5. Fruit enlargement and colour change, with fruit first reaching full green size and then, if left on the plant, ripening to red, yellow, or orange; harvesting continues over many weeks.

## Harvest Indicators
- **Green (mature-green) fruit:** Harvest when fruit reach full size, are firm, blocky, and glossy but still green, for the large green-pepper market; this is the most common harvest stage and gives the highest total yield.
- **Coloured (ripe) fruit:** Leave fruit on the plant to change from green to red, yellow, or orange for the premium coloured market; this takes longer, reduces total number of fruit, but fetches higher prices.
- First harvest typically begins about 9-12 weeks after transplanting, depending on variety and climate.
- Cut fruit from the plant with scissors, secateurs, or a knife, leaving a short stalk, rather than pulling, which damages the brittle branches.
- Pick regularly (every 1-2 weeks); frequent picking of green fruit encourages continued flowering and higher total yield.

## Post-Harvest Handling
- Harvest in the cool of the morning and keep fruit shaded to reduce field heat and moisture loss.
- Handle gently, as the fleshy fruit bruise and puncture easily; use clean ventilated crates rather than tightly packed sacks.
- Sort and grade by size, shape, colour, and freedom from blemishes, sunscald, bruising, and disease; sweet pepper is a visual, premium product and grading strongly affects price.
- Remove any diseased, damaged, or misshapen fruit before packing, and keep coloured and green fruit separate for their different markets.
- See `harvesting/harvesting_best_practices.md`, `marketplace/product_grading_and_quality.md`, and `marketplace/buyer_preferences_and_seasonal_demand.md`.

## Storage
- Sweet pepper is perishable; store at about 7-10°C and 90-95% relative humidity for short-term holding of 1-3 weeks.
- Temperatures below about 7°C cause chilling injury (pitting, softening, and darkening), so do not store too cold.
- Coloured (ripe) fruit are more perishable than mature-green fruit and should be marketed sooner.
- Keep fruit out of direct sun and in a cool, shaded, ventilated place if refrigeration is unavailable, and market quickly.
- Avoid storing with ethylene-producing produce, which hastens ripening and softening.
- See `storage/perishable_storage.md` for general guidance on storing perishable produce.

## Expected Yield
- Smallholder open-field systems with basic management: approximately 8-15 tonnes/ha.
- Improved management with quality seedlings, balanced fertiliser, irrigation, staking, and good pest/disease control: approximately 20-35 tonnes/ha.
- High-input greenhouse or intensive systems: 40-80+ tonnes/ha.
- Actual yield depends strongly on variety, plant population, water and nutrient management, harvest stage (green versus coloured), and losses to pests, diseases, and sunscald.

## Common Mistakes
- Allowing soil moisture to fluctuate, causing widespread blossom-end rot and fruit drop.
- Planting on poorly drained soil or after solanaceous crops, leading to bacterial wilt losses.
- Removing too many leaves or letting pests and diseases strip the canopy, exposing fruit to sunscald.
- Applying excess nitrogen, producing leafy plants with poor fruit set.
- Overhead irrigation and overcrowding, which raise humidity and drive fungal and bacterial diseases.
- Rough harvesting and poor grading, which bruise the fleshy fruit and lower the price of a premium product.
- Delaying pest scouting so aphids, whiteflies, and thrips build up and transmit viruses.

## Best Farming Practices
- Choose varieties suited to the target market and local disease pressure, using certified, disease-free seed.
- Raise strong, uniform seedlings in a clean nursery and harden them before transplanting.
- Plant on well-drained raised beds and rotate away from solanaceous crops for at least 3-4 seasons.
- Maintain steady soil moisture with drip or furrow irrigation and mulch to prevent blossom-end rot and conserve water.
- Stake or support plants to carry the heavy fruit, keep the canopy healthy to shade fruit, and improve airflow.
- Scout weekly and apply IPM, using registered products only when justified and confirmed with an extension officer, then harvest gently and grade carefully.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Why do my sweet peppers develop a soft, sunken, dark patch at the bottom of the fruit?**
A: This is blossom-end rot, caused by calcium failing to reach the developing fruit, almost always due to uneven soil moisture rather than a soil calcium shortage. Sweet pepper is especially prone. Keep soil moisture consistent, mulch to buffer moisture, avoid excess nitrogen, and do not let plants swing between drought and heavy watering.

**Q: My fruit have pale, papery, sunken patches on the side facing the sun. What causes this?**
A: This is sunscald, a physiological injury from direct sun on exposed fruit, not a disease. It is worse when pests or diseases strip the leaf canopy. Prevent it by keeping a healthy canopy for shade, using adequate plant spacing, controlling leaf-damaging pests and diseases, and avoiding heavy leaf removal.

**Q: How is sweet pepper different from chilli to grow?**
A: Sweet pepper is a mild-fruited member of the same species but is more demanding: it needs more water and nutrients, is more sensitive to heat, cold nights, and sunscald, and produces large fleshy fruit that require careful handling and grading. Otherwise its nursery raising, transplanting, and pest and disease management are very similar to chilli. See `crops/chilli_pepper.md`.

**Q: Should I harvest peppers green or wait for them to turn colour?**
A: Green (mature-green) harvesting gives the highest total yield and a quicker, steadier income, while leaving fruit to colour up to red, yellow, or orange fetches a premium but takes longer and reduces the number of fruit per plant. Choose based on your market and prices; many farmers pick some green and leave some to colour.

**Q: My pepper flowers keep dropping without setting fruit. Why?**
A: Flower drop is usually caused by temperature extremes (nights below about 15°C or days above about 30-32°C), water stress, or excess nitrogen. Maintain even moisture and balanced nutrition, and where possible time the crop so flowering avoids the hottest or coldest periods.

**Q: Do sweet peppers need staking?**
A: Staking or trellising is strongly recommended because the plants are brittle and carry heavy fruit that can snap branches or bend plants to the ground, where fruit rot. Support keeps fruit clean, improves airflow, reduces disease, and makes harvesting easier.

**Q: My pepper plants wilted suddenly even though the soil was wet. What happened?**
A: Sudden wilting of green plants in warm, wet soils is often bacterial wilt, a soil-borne disease with no chemical cure. Remove and destroy affected plants, improve drainage with raised beds, avoid replanting solanaceous crops there for several seasons, and use tolerant varieties. See `diseases/tomato_bacterial_wilt.md`.

**Q: How do I get a good price for my sweet peppers?**
A: Sweet pepper is a visual, premium product, so gentle handling and careful grading matter. Harvest into ventilated crates, avoid bruising, sort by size, colour, and freedom from blemishes and sunscald, and keep green and coloured fruit separate. See `marketplace/product_grading_and_quality.md` and `marketplace/buyer_preferences_and_seasonal_demand.md`.
