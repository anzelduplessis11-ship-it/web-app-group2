# Chilli Pepper

## Overview
Chilli pepper (hot pepper) is a high-value spice and vegetable crop grown widely across Africa by smallholder farmers for fresh sale, drying, and processing into powder and paste. Because a small area can generate substantial income and dried chilli stores and transports well, it is an attractive crop for farmers who lack cold storage or reliable roads to market. Chilli belongs to the Solanaceae family and is a close relative of tomato, sweet pepper, eggplant, and potato, which means it shares many of the same pests and diseases and should be managed within the same rotation planning (see `crops/tomato.md`, `crops/sweet_pepper.md`, and `crops/eggplant.md`). It is a warm-season, frost-sensitive crop usually raised in a nursery and then transplanted. Pungency (heat), measured in Scoville Heat Units, varies enormously between types, from mild through the very hot bird's-eye and Scotch bonnet types, and heat is influenced by both variety and growing conditions such as water stress and high temperature. Chilli rewards careful management: consistent moisture, balanced nutrition, disciplined pest and disease control, and clean drying determine both yield and quality.

## Scientific Name
*Capsicum frutescens* L. and *Capsicum annuum* L. The very hot habanero and Scotch bonnet types belong to *Capsicum chinense* Jacq. These species are all members of the Solanaceae family and are closely related to tomato (*Solanum lycopersicum*), eggplant (*Solanum melongena*), and sweet pepper (*Capsicum annuum* Grossum Group).

## Suitable Climate
- Optimal growing temperature: 20-30°C during the day and 15-20°C at night; growth slows below 15°C and flowers and small fruit drop above roughly 32-35°C.
- Warm-season crop that is very sensitive to frost; even a light frost kills or severely damages plants.
- Requires approximately 600-900 mm of water over the growing cycle, supplied by rainfall and/or irrigation; even moisture gives better fruit set and quality.
- Prefers a warm, relatively dry ripening and drying period, which reduces fruit rots and makes sun-drying easier.
- High humidity and prolonged leaf wetness increase fungal and bacterial disease pressure, so hot humid lowland zones need extra disease vigilance.
- Water stress and hot conditions during fruiting tend to increase pungency, while very heavy rain and waterlogging cause flower drop, fruit rot, and root disease.

## Suitable Soil
- Deep, well-drained sandy loam to loam soils rich in organic matter are ideal.
- Optimal pH: 5.5-6.8; strongly acid soils reduce nutrient availability and increase aluminium toxicity risk, while very alkaline soils reduce micronutrient uptake.
- Good drainage is essential, as chilli is highly susceptible to root and vascular diseases such as bacterial wilt in waterlogged soils.
- Avoid heavy clay soils that hold water; if such soils must be used, plant on raised beds or ridges.
- See `soil/soil_types_africa.md` for matching crops to local soil conditions and `soil/soil_testing_and_fertility.md` for testing guidance.

## Soil Preparation
- Plough and harrow to a fine, level tilth to a depth of 20-30 cm so the small transplants establish easily and roots can penetrate.
- Incorporate 10-20 tonnes/ha of well-decomposed manure or compost several weeks before transplanting to build organic matter and improve moisture retention.
- Form raised beds or ridges in heavy soils or high-rainfall areas to prevent waterlogging and reduce bacterial wilt risk.
- Remove and destroy crop residues from previous solanaceous crops (tomato, pepper, eggplant, potato) to reduce carry-over of shared pests and diseases.
- Avoid fields with a recent history of solanaceous crops or a known history of bacterial wilt; rotate with cereals or legumes instead.
- Where possible, conduct a soil test to guide liming and fertiliser decisions; see `soil/soil_testing_and_fertility.md`.

## Seed or Seedling Requirements
- Chilli is almost always **raised from seed in a nursery and then transplanted**, which gives uniform, vigorous plants and better early weed and disease control.
- Use certified, disease-free seed of a variety suited to the local market (fresh, dried, or powder) and to local disease pressure; where bacterial wilt is common, choose tolerant varieties.
- Seed rate: approximately 200-400 g/ha for nursery raising, depending on variety and germination percentage.
- **Nursery management:** Sow in clean, well-drained nursery beds or trays using sterilised or fresh media to avoid damping-off. Keep beds moist but not waterlogged, provide light shade until germination, then gradually expose seedlings to full sun.
- Seedlings are usually ready to transplant at 5-7 weeks old, when they have 4-6 true leaves and are 10-15 cm tall.
- Harden seedlings for about a week before transplanting by reducing watering and increasing sun exposure so they tolerate field conditions.
- Transplant in the cool of late afternoon or on an overcast day, and water in well to reduce transplant shock.

## Plant Spacing
- Standard spacing: 45-75 cm between rows and 30-45 cm between plants, adjusted to variety vigour and whether plants will be staked.
- Small bird's-eye types can be spaced more closely; large-bushed varieties need wider spacing for airflow.
- Adequate spacing improves air circulation, which lowers humidity in the canopy and reduces fungal and bacterial disease.
- Overcrowding encourages disease, makes spraying and harvesting difficult, and reduces fruit size and quality.

## Water Requirements
- Chilli needs consistent, even soil moisture; both drought stress and waterlogging reduce yield and quality.
- The most water-sensitive stages are establishment after transplanting, flowering, and fruit development.
- Irregular watering during fruiting causes flower and fruit drop and contributes to blossom-end rot.
- Reduce irrigation slightly as fruit approaches ripening and drying to concentrate flavour and heat and to ease sun-drying, but do not let plants wilt severely.
- Drip or furrow irrigation is strongly preferred over overhead watering, which wets foliage and fruit and spreads fungal and bacterial diseases; see `irrigation/irrigation_methods.md` and `irrigation/irrigation_scheduling.md`.

## Fertiliser Recommendations
- Basal application at transplanting: a compound fertiliser supplying phosphorus to support root establishment, plus incorporated organic matter.
- Topdress with nitrogen and potassium in split applications through the season; increase potassium during flowering and fruiting to support fruit development and quality.
- General guideline in the absence of a soil test: approximately 80-120 kg N/ha, 40-80 kg P2O5/ha, and 80-120 kg K2O/ha, split over the season and adjusted to soil fertility.
- Excess nitrogen produces lush leafy growth with poor flowering and fruit set and increases disease susceptibility, so keep nitrogen balanced.
- Adequate calcium and even moisture help prevent blossom-end rot; calcium deficiency is usually a moisture and uptake problem rather than a soil shortage.
- Organic manure and compost improve soil structure and slow-release nutrition; see `fertilisers/fertiliser_basics.md`, `fertilisers/organic_fertilisers.md`, and `fertilisers/nutrient_deficiencies_guide.md`.

## Weed Management
- Keep the crop weed-free during the first 6-8 weeks after transplanting, when chilli competes poorly with weeds.
- Weed by hand or with shallow hoeing to avoid damaging the shallow feeder roots.
- Mulching between rows with organic material suppresses weeds, conserves moisture, and reduces soil splash that spreads soil-borne diseases onto lower leaves and fruit.
- Weeds also host aphids, whiteflies, thrips, and viruses, so weed control is part of pest and disease management.
- See `weeds/general_weed_management.md` for integrated weed control principles.

## Pest Management
Chilli is attacked by several insect and mite pests; use integrated pest management (IPM) that combines resistant varieties, field sanitation, scouting, and targeted control.
- **Aphids:** Suck sap and, more importantly, transmit viruses; they cluster on shoot tips and leaf undersides. See `pests/aphids.md`.
- **Whitefly:** Sucks sap, excretes honeydew that causes sooty mould, and transmits viral diseases; a major concern in warm areas. See `pests/whitefly.md`.
- **Thrips:** Rasp leaf and flower surfaces causing silvery scarring and distortion, thrive in hot dry weather, and transmit viruses. See `pests/thrips.md`.
- **Fruit borers:** Caterpillars bore into pods, causing rot and rendering fruit unmarketable; hand-pick and destroy infested fruit and use pheromone traps where available.
- **Red spider mites:** Tiny mites on leaf undersides cause stippling, bronzing, and fine webbing in hot dry conditions; maintaining plant moisture and encouraging natural predators helps.
- **IPM practices:** Scout fields at least weekly, especially leaf undersides and shoot tips; rotate away from solanaceous crops; remove weeds and volunteer plants; conserve natural enemies such as ladybird beetles and predatory mites; and use reflective mulches to deter whitefly and aphids.
- Where chemical control is necessary, use only locally registered products, refer to active-ingredient classes rather than brand names, rotate between classes to slow resistance, observe pre-harvest intervals, and confirm product choice and dosage with a qualified agricultural extension officer. See `regulations/pesticide_safety_and_handling.md` and `emergency_guides/pest_outbreak_emergency_guide.md`.

## Disease Management
- **Bacterial wilt:** A destructive soil-borne bacterial disease causing sudden wilting of green plants; favoured by warm, wet, waterlogged soils. Manage with resistant varieties, good drainage, raised beds, rotation away from solanaceous crops for at least 3-4 seasons, and removal of infected plants. See `diseases/tomato_bacterial_wilt.md`.
- **Anthracnose and fruit rot:** Fungal disease causing sunken, dark, water-soaked lesions on ripening fruit, worsened by wet, humid conditions; use clean seed, wider spacing for airflow, prompt harvesting of ripe fruit, and removal of infected fruit.
- **Powdery mildew:** White powdery growth mainly on leaf undersides causing leaf drop; favoured by warm conditions with moderate humidity. Improve airflow and manage with approved fungicides only if severe.
- **Viral diseases:** Several viruses spread by aphids and whiteflies cause mosaic, leaf curl, stunting, and distorted fruit; there is no cure, so focus on virus-free seed, controlling the insect vectors, removing infected plants, and using tolerant varieties.
- **Damping-off in the nursery:** A fungal complex that rots seedlings at the soil line; prevent with clean media, good drainage, avoiding overwatering, and not sowing too densely.
- **Blossom-end rot:** A dark, sunken, leathery patch at the fruit tip caused by calcium not reaching the fruit, usually due to uneven watering rather than soil calcium shortage; correct by maintaining even soil moisture and mulching.
- Cross-reference the closely related late blight, which can affect solanaceous crops, in `diseases/tomato_late_blight.md`, and use `emergency_guides/crop_emergency_guide.md` for severe or unclear outbreaks.
- For any chemical disease control, confirm locally registered, approved products and correct dosages with a qualified agricultural extension officer, and rely primarily on resistant varieties, sanitation, rotation, and drainage.

## Growth Stages
1. Nursery/seedling stage (0-5-7 weeks before transplanting).
2. Establishment after transplanting (1-3 weeks) as roots recover and new growth begins.
3. Vegetative growth (3-7 weeks after transplanting) as the plant builds its frame of stems and leaves.
4. Flowering and fruit set, beginning roughly 6-9 weeks after transplanting and continuing over an extended period.
5. Fruit development and ripening, with fruit changing from green to the mature colour (red, orange, or yellow depending on variety); harvesting continues over many weeks (an indeterminate, multiple-harvest crop).

## Harvest Indicators
- **Green (immature) chilli:** Harvest when fruit reach full size, are firm and glossy, but still green, for fresh markets that prefer green pods.
- **Ripe (mature) chilli:** Harvest when fruit have fully changed to their mature colour (usually red), which is required for drying and powder and gives the highest pungency and colour.
- First harvest typically begins about 8-12 weeks after transplanting, depending on variety and climate.
- Pick regularly (every 1-2 weeks) with a short stalk attached, using scissors or a careful twist, to encourage continued flowering and fruiting.
- The total harvest period can extend for several months in warm conditions, giving multiple pickings from the same plants.
- Wear gloves and avoid touching the eyes and face when handling hot varieties, as capsaicin causes burning irritation.

## Post-Harvest Handling
- Harvest in the cool of the morning and keep picked fruit shaded to reduce heat and moisture loss.
- Handle fruit gently to avoid bruising and breaking the skin, which invites rot; use ventilated crates rather than tightly packed sacks.
- Sort by ripeness, size, and quality, and remove any diseased, insect-damaged, split, or rotting fruit immediately, as one rotten pod spoils many.
- **For drying:** Sort ripe red fruit and dry promptly to prevent mould. Spread thinly on raised, clean drying racks, mats, or tarpaulins in full sun, turning regularly, and protect from rain, dew, dust, and animals. Solar dryers give cleaner, faster, better-quality product than bare ground.
- **Value addition:** Well-dried chilli can be sold whole or ground into powder or flakes, stored, and sold when prices are higher, adding value and reducing losses. Grinding, packaging, and grading dried chilli are common income-boosting steps for smallholders.
- See `harvesting/harvesting_best_practices.md`, `marketplace/product_grading_and_quality.md`, and `pricing/price_negotiation_and_value_addition.md`.

## Storage
- **Fresh chilli:** Highly perishable; store briefly at about 7-10°C and 90-95% relative humidity. Temperatures below about 7°C cause chilling injury (pitting and softening), so do not store too cold.
- **Dried chilli:** When dried to a low moisture content (fruit snap rather than bend), store in clean, dry, airtight or well-sealed containers in a cool, dark, dry place away from moisture and pests.
- Poorly dried or re-moistened chilli develops mould and can produce harmful toxins, so thorough drying and dry storage are essential for safety and quality.
- Inspect stored dried chilli regularly for signs of moisture, mould, or storage insects, and keep containers sealed against pests and humidity.
- See `storage/perishable_storage.md` for fresh produce and general dry-storage principles.

## Expected Yield
Yields are commonly quoted as fresh fruit; dried yield is roughly a quarter to a fifth of fresh weight.
- Smallholder systems with basic management: approximately 3-8 tonnes/ha of fresh chilli.
- Improved management with quality seedlings, balanced fertiliser, irrigation, and good pest/disease control: approximately 10-20 tonnes/ha fresh.
- High-input commercial systems: 20-30+ tonnes/ha fresh.
- Dried chilli yields therefore range from roughly 0.7-1.5 tonnes/ha under smallholder conditions to 3-6+ tonnes/ha under high-input systems.
- Actual yield depends strongly on variety, plant population, water and nutrient management, and losses to pests and diseases.

## Common Mistakes
- Planting on poorly drained soil or in fields with a history of bacterial wilt or recent solanaceous crops, leading to severe wilt losses.
- Using saved or uncertified seed that carries seed-borne diseases and viruses.
- Overhead irrigation and overcrowding, which raise canopy humidity and drive fungal and bacterial diseases.
- Applying too much nitrogen, producing leafy plants with poor fruit set.
- Irregular watering during fruiting, causing flower drop, fruit drop, and blossom-end rot.
- Delaying pest scouting so aphids, whiteflies, and thrips build up and spread viruses before control begins.
- Drying chilli on bare ground or too slowly, causing contamination, mould, and loss of colour and market value.

## Best Farming Practices
- Choose varieties suited to the target market and to local disease pressure, using certified, disease-free seed.
- Raise strong, uniform seedlings in a clean nursery and harden them before transplanting.
- Plant on raised beds with good drainage and rotate away from solanaceous crops for at least 3-4 seasons.
- Maintain even soil moisture with drip or furrow irrigation and mulch to conserve water and suppress weeds.
- Scout weekly for pests and diseases and apply IPM, using registered products only when justified and confirmed with an extension officer.
- Harvest regularly and dry promptly on clean raised racks or solar dryers to protect quality and enable value addition.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Why are the flowers and small fruits on my chilli plants dropping off?**
A: Flower and young-fruit drop is usually caused by heat stress (day temperatures above about 32-35°C), water stress or waterlogging, or excessive nitrogen that favours leaves over fruit. Maintain even soil moisture, provide balanced nutrition rather than too much nitrogen, and where possible plant so that flowering avoids the hottest, driest period.

**Q: My chillies are not very hot this season. What affects pungency?**
A: Pungency (Scoville heat) is set mainly by variety but is also increased by moderate water stress and high temperatures during fruiting and by allowing fruit to ripen fully. Very high soil fertility, heavy watering, and harvesting fruit while still green can all reduce perceived heat. Choose a hot variety and allow fruit to ripen for maximum pungency.

**Q: A dark, sunken, leathery patch is forming at the tip of my chilli fruit. What is it?**
A: This is blossom-end rot, caused by calcium failing to reach the developing fruit, almost always due to uneven soil moisture rather than a soil calcium shortage. Keep soil moisture consistent, mulch to buffer moisture, and avoid alternating drought and heavy watering.

**Q: How do I dry chilli properly for the powder market?**
A: Harvest fully ripe red fruit, sort out damaged pods, and dry promptly. Spread the fruit thinly on clean raised racks, mats, or in a solar dryer in full sun, turn regularly, and protect from rain, dew, and dust until the fruit are brittle and snap. Clean, fast drying preserves colour and prevents mould, which is essential for a safe, high-value product.

**Q: Should I plant chilli seed directly or raise seedlings first?**
A: Most farmers raise seedlings in a nursery for about 5-7 weeks and then transplant, because this gives uniform, vigorous plants, uses seed efficiently, and makes early weed and disease control easier. Direct seeding is possible but generally gives less uniform stands and is harder to manage.

**Q: My chilli plants suddenly wilted and died even though the soil was moist. Why?**
A: Sudden wilting of green plants in warm, wet soils is often bacterial wilt, a soil-borne disease with no chemical cure. Remove and destroy affected plants, improve drainage with raised beds, avoid replanting solanaceous crops in that field for several seasons, and use tolerant varieties. See `diseases/tomato_bacterial_wilt.md`.

**Q: How can I control aphids and whiteflies without heavy pesticide use?**
A: Use IPM: scout leaf undersides weekly, remove weeds that host these pests, conserve natural enemies such as ladybird beetles, use reflective mulch to deter them, and remove virus-infected plants. If spraying is needed, use only locally registered products, rotate active-ingredient classes, and confirm the product and dose with an extension officer. See `pests/aphids.md` and `pests/whitefly.md`.

**Q: Is chilli safe to grow near tomato, eggplant, and sweet pepper?**
A: These are all Solanaceae and share pests and diseases such as bacterial wilt, aphids, whiteflies, and viruses, so growing them together or in close rotation allows problems to build up. Separate them in the field where possible and rotate the whole family with cereals or legumes for several seasons. See `crops/tomato.md`, `crops/eggplant.md`, and `crops/sweet_pepper.md`.
