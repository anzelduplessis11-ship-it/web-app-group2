# Chickpea (Cicer arietinum)

## Overview

Chickpea is a cool-season grain legume (pulse) grown widely for its protein-rich seeds, which are consumed as whole dry grain, split dhal, flour (besan) and green immature pods. It is one of the most important food legumes for African smallholders because it fixes atmospheric nitrogen through a symbiosis with soil rhizobia, thrives on residual soil moisture after the rains, and provides a nutritious, storable, cash-generating crop during the dry season when few other crops grow.

Two market types dominate. **Desi** types are small, angular, coloured (brown, yellow, green or black) seeds with a thick, rough seed coat; they are hardy, drought-tolerant and preferred in East African and South Asian dhal markets. **Kabuli** types are large, round, cream-white seeds with a thin coat; they command higher prices in export and urban markets but need slightly better soils and cooler conditions. Choosing the right type for the target buyer and agro-ecology is one of the most important early decisions a farmer makes.

For African smallholders chickpea offers several advantages: it needs little or no nitrogen fertiliser, it grows on stored soil moisture (a "relay" or post-rains crop in the Ethiopian highlands, Sudan Gezira, and parts of Tanzania, Malawi and Kenya), it improves soil fertility for the following cereal, and its residues are valuable livestock fodder. It also carries risks that this document addresses in detail: sensitivity to waterlogging, vulnerability to the fungal disease ascochyta blight and the wilt/root-rot complex, and losses to pod borer and to storage weevils (bruchids).

This document should be read alongside `soil/soil_testing_and_fertility.md`, `soil/nitrogen_fixation_and_legumes.md`, `water/irrigation_scheduling.md`, and the pest and disease sibling notes referenced below.

## Scientific Name

- **Botanical name:** *Cicer arietinum* L.
- **Family:** Fabaceae (Leguminosae), subfamily Faboideae
- **Common names:** chickpea, gram, Bengal gram, garbanzo bean; *shimbra* (Amharic); *pois chiche* (French); *dengu/mbaazi* varies regionally (note: "mbaazi" often refers to pigeon pea — confirm local usage)
- **Chromosome number:** 2n = 2x = 16
- **Life cycle:** annual, self-pollinating

The genus name *Cicer* is Latin for the plant; the species epithet *arietinum* ("like a ram's head") refers to the shape of the seed. Chickpea is closely related to other cool-season food legumes such as lentil (*Lens culinaris*), field pea (*Pisum sativum*) and faba bean (*Vicia faba*); see `crops/lentil.md` and `crops/faba_bean.md` for comparison.

## Suitable Climate

Chickpea is a **cool-season, long-day-adapted** crop that performs best under mild temperatures and a dry, rain-free grain-filling period.

- **Optimum temperature:** roughly 18-29 degrees Celsius during vegetative and reproductive growth. Cool nights (15-20 degrees Celsius) favour flowering and pod set.
- **Temperature stress:** flowering and pod set fail below about 10-15 degrees Celsius (flower drop and pod abortion from cold) and above about 30-35 degrees Celsius (heat-induced flower and pod abortion). Frost during flowering is damaging.
- **Altitude:** in tropical East Africa chickpea is largely a **mid- to high-altitude** crop, typically 1,400-2,500 m in Ethiopia, Kenya and Tanzania, where temperatures are moderate. In subtropical zones (Sudan, southern Africa winter cropping) it is grown at lower altitude during the cool season.
- **Rainfall:** chickpea is drought-tolerant and is usually grown on **residual soil moisture** with 300-600 mm of seasonal moisture. It dislikes high humidity and rain during flowering and podding because moisture on the canopy triggers ascochyta blight and botrytis grey mould.
- **Daylength and photoperiod:** most types flower earlier under longer days; matching variety maturity to the local season length is essential.

Climate-smart point: because chickpea uses stored moisture and needs a dry finish, it is well suited to **rain-fed relay cropping** after a cereal or after the main rains, spreading a farm's production risk. See `climate/climate_smart_agriculture.md`.

## Suitable Soil

- **Texture:** deep, well-drained loams, sandy loams and clay loams are ideal. Vertisols (black cotton soils) are widely used in Ethiopia and Sudan when drainage is managed.
- **Drainage:** critical. Chickpea is **very intolerant of waterlogging**; even short periods of saturated soil kill roots and promote root-rot and wilt. Avoid low-lying, poorly drained fields.
- **pH:** optimum 6.0-8.0; tolerates mildly alkaline soils better than most legumes but is sensitive to strong acidity (below pH 5.5) and to iron-deficiency chlorosis on very calcareous soils.
- **Salinity:** low tolerance; avoid saline or sodic patches.
- **Fertility:** moderate fertility is adequate because nitrogen is fixed biologically. Phosphorus availability is the main soil constraint. Good soil organic matter improves moisture holding and nodulation.

Test soil before the season where possible; see `soil/soil_testing_and_fertility.md`. On strongly acid soils, correcting pH (for example with agricultural lime) over time widens the range of legumes that can be grown.

## Soil Preparation

Chickpea has a strong taproot and needs a deep, reasonably fine but firm seedbed that conserves moisture.

1. **Primary tillage:** if the field grew a cereal, incorporate or manage residues early. One deep ploughing after the previous crop opens the profile and lets rainfall recharge, which is essential for a residual-moisture crop.
2. **Secondary tillage:** one or two harrowings to break clods and produce a seedbed fine enough for even seed-to-soil contact but not powdery (a too-fine bed crusts and impedes emergence).
3. **Moisture conservation:** minimise the number of passes and the delay between final tillage and sowing so stored moisture is not lost. On sloping land, tie ridges or contour furrows reduce runoff and erosion.
4. **Drainage:** on vertisols and heavy clays, form broad raised beds and furrows (the traditional broad-bed-and-furrow system in Ethiopia) so excess water drains away from the root zone.
5. **Conservation tillage option:** chickpea establishes well under reduced or zero tillage into standing cereal stubble where equipment and weed control allow; this conserves moisture and protects soil structure. See `soil/conservation_tillage.md`.

Avoid working the soil when it is too wet (it smears and compacts) or too dry (it produces large clods).

## Planting Season by African Region

Planting is timed so that flowering and podding fall in cool, dry weather and the crop matures on residual moisture. Exact dates vary by locality, altitude, variety maturity and the onset of rains — confirm with your local extension officer.

| Region | Typical Planting Window | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Ethiopian highlands | August-September (end of the *meher*/main rains) | Sown into residual moisture as rains recede; broad-bed-and-furrow on vertisols; major producing zones (Shewa, Gojam, Tigray). |
| Sudan (Gezira, Northern State) | October-November (winter, cool dry season) | Irrigated or on Nile residual moisture; a true cool-season winter crop maturing before hot weather. |
| Kenya (Rift Valley, Eastern highlands) | September-November, after the long rains | Mid- to high-altitude; grown on residual moisture; desi types common. |
| Tanzania (Northern, Central highlands) | May-July, following the main rains | Cool-dry-season relay crop after cereals; both desi and kabuli. |
| Malawi / Zambia (mid-altitude) | March-May, at the end of the rains | Planted into moist soil on residual moisture (residual/"dimba" cropping). |
| Southern Africa winter zones (e.g. parts of South Africa, Zimbabwe) | April-June (winter) | Cool-season crop; supplementary irrigation where rainfall is inadequate. |
| Morocco / North Africa (Mediterranean) | November-February (winter) or early spring | Winter sowing gives higher yields where ascochyta-resistant varieties are used. |

## Seed or Seedling Requirements

Chickpea is **direct-seeded**; it is not transplanted.

- **Seed rate:** desi types roughly **60-100 kg/ha**; large-seeded kabuli types **100-150 kg/ha** or more because individual seeds are heavier. Higher rates are used for late sowing and to suppress weeds.
- **Target plant population:** approximately **25-45 plants per square metre**, adjusted for seed size and season.
- **Seed quality:** use certified or well-selected, disease-free seed with high germination (aim for above 85 percent) and no cracked seed coats (cracking reduces germination and lets pathogens in). Grade out shrivelled, insect-damaged and off-type seed.
- **Seed treatment:** protect seed against seed- and soil-borne fungi (damping-off, ascochyta, wilt) using a registered seed-dressing fungicide class — confirm the specific product, rate and pre-harvest interval with a qualified agricultural extension officer. Where bruchid-damaged seed is a concern, use clean seed.
- **Rhizobium inoculation:** where chickpea is new to a field or has not been grown for several years, inoculate seed with the correct chickpea-specific *Mesorhizobium ciceri* inoculant to ensure effective nodulation and nitrogen fixation. Inoculate in shade, keep treated seed cool and sow the same day. Do not mix inoculant directly with acidic fungicide slurries; if both are used, apply the fungicide first, let seed dry, then inoculate. See `soil/nitrogen_fixation_and_legumes.md`.

## Plant Spacing

- **Row spacing:** commonly **30-45 cm** between rows for desi types; **40-60 cm** for large kabuli types and where mechanised weeding is used.
- **In-row spacing:** about **10-15 cm** between plants.
- **Sowing depth:** **5-8 cm**, deeper (up to 10 cm) into drying soil to reach moisture; chickpea emerges from depth better than many legumes because the cotyledons stay below ground (hypogeal emergence).
- **Method:** drilling in rows gives even stands and easier weeding than broadcasting; if broadcasting, sow at the higher seed rate and cover well.

Wider rows aid air movement through the canopy, which lowers humidity and reduces ascochyta blight and grey mould pressure — a practical, no-cost disease-management step.

## Water Requirements

Chickpea is predominantly a **rain-fed, residual-moisture** crop and is one of the more drought-tolerant pulses, but water management still governs yield.

- **Seasonal water use:** roughly **300-450 mm** over the growing season.
- **Critical stages:** the most sensitive periods are **flowering and pod filling**. Moisture stress at flowering causes flower drop; stress during pod fill reduces seed size and number.
- **Irrigation (where available):** one or two supplementary irrigations at flowering and early pod fill markedly stabilise yields in drier zones. Use light, infrequent irrigation; avoid heavy or frequent watering.
- **Avoid excess water:** waterlogging and high humidity are more dangerous than mild drought. Never irrigate to saturation; ensure furrows drain freely. Late-season rain or irrigation delays maturity and promotes disease and lodging.

For scheduling principles and low-cost methods (furrow, deficit irrigation), see `water/irrigation_scheduling.md` and `water/water_conservation_techniques.md`.

## Fertiliser Recommendations

Because chickpea fixes most of its own nitrogen, fertiliser strategy centres on **phosphorus, a small starter nitrogen dose, and effective nodulation** rather than heavy nitrogen. Actual rates depend on a soil test — see `soil/soil_testing_and_fertility.md`. The ranges below are agronomic guidance only; local recommended blends and rates vary by locality, season and soil, so confirm with an extension officer.

- **Nitrogen (N):** a small **"starter" dose of about 15-25 kg N/ha** at sowing helps early growth before nodules become active. Do **not** apply high nitrogen — it suppresses nodulation and wastes money.
- **Phosphorus (P):** usually the key limiting nutrient; **about 20-40 kg P2O5/ha** applied at or before sowing (banded near the seed row) improves root growth, nodulation and yield. Many farmers meet both starter N and P by band-placing a compound phosphate fertiliser at planting.
- **Potassium (K):** apply only where a soil test shows deficiency.
- **Sulphur, zinc, boron, iron:** micronutrient deficiencies occur on some soils; zinc and boron respond in specific areas, and iron chlorosis appears on very calcareous soils. Correct only where symptoms or tests indicate.
- **Inoculant:** in low-fertility fields, correct *Mesorhizobium* inoculation is often more valuable than fertiliser nitrogen.
- **Organic matter:** well-rotted manure or compost (for example a few tonnes per hectare) applied to the field, ideally to the previous crop, improves phosphorus availability, moisture holding and soil life. See `soil/composting_and_manure.md`.

## Weed Management

Chickpea is a poor early competitor because it grows slowly at first and has an open canopy, so weeds cause heavy yield loss if uncontrolled during the first 30-45 days.

- **Critical period:** keep the crop weed-free for roughly the **first 4-6 weeks** after emergence.
- **Cultural methods:** a clean, stale seedbed (allow a flush of weeds to germinate, then destroy them before sowing), correct plant population, and row planting to allow inter-row cultivation.
- **Mechanical/manual:** one to two hand weedings or hoeings, the first around 20-30 days after emergence and the second before flowering. Inter-row cultivation with a hoe or animal-drawn implement is effective in row-planted crops. Avoid deep cultivation that damages the shallow feeder roots and nodules.
- **Mulching:** crop-residue mulch suppresses weeds and conserves moisture where residues are available.
- **Chemical control:** pre-emergence or early post-emergence herbicides from appropriate herbicide classes are used in larger plots; because chickpea is sensitive to some herbicides, always confirm the locally registered product, crop-safe rate and timing with a qualified agricultural extension officer. Follow all label safety and pre-harvest-interval instructions.
- **IPM point:** healthy, weed-free early growth also reduces humidity around plants and the alternate hosts of pests and diseases.

## Pest Management

Manage pests with an **integrated pest management (IPM)** approach: resistant/tolerant varieties, clean fields, monitoring/scouting, natural enemies, and chemical control only as a last resort and only with locally registered products. See `pests/integrated_pest_management.md`.

- **Pod borer (*Helicoverpa armigera*)** — the single most important field pest. Larvae bore into pods and eat developing seeds.
  - Monitor with pheromone traps and by scouting from flowering; act on economic thresholds (for example small larvae exceeding local threshold counts per metre of row).
  - Encourage natural enemies (parasitic wasps, predators), use biological options such as *Bacillus thuringiensis* (Bt) formulations or a nucleopolyhedrovirus (NPV) where available, and place bird perches.
  - If spraying is justified, use a registered insecticide class targeting young larvae; rotate modes of action to avoid resistance. Confirm product, rate and pre-harvest interval with an extension officer. See `pests/pod_borer_helicoverpa.md`.
- **Aphids** — suck sap and can transmit viruses. Favoured by dense, lush canopies. Conserve ladybird beetles and parasitoids; treat only above threshold. See `pests/aphids.md`.
- **Cutworms and termites** — attack seedlings and stems, especially in dry, sandy soils; good seedbed hygiene and destroying crop residues that harbour termites help.
- **Leaf miner and semiloopers** — usually minor; rarely need spraying.
- **Nematodes (root-knot, cyst)** — cause stunting and poor nodulation; manage with rotation and resistant varieties. See `pests/root_knot_nematodes.md`.
- **Storage pest (bruchid/pulse beetle, *Callosobruchus* spp.)** — the most damaging pest overall but managed at storage; see Storage below and `pests/storage_weevils_bruchids.md`.

Always wear protective clothing, keep children and livestock away during and after spraying, and observe pre-harvest intervals. See `safety/pesticide_safety_and_handling.md`.

## Disease Management

The main diseases are fungal and are strongly driven by moisture and humidity, so cultural control (drainage, spacing, rotation, clean seed, resistant varieties) is the foundation.

- **Ascochyta blight (*Ascochyta rabiei*)** — the most serious foliar disease in cool, wet, humid conditions. Causes brown lesions with dark margins on leaves, stems and pods, girdling and breaking stems. 
  - Manage with **resistant/tolerant varieties** (the single most effective step), disease-free seed, seed dressing, wider spacing for air movement, crop rotation of at least 3-4 years away from chickpea, deep burial or removal of infected residue, and avoiding overhead irrigation. Registered foliar fungicide classes (for example strobilurin or triazole fungicides) may be applied preventively in high-risk seasons — confirm product and timing with an extension officer.
- **Fusarium wilt (*Fusarium oxysporum* f. sp. *ciceris*)** — a soil-borne disease causing sudden wilting, yellowing and death, often in patches; internal stem discoloration is diagnostic.
  - Manage with resistant varieties, long rotations, well-drained soil, clean seed, and avoiding movement of infested soil. There is no effective cure once plants are infected. See `diseases/fusarium_wilt.md`.
- **Root rots and collar rot (*Rhizoctonia*, *Sclerotium*, *Pythium*)** — favoured by waterlogging and dense residue near the collar; manage with drainage, seed treatment, and avoiding excess moisture.
- **Botrytis grey mould (*Botrytis cinerea*)** — grey fuzzy growth on flowers and pods in dense, humid canopies during flowering; manage with wider rows, lower plant density and resistant varieties.
- **Dry root rot (*Macrophomina phaseolina*)** — appears under terminal drought and heat; managed by conserving moisture and avoiding heat stress at pod fill.
- **Viruses (e.g. stunt/luteoviruses)** — spread by aphids; manage aphids and use tolerant varieties.

Cross-cutting best practice: rotate away from all legumes for several seasons, never save seed from a diseased crop, and destroy or deep-bury infected debris. See `diseases/crop_rotation_for_disease_control.md`.

## Growth Stages

1. **Germination and emergence (0-10 days):** seed imbibes moisture; the shoot pushes up while cotyledons remain below ground. Even emergence depends on good seed-soil contact and depth.
2. **Vegetative growth (about 10-45 days):** branching and leaf development; root nodules form and nitrogen fixation begins about 2-3 weeks after emergence. Slow canopy growth means weeds must be controlled now.
3. **Flowering (about 45-70 days):** small white, pink or blue flowers appear; the crop is highly sensitive to heat, cold and moisture stress at this stage.
4. **Pod formation and filling (about 70-110 days):** pods set and seeds enlarge — the most yield-determining phase and the most vulnerable to pod borer and to moisture stress.
5. **Maturity (about 100-150 days depending on type and altitude):** leaves and pods yellow then dry; seeds harden and reach storage moisture. Desi types often mature earlier than large kabuli types.

Total crop duration is commonly **90-150 days**; short-duration varieties help escape terminal drought and heat.

## Harvest Indicators

- Plants and pods turn **yellow to straw-brown** and most leaves have dried or dropped.
- Pods are dry and rattle; **seeds are hard** and cannot be dented by a thumbnail.
- Seed moisture at harvest is roughly **15-20 percent**, dried down for threshing and storage.
- Harvest promptly once mature: over-dry crops shatter and drop pods, and standing crops are exposed to late rain, birds and pod borer.
- **Timing of day:** harvest in the cooler morning hours to reduce pod shattering.
- **Method:** smallholders usually hand-pull or cut whole plants at or just above ground level (pulling on light soils removes root nodules and nitrogen, so cutting is preferred where soil fertility carry-over matters), then dry and thresh.

## Post-Harvest Handling

1. **Curing/field drying:** stack or windrow cut plants to dry for a few days until pods are brittle. Protect from dew and rain.
2. **Threshing:** thresh by beating, treading, animal trampling or a mechanical thresher. Handle gently — cracked or split seeds lose value (especially kabuli) and store poorly.
3. **Winnowing and cleaning:** winnow to remove chaff, dust and immature seed; hand-pick or sieve out stones, soil, damaged and off-colour seed. Clean, uniform, undamaged grain of a single type fetches the best price.
4. **Drying to storage moisture:** sun-dry the clean grain to about **10-12 percent moisture** before storage. Dry on clean tarpaulins or mats, not bare soil, to avoid contamination and re-wetting.
5. **Grading:** grade by size and type; large, bright, uniform kabuli seed is a premium market class.

Good hygiene at this stage — clean stores, no mixing with old infested grain — is the key defence against storage beetles.

## Storage

- **Moisture is critical:** store only well-dried grain (about 10-12 percent moisture or lower). Damp grain moulds, heats and is destroyed by beetles.
- **Main storage pest:** bruchids/pulse beetles (*Callosobruchus* spp.). Infestation often begins in the field and continues in store, hollowing out seeds. See `pests/storage_weevils_bruchids.md`.
- **Low-cost protection:**
  - **Hermetic (airtight) storage** — sealed metal drums, or triple-layer hermetic grain bags — is highly effective and chemical-free: it starves insects of oxygen. Strongly recommended for smallholders.
  - **Botanicals and inert dusts** — clean ash, sand or food-grade diatomaceous earth, and neem-based treatments, reduce beetle damage in traditional stores.
  - **Sun heating** — solar drying/heat treatment of infested grain kills some stages.
- **Chemical protection:** where used, apply only storage-approved, registered grain protectants at label rates; never use field insecticides on stored food grain. Confirm approved products with an extension officer, and keep a clear pre-consumption interval.
- **Store structure:** cool, dry, well-ventilated, rodent-proof stores on raised platforms; clean and, if needed, treat the store before loading; never mix new grain with old infested stock.
- **Seed for next season:** store the best-graded, undamaged seed separately in airtight containers to preserve germination.

See `storage/grain_storage_best_practices.md`.

## Expected Yield

Yields vary widely with variety, moisture, disease pressure and management. As guidance (values vary by locality, season and variety):

- **Smallholder rain-fed, low input:** about **0.5-0.8 t/ha**.
- **Improved varieties with good agronomy (inoculation, phosphorus, timely weeding, one supplementary irrigation):** about **1.0-2.0 t/ha**.
- **Well-managed / research or irrigated conditions:** up to **2.5-3.0 t/ha** or more.

The largest, most reliable gains for smallholders come from: sowing on time into good moisture, using disease-resistant improved varieties, effective rhizobium inoculation, phosphorus at planting, controlling weeds early, and preventing pod borer and storage losses.

## Common Mistakes

- **Planting on poorly drained land or over-irrigating**, causing root rot, wilt and stand loss.
- **Applying high nitrogen fertiliser**, which suppresses nodulation and wastes money.
- **Skipping rhizobium inoculation** in fields new to chickpea, leaving the crop nitrogen-starved.
- **Late planting**, pushing flowering and pod fill into heat, drought or late rain.
- **Growing chickpea after chickpea (or other legumes) without rotation**, building up wilt and ascochyta.
- **Using susceptible varieties in ascochyta-prone wet highlands.**
- **Dense planting/narrow rows in humid areas**, raising humidity and disease.
- **Neglecting early weed control** during the slow-growing first six weeks.
- **Harvesting too late**, losing grain to shattering, birds and pod borer.
- **Storing under-dried grain or mixing with old infested stock**, leading to bruchid destruction.

## Best Farming Practices

- **Choose the right variety and type** for the market (desi vs kabuli) and agro-ecology, favouring improved, ascochyta- and wilt-resistant, appropriately-maturing varieties.
- **Rotate** chickpea with cereals (maize, wheat, sorghum, teff) and leave 3-4 years before returning to chickpea or another legume; capture the nitrogen and disease-break benefits for the following crop.
- **Inoculate** with chickpea-specific *Mesorhizobium* and apply phosphorus at planting; add only a small starter nitrogen dose.
- **Sow on time into stored moisture**, at correct depth, in rows, at the recommended population.
- **Keep the first six weeks weed-free** and cultivate shallowly.
- **Scout weekly from flowering** for pod borer and manage with IPM, spraying only above threshold with registered products.
- **Manage disease culturally first:** drainage, wider rows, clean seed, resistant varieties, residue removal.
- **Provide supplementary irrigation at flowering/pod fill** only where moisture is short, and never to waterlogging.
- **Harvest promptly, dry to safe moisture, and store hermetically.**
- **Keep records** of variety, dates, inputs, pests and yield to improve each season. See `general/farm_record_keeping.md`.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Do I need to add nitrogen fertiliser to chickpea?**
A: No large amount. Chickpea fixes most of its own nitrogen when well nodulated, so only a small starter dose of about 15-25 kg N/ha at sowing is helpful. High nitrogen actually reduces nodulation and wastes money. Focus instead on phosphorus and on inoculating the seed.

**Q: What is the difference between desi and kabuli chickpea, and which should I grow?**
A: Desi types are small, coloured, thick-coated, hardy and drought-tolerant, and suit dhal and local markets. Kabuli types are large, cream-white, thin-coated and fetch higher prices in urban and export markets but need better soils and cooler, less-humid conditions. Grow the type your buyer wants and that suits your climate.

**Q: Why did my chickpea plants suddenly wilt and die in patches?**
A: This is typically fusarium wilt, a soil-borne fungus, often made worse by waterlogging or by growing chickpea too frequently. There is no cure once infected. Prevent it with resistant varieties, well-drained soil, clean seed, and rotations of at least 3-4 years away from chickpea.

**Q: When is the best time to plant chickpea?**
A: Plant so that flowering and pod filling fall in cool, dry weather and the crop finishes on residual moisture — usually as the main rains recede or in the cool dry season. Exact windows differ by region and altitude (see the planting table); confirm local dates with your extension officer.

**Q: How can I stop insects destroying my chickpea in storage?**
A: Storage beetles (bruchids) are the main threat. Dry grain well (about 10-12 percent moisture), clean it, and store it airtight — sealed drums or triple-layer hermetic bags are very effective and chemical-free. Never mix new grain with old infested stock. Only use grain protectants that are registered for stored food, at label rates.

**Q: My leaves and pods have brown spots and stems are breaking — what is it?**
A: These are symptoms of ascochyta blight, which spreads in cool, wet, humid weather. Use resistant varieties, disease-free seed, wider row spacing for air movement, and rotation, and remove infected residue. Registered triazole or strobilurin fungicide classes can be applied preventively in high-risk seasons — confirm the approved product and timing with an extension officer.

**Q: Does chickpea need irrigation?**
A: Usually it is grown rain-fed on stored soil moisture and is fairly drought-tolerant. Where the season is dry, one or two light supplementary irrigations at flowering and early pod fill stabilise yield. Never irrigate to waterlogging — excess water is more harmful than mild drought.

**Q: What crop should follow chickpea?**
A: A cereal such as maize, wheat, sorghum or teff benefits from the nitrogen chickpea leaves behind and helps break chickpea diseases. Avoid following chickpea with another legume, and avoid returning to chickpea for several seasons. See `soil/nitrogen_fixation_and_legumes.md`.
