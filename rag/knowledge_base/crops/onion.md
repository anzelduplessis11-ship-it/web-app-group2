# Onion

## Overview
Onion is one of the most widely grown and traded vegetable crops in Africa, valued as an everyday cooking ingredient with strong and steady market demand. It is a high-value crop that stores comparatively well, allowing farmers to sell over an extended period. Onion is usually raised in a nursery and then transplanted to the field, and it is sensitive to day length, spacing, and water management, so bulb size and quality depend heavily on good management and the right variety for the location.

## Scientific Name
*Allium cepa* L.

## Suitable Climate
- Optimal growing temperature: 15-25°C during bulb development; extreme heat can cause small bulbs and premature bolting.
- Requires 350-550 mm of water over the growing cycle, through rainfall and/or irrigation.
- Day length strongly influences bulbing: short-day and intermediate-day varieties are generally suited to tropical and subtropical Africa, while long-day types are unsuitable near the equator.
- Grows across a range of altitudes; cool, dry conditions during bulbing and curing improve quality and storability.
- Wet, humid conditions late in the season increase disease and reduce storage life.

## Suitable Soil
- Well-drained, fertile sandy loam or loam soils rich in organic matter are ideal.
- Optimal pH: 6.0-7.0.
- Avoid heavy, waterlogged soils, which cause bulb rots and poor development.
- Onion has shallow roots, so a friable, fertile topsoil and consistent moisture are important.

## Soil Preparation
- Plough and harrow to a fine tilth, as onion seed and seedlings are small and need a firm, level seedbed.
- Incorporate well-decomposed manure or compost before planting; avoid fresh manure, which encourages excessive leaf growth and forked bulbs.
- Form raised beds in areas with heavy soil or high rainfall to improve drainage.
- Ensure the field is free of clods and stones so bulbs can develop evenly.
- Conduct a soil test where possible to guide fertiliser decisions; see `soil/soil_testing_and_fertility.md`.

## Planting Season by African Region
| Region | Typical Planting Window | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| West Africa | Dry season (Oct-Feb) under irrigation | Cool, dry season favours bulbing and curing; short-day varieties suited |
| East Africa | Two seasons aligned with rains (Feb-Apr and Sept-Nov) | Requires dry conditions for curing; irrigate in dry spells |
| Southern Africa | Cool-season planting (autumn to winter) under irrigation | Choose varieties matched to local day length and temperature |
| Central Africa | Drier windows in bimodal zones, under irrigation | High humidity increases disease risk; ensure good drainage |
| North Africa | Cool season under irrigation (autumn-winter into spring) | Well suited to irrigated production in cooler months |

## Seed or Seedling Requirements
- Onion is usually **raised from seed in a nursery and then transplanted**, though direct seeding and sets (small immature bulbs) are also used.
- Use certified seed of a variety suited to local day length (short-day or intermediate-day near the equator) and market preference.
- **Nursery raising:** Sow seed thinly in well-prepared, fertile nursery beds or trays; keep beds moist and lightly shaded until germination, then gradually expose seedlings to full sun. Seedlings are usually ready to transplant at about 6-8 weeks, when they are pencil-thick at the base with several leaves.
- Seed rate: approximately 3-4 kg/ha of nursery seed to raise enough seedlings for one hectare.
- Harden seedlings by reducing watering for a few days before transplanting, and trim very long leaves and roots slightly to reduce transplant shock.
- Transplant in the cool of late afternoon or on an overcast day, then water in well.

## Plant Spacing
- Standard spacing: approximately 30-40 cm between rows and 8-10 cm between plants, giving a high plant population suited to bulb onions.
- Closer spacing tends to produce smaller bulbs (useful where small bulbs are preferred); wider spacing produces larger bulbs.
- Plant seedlings shallowly, covering only the roots and base; planting too deep delays bulbing and can cause neck rot.

## Water Requirements
- Requires consistent, even soil moisture, especially during bulb development.
- Critical water-sensitive stages are establishment after transplanting and active bulb enlargement.
- Reduce or stop irrigation as the crop approaches maturity and tops begin to fall over, to aid curing and reduce bulb rots.
- Drip or furrow irrigation is preferred over overhead watering, which wets foliage and encourages leaf diseases; see `irrigation/irrigation_methods.md`.

## Fertiliser Recommendations
- Basal application at transplanting: a compound fertiliser supplying phosphorus and some nitrogen to support root and early leaf growth.
- Topdress with nitrogen in one or two splits during early growth and leaf development, but stop nitrogen once bulbing begins, as late nitrogen delays maturity and reduces storage life.
- Ensure adequate potassium and phosphorus for good bulb development and quality.
- General guideline in the absence of a soil test: moderate, balanced nutrition with emphasis on phosphorus at planting and controlled nitrogen early on, adjusted to soil fertility.
- Apply well-decomposed organic matter for soil structure; avoid fresh manure.
- See `fertilisers/fertiliser_basics.md` for application methods and timing principles.

## Weed Management
- Keep the crop weed-free, as onions compete poorly with weeds due to their sparse, upright foliage and shallow roots.
- Weed by hand or with shallow cultivation to avoid damaging shallow roots and bulbs.
- Mulching between rows can suppress weeds and conserve moisture.
- The critical period for weed control is the first several weeks after transplanting, before the crop establishes.
- See `weeds/general_weed_management.md` for integrated weed control principles.

## Pest Management
- Key pests: onion thrips (the most important), cutworms, and onion maggots/flies.
- Thrips cause silvery streaking and distorted leaves and thrive in hot, dry conditions; scout regularly, especially in dry weather.
- Practice field sanitation and rotation away from other *Allium* crops (onion, garlic, leek) to reduce pest carry-over.
- Where chemical control is needed, use only locally registered products, rotate active-ingredient classes to manage resistance, and confirm choices with an extension officer.
- See `pests/aphids.md` for related sucking-pest management principles and `emergency_guides/pest_outbreak_emergency_guide.md` for outbreak situations.

## Disease Management
- Key diseases: purple blotch, downy mildew, neck rot, and various bulb rots.
- Use certified seed, rotate away from *Allium* crops for at least 2-3 seasons, and ensure good drainage and airflow.
- Avoid overhead irrigation and dense planting, which prolong leaf wetness and promote fungal diseases.
- Proper curing and careful handling at harvest greatly reduce neck rot and storage rots.
- See `emergency_guides/crop_emergency_guide.md` for guidance on severe or unclear disease situations.

## Growth Stages
1. Nursery/seedling stage (0-6-8 weeks before transplanting).
2. Establishment after transplanting (1-3 weeks).
3. Vegetative leaf growth (3-8 weeks after transplanting).
4. Bulb initiation and bulbing, triggered by day length and temperature.
5. Maturation: tops soften and fall over ("neck fall"), and bulb skins dry, marking readiness for harvest.

## Harvest Indicators
- Tops (leaves) begin to yellow, soften at the neck, and fall over; when roughly half to three-quarters of the tops have fallen, the crop is nearing harvest.
- Bulbs are firm and the outer skins have begun to dry and colour up.
- Stop watering as tops fall to help skins cure and bulbs firm up.
- Harvest in dry weather by lifting bulbs carefully; total time from transplanting to harvest is typically 3-5 months depending on variety.
- Avoid leaving mature bulbs in wet soil, as this promotes rots and reduces storage life.

## Post-Harvest Handling
- Lift bulbs carefully to avoid bruising, and leave them to cure.
- **Curing:** Dry the bulbs with tops attached in a shaded, well-ventilated place (or windrowed in the field in dry climates) for one to three weeks until the necks are tight and the outer skins are papery.
- After curing, trim the tops to a short neck and trim roots, handling bulbs gently.
- Sort out thick-necked ("bull-neck"), bruised, split, or diseased bulbs, which do not store well and should be used first.
- Curing is the single most important step for good onion storage.

## Storage
- Store well-cured, sound bulbs in a cool, dry, well-ventilated place; good airflow is essential to prevent rots.
- Use nets, slatted crates, or well-ventilated stores rather than closed sacks that trap moisture.
- High humidity and poor ventilation cause sprouting, root regrowth, and rots.
- Storage life varies greatly by variety; some short-day types store for only a few weeks to a couple of months, while better-storing types last several months.
- Inspect stored bulbs regularly and remove any that are sprouting or rotting.
- See `storage/perishable_storage.md` for general guidance on storing produce.

## Expected Yield
- Smallholder systems with basic management: approximately 10-20 tonnes/ha.
- Improved management with quality seedlings, adequate fertiliser, and good irrigation: approximately 25-40 tonnes/ha.
- High-input commercial systems: 40-50+ tonnes/ha.
- Actual yield depends heavily on variety and day-length match, plant population, water management, and pest/disease control.

## Common Mistakes
- Using a variety with the wrong day-length requirement (e.g., long-day types near the equator), resulting in poor or no bulbing.
- Applying nitrogen or irrigation late into bulbing, delaying maturity and reducing storage life.
- Transplanting seedlings too deep, delaying bulbing and encouraging neck rot.
- Skipping or rushing curing, leading to heavy storage losses from rots and sprouting.
- Storing bulbs in closed sacks or humid, poorly ventilated conditions.

## Best Farming Practices
- Choose a variety matched to your local day length and market, and raise strong, uniform seedlings in a well-managed nursery.
- Maintain even soil moisture during bulbing, then stop watering as tops fall over.
- Control nitrogen timing, stopping applications once bulbing begins.
- Cure bulbs thoroughly after harvest before storage.
- Rotate away from other *Allium* crops for at least 2-3 seasons to reduce pest and disease build-up.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: My onions grew lots of leaves but the bulbs stayed very small. What went wrong?**
A: Small bulbs are often caused by using a variety with the wrong day-length requirement for your location, by too-close spacing, or by excess nitrogen and watering late in the season that keeps the plant producing leaves instead of bulbing. Choose a short-day or intermediate-day variety near the equator, control nitrogen timing, and use appropriate spacing.

**Q: Some of my onions produced a flower stalk instead of a good bulb. Why?**
A: This is bolting (premature flowering), often triggered by cold spells, stress, or transplanting seedlings that are too large or too old. Use appropriately sized seedlings, transplant at the right stage, and avoid unnecessary stress; bolted plants generally produce poor-quality bulbs that should be used first.

**Q: Why do my stored onions rot or sprout after only a few weeks?**
A: Poor storage life usually comes from inadequate curing, late nitrogen or watering, thick-necked bulbs, or storing in humid, poorly ventilated conditions. Cure bulbs thoroughly until the necks are tight, sort out thick-necked and damaged bulbs, and store in a cool, dry, well-ventilated space.

**Q: Should I plant onion seed directly in the field or raise seedlings first?**
A: Both are possible, but most smallholders raise seedlings in a nursery for about 6-8 weeks and then transplant, which gives more uniform, easier-to-manage crops and better weed control early on. Direct seeding and planting sets are alternatives depending on your resources and local practice.
