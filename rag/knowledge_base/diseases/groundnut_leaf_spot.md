# Groundnut Leaf Spot

## Disease Name
Groundnut Leaf Spot (Early and Late Leaf Spot)

## Scientific Name
Groundnut leaf spot disease is caused by two related but distinct fungi, which often occur together in the same field and sometimes on the same plant:
- **Early leaf spot:** *Passalora arachidicola* (previously classified as *Cercospora arachidicola*; sexual stage *Mycosphaerella arachidis*).
- **Late leaf spot:** *Nothopassalora personata* (previously classified as *Cercosporidium personatum* or *Phaeoisariopsis personata*; sexual stage *Mycosphaerella berkeleyi*).

## Crops Affected
- Groundnut, also called peanut (*Arachis hypogaea*), is the primary and effectively only significant host crop of economic concern (see `crops/groundnut.md`).
- Wild *Arachis* species can host the fungi to a lesser degree but are not of practical concern to farmers.
- Both fungi can occur together within the same crop and even the same field season, with late leaf spot often becoming the dominant problem as the season progresses.

## Cause
Groundnut leaf spot disease results from infection of groundnut foliage by one or both of the causal fungi. Early leaf spot tends to appear first, usually from around three to six weeks after crop emergence, while late leaf spot typically develops somewhat later in the season and often becomes the more damaging of the two as the crop approaches maturity. Both fungi survive between cropping seasons mainly in infected crop debris left on or near the soil surface.

## Symptoms
Symptoms differ enough between the two diseases to allow field identification, though both produce circular to sub-circular leaf lesions.

| Feature | Early Leaf Spot | Late Leaf Spot |
|---|---|---|
| Lesion colour | Reddish-brown to dark brown | Dark brown to near-black |
| Yellow halo | Usually present, often prominent | Usually absent or faint |
| Leaf surface most affected | Upper surface mainly | Lower surface mainly |
| Lesion shape | Circular, fairly uniform | Circular to irregular |
| Sporulation | Sparse, greyish, mainly on the upper surface | Dense, dark, velvety mass visible on the lower surface in humid weather |
| Typical timing in season | Earlier | Later |

- Lesions also occur on petioles, stems, and pegs (the stalks that carry developing pods down into the soil), appearing as elongated dark spots.
- As lesions mature and multiply, affected leaflets turn yellow and drop prematurely; severe infection causes extensive defoliation that starts with the lower, older leaves and progresses upward through the canopy.
- Severe defoliation before pod fill reduces the photosynthetic area available to fill developing pods, weakens pegs, and can increase pod loss during harvest (lifting), since defoliated plants shed pods more easily from the soil.
- Mixed infections with both fungi on the same plant are common in many groundnut-growing areas, and overall symptom severity generally increases through the season if favourable conditions persist.

## Conditions Favouring Infection
- Warm temperatures, generally in the range of 20 to 30 degrees Celsius, with late leaf spot tending to tolerate slightly warmer and more humid conditions than early leaf spot.
- High relative humidity and extended periods of leaf wetness from rain, heavy dew, or overhead irrigation, both of which are required for spore germination and successful infection.
- Frequent rainfall through the mid- to late-season period, when the crop canopy is dense and retains moisture for longer.
- Dense canopies and close plant spacing that restrict airflow and prolong leaf wetness within the crop.
- Continuous or frequent groundnut cropping in the same fields, which allows carryover of infected debris and a build-up of inoculum across seasons.
- Susceptible varieties grown without a fungicide programme in high-rainfall zones.

## How It Spreads
- Both fungi produce airborne conidia (asexual spores) that are dispersed by wind and rain splash to healthy leaves within and between fields.
- Rain splash is particularly important for moving spores from lower, older, infected leaves upward and outward within the canopy over the course of a season.
- The fungi survive between cropping seasons primarily in infected crop debris left on or near the soil surface, which is the main source of inoculum for the following crop.
- Volunteer groundnut plants emerging from pods left in the soil from a previous season can also carry the fungi forward into a new planting.
- Seed transmission is considered minor compared with debris-borne and airborne spread, although heavily infected pods and shells can occasionally carry some inoculum.

## Risk Level
- High in humid, high-rainfall groundnut-growing regions, particularly where susceptible varieties are grown without fungicide protection or crop rotation.
- Considered one of the most economically important foliar diseases of groundnut across Africa, capable of causing severe, progressive defoliation.
- Yield losses can exceed 50 percent in severe, unmanaged epidemics, resulting from a combination of reduced pod fill, weakened pegs, and increased pod loss at harvest.
- Risk is lower in drier growing seasons and regions, and on well-managed crops using resistant or tolerant varieties combined with timely fungicide use.

## Treatment Options
- Protectant fungicide programmes are the main chemical control tool and are most effective when applied preventively, before disease becomes established, and continued at appropriate intervals through the susceptible growth period.
- Effective active-ingredient classes commonly used against groundnut leaf spots include chlorothalonil (a multi-site protectant), triazole (DMI) fungicides, and strobilurin (QoI) fungicides, often used in rotation or as mixtures to help manage the risk of fungicide resistance.
- Spray timing is frequently guided by a fixed calendar schedule, for example starting around six weeks after emergence and continuing at roughly two-week intervals, or by a weather-based advisory or threshold system where one is available locally.
- Removing and destroying infected crop debris after harvest reduces carryover of inoculum to the following season, although it does not treat a standing, already-infected crop.
- Confirm currently registered fungicide active ingredients, application rates, spray intervals, and pre-harvest intervals for groundnut in your country with a national agricultural regulatory authority or extension officer before purchasing or applying any product.

## Prevention
- Grow resistant or tolerant groundnut varieties where available and suited to local conditions; variety choice is one of the most cost-effective long-term controls for this disease.
- Rotate groundnut with non-legume crops for at least two to three seasons to allow infected debris to break down and reduce inoculum carryover.
- Remove and destroy or bury crop debris after harvest rather than leaving it on the soil surface.
- Plant at the recommended spacing to avoid overly dense canopies that trap humidity and prolong leaf wetness.
- Avoid overhead irrigation where possible, or irrigate in a way that allows foliage to dry quickly, such as early-morning irrigation.
- Plant on time according to local planting calendars, since well-timed, vigorous crops are generally better able to tolerate leaf spot pressure.
- Remove volunteer groundnut plants that could carry the disease forward into a new season.

## Monitoring Recommendations
- Begin scouting from around four to six weeks after crop emergence, checking both the upper and lower surfaces of lower, older leaves first, since these are usually infected before the rest of the canopy.
- Walk a zig-zag pattern through the field and assess a representative number of plants at multiple points, not only at the edges.
- Estimate the percentage of leaf area affected and the level of defoliation on lower leaves to help decide whether and when to begin or continue a fungicide programme.
- Distinguish early from late leaf spot using lesion colour, presence or absence of a yellow halo, and which leaf surface is most affected, since the balance between the two diseases can shift as the season progresses.
- Monitor weather conditions, particularly extended wet or humid periods, which signal a higher infection risk and the need for closer scouting or protective spraying.
- Keep season records of which fields and varieties showed heavy leaf spot pressure to guide future variety choice and rotation planning.

## When to Consult an Agricultural Extension Officer or Plant Health Specialist
- When leaf spot symptoms are first observed, to confirm whether early leaf spot, late leaf spot, or both are present, and to plan an appropriate response.
- Before starting or adjusting a fungicide programme, to confirm locally registered active ingredients, correct rates, spray intervals, and pre-harvest intervals.
- Before selecting a groundnut variety for planting, to confirm which varieties offer useful resistance or tolerance to leaf spot in the local area.
- If defoliation is severe and widespread despite management, to reassess the control programme and rule out other contributing stresses.
- If uncertain whether symptoms are due to leaf spot, a nutrient deficiency, or another leaf disease, since some symptoms can appear similar without close inspection.
