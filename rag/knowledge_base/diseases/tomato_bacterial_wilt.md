# Tomato Bacterial Wilt

## Disease Name
Bacterial Wilt

## Scientific Name
Caused by the soil-borne bacterium *Ralstonia solanacearum* (a species complex with several strains that differ in host range and aggressiveness).

## Crops Affected
- Tomato (*Solanum lycopersicum*) is a major host (see `crops/tomato.md`).
- Also affects other members of the nightshade family, including potato, eggplant (aubergine), and pepper, as well as some other crops depending on the bacterial strain present.

## Cause
Infection by the soil-borne bacterium *Ralstonia solanacearum*, which enters plants through the roots (often via wounds) and multiplies in the water-conducting tissue, blocking water movement and causing the plant to wilt.

## Symptoms
- Sudden wilting of the plant, often starting with the younger leaves or one side of the plant, while the leaves may still appear green (rather than yellowing first).
- Wilting is typically most noticeable during the heat of the day and may partially recover overnight in early stages before becoming permanent.
- Rapid, complete collapse of the plant as the disease progresses.
- Brown discolouration of the internal stem tissue when a lower stem is cut across.
- A simple field indicator: when a cut piece of infected stem is suspended in clear water, a milky, thread-like ooze of bacteria may stream out from the cut end. Symptom expression can vary with temperature, strain, and plant age.

## Conditions Favouring Infection
- Warm temperatures and high soil moisture, which strongly favour bacterial activity and spread.
- Poorly drained or waterlogged soils, which promote root infection and movement of the bacterium.
- Fields with a history of bacterial wilt or recent cultivation of susceptible nightshade crops.
- Root damage from cultivation, transplanting, or root-feeding pests, which provides entry points for the bacterium.

## How It Spreads
- Spread through contaminated soil, irrigation and surface water, and infected plant debris.
- Moved on tools, footwear, machinery, and hands, and in soil clinging to transplants.
- Introduced through infected transplants or, for some strains, contaminated planting material.
- Not primarily an airborne disease; it does not spread through the air like many fungal diseases, but it moves readily with water and soil.

## Risk Level
- High to very high in warm, wet conditions and in fields with a history of the disease.
- Particularly serious because the bacterium can persist in soil and water for extended periods and there is no chemical cure once plants are infected.
- Losses can range from scattered plant deaths to complete loss of a susceptible crop under favourable conditions.

## Treatment Options
- There is no effective chemical cure for plants once they are infected; bactericides do not reliably control the disease in the field.
- Management is based on prevention, sanitation, and removing infected plants.
- Promptly remove and destroy wilted plants together with the soil immediately around the roots where practical, and do not compost them.
- Avoid working in wet infected fields, which spreads the bacterium in soil and water.

## Prevention
- Use resistant or tolerant varieties and rootstocks where available and suited to local conditions.
- Rotate away from tomato and other nightshade crops for several seasons in affected fields, using non-host crops such as cereals.
- Plant in well-drained soil and improve drainage to avoid waterlogging.
- Use clean, disease-free transplants and avoid moving soil or transplants from affected areas.
- Clean tools, footwear, and equipment when moving between fields to avoid carrying contaminated soil.
- Avoid unnecessary root injury during transplanting and cultivation, and manage root-feeding pests.

## Monitoring Recommendations
- Scout fields regularly, especially during warm, wet periods, for early or one-sided wilting of plants that remain green.
- Use the cut-stem-in-water test as a simple field check when bacterial wilt is suspected.
- Note and map where wilting plants occur, since the disease often appears in patches related to soil moisture and previous infection.
- Track the cropping history of each field to identify plots at higher risk.

## When Farmers Should Consult an Agricultural Extension Officer or Plant Health Specialist
- As soon as sudden wilting of green plants is observed, to confirm whether the cause is bacterial wilt rather than fusarium wilt, waterlogging, or another problem, since management differs.
- Before replanting nightshade crops in a field where bacterial wilt has occurred, to plan rotation and variety choices.
- If the disease appears to be spreading through a field or between fields, to advise on sanitation and water management.
- When selecting resistant varieties or rootstocks, to confirm which options perform well against the strains present in the local area.
