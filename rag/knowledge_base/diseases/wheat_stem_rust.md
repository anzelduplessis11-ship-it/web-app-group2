# Wheat Stem Rust

## Disease Name
Wheat Stem Rust (also known as Black Rust or Cereal Stem Rust)

## Scientific Name
Caused by *Puccinia graminis* f. sp. *tritici*, a basidiomycete (rust) fungus. Several virulent races exist, including the Ug99 lineage (first identified in Uganda in 1998–1999), which overcame widely deployed resistance genes such as *Sr31* and subsequently spread across East Africa, southern Africa, and into parts of the Middle East and Asia.

## Crops Affected
- Bread wheat (*Triticum aestivum*) and durum wheat (*Triticum durum*), which are highly susceptible to virulent races.
- Barley (*Hordeum vulgare*) can be affected by related formae speciales, though *P. graminis* f. sp. *tritici* primarily targets wheat and triticale.
- Triticale, as a wheat-rye hybrid, is also susceptible.
- Barberry (*Berberis* spp.) serves as the alternate host required for the fungus's sexual cycle and is a source of new virulent races in regions where it grows near wheat fields.

## Cause
Infection by the fungus *Puccinia graminis* f. sp. *tritici*. The pathogen is an obligate biotroph, meaning it can only grow and reproduce on living wheat tissue. It has a complex life cycle with multiple spore stages:
- **Urediniospores** (reddish-brown, powdery) are the repeating stage responsible for rapid disease build-up and spread within and between wheat fields during the growing season.
- **Teliospores** (black, thick-walled) form late in the season on senescing stems and leaves and allow the fungus to survive between seasons on crop residue in some environments.
- **Aeciospores**, produced on barberry after sexual recombination, can generate new races with novel virulence, which is a key reason barberry eradication programmes have been used historically to slow the emergence of new races.

## Symptoms
- Elongated, blister-like pustules (uredinia) that are reddish-brown to brick-red, most commonly on stems, leaf sheaths, and sometimes leaf blades, unlike leaf rust which is confined mainly to leaves.
- Pustules rupture the epidermis and curl it back, giving a ragged, torn appearance distinct from the smoother pustules of leaf or yellow rust.
- Pustules are often larger and more scattered than those of leaf rust, and can coalesce to cover large areas of the stem.
- Severely infected stems become weakened and prone to lodging (falling over) and breakage, particularly at the site of heavy pustule concentration.
- Late in the season, pustules turn black as teliospores form, giving the disease its common name "black rust."
- Infected plants show shrivelled, poorly filled grain (low test weight) due to disruption of water and nutrient transport through infected stem tissue.
- In severe, fast-moving epidemics, entire fields can turn black and collapse within one to two weeks of symptom onset.

| Growth Stage at Infection | Typical Impact |
|---|---|
| Seedling to tillering | Lower yield impact if controlled early; can still serve as inoculum source |
| Stem elongation to booting | High risk period; significant yield loss if uncontrolled |
| Heading to grain fill | Most damaging stage; direct interference with grain filling causes severe yield and quality loss |

## Conditions Favouring Infection
- Warm temperatures, with an optimum range of approximately 15–35°C and fastest disease development around 25–30°C, generally warmer than what favours yellow rust.
- Free moisture on plant surfaces (dew, light rain, or high humidity) for at least 6–8 hours is needed for spore germination and infection.
- High relative humidity combined with warm nights.
- Continuous or overlapping wheat cropping cycles across a region, which allow the pathogen to persist and multiply between seasons ("green bridge").
- Presence of susceptible barberry bushes near wheat-growing areas, which can support sexual recombination and generate new, more virulent races.
- Wide-scale cultivation of genetically uniform, susceptible wheat varieties, which increases regional vulnerability if a matching virulent race is present.

## How It Spreads
- Urediniospores are dispersed primarily by wind, and can travel extremely long distances — hundreds to thousands of kilometres — enabling rapid movement of the disease and new races across countries and even continents.
- Local spread within and between neighbouring fields occurs via wind and, to a lesser extent, rain splash.
- Volunteer wheat plants and self-sown grain from a previous season can carry the pathogen between cropping cycles.
- Movement of infected plant material, straw, or contaminated equipment can introduce the pathogen to new areas, although wind dispersal is by far the dominant long-distance pathway.
- Sexual recombination on barberry, where present, can generate genetically new races that may overcome previously effective resistance genes, contributing to periodic large-scale epidemics.

## Risk Level
- Very high. Wheat stem rust is historically one of the most destructive wheat diseases worldwide and remains a serious threat to food security in Africa.
- The emergence of the Ug99 race lineage and its derivatives (which have overcome resistance genes deployed in a large proportion of the world's wheat varieties) has elevated risk substantially in East Africa and beyond since the early 2000s.
- Losses can reach 50–100% in susceptible varieties under favourable weather when the matching virulent race is present and control measures are not applied in time.
- Because of long-distance wind dispersal, an outbreak in one region can pose a risk to wheat crops in neighbouring countries within a single season.

## Treatment Options
- Foliar fungicides from the triazole (DMI) and strobilurin (QoI) chemical classes are effective against stem rust when applied preventively or at the earliest sign of infection; some regions use pre-mixed products combining both modes of action to reduce the risk of fungicide resistance developing.
- Fungicide timing is critical: applications made before or at the very onset of visible pustules are far more effective than those applied once the disease is well established, given the speed of stem rust development under favourable conditions.
- Two or more well-timed applications may be needed during periods of high disease pressure, particularly from stem elongation through grain fill.
- There is no way to reverse damage already done to infected stem tissue; fungicides protect uninfected tissue and slow further spread but cannot restore lodged or already-collapsed stems.
- Always confirm currently registered fungicide active ingredients, approved rates, and pre-harvest intervals with a national plant protection authority or agricultural extension officer, as registered products and resistance-management guidance vary by country and change over time.

## Prevention
- Plant resistant or tolerant wheat varieties as the primary and most cost-effective line of defence; use varieties with resistance genes confirmed to be effective against locally present races, including Ug99-lineage races where relevant.
- Avoid growing a single resistant variety across very large, contiguous areas for many consecutive seasons, since this increases the risk that a new virulent race will emerge and spread rapidly ("boom and bust" resistance breakdown).
- Destroy volunteer wheat plants and self-sown grain between seasons to remove potential sources of carryover infection ("green bridge").
- Where barberry is present near wheat-growing areas, follow national guidance on management of alternate hosts, as this can reduce the risk of new race formation through sexual recombination.
- Avoid excessive nitrogen fertilisation, which can promote lush, susceptible growth; use balanced fertilisation based on soil testing.
- Stagger planting dates within a region only where consistent with other agronomic recommendations, since synchronised planting can sometimes reduce the window of vulnerable growth stages exposed to peak spore loads, though this must be balanced against other pest and disease considerations.
- Participate in regional rust surveillance and early-warning networks where available, since early detection of new races or outbreaks allows faster coordinated response.

## Monitoring Recommendations
- Scout fields at least weekly from stem elongation through grain fill, focusing on stems and leaf sheaths as well as leaves.
- Look first at the base of the canopy and lower stems, where infection often begins, then check upper stems and flag leaves as the season progresses.
- Pay close attention after periods of warm, humid weather or light rain, which favour rapid pustule development.
- Record the proportion of plants and stem area affected to track disease progression and inform fungicide timing decisions.
- Stay informed about regional rust race surveillance reports and alerts where such networks exist, since new or highly virulent races can spread into an area with little local warning.
- Inspect any nearby barberry bushes for aecial infection (orange cup-like structures) as an indicator of local sexual recombination risk, where relevant to the region.

## When to Consult an Agricultural Extension Officer or Plant Health Specialist
- As soon as reddish-brown pustules are observed on stems or leaf sheaths, given how quickly stem rust can escalate under favourable weather.
- Before selecting a wheat variety for planting, to confirm which resistance genes are currently effective against races present in the local area.
- If disease continues to spread despite fungicide application, as this may indicate a resistance-breaking race or incorrect timing/product choice requiring specialist diagnosis.
- When an outbreak appears unusually severe or widespread, so that it can be reported to regional surveillance systems and inform wider response efforts.
- To confirm currently locally registered fungicide products, application rates, and timing recommendations before starting a spray programme.
