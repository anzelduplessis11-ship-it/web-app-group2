# Septoria Leaf Spot of Tomato

## Overview
Septoria leaf spot is a common fungal disease of tomato foliage that causes numerous small, distinctive spots on the leaves, leading to yellowing and premature leaf drop. It is primarily a disease of the leaves, and unlike late blight it does not usually attack the fruit directly. However, by stripping the plant of its leaves it exposes fruit to sunscald and greatly reduces the plant's ability to fill and ripen a good crop. Septoria leaf spot is favoured by warm, wet, humid conditions and is especially troublesome during rainy seasons and where tomatoes are grown densely and irrigated from overhead.

For African smallholder farmers, Septoria leaf spot is important because it is widespread, because it can be confused with early blight (see `diseases/tomato_early_blight.md`), and because it is best controlled by a combination of sanitation, cultural practices, and only-when-justified protectant spraying rather than by any single measure. The disease survives in crop debris and on related weeds, so fields cropped continuously with tomato and left uncleaned tend to suffer repeatedly. This document should be read together with the tomato crop guide (`crops/tomato.md`) and the other tomato foliar disease guides in this knowledge base.

## Scientific Name
Septoria leaf spot of tomato is caused by the fungus *Septoria lycopersici*. The fungus produces its spores in tiny, dark, flask-shaped fruiting bodies called pycnidia, which appear as minute black dots in the centre of the spots and are a key diagnostic feature when viewed closely.

## Crops Affected
- Tomato (*Solanum lycopersicum*) is the principal host and the crop of main concern (see `crops/tomato.md`).
- Other members of the nightshade family can be affected, including eggplant (aubergine) and some peppers, though far less severely than tomato.
- Solanaceous weeds, particularly black nightshade and horse nettle, act as alternative hosts and reservoirs of the fungus between tomato crops.

Because the fungus is largely specific to the nightshade family, rotation with unrelated crops is an effective way to reduce it.

## Cause
Septoria leaf spot is caused by infection with the fungus *Septoria lycopersici*. The fungus survives between seasons in infected tomato debris left in or on the soil, on volunteer tomato plants, and on solanaceous weeds. During warm, wet weather it produces spores in its pycnidia. These spores are spread by splashing water onto healthy leaves, where, if the leaf stays wet long enough, they germinate and infect the tissue, producing new spots within one to two weeks. Each new generation of spots produces more spores, so the disease can build rapidly during prolonged wet weather, moving steadily up the plant from the lower leaves.

## Symptoms
Septoria leaf spot has a very recognisable appearance that, once learned, is easy to distinguish from other tomato leaf diseases.

- **Many small, circular spots:** The leaves develop numerous small, roughly circular spots, typically 1 to 3 millimetres across, far more numerous than early blight spots.
- **Grey or tan centres with dark borders:** Each spot has a light grey to tan centre surrounded by a distinct dark brown margin. This "grey centre, dark ring" appearance is characteristic.
- **Tiny black dots in the centre:** Within the grey centres, minute black specks (the pycnidia) can be seen, especially with a hand lens. This is the single most reliable feature for confirming Septoria leaf spot.
- **No concentric rings:** Unlike early blight, Septoria spots do not show the target-board pattern of concentric rings.
- **Lower leaves first:** Spots appear first on the oldest, lowest leaves, usually after the first fruit has set, then progress upward.
- **Yellowing and defoliation:** Heavily spotted leaves turn yellow, then brown, wither, and drop. Severe infections can defoliate a plant from the bottom up, leaving fruit exposed.
- **Stems and calyces:** Spots may also occur on stems, leaf stalks, and the calyx of the fruit, but the fruit flesh itself is rarely affected.

## Conditions Favouring Infection
- Warm temperatures, roughly 20°C to 25°C, combined with high humidity.
- Wet foliage from rain, heavy dew, fog, or overhead irrigation, with prolonged leaf wetness allowing spores to germinate.
- Frequent rainfall and extended rainy seasons that keep leaves wet and splash spores.
- Dense planting and unstaked, sprawling plants that trap humidity and keep the canopy wet.
- Presence of infected crop debris, volunteer tomatoes, and solanaceous weeds providing initial spores.
- Continuous tomato cultivation on the same land without rotation or sanitation.

## How It Spreads
- Spores are spread mainly by splashing water, from rain and from overhead irrigation, which carries them from the soil and lower leaves up onto healthy foliage.
- Wind-driven rain can move spores between plants and rows.
- People, animals, and tools moving through a wet crop pick up and carry spores from plant to plant.
- Infected crop debris, volunteer plants, and solanaceous weeds are the source of the first infections each season.
- The disease does not spread far on dry wind alone, so its movement is closely tied to wet conditions.

## Risk Level
Moderate to high during warm, wet seasons on susceptible plantings. Septoria leaf spot rarely kills plants outright, but severe defoliation can cut yield substantially, commonly by 20 to 40 percent and more in wet years, and exposes fruit to sunscald that lowers quality. The risk is highest in prolonged rainy periods, in dense unstaked plantings, in fields with a history of the disease, and where overhead irrigation keeps foliage wet. In hot, dry conditions the disease largely stops advancing.

## Treatment Options
Management of an active outbreak combines sanitation, cultural adjustments, and, where justified, protectant fungicides.

- **Remove infected lower leaves:** At the first signs, pick off and destroy badly spotted lower leaves to slow the upward spread, and remove them from the field by burial or burning.
- **Keep foliage dry:** Switch from overhead to drip or furrow irrigation where possible, water early in the day, and avoid working among plants while wet, so that spores are less able to spread and germinate.
- **Improve airflow:** Stake, prune, and space plants to open the canopy so leaves dry faster after rain or dew.
- **Protectant fungicides:** When the disease is active and wet weather continues, protectant (contact) fungicides can limit spread. Relevant active-ingredient classes include multi-site protectant contact fungicides (for example the dithiocarbamate group and chlorothalonil-type contact fungicides) and copper-based products. Systemic classes such as the strobilurins (QoI group) may be used under high pressure, ideally in rotation with contact products to reduce resistance risk. Fungicides protect healthy tissue and are most effective applied preventively or at the very first spots, not on an already-defoliated crop.
- **Confirm products locally:** Farmers must confirm which specific products are currently registered, approved, and legal in their country, along with correct dosage, spray interval, and pre-harvest interval, with a qualified agricultural extension officer. Registration and recommendations vary by country and change over time.

## Prevention
- **Resistant or tolerant varieties:** Where tomato varieties with tolerance to Septoria leaf spot are available and locally adapted, choose them as a first line of defence.
- **Crop rotation:** Rotate away from tomato and other nightshade crops for at least two to three seasons in affected fields, using unrelated crops such as cereals or legumes, to let the fungus in debris break down.
- **Field sanitation:** Remove and destroy tomato crop debris after harvest, control volunteer tomato plants, and remove solanaceous weeds that host the fungus.
- **Mulching:** Apply mulch to reduce splashing of spores from the soil onto the lower leaves and to keep soil moisture even.
- **Spacing and staking:** Use recommended spacing and stake plants to keep leaves off the soil and improve air movement.
- **Clean seed and seedlings:** Use good-quality seed and healthy transplants, and inspect nursery plants for spotting before planting out.
- **Watering practice:** Water at the base of plants early in the day so foliage dries quickly, and avoid overhead irrigation late in the day.

## Monitoring Recommendations
- Scout at least once or twice a week, more often during rainy, humid weather, focusing first on the lower leaves where spots appear first.
- Use a hand lens to look for the tiny black pycnidia in the grey centres of the spots, the most reliable way to confirm Septoria leaf spot.
- Distinguish it from early blight (fewer, larger spots with concentric rings and yellow halos; see `diseases/tomato_early_blight.md`) and from late blight (large water-soaked lesions with white growth; see `diseases/tomato_late_blight.md`).
- Track how quickly spotting is moving up the plant to judge whether intervention is needed.
- Note fields with a history of the disease for closer monitoring and rotation planning, and watch weather for extended wet periods.

## When to Consult an Agricultural Extension Officer or Plant Health Specialist
- When small leaf spots appear and the farmer is unsure whether the cause is Septoria leaf spot, early blight, or another problem, since the confirmation guides management.
- Before buying or applying any fungicide, to confirm locally registered and approved products and to obtain correct dosage, timing, spray interval, and pre-harvest interval.
- When the disease keeps advancing despite sanitation and spraying, which may point to poor timing, wrong product choice, or resistance.
- When planning rotation, variety choice, and irrigation changes to reduce recurring problems in fields with a history of the disease.
- When several leaf diseases appear together, to design a combined foliar disease management plan.

## Frequently Asked Questions
**Q: How is Septoria leaf spot different from early blight?**
A: Septoria produces many small spots (1 to 3 mm) with grey or tan centres, dark borders, and tiny black dots (pycnidia) in the centre, and no concentric rings. Early blight produces fewer, larger spots with a target-board pattern of concentric rings and yellow halos. Both start on the lower leaves, so look closely, ideally with a hand lens, at the spot centres. See `diseases/tomato_early_blight.md`.

**Q: Does Septoria leaf spot damage the fruit?**
A: It rarely infects the fruit flesh directly. The main harm comes from destroying the leaves, which reduces yield and exposes fruit to sunscald. Protecting the foliage protects the crop.

**Q: Will the fungus survive in my field to next season?**
A: Yes. It survives in infected tomato debris, on volunteer tomato plants, and on solanaceous weeds. Removing debris, controlling volunteers and weeds, and rotating crops all reduce this carry-over.

**Q: Can I control it without spraying?**
A: Often, yes, in less severe situations. Removing infected lower leaves, mulching, staking, spacing, avoiding overhead irrigation, and rotating crops can keep the disease below damaging levels. Spraying is an added tool when wet weather drives rapid spread, and should be justified rather than routine.

**Q: Why does it always start on the bottom leaves?**
A: The fungus overwinters in the soil and debris, and spores splash up from there onto the lowest leaves first. Those older leaves also stay wetter and shadier within the canopy, favouring infection, so the disease then climbs the plant.

**Q: Is it safe to eat tomatoes from an infected plant?**
A: Yes, the fruit itself is generally unaffected and safe to eat, provided any fungicide used has observed its pre-harvest interval. The disease reduces how much fruit you get rather than making the fruit unsafe.

**Q: I removed the spotted leaves but new spots keep appearing. What now?**
A: During prolonged wet weather the fungus can keep producing new infections faster than you can remove leaves. Combine leaf removal with keeping foliage dry, improving airflow, and, if justified, a protectant fungicide programme, and consult an extension officer to confirm products and timing.
