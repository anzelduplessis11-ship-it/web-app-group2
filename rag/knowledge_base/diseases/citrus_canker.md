# Citrus Canker

## Disease Name
Citrus Canker (also called citrus bacterial canker).

## Scientific Name
Caused by the bacterium *Xanthomonas citri* pv. *citri* (previously classified in some literature as *Xanthomonas axonopodis* pv. *citri* or *Xanthomonas campestris* pv. *citri*).

## Crops Affected
- Most cultivated citrus species and hybrids are susceptible to some degree, including grapefruit and lime, which are highly susceptible; sweet orange and mandarin, which show intermediate susceptibility; and some cultivars that are more tolerant.
- Certain citrus relatives in the family Rutaceae can also be affected and may act as alternative hosts in some regions.

## Cause
Citrus canker is caused by infection of citrus leaves, stems, thorns, and fruit by the bacterium *Xanthomonas citri* pv. *citri*. The bacterium enters plant tissue through natural openings such as stomata, as well as through wounds, including those created by wind-driven sand or rain, thorns, and feeding damage from insects such as the citrus leafminer, which creates tunnels that provide an easy entry point for the bacteria.

## Symptoms
- **Leaf lesions**: initially small, water-soaked, translucent spots that develop into raised, corky lesions with a yellow halo; lesions are typically visible on both leaf surfaces and often have a rough, crater-like appearance with a raised margin and sunken centre as they age.
- **Stem and twig lesions**: similar raised, corky lesions on green stems and twigs, which can lead to twig dieback in severe infections.
- **Fruit lesions**: raised, corky, brown lesions on the fruit rind, often surrounded by an oily-looking, water-soaked margin and yellow halo in early stages; lesions are typically superficial, affecting fruit appearance and marketability more than internal fruit quality, though severe infections can cause premature fruit drop.
- **Defoliation and fruit drop**: heavily infected trees, particularly young trees or highly susceptible varieties, can show significant leaf drop and premature fruit drop, weakening overall tree vigour over time.
- **Association with leafminer damage**: lesions frequently follow the winding tunnel patterns created by citrus leafminer larvae, since these wounds provide an easy entry route for the bacterium, particularly on young, expanding leaves.
- Young, actively expanding leaves, shoots, and fruit are considerably more susceptible to new infection than mature tissue, which is more resistant once fully hardened.

## Conditions Favouring Infection
| Factor | Effect on disease risk |
|---|---|
| Warm, wet weather | Favours bacterial multiplication and infection |
| Wind-driven rain | Disperses bacteria and creates the leaf and fruit wounds needed for entry |
| High humidity | Prolongs the moisture film bacteria need to enter through stomata and wounds |
| Presence of citrus leafminer damage | Creates wounds that greatly increase infection likelihood on young leaves |
| Young, actively growing flush and fruit | More susceptible to infection than mature, hardened tissue |
| Overhead irrigation | Can mimic rain in spreading bacteria and wetting foliage |
| Dense plantings and windbreak absence | Increase exposure to wind-driven rain and bacterial dispersal |

## How It Spreads
- Wind-driven rain is the primary means of local, short-distance spread, physically carrying bacteria from existing lesions onto healthy leaves, stems, and fruit, while also creating the wounds needed for infection.
- Long-distance spread is most commonly associated with movement of infected nursery stock, budwood, plant material, or fruit from an infected area into a previously unaffected one.
- Contaminated tools, equipment, clothing, and hands moving through wet, infected foliage can mechanically spread the bacteria between trees and across an orchard.
- Citrus leafminer activity indirectly promotes spread by wounding young leaves, making them more vulnerable to infection wherever inoculum is present.
- The disease is not generally spread through soil or true seed, but any movement of contaminated plant material, including grafting material, poses a significant risk of introducing the bacterium to new areas.

## Risk Level
- High. Citrus canker is treated as a serious quarantine and regulatory concern in many countries due to its potential to affect fruit marketability, restrict trade, and spread rapidly under favourable weather.
- The disease rarely kills mature trees outright but can cause substantial economic loss through reduced fruit marketability (due to unsightly rind lesions), premature fruit and leaf drop, and reduced overall tree vigour with repeated severe infections.
- Highly susceptible varieties, such as grapefruit and lime, and young trees or nursery stock are at particularly high risk, especially in regions with frequent wind-driven rain events.

## Treatment Options
- There is no treatment that cures existing canker lesions on leaves, stems, or fruit; management is focused entirely on protecting healthy tissue from new infection and reducing the spread of existing inoculum.
- Copper-based bactericides (such as copper hydroxide or copper oxychloride) are the primary chemical tool used, applied as protectant sprays timed to coincide with new flush growth and ahead of forecast wind-driven rain events, when infection risk is highest.
- Because repeated copper use over time can contribute to phytotoxicity on fruit and foliage and to reduced effectiveness, spray programmes should follow locally recommended rates, timing, and intervals rather than being applied continuously without guidance.
- Control of citrus leafminer, using appropriate insecticides or other integrated pest management measures, helps reduce the wound sites that facilitate canker infection on young leaves.
- Farmers should confirm with a local agricultural extension officer or agro-input supplier which copper-based products and leafminer control options are currently registered locally for citrus, along with correct rates and timing, since registrations and recommendations vary by location.
- In some regulatory contexts, confirmed infected trees or blocks may be subject to mandatory removal or restricted movement requirements set by plant health authorities, particularly in areas seeking to maintain canker-free status for trade purposes.

## Prevention
- Use only certified, disease-free nursery stock and budwood from reputable sources when establishing new citrus plantings.
- Plant less susceptible varieties where local conditions and market requirements allow, particularly in high-risk, wind- and rain-prone areas.
- Establish windbreaks around orchards to reduce the force and reach of wind-driven rain, which is a major factor in local disease spread.
- Avoid overhead irrigation where possible, favouring irrigation methods that minimise prolonged wetting of foliage and fruit.
- Control citrus leafminer populations to reduce the wounds that facilitate bacterial entry into young leaves.
- Disinfect pruning tools and equipment between trees, particularly when moving from a known-infected tree or block to a healthy one.
- Restrict and carefully manage the movement of citrus plant material, budwood, and fruit from areas with known canker presence into clean areas, and comply with any quarantine or movement restrictions set by local plant health authorities.
- Apply protectant copper sprays ahead of high-risk weather events (particularly windstorms with rain) during periods of active flush growth, following locally recommended programmes.

## Monitoring Recommendations
- Inspect trees regularly, especially after wind and rain events, for raised, corky lesions with a water-soaked or yellow-haloed margin on leaves, stems, and fruit.
- Pay particular attention to new flush growth and young fruit, since these tissues are far more susceptible to new infection than mature tissue.
- Check for citrus leafminer tunnelling damage on young leaves, and treat any leafminer-damaged flush as being at elevated risk of subsequent canker infection.
- Examine nursery stock and any newly introduced planting material carefully before planting or distribution, since introducing infected material is a major route for establishing the disease in a new area.
- Report suspected new outbreaks, particularly in areas with no prior record of citrus canker, to local plant health or extension authorities promptly, given the disease's regulatory and quarantine significance.

## When to Consult an Agricultural Extension Officer or Plant Health Specialist
- As soon as raised, corky lesions with a water-soaked or yellow-haloed margin are noticed on leaves, stems, or fruit, to confirm the diagnosis, given the quarantine significance of this disease in many regions.
- Before sourcing new citrus planting material, budwood, or nursery stock, to confirm it is certified disease-free and to check any local movement restrictions that may apply.
- When planning a copper spray programme, to confirm currently registered products, correct rates, timing relative to flush growth and weather events, and safe application intervals.
- If citrus canker is confirmed on a farm, to understand any regulatory requirements for reporting, tree removal, or movement restrictions that may apply locally.
- When establishing a new citrus orchard in an area with any history of citrus canker, to receive guidance on variety selection, windbreak design, and an appropriate preventive spray and monitoring programme before planting.
