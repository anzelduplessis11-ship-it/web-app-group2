# Sorghum Grain Mold Complex

## Disease Name
Sorghum Grain Mold (Grain Mold Complex)

## Scientific Name
Caused by a complex of multiple fungal species that colonise developing and maturing sorghum grain, rather than a single pathogen. Commonly involved genera and species include *Fusarium* spp. (including *Fusarium thapsinum* and other *Fusarium* species), *Curvularia lunata*, *Alternaria* spp., *Bipolaris* spp., *Phoma* spp., *Colletotrichum sublineola* (also the cause of sorghum anthracnose), and various *Aspergillus* species, among others. Different fungi may dominate depending on weather conditions, region, and grain type.

## Crops Affected
- Sorghum (*Sorghum bicolor*), particularly grain sorghum types; forage and sweet sorghum can also be affected, though grain mold is of greatest concern where grain quality and marketability matter most.
- White-grained, non-tannin (non-pigmented) sorghum varieties are generally more susceptible than red or brown, tannin-containing varieties, which have some natural resistance associated with tannin and pigment content.
- Loose-panicle types and varieties with poor glume (husk) coverage of the grain tend to be more susceptible than compact-panicle types with better grain coverage.

## Cause
Grain mold results from infection of developing sorghum grain by one or more of the fungal species listed above, generally beginning around flowering (anthesis) and continuing through grain fill and maturity. Because multiple fungal species with different environmental preferences are typically involved, grain mold severity and the specific fungi present can vary considerably between seasons, regions, and even individual fields.

## Symptoms
- Discoloration of grain surfaces, ranging from grey, pink, or reddish to black or dark green, depending on which fungal species are dominant.
- Visible fungal growth (mold) on the grain surface, sometimes appearing fuzzy, powdery, or as a discrete discoloured patch, particularly at the point where the grain attaches to the panicle.
- Grain may appear discoloured only on the surface (superficial molding) or may be more deeply colonised, affecting internal grain quality, weight, and germination ability.
- Infected grain is often shrivelled, lightweight, and of reduced test weight compared with healthy grain.
- Reduced germination and seedling vigour in severely molded grain intended for use as seed.
- In severe cases, entire panicles may appear visibly discoloured or "moldy" at a distance, particularly following prolonged wet weather during grain maturation.
- Some of the fungi involved (particularly certain *Fusarium* species) are capable of producing mycotoxins, which can pose a food and feed safety concern in heavily molded grain, though the presence and level of mycotoxins cannot be determined by visual inspection alone.

## Conditions Favouring Infection
- Warm temperatures combined with high humidity or frequent rainfall during the flowering to grain maturity period is the single most important factor favouring grain mold development.
- Prolonged wet weather during grain fill and especially during the period between physiological maturity and harvest substantially increases mold severity, since delayed harvest under wet conditions extends the time grain is exposed to infection.
- Extended dew periods or high humidity at night during flowering and grain development.
- Cultivation of susceptible white-grained, non-tannin varieties with loose panicle architecture and poor glume coverage in humid or high-rainfall environments.
- Dense canopy and lodged plants that trap moisture around developing grain heads.
- Insect or bird damage to grain heads, which can create entry points for fungal colonisation.

## How It Spreads
- The causal fungi are generally common and widespread in the environment, surviving on crop residue, alternative host plants, and in the soil; spores are dispersed primarily by wind and rain splash onto flowering panicles.
- Infection is initiated mainly through the flower parts (via the stigma) during flowering, or through wounds and natural openings on developing grain later in grain fill.
- Infected seed can carry some of the causal fungi, contributing to disease carryover and potential establishment in subsequent plantings if infected seed is used.
- Because multiple fungal species with different biology are involved, there is no single, uniform spread pathway; instead, spread depends on the combination of fungi present locally and prevailing weather during the susceptible flowering-to-maturity window.

## Risk Level
- High in warm, humid sorghum-growing regions, particularly where rainfall coincides with flowering through grain maturity, and where predominantly white, non-tannin grain varieties are grown for food or market.
- Risk is comparatively lower in drier production environments where grain matures and dries under low humidity conditions.
- Beyond yield loss from shrivelled and lightweight grain, grain mold significantly reduces market value and food/feed quality, and raises potential mycotoxin contamination concerns in severely affected grain, making it an economically important constraint even at moderate severity levels.

## Treatment Options
- Fungicide seed treatments can help protect young seedlings from seed-borne inoculum but do not address infection occurring later at flowering and grain fill.
- Foliar/panicle fungicide applications timed around flowering, using triazole (DMI) or strobilurin (QoI) chemical classes, can reduce grain mold severity in high-risk situations, though effectiveness depends on correct timing relative to flowering and on weather conditions during grain fill.
- Because grain mold results from a complex of fungi with different sensitivities, fungicide programmes should be guided by locally validated recommendations rather than assuming a single product will control all contributing organisms.
- There is no way to reverse mold damage or remove mycotoxin contamination from grain already affected; management of already-molded grain focuses on careful post-harvest handling, sorting, and, where mycotoxin contamination is suspected, avoiding use of severely affected grain for food, feed, or seed pending appropriate testing.
- Confirm currently locally registered fungicide active ingredients, timing, and application guidance with a national agricultural authority or extension officer.

## Prevention
- Plant resistant or tolerant varieties where available; red or brown tannin-containing sorghum varieties and compact-panicle types with good glume coverage generally show greater resistance than white, non-tannin, loose-panicle types, though agronomic and market requirements must also be considered when choosing varieties.
- Where possible, select planting dates so that flowering and grain maturation avoid the period of peak seasonal rainfall in the local area, reducing exposure of susceptible grain stages to wet conditions.
- Harvest grain promptly at physiological maturity rather than allowing it to stand in the field for extended periods, particularly during wet weather, since delayed harvest is one of the strongest risk factors for severe grain mold.
- Dry harvested grain quickly and thoroughly to a safe moisture level for storage, and avoid storing grain with elevated moisture content, since continued fungal activity can occur in improperly dried grain.
- Use good quality, disease-free seed from a reliable source for planting.
- Manage crop residue and practice rotation where practical to reduce the buildup of causal fungi in the field environment over successive seasons.
- Control insect and bird damage to grain heads where feasible, since damage can create entry points for fungal colonisation.

## Monitoring Recommendations
- Begin close inspection of grain heads at flowering and continue through grain fill and maturity, since infection is initiated at flowering even though visible symptoms may not appear until later.
- Examine grain heads for surface discoloration, visible mold growth, or a musty appearance, particularly following periods of wet or humid weather during grain development.
- Assess grain samples at harvest for discoloration, shrivelling, and overall grain quality to estimate the extent of mold impact on yield and marketability.
- Monitor weather conditions during the flowering-to-maturity window as an indicator of grain mold risk for the season, since sustained wet weather during this period is the primary driver of severe outbreaks.
- Keep records of grain mold severity by variety and field across seasons to guide future variety selection and planting date decisions.

## When to Consult an Agricultural Extension Officer or Plant Health Specialist
- If grain heads show significant surface mold or discoloration approaching or at harvest, to assess the extent of the problem and discuss appropriate handling of the harvested grain.
- Before selecting a sorghum variety for planting in a known high-risk (warm, humid) area, to confirm which varieties have documented grain mold tolerance suited to local conditions and market requirements.
- If mycotoxin contamination is suspected in heavily molded grain intended for food, feed, or seed use, to arrange appropriate testing and guidance before use.
- When planning planting dates to avoid overlap between flowering/grain maturation and peak seasonal rainfall, to get locally calibrated timing advice.
- If grain mold problems persist across seasons despite variety choice and harvest timing adjustments, to review the overall management approach with a specialist.
