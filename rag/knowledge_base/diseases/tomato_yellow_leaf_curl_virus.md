# Tomato Yellow Leaf Curl Virus

## Overview
Tomato Yellow Leaf Curl Virus (TYLCV) is one of the most damaging viral diseases of tomato in warm regions of the world, including much of sub-Saharan Africa, North Africa, and the Middle East. It is transmitted by the whitefly insect and can cause total crop failure when plants are infected while young. Unlike fungal diseases, a virus cannot be "sprayed off" a plant; once a tomato plant is infected, it stays infected for life. This makes TYLCV fundamentally a disease of prevention, focused on managing the whitefly vector, using resistant varieties, and protecting young plants during the vulnerable seedling and early growth stages.

For African smallholder farmers, TYLCV is especially serious because whiteflies thrive in the hot, dry conditions common in many tomato-growing areas, because tomato is often grown continuously with overlapping crops that keep whitefly populations high, and because resistant seed and protective netting are not always available or affordable. The disease frequently occurs together with other whitefly-borne problems and can be confused with nutrient deficiencies or physiological leaf curling. This document should be used alongside the tomato crop guide (`crops/tomato.md`) and the pest guidance on whiteflies, and it complements the other tomato disease guides in this knowledge base.

## Scientific Name
Tomato Yellow Leaf Curl Virus is not a single organism but a complex of closely related viruses in the genus *Begomovirus* (family *Geminiviridae*). The best known is *Tomato yellow leaf curl virus* itself, but in Africa several related begomoviruses cause very similar diseases on tomato, and mixed infections are common. For practical smallholder management, all are handled the same way because they share the same whitefly vector and the same prevention-based control.

## Crops Affected
- Tomato (*Solanum lycopersicum*) is the primary and most severely affected crop (see `crops/tomato.md`).
- Sweet and hot peppers, beans, and several other vegetables can be affected by related begomoviruses, sometimes without obvious symptoms.
- Many common weeds and ornamental plants act as reservoirs of begomoviruses and as breeding hosts for whiteflies, allowing the virus to persist between tomato crops.

Because the virus and its whitefly vector move readily between tomato and other hosts, managing surrounding vegetation and other crops is part of controlling the disease.

## Cause
TYLCV disease is caused by infection with a begomovirus. The virus is introduced into a tomato plant when an infected whitefly feeds on it. The virus then multiplies and spreads throughout the plant, disrupting normal growth and development. The disease cannot spread from plant to plant by touch, by tools, by rain splash, or (for practical purposes) through seed of most varieties; it requires the whitefly to move it. This is why the disease is best understood as a partnership between the virus and its insect vector: controlling the whitefly is the same as controlling the virus.

## Symptoms
Symptoms depend heavily on the age of the plant at infection. Plants infected young are worst affected; plants infected after they are well established and already carrying fruit may still yield acceptably.

- **Upward leaf curling (cupping):** Leaflets curl upward and inward, forming a cup or boat shape. This is the most characteristic symptom and the source of the name.
- **Yellowing (chlorosis):** The margins and areas between the veins of young leaves turn yellow, while veins may stay greener, giving a mottled or interveinal yellowing.
- **Leaf reduction and crumpling:** New leaves become small, crumpled, thickened, and leathery.
- **Stunting and bushiness:** The whole plant is severely stunted, with shortened internodes, giving a bushy, "bunched" appearance.
- **Flower and fruit loss:** Infected plants drop flowers and set little or no fruit. Fruit that does form is small and few in number.
- **No spots or rot:** Unlike fungal diseases, TYLCV produces no leaf spots, lesions, or rotting; the damage is all in growth distortion and colour change.

Similar leaf curling can be caused by herbicide drift, mite feeding, physiological stress, or nutrient problems, so the combination of upward cupping, interveinal yellowing, severe stunting, and the presence of whiteflies is what points to TYLCV.

## Conditions Favouring Infection
- Hot, dry weather, which favours rapid whitefly breeding and activity.
- High whitefly populations, whether from nearby infected crops, weeds, or continuous tomato cultivation.
- Young, tender plants, which are far more susceptible than mature ones.
- Dry-season and early-season tomato crops planted when whitefly numbers are building.
- Continuous or overlapping tomato production in an area, providing year-round hosts for both virus and vector.
- Absence of resistant varieties and lack of protective barriers such as nets during the nursery stage.

## How It Spreads
- The virus is spread exclusively by the whitefly *Bemisia tabaci*. Once a whitefly acquires the virus by feeding on an infected plant, it can transmit it for the rest of its life.
- Whiteflies are carried by wind over long distances, so infection can arrive from fields far away.
- Movement of infected transplants and seedlings between nurseries and fields spreads the virus over even greater distances and is a major way it enters new areas.
- Whiteflies breed on many crops and weeds, so a build-up on any host near a tomato field increases risk.
- The virus is generally not transmitted through seed of tomato, nor by handling, tools, grafting between touching plants, or rain splash.

## Risk Level
Very high in warm regions with high whitefly pressure. TYLCV can cause yield losses approaching 100 percent when plants are infected at the seedling or early vegetative stage, because such plants set almost no fruit. The risk is somewhat lower where plants are infected only after they are well established. Because the disease is incurable once a plant is infected, because the vector is highly mobile, and because a single infected transplant can seed an outbreak, TYLCV is regarded as one of the highest-priority threats for smallholder tomato production wherever whiteflies are abundant.

## Treatment Options
There is no cure for a plant once it is infected with TYLCV; management focuses on limiting spread and protecting healthy plants. "Treatment" of an outbreak therefore means acting on the vector and on infected plants, not on the virus itself.

- **Rogue infected plants:** As soon as clearly infected plants are found, especially early in the season, carefully remove and destroy them by burial or burning. Removing them promptly reduces the pool of virus that whiteflies can pick up and spread to healthy plants. Handle plants gently and, where possible, bag them before removal to avoid disturbing whiteflies into flight.
- **Manage the whitefly vector:** Reduce whitefly numbers using an integrated approach. Yellow sticky traps help monitor and reduce adults. Reflective or silver mulches deter whiteflies from settling. Natural enemies such as predatory bugs and parasitic wasps should be conserved by avoiding broad-spectrum insecticide overuse.
- **Insecticides, only when justified:** Where whitefly pressure is very high, insecticides from appropriate active-ingredient classes may be used against the vector, but whiteflies develop resistance quickly and heavy spraying kills natural enemies and can make matters worse. Any insecticide use must follow an integrated plan, rotate between different active-ingredient classes, and be based on locally registered, approved products. Farmers must confirm the specific products, dosages, and pre-harvest intervals with a qualified agricultural extension officer.
- **Do not rely on chemicals alone:** Chemical control of whiteflies rarely prevents TYLCV on its own, because even a few surviving viruliferous whiteflies can infect a crop. It must be combined with resistant varieties and physical protection.

## Prevention
Prevention is the core of TYLCV management and is far more effective than any response after infection.

- **Resistant or tolerant varieties:** Plant TYLCV-resistant or tolerant tomato varieties where they are available and suited to local conditions and markets. This is the most powerful single tool; resistant plants may still become infected but usually continue to yield.
- **Protect the nursery:** Raise seedlings under insect-proof netting or fine mesh, away from older tomato crops and weedy areas, so that plants are not infected before transplanting. Protecting young plants during the first four to six weeks after transplanting is critical.
- **Use clean transplants:** Never plant seedlings showing curling or yellowing, and source transplants from reputable, whitefly-protected nurseries.
- **Time planting to avoid whitefly peaks:** Where possible, adjust planting dates to avoid the hottest, driest periods when whitefly populations peak, and coordinate with neighbours to include a tomato-free break in the area.
- **Barriers and mulches:** Use reflective or silver-coloured mulches and, where feasible, tall barrier crops or netting to reduce whitefly landing on young tomato plants.
- **Field hygiene and weed control:** Remove weeds in and around fields that host whiteflies and begomoviruses, and destroy old tomato crops promptly after final harvest so they do not become a reservoir.
- **Spacing and rogueing:** Inspect regularly and remove infected plants early, and avoid overlapping successive tomato plantings that keep the virus and vector cycling continuously.

## Monitoring Recommendations
- Inspect seedlings in the nursery and for the first six weeks after transplanting at least twice a week, since early infection is the most damaging.
- Turn over leaves to check for whiteflies, which are tiny, white, moth-like insects that fly up in a cloud when disturbed; use yellow sticky traps to track their numbers.
- Look for the earliest signs of upward leaf cupping, interveinal yellowing of young leaves, and stunting.
- Mark and record the location of infected plants so they can be removed and so hotspots can be identified.
- Monitor neighbouring fields, weedy margins, and other crops for whitefly build-up, and watch for hot, dry spells that favour the vector.

## When to Consult an Agricultural Extension Officer or Plant Health Specialist
- When leaf curling, yellowing, and stunting appear and the farmer is unsure whether the cause is TYLCV, a nutrient deficiency, mite damage, or herbicide injury, since these require very different responses.
- Before selecting varieties, to identify resistant or tolerant options that are locally adapted and available.
- Before using any insecticide against whiteflies, to confirm registered products, correct rotation of active-ingredient classes, dosages, and pre-harvest intervals, and to avoid harming natural enemies.
- When the disease appears in a new area or spreads rapidly, so that area-wide management, such as coordinated planting breaks, can be organised.
- When whitefly populations remain high despite control efforts, indicating possible resistance or a need to revise the management plan.

## Frequently Asked Questions
**Q: Can I cure a tomato plant that has TYLCV?**
A: No. Once a plant is infected, it stays infected for life and cannot be cured. Management focuses on preventing infection, protecting young plants, controlling whiteflies, and promptly removing infected plants to protect the rest of the crop.

**Q: If I spray insecticide, will that stop the virus?**
A: Not on its own. Insecticides can reduce whitefly numbers, but even a few surviving virus-carrying whiteflies can infect the crop, and heavy spraying kills the natural enemies that help control whiteflies. Chemicals must be part of an integrated plan alongside resistant varieties and physical protection.

**Q: Will the virus spread from an infected plant to nearby plants by touch or on my tools?**
A: No. TYLCV spreads only through the feeding of whiteflies. It does not spread by touch, tools, rain splash, or handling. However, an infected plant is a source that whiteflies can pick the virus up from, so removing infected plants still helps.

**Q: Can I still get a harvest if my plants are infected?**
A: It depends on when they were infected. Plants infected while young are usually stunted and set almost no fruit. Plants infected after they are well established and already fruiting may still give a reasonable harvest, which is why protecting plants during the first weeks is so important.

**Q: Is the virus carried in tomato seed?**
A: Generally no. TYLCV is not spread through tomato seed in most cases, so it will not arrive with your seed. It arrives with infected transplants or with virus-carrying whiteflies, which is why clean, protected seedlings matter.

**Q: Why is TYLCV worse in the hot, dry season?**
A: Whiteflies breed and move fastest in hot, dry weather, so their populations peak then and more of them carry the virus. Planting to avoid these peaks, and protecting young plants, reduces the risk.

**Q: What is the single most effective thing I can do?**
A: Combine two measures: plant a resistant or tolerant variety and protect your seedlings under netting so they are not infected before or soon after transplanting. Together these address both the plant's susceptibility and the critical early infection window.
