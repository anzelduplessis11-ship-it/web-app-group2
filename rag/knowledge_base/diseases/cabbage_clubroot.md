# Cabbage Clubroot

## Disease Name
Cabbage Clubroot

## Scientific Name
Caused by *Plasmodiophora brassicae*, an obligate biotrophic protist in the phylum Rhizaria (order Plasmodiophorida). It was historically classified with the slime moulds but is no longer considered a true fungus.

## Crops Affected
- Cabbage (*Brassica oleracea* var. *capitata*) is a primary and highly susceptible host (see `crops/cabbage.md`).
- All cultivated brassica (cole) crops are susceptible, including cauliflower, broccoli, Brussels sprouts, kale, kohlrabi, and Chinese cabbage.
- Related oilseed and root brassicas, such as canola/rapeseed, mustard, turnip, radish, and swede, can also be infected.
- Numerous cruciferous (Brassicaceae) weeds, such as wild mustard, shepherd's purse, and wild radish, can host the pathogen and allow it to persist in soil even without a brassica crop present.

## Cause
Clubroot is caused by infection of brassica roots by the soilborne protist *Plasmodiophora brassicae*. The pathogen produces long-lived resting spores in the soil that germinate to release motile zoospores. These zoospores swim through soil water to infect root hairs and, later, the cortical tissue of the main and lateral roots, stimulating abnormal cell division and enlargement that produces the characteristic swollen galls.

## Symptoms
- **Above ground:** stunted growth, pale or yellowing outer leaves, and wilting during the heat of the day. In early stages, wilted plants often recover overnight as cooler conditions reduce water demand, but as the disease progresses the root system loses its capacity to take up water and wilting becomes permanent.
- Poor head formation in cabbage, and reduced size or absence of curds and heads in cauliflower and broccoli.
- Affected plants are often easily pulled from the soil because of the reduced, distorted root system providing weak anchorage.
- **Below ground (the diagnostic sign):** swollen, distorted, club-shaped or spindle-shaped galls on the main taproot and lateral roots, ranging from small nodules to large, irregular masses that can dwarf the original root diameter several times over.
- Galls are initially firm and white to cream inside but later become soft and discoloured as they are invaded by secondary soil bacteria and other decay organisms, eventually rotting and developing a foul odour.
- Symptom severity depends heavily on the growth stage at which infection occurs: seedlings infected soon after transplanting are the most severely stunted and may die, while plants infected later in the season show milder galling and a smaller yield penalty.
- Because the visible above-ground symptoms (stunting, wilting, yellowing) are non-specific, clubroot is easy to overlook or misattribute to nutrient deficiency, waterlogging, or root-feeding pests until affected plants are uprooted and the roots examined directly.

## Conditions Favouring Infection
- Acidic soils, with disease severity greatest at soil pH below about 6.5 and most severe near pH 5.5 to 6.0; the pathogen is strongly suppressed in soils with pH above about 7.2.
- Wet, poorly drained, or waterlogged soils, since free soil moisture is essential for the motile zoospores to swim to and infect root hairs.
- Soil temperatures in the range of roughly 18 to 25 degrees Celsius, with infection slowing markedly outside this range.
- Continuous or frequent brassica cropping on the same land, which allows resting-spore numbers in the soil to build up over successive seasons.
- Fine-textured, moisture-retentive soils, which stay wet for longer periods than sandy, free-draining soils and so favour zoospore movement.
- A high concentration of resting spores already present in the soil, whether from a long cropping history, contaminated transplants, or contaminated manure or compost.

## How It Spreads
- Resting spores in contaminated soil are the primary means of spread. They can survive in soil for many years, commonly cited as up to 15 to 20 years, even without a susceptible host present, which makes eradication from infested land very difficult once the pathogen is established.
- Movement of contaminated soil on farm tools, footwear, tractor tyres, irrigation water, and floodwater or field runoff readily carries the pathogen between fields, farms, and districts.
- Infected transplants or seedlings raised in contaminated nursery soil or seedbeds are a major pathway for introducing the disease into previously clean fields; a single batch of contaminated transplants can seed a new outbreak.
- Manure from animals that have eaten infected crop material can carry viable resting spores, as can compost made from infected plant debris that has not reached sufficiently high temperatures for a long enough period to destroy the spores.
- The disease is not carried on true botanical seed to any significant extent; the overwhelming risk comes from contaminated soil, planting material, and water rather than from seed itself.

## Risk Level
- High in fields with a known history of clubroot, acidic soil, poor drainage, and continuous brassica cropping.
- Once established in a field, the pathogen is extremely persistent, and the field remains at elevated risk for many years even after control measures are applied, because resting spores decline only gradually.
- Yield impact ranges from minor stunting in lightly infested fields to complete crop failure in heavily infested, low-pH, waterlogged fields, particularly when young seedlings are infected soon after transplanting.
- Risk is comparatively lower, though not absent, in fields with no history of brassica cropping, well-drained soil, and soil pH maintained above about 7.0.

## Treatment Options
- There is no curative treatment that removes the pathogen from already-infected plants; management is overwhelmingly preventive and long-term, centred on reducing soil spore load and raising soil pH rather than treating a standing crop.
- Soil-applied fungicide or biocide drenches from active-ingredient classes such as fluazinam have shown some suppression of clubroot in certain cropping systems, but efficacy is inconsistent, costly to apply at field scale, and does not eliminate resting spores from the soil; such products should be used only as part of a broader integrated programme, never as a stand-alone fix.
- Liming to raise soil pH, commonly using agricultural lime or hydrated lime applied well ahead of planting and thoroughly incorporated into the topsoil, is one of the more reliable interventions available, since the pathogen is far less active in near-neutral to alkaline soils.
- Removing and destroying (not composting) severely infected plants and their root galls as soon as they are found reduces the addition of fresh resting spores to the soil for future seasons.
- Confirm any locally registered soil treatment products, the correct lime type and rate for your soil, and the appropriate application timing with an agricultural extension officer, since recommendations vary by soil type and region.

## Prevention
- Practice long crop rotations away from all brassica crops and cruciferous weeds in infested fields, ideally five to seven years or longer, since resting spore numbers decline only gradually over time.
- Test and manage soil pH proactively, liming acidic soils to bring pH above roughly 7.0 to 7.2 well before planting brassicas, as this is one of the most reliable non-chemical controls available to farmers.
- Improve field drainage and avoid planting brassicas in low-lying, poorly drained areas prone to waterlogging.
- Use only certified, disease-free transplants and seedlings raised in pathogen-free seedbeds or sterilised potting media; avoid raising seedlings in field soil taken from land with a clubroot history.
- Grow resistant or tolerant cabbage and other brassica varieties where available and suited to local conditions, while recognising that resistance can be overcome by certain pathogen strains over time and should not be relied on alone.
- Control cruciferous weeds in and around fields, since they can maintain the pathogen in soil even during years without a brassica crop.
- Practice strict field sanitation: clean soil off tools, boots, tyres, and equipment before moving between fields, and avoid transferring soil or water from infested fields to clean ones.
- Avoid applying manure or compost that may contain infected brassica material unless it has undergone thorough, sustained high-temperature composting.

## Monitoring Recommendations
- Regularly scout brassica crops for stunting, pale foliage, and daytime wilting that recovers overnight in the early stages, paying particular attention to patches or low-lying, wetter parts of a field.
- Periodically uproot a sample of plants, including apparently healthy ones near field margins, to inspect roots for early, small galls before symptoms become obvious above ground.
- Test soil pH regularly in fields used for brassica production, especially before establishing a new planting, to guide liming decisions in advance.
- Where laboratory testing is accessible, soil bioassays or molecular (PCR) testing can detect resting-spore presence and help estimate risk before planting a susceptible crop.
- Keep field-by-field records of clubroot history, soil pH, and drainage status to inform rotation planning and to flag high-risk fields for closer monitoring.
- Inspect incoming transplants and seedlings for any sign of root swelling before planting them into clean fields.

## When to Consult an Agricultural Extension Officer or Plant Health Specialist
- When wilting, stunting, and yellowing appear in a brassica crop and the cause is not obvious, so that roots can be examined and the diagnosis confirmed against other possible causes such as nutrient deficiency, waterlogging, or root-feeding pests.
- Before liming a field, to confirm the correct lime type, rate, and timing for the specific soil type and to arrange soil pH testing.
- Before sourcing transplants or seedlings from an unfamiliar nursery, to verify they are free of clubroot.
- When planning crop rotations for a field with a known or suspected clubroot history, to determine an appropriate rotation length and suitable resistant varieties.
- If clubroot is suspected for the first time in a field or district, to confirm the diagnosis, assess the scale of the problem, and help prevent further spread to neighbouring fields.
