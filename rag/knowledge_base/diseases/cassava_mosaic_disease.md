# Cassava Mosaic Disease

## Disease Name
Cassava Mosaic Disease (CMD)

## Scientific Name
Caused by several species of Cassava mosaic geminiviruses (genus *Begomovirus*, family Geminiviridae), including African cassava mosaic virus (ACMV) and East African cassava mosaic virus (EACMV), often occurring in mixed infections.

## Crops Affected
- Cassava (*Manihot esculenta*) is the primary and effectively only significant host crop.

## Cause
Infection by cassava mosaic geminiviruses, transmitted by the whitefly (*Bemisia tabaci*) and through infected stem cuttings used as planting material.

## Symptoms
- Yellow-green mosaic (patchy, irregular discolouration) pattern on leaves.
- Leaf distortion, reduced leaf size, and twisting or puckering of leaflets.
- Stunted plant growth and reduced root yield, with severity depending on the virus strain and time of infection.
- Symptom severity can vary with variety, environmental conditions, and virus strain, sometimes making mild infections difficult to distinguish from nutrient deficiency without close inspection.

## Conditions Favouring Infection
- High whitefly vector populations, favoured by warm temperatures and the presence of susceptible host plants nearby.
- Continuous cultivation of cassava in the same area without a break, allowing disease and vector carryover.
- Use of infected planting material from previous seasons, which is the primary means of disease introduction into new fields.

## How It Spreads
- Primarily spread through the use of infected stem cuttings as planting material (vegetative propagation directly transmits the virus).
- Secondarily spread by whitefly vectors moving between infected and healthy plants within and between fields.
- Not spread through soil, and not seed-transmitted in the true botanical seed (cassava is rarely propagated by true seed commercially).

## Risk Level
- High in areas with a history of severe outbreaks and where certified disease-free planting material is not used.
- Can cause yield losses ranging from 20% to complete crop failure depending on virus strain, variety susceptibility, and time of infection.

## Treatment Options
- No curative treatment exists for virus-infected plants.
- Management relies entirely on prevention: removing infected plants and using clean planting material.
- Roguing (removal and destruction) of infected plants, especially early in the season, can reduce the source of infection for whitefly spread within a field.

## Prevention
- Use certified, disease-free planting material as the single most effective prevention measure.
- Select and propagate cuttings only from visibly healthy mother plants.
- Plant resistant or tolerant varieties where available and suited to local conditions.
- Practice roguing: promptly remove and destroy plants showing symptoms, particularly in the first few months after planting.
- Control whitefly populations through field sanitation and, where necessary, approved insecticides, in coordination with local extension guidance.
- Avoid sourcing cuttings from fields with visible mosaic symptoms, even if only some plants are affected.

## Monitoring Recommendations
- Inspect fields monthly for mosaic symptoms, particularly in the first 3-4 months after planting when whitefly transmission risk is highest.
- Monitor whitefly populations on the underside of leaves, especially during warm, dry periods.
- Track the source and health status of planting material used each season.

## When Farmers Should Consult an Agricultural Extension Officer or Plant Health Specialist
- If a significant proportion of a field (more than 20-30%) shows mosaic symptoms, to assess whether replanting with clean material is warranted.
- Before sourcing planting material from an unfamiliar source, to verify it is certified disease-free where certification schemes exist.
- If unsure whether symptoms are due to cassava mosaic disease, nutrient deficiency, or another cause, since visual symptoms can overlap.
- When selecting resistant varieties, to confirm which varieties have documented resistance to the virus strains present in the local area.
