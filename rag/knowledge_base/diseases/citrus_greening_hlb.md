# Citrus Greening (Huanglongbing)

## Disease Name
Citrus Greening Disease, more widely known by its scientific-derived name Huanglongbing (HLB), meaning "yellow dragon disease" or "yellow shoot disease."

## Scientific Name
Caused by phloem-limited, unculturable bacteria in the genus *Candidatus Liberibacter*, principally *Candidatus Liberibacter asiaticus*, *Candidatus Liberibacter africanus*, and *Candidatus Liberibacter americanus*. *Candidatus Liberibacter africanus* is the species most commonly associated with HLB in parts of Africa and is generally favoured by cooler highland conditions, while *Candidatus Liberibacter asiaticus* tends to tolerate a wider range of temperatures.

## Crops Affected
- All commercially grown citrus species and cultivars are susceptible to some degree, including sweet orange, mandarin, lime, lemon, grapefruit, and pomelo, with sweet orange and mandarin generally showing severe symptoms.
- Some citrus relatives and ornamental species in the family Rutaceae can also host the bacteria or serve as reservoirs in certain regions.

## Cause
Huanglongbing is caused by infection of the phloem (the plant's nutrient-conducting tissue) by *Candidatus Liberibacter* bacteria. These bacteria are transmitted by two psyllid insect species: the Asian citrus psyllid (*Diaphorina citri*) and the African citrus psyllid (*Trioza erytreae*), both of which feed on citrus and acquire and transmit the bacteria while probing and feeding on phloem tissue.

## Symptoms
- **Blotchy mottle**: an asymmetric, blotchy yellowing pattern on leaves that does not mirror across the midrib, which is a key feature distinguishing HLB from nutrient deficiencies (which typically produce symmetric patterns).
- **Yellow shoots**: yellowing of individual shoots or branches, which is often one of the earliest visible signs and gave the disease its Chinese-derived name.
- **Small, misshapen fruit**: fruit from infected trees are often smaller, lopsided or asymmetric in shape, and may fail to colour properly.
- **Colour inversion**: affected fruit sometimes stay green at the base (stylar end) while the stem end colours, the opposite of normal ripening, which is another distinctive diagnostic feature.
- **Bitter, poor-quality juice**: fruit from infected trees often taste bitter or salty and contain aborted, dark seeds, making them unsuitable for fresh market or juice use.
- **Premature fruit drop**: infected trees frequently drop fruit before maturity, further reducing marketable yield.
- **Twig dieback and root decline**: as infection progresses, twig and branch dieback increases, root systems decline, and overall tree vigour steadily worsens.
- **Gradual tree decline**: infected trees typically decline progressively over one to several years, with reduced canopy density, sparse and stunted new growth, and eventual death in many cases, particularly in young trees or under high inoculum pressure.

## Conditions Favouring Infection
- Presence and abundance of psyllid vector populations (Asian citrus psyllid or African citrus psyllid) in and around citrus growing areas.
- Availability of young flush growth, since psyllids preferentially feed and lay eggs on new, tender shoots, and this is also when transmission risk to and from the tree is highest.
- Warm temperatures that favour psyllid reproduction and population growth, although the African citrus psyllid and *Ca.* L. africanus are also well adapted to cooler highland conditions in parts of Africa.
- Proximity to existing infected trees, abandoned citrus plantings, or unmanaged host plants that can serve as a source of both the bacteria and psyllid populations.
- Dense plantings and continuous availability of flush growth throughout the year, which can sustain larger psyllid populations and more frequent transmission opportunities.
- Movement of infected nursery stock or budwood into new or previously unaffected growing areas.

## How It Spreads
- The disease is spread primarily by psyllid vectors, which acquire the bacteria by feeding on an infected tree and can transmit it to healthy trees during subsequent feeding on new flush growth.
- Long-distance spread most commonly occurs through movement of infected nursery plants, budwood, or other propagative citrus material from an infected area to a new location.
- Once psyllids and inoculum are present in an area, local spread proceeds via psyllid movement between neighbouring trees and orchards, which can occur over a wide area given the mobility of psyllids, especially when assisted by wind.
- The disease is not generally considered to be transmitted through true citrus seed, though this route is not always completely excluded and certified, pathogen-tested planting material remains important regardless.
- Grafting infected budwood onto healthy rootstock will transmit the bacteria directly, making the use of clean, certified budwood essential in nursery propagation.

## Risk Level
- Very high. Huanglongbing is widely regarded as one of the most destructive citrus diseases in the world, having severely damaged citrus industries in multiple countries and regions where it has become established.
- There is no cure for infected trees, and infected trees typically decline progressively and often die, while surviving trees produce fruit of much lower quality and yield.
- Because of its severity and the difficulty of controlling the psyllid vectors, HLB is treated as a serious quarantine and regulatory concern in many countries, with reporting and containment measures often mandated by plant health authorities.

## Treatment Options
- There is no cure for citrus greening disease once a tree is infected; current management strategies focus on suppressing psyllid vector populations, removing infected trees, and supporting the health of remaining trees rather than curing the pathogen itself.
- Insecticide programmes targeting psyllid populations, using products and timing appropriate to the local vector species and growing season, are a central part of area-wide management in HLB-affected regions.
- Biological control of psyllids, including the use of natural enemies such as parasitic wasps that attack psyllid nymphs, has been used in some regions as part of an integrated vector management approach.
- Nutritional support programmes (balanced fertilisation and micronutrient management) are sometimes used to help maintain tree vigour and extend the productive life of infected trees, though this does not eliminate the bacteria or reverse underlying disease progression.
- Some antimicrobial or trunk-injection treatments have been investigated and used in certain regions as experimental or supplementary measures, but their availability, registration status, and demonstrated effectiveness vary considerably by country; farmers should not rely on such treatments as a cure and should confirm with a local agricultural extension officer or plant health specialist which, if any, such products are locally registered and recommended.
- Removal of confirmed infected trees remains a core management step to reduce the source of bacteria available for psyllids to acquire and spread to healthy trees.

## Prevention
- Use only certified, pathogen-tested nursery stock and budwood from reputable, disease-free sources when establishing new citrus plantings.
- Support and participate in area-wide psyllid monitoring and control programmes where these exist, since individual farm action alone is generally insufficient against a highly mobile insect vector.
- Regularly scout and promptly remove and destroy trees confirmed as infected, in line with local extension or regulatory guidance, to reduce the reservoir of bacteria available for further spread.
- Avoid transporting citrus nursery plants, budwood, or plant material from areas known to have HLB into disease-free areas, and comply with any quarantine restrictions in place.
- Encourage and protect natural enemies of psyllids where compatible with local integrated pest management recommendations.
- Where planting in an HLB-risk area, consider coordinating with neighbouring growers on vector management, since psyllids move readily between adjoining orchards.
- Maintain overall tree health through balanced nutrition and good orchard management, since vigorous trees may better tolerate low-level infection pressure, although this does not prevent infection itself.

## Monitoring Recommendations
- Inspect trees regularly, especially new flush growth, for asymmetric blotchy mottle on leaves and yellowing of individual shoots or branches.
- Examine fruit for lopsided shape, colour inversion (green at the stylar end when otherwise coloured), and internal seed abortion, particularly on trees showing any leaf symptoms.
- Monitor for psyllid activity on new flush using visual inspection or yellow sticky traps where available, since flush periods are when psyllid populations and transmission risk peak.
- Where laboratory testing (such as PCR-based diagnostic testing) is accessible through local agricultural authorities, submit samples from suspect trees for confirmation, since visual symptoms alone can sometimes resemble nutrient deficiencies or other stresses.
- Keep records of suspect and confirmed cases by location within the orchard to help identify patterns of spread and inform vector control priorities.

## When to Consult an Agricultural Extension Officer or Plant Health Specialist
- Immediately upon suspecting Huanglongbing symptoms, given the severity of the disease and the fact that it is a regulated, reportable pest or disease in many countries requiring prompt notification of the relevant plant health authority.
- Before sourcing any new citrus planting material, budwood, or nursery stock, to confirm it is certified disease-free and sourced from an appropriate supplier.
- When planning psyllid control measures, to confirm which insecticide or biological control options are currently locally registered and recommended, and to coordinate timing with area-wide management efforts where these exist.
- If HLB is confirmed on a farm, to receive guidance on tree removal protocols, replanting timelines, and ongoing vector management for the surrounding orchard.
- When establishing a new citrus orchard in a region with known HLB presence or psyllid populations, to obtain tailored advice on site selection, variety choice, and a proactive vector management plan before planting.
