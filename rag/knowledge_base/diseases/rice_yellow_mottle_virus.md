# Rice Yellow Mottle Virus

## Disease Name
Rice Yellow Mottle Disease (RYMD)

## Scientific Name
Caused by Rice yellow mottle virus (RYMV), a *Sobemovirus*. RYMV is endemic to Africa and is not currently known to occur outside the continent, making it a distinctly African rice disease of major regional importance.

## Crops Affected
- Rice (*Oryza sativa*, the Asian rice species widely cultivated across Africa, and *Oryza glaberrima*, African rice), both of which are susceptible, though susceptibility varies by variety.
- Wild rice relatives, including *Oryza barthii*, *Oryza longistaminata*, and other wild *Oryza* species, as well as certain wild grasses such as *Echinochloa* species, can host the virus and act as reservoirs in and around rice-growing areas.

## Cause
Infection by Rice yellow mottle virus, a virus that is not seed-borne to any significant degree and cannot be controlled through curative chemical treatment once a plant is infected. The virus is transmitted mechanically and by certain insect vectors, and it persists in the landscape through infected wild grasses, ratoon (regrowth) rice, and volunteer plants between main cropping seasons.

## Symptoms
- Yellow to orange mottling or streaking of leaves, often appearing first as a mild mottled pattern that can be easily overlooked in the early stages.
- Leaf discoloration can range from a mild yellow-green mottle to pronounced yellow-orange streaking as infection progresses.
- Stunted plant growth, with infected plants noticeably shorter than healthy neighbouring plants.
- Reduced tillering (fewer stems per plant) compared with healthy plants.
- Partial to complete sterility of panicles (grain heads), with poor grain fill and reduced or empty grains in severely affected plants.
- Symptom severity and yield impact are generally greatest when infection occurs early in the plant's growth (seedling to early tillering stage); later infection tends to cause milder symptoms and less yield loss.
- Symptom expression can vary considerably between rice varieties and virus strains, with some combinations showing only mild symptoms despite significant underlying infection.

## Conditions Favouring Infection
- Continuous or intensive rice cultivation, particularly in irrigated and lowland systems where rice is grown in multiple overlapping cropping cycles, providing a constant host supply for vectors and mechanical spread.
- Proximity to wild rice relatives and grass weed reservoirs (such as *Oryza barthii*, *Oryza longistaminata*, and *Echinochloa* species) in and around fields, bunds, and irrigation channels.
- High populations of insect vectors, including certain beetles (such as chrysomelid beetles) and grasshoppers, which can build up where continuous host plants are available.
- Farming practices that involve significant plant-to-plant contact or shared tools/equipment between fields, which favour mechanical transmission.
- Water management practices that allow contaminated irrigation water to move between fields.
- Grazing of livestock through infected fields followed by grazing in healthy fields, which can mechanically transfer the virus on animals.

## How It Spreads
- Transmitted by insect vectors, most notably certain leaf-feeding beetles (including chrysomelid beetles) and grasshoppers, which acquire the virus while feeding on infected plants and transmit it to healthy plants.
- Spread mechanically through plant-to-plant contact, contaminated tools, equipment, hands, and clothing during routine field operations such as weeding, thinning, and harvesting.
- Contaminated irrigation water can carry the virus between plants and between fields sharing the same water source.
- Grazing animals moving through infected fields can mechanically transfer the virus to other fields on their bodies.
- Wild rice relatives and grass weed hosts serve as a reservoir that maintains the virus in the environment between rice cropping seasons, from which vectors or mechanical contact can reintroduce it into newly planted rice.
- Seed transmission is not considered a significant pathway for this virus, in contrast to some other rice diseases.

## Risk Level
- High in many irrigated and lowland rice-growing areas of sub-Saharan Africa, where RYMV is considered one of the most economically important rice diseases due to its wide distribution and potential for severe yield loss.
- Yield losses can range from minor to complete crop failure depending on the timing of infection (with early infection being most damaging), the susceptibility of the variety grown, and the prevailing virus strain, since RYMV strain diversity varies by region.
- Because the virus is endemic and reservoir hosts are widespread in many rice-growing landscapes, background risk tends to persist across seasons rather than being a one-off outbreak event.

## Treatment Options
- There is no curative chemical treatment for plants once infected with Rice yellow mottle virus; management is entirely preventive and relies on resistant varieties combined with cultural and sanitation practices.
- Insecticide use to control insect vectors may reduce transmission in some situations, but since mechanical transmission through routine field activities is also a major pathway, insecticides alone will not fully prevent spread and should not be relied upon as a standalone solution.
- Roguing (removal and destruction) of infected plants identified early in the season, particularly in nursery or early transplanted fields, can help reduce the source of infection available for further spread within a field.
- Confirm any locally recommended vector management approaches, including whether insecticide use is advised and which products are currently registered, with a national agricultural authority or extension officer.

## Prevention
- Plant resistant or tolerant rice varieties where available; several rice varieties and breeding lines with documented resistance to RYMV (including sources of resistance associated with the *rymv1* resistance gene and others) have been developed and released in various African rice-growing countries, and using locally confirmed resistant varieties is the most effective long-term strategy.
- Control wild rice relatives and grass weed hosts in and around fields, bunds, and irrigation channels to reduce reservoir populations that maintain the virus between seasons.
- Manage volunteer rice and ratoon (regrowth) rice plants between cropping cycles, since these can harbour the virus and continue the infection cycle.
- Clean tools, equipment, and hands between working in different fields, particularly when moving from a field with known infection to a healthy field.
- Avoid allowing livestock to graze through infected fields and then move directly into healthy rice fields without a break.
- Where possible, coordinate planting timing within a community to reduce continuous availability of susceptible young rice plants to vector populations across the season.
- Practice good field sanitation, including managing irrigation water sources to reduce the risk of contaminated water spreading the virus between fields.

## Monitoring Recommendations
- Scout fields regularly from the seedling stage through tillering, since early infection causes the most severe symptoms and yield impact, and early detection allows for timely roguing.
- Look for mottled yellow-orange leaf discoloration, stunted growth, and reduced tillering compared with surrounding healthy plants.
- Pay particular attention to field edges, bunds, and areas near irrigation channels or wild grass reservoirs, where initial infection often appears first.
- Monitor for insect vector populations (beetles, grasshoppers) as an indicator of transmission risk, particularly in fields near known reservoir hosts.
- Keep records of affected areas within fields and across seasons to help identify persistent reservoir sources and inform sanitation priorities.

## When to Consult an Agricultural Extension Officer or Plant Health Specialist
- If mottled yellowing, stunting, or reduced tillering consistent with RYMV is observed, particularly if it is unclear whether the cause is viral infection, nutrient deficiency, or another disease.
- Before selecting a rice variety for planting, to confirm which locally available varieties have documented resistance to RYMV strains present in the area.
- If infection appears widespread across a field or region, to discuss coordinated sanitation, reservoir host management, and vector control strategies with neighbouring farmers.
- When planning management of wild rice relatives, volunteer rice, or ratoon growth around fields, to get guidance suited to the local landscape and cropping calendar.
- If yield losses attributed to RYMV recur across seasons despite preventive measures, to review the overall management approach and consider additional resistant variety options.
