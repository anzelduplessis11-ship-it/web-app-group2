# Wheat Yellow Rust

## Disease Name
Wheat Yellow Rust (also known as Wheat Stripe Rust)

## Scientific Name
Caused by *Puccinia striiformis* f. sp. *tritici*, a basidiomycete (rust) fungus. Numerous physiological races exist, and new races capable of overcoming resistance genes or adapting to warmer temperatures have emerged periodically, contributing to periodic large-scale epidemics across wheat-growing regions, including the East African highlands.

## Crops Affected
- Bread wheat (*Triticum aestivum*) and durum wheat (*Triticum durum*).
- Triticale, which can also become infected.
- Barley is affected by a related forma specialis (*P. striiformis* f. sp. *hordei*), which is a distinct pathogen population from the wheat-infecting form.
- Certain wild and cultivated grasses can act as alternative hosts, though wheat is the primary economic host in most African production systems.
- Barberry (*Berberis* spp.) has been confirmed as an alternate host for the sexual stage of the fungus in some regions, contributing to new race development.

## Cause
Infection by the fungus *Puccinia striiformis* f. sp. *tritici*, an obligate biotroph that requires living wheat tissue to grow and reproduce. The pathogen produces yellow-orange urediniospores during the repeating (asexual) stage responsible for epidemic development within a season, and black teliospores later in the season. Because the fungus favours cooler conditions than stem rust, it is often a greater concern in cooler highland areas and during cooler growing seasons. Once a spore lands on a susceptible leaf and moisture and temperature conditions are suitable, the fungus penetrates the leaf, establishes itself within the tissue, and begins producing new pustules within one to two weeks under favourable conditions, allowing several disease cycles to occur within a single growing season and enabling explosive epidemic development once conditions are consistently favourable.

## Symptoms
- Bright yellow to orange pustules arranged in narrow, well-defined stripes running parallel to the leaf veins, giving the disease its "stripe rust" name.
- Stripe patterns are most distinct on leaves; in severe infections, pustules can also appear on leaf sheaths, glumes, and awns.
- Infected leaves often show a powdery yellow-orange coating that rubs off easily onto skin or clothing when touched.
- Older, more established stripes may fade to a lighter colour as the tissue around them dies (necrosis).
- Severely infected leaves yellow prematurely and die, reducing the photosynthetic area available for grain fill.
- Infection during early growth stages (tillering to stem elongation) tends to cause the greatest yield loss, as it reduces the plant's capacity to establish tillers and fill grain.
- Grain from severely affected plants is often shrivelled and lightweight due to reduced photosynthesis and disrupted nutrient movement.

| Feature | Yellow Rust | Leaf Rust (for comparison) |
|---|---|---|
| Pustule colour | Yellow-orange | Orange-brown |
| Pustule arrangement | Narrow stripes along veins | Scattered, circular |
| Location | Mainly leaves, can extend to sheaths/glumes | Mainly leaf blades |
| Temperature preference | Cooler (optimum ~10-15°C) | Warmer (optimum ~15-22°C) |

## Conditions Favouring Infection
- Cool temperatures, with an optimum development range of approximately 7–15°C; disease development slows considerably above 20–25°C, although some newer races show increased tolerance to warmer temperatures.
- High humidity or extended periods of leaf wetness from dew, fog, or light rain.
- Cool nights combined with mild, humid days, which is common in high-altitude wheat-growing areas and during cooler seasons.
- Dense canopy and lush growth from excessive nitrogen fertilisation, which can prolong leaf wetness and provide more susceptible tissue.
- Wide cultivation of susceptible varieties across large contiguous areas, especially where a matching virulent race is present.

## How It Spreads
- Urediniospores are dispersed primarily by wind and can travel very long distances, similar to stem rust, enabling rapid regional and even international spread of the disease and of new races.
- Because the pathogen thrives at cooler temperatures, spores can also survive and be transported effectively at higher altitudes and during cooler seasons, extending the geographic and seasonal window for spread compared with stem rust.
- Volunteer wheat plants and off-season self-sown wheat can maintain inoculum between main cropping seasons in areas where temperatures remain suitable.
- Local spread within a field occurs through wind and rain splash, with the disease commonly starting in patches and expanding outward.
- Movement of infected plant material or contaminated equipment between fields or regions can also contribute to spread, though wind-borne transport dominates over long distances.

## Risk Level
- High, particularly in cooler highland wheat-growing areas of East and southern Africa where temperature conditions closely match the pathogen's optimum range.
- Periodic emergence of new, more aggressive or warm-tolerant races has caused severe epidemics in areas previously considered lower risk.
- Yield losses can range from moderate to complete crop failure in susceptible varieties under sustained favourable conditions, particularly when infection occurs early in the season.
- Because of rapid wind-borne spread, an outbreak in one area can quickly threaten wheat crops across a wider region within the same season.

## Treatment Options
- Foliar fungicides from the triazole (DMI) and strobilurin (QoI) chemical classes are effective when applied preventively or at early disease onset; combination products using both modes of action are often recommended to help manage fungicide resistance risk.
- Early and well-timed fungicide application is important, since yellow rust can spread and intensify rapidly under cool, humid conditions, and control becomes more difficult once infection is widespread.
- Repeat applications may be necessary during prolonged periods of cool, wet weather, especially from tillering through grain fill.
- Fungicides protect healthy tissue and slow disease spread but do not reverse damage to already-infected leaves.
- Confirm currently registered fungicide active ingredients, rates, and application timing with a national plant protection authority or local agricultural extension officer, as recommendations and product registrations differ by country and change as new races emerge.

## Prevention
- Plant resistant or tolerant wheat varieties as the most effective and sustainable management strategy, ensuring the resistance genes used are effective against races currently present locally.
- Where seed of multiple resistant varieties is available, consider growing more than one variety across a farm or community rather than relying on a single genetic background, to reduce the risk that one new race can affect an entire area's crop in a single season.
- Avoid relying on a single resistant variety across very large areas for consecutive seasons, since this raises the risk of a new virulent race overcoming resistance and spreading widely.
- Avoid excessive nitrogen application, which promotes dense, susceptible canopy growth; base fertiliser rates on soil testing and crop needs.
- Destroy volunteer and self-sown wheat plants between seasons, particularly in cooler areas where the pathogen may otherwise survive between cropping cycles.
- Where feasible, avoid planting highly susceptible varieties in known high-risk cool, humid zones or at high altitude without confirming resistance status first.
- Participate in regional rust surveillance and early-warning systems where available, since new races can appear and spread with little local warning.

## Monitoring Recommendations
- Scout fields at least weekly from tillering through grain fill, paying particular attention during cool, humid weather or after periods of fog or light rain.
- Check lower and middle canopy leaves first, as stripe rust often establishes there before moving to upper leaves.
- Look specifically for narrow yellow-orange stripes running along leaf veins, and confirm by lightly rubbing a suspected pustule to check for the characteristic powdery yellow spore mass.
- Record the extent and severity of infection across the field to guide fungicide timing decisions.
- Monitor regional rust surveillance reports and alerts where available, since yellow rust can move into new areas rapidly on wind currents.
- Pay special attention at field edges and in low-lying, shaded, or poorly drained patches within a field, since these areas retain moisture longer and often show the first signs of infection before the disease becomes visible elsewhere.
- Where a crop has previously been affected on the same land, prioritise early-season scouting from the seedling stage, since local carryover inoculum from volunteer wheat can lead to earlier-than-expected disease onset.

## When to Consult an Agricultural Extension Officer or Plant Health Specialist
- As soon as yellow-orange striping is observed on leaves, given how quickly the disease can spread under favourable cool, humid conditions.
- Before choosing a wheat variety for planting, to confirm which resistance genes remain effective against races currently active in the local area.
- If disease continues to spread despite fungicide treatment, as this may indicate a new or resistance-breaking race requiring specialist diagnosis and a revised management approach.
- When symptoms appear unusually early in the season or across an unusually wide area, so the outbreak can be reported to regional surveillance networks.
- To confirm currently registered and effective fungicide products, rates, and timing before beginning a spray programme.
