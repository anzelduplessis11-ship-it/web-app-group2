# Bean Anthracnose

## Disease Name
Bean Anthracnose

## Scientific Name
Caused by the fungus *Colletotrichum lindemuthianum*.

## Crops Affected
- Common bean (*Phaseolus vulgaris*) is the primary host (see `crops/common_beans.md`).
- Some other legumes can be affected to a lesser degree, but common bean is the crop of main concern.

## Cause
Infection by the fungus *Colletotrichum lindemuthianum*, which is carried on and in seed and survives in infected crop debris, and spreads under cool, wet conditions.

## Symptoms
- Dark brown to black, sunken lesions (cankers) on stems, leaf stalks (petioles), and the underside veins of leaves.
- Angular dark lesions along the veins on the underside of leaves.
- Small, dark, sunken, circular spots with slightly raised darker borders on the pods; these lesions may ooze pinkish spore masses in humid conditions.
- Seeds from infected pods may be discoloured with brown or black blemishes and can carry the fungus to the next crop.
- Severity depends on variety, weather, and the level of seed and debris infection.

## Conditions Favouring Infection
- Cool to moderate temperatures combined with wet, humid conditions and prolonged leaf and pod wetness.
- Frequent rain, heavy dew, or overhead irrigation that keeps foliage wet and splashes spores.
- Dense planting that restricts airflow and keeps the canopy humid.
- Use of infected seed and the presence of infected bean debris from previous crops.

## How It Spreads
- Primarily introduced through infected seed, which is the main way the disease enters a new field.
- Spread within a crop by rain splash, overhead irrigation, wind-driven rain, and movement of people, animals, and tools through wet foliage.
- Survives between crops in infected plant debris left in or on the soil.
- Spread is greatly reduced under dry conditions, when spores are not readily splashed or produced.

## Risk Level
- Moderate to high in cool, wet growing seasons and where infected seed and crop debris are present.
- Lower in hot, dry conditions that are unfavourable to the fungus.
- Losses can range from minor pod blemishing to substantial yield and seed-quality loss under prolonged wet weather on susceptible varieties.

## Treatment Options
- Protectant fungicide sprays can help limit spread when applied early and during favourable (cool, wet) weather, but are most effective as part of a preventive programme rather than as a cure for heavily infected crops.
- Remove and destroy infected plant debris after harvest to reduce carryover, ideally by burial or burning rather than composting.
- Avoid working in the crop when foliage is wet, to reduce spread of spores from plant to plant.
- Confirm currently approved and locally registered fungicide active ingredients with a national agricultural regulatory authority or extension officer, since registered products vary by country.

## Prevention
- Use certified, disease-free seed, since infected seed is the main source of the disease.
- Plant resistant or tolerant varieties where available and suited to local conditions.
- Rotate away from beans for at least two to three seasons in affected fields to allow debris to break down.
- Ensure adequate plant spacing and, where possible, avoid overhead irrigation to reduce leaf wetness.
- Do not save seed from infected crops for planting.
- Practice good field sanitation, removing and destroying bean debris after harvest.

## Monitoring Recommendations
- Scout fields regularly during cool, wet weather, focusing on stems, leaf undersides, and developing pods for characteristic dark, sunken lesions.
- Inspect seed lots before planting for discoloured or blemished seed that may indicate infection.
- Pay particular attention to fields with a history of anthracnose or where beans have been grown repeatedly.
- Monitor weather for cool, wet periods that favour the disease so that preventive action can be timed.

## When Farmers Should Consult an Agricultural Extension Officer or Plant Health Specialist
- If characteristic lesions appear and are spreading, to confirm the diagnosis and decide whether a fungicide programme is warranted.
- Before selecting seed or a fungicide programme, to confirm certified seed sources and locally registered products and correct timing.
- If the disease recurs season after season despite management, to review rotation, seed sourcing, and variety choice.
- If uncertain whether symptoms are due to anthracnose versus another bean disease, since some leaf and pod spots can look similar.
