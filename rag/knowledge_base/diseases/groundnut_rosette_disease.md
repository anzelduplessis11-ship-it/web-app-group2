# Groundnut Rosette Disease

## Disease Name
Groundnut Rosette Disease (GRD)

## Scientific Name
Caused by a complex of three agents that must occur together: Groundnut rosette virus (GRV), its associated satellite RNA, and Groundnut rosette assistor virus (GRAV). All three are needed for the disease and its transmission.

## Crops Affected
- Groundnut, also called peanut (*Arachis hypogaea*), is the primary and effectively only significant host crop of economic concern.
- A few wild legumes can host the agents, but groundnut is the crop mainly affected.

## Cause
Infection by the groundnut rosette disease complex, transmitted by aphids (see `pests/aphids.md`), principally the groundnut aphid (*Aphis craccivora*). The disease is not seed-transmitted, so it does not spread through groundnut seed.

## Symptoms
- Two main forms are recognised: "chlorotic rosette" (yellowing of leaves) and "green rosette" (leaves remain green but are distorted).
- Stunting of plants with shortened stems, giving a bushy, clustered "rosette" appearance of the foliage.
- Small, distorted, and curled leaflets.
- Plants infected early are severely stunted and produce few or no pods; later infection has a milder effect.
- Symptom type and severity can vary with the strain of the complex, the variety, and the growth stage at infection.

## Conditions Favouring Infection
- High populations of the groundnut aphid vector, which build up under warm, dry conditions.
- Late planting and sparse or gappy plant stands, which are more attractive to migrating aphids and associated with higher disease.
- Continuous groundnut cropping and the presence of infected volunteer groundnut plants or wild legume hosts nearby.
- Early-season aphid migration into young crops, especially where planting is staggered across a region.

## How It Spreads
- Spread exclusively by aphid vectors; all three components of the complex must be acquired for the disease to be transmitted to a healthy plant.
- Not spread through seed, soil, or mechanical contact (tools, hands).
- Regional spread is influenced by aphid migration, weather, and the availability of infected host plants between seasons.

## Risk Level
- High in seasons and areas with large aphid populations and susceptible varieties, and can occur as damaging localised or regional outbreaks.
- Early-season infection is the most damaging and can cause severe to near-total loss of pod yield in affected plants.
- Risk varies significantly by season and planting date and is difficult to predict without local monitoring of aphid populations.

## Treatment Options
- There is no curative treatment for infected plants; management is entirely preventive.
- Early-season control of the aphid vector can reduce spread but does not cure already-infected plants and is only one part of management.
- Remove and destroy severely infected plants and volunteer groundnuts early where practical, to reduce sources of infection for aphids.

## Prevention
- Plant resistant or tolerant varieties where available and suited to local conditions, as resistance is a key management tool for this disease.
- Plant early and at a recommended, dense spacing to establish a full, uniform stand that is less attractive to migrating aphids and escapes early infection.
- Avoid late planting and gappy stands where possible.
- Control the groundnut aphid vector through field monitoring and, where necessary, approved insecticides, in coordination with local extension guidance.
- Remove volunteer groundnut plants and manage wild legume hosts near fields.

## Monitoring Recommendations
- Scout young groundnut crops weekly for early stunting, leaf yellowing or distortion, and clustered rosette growth.
- Monitor aphid populations on young plants, since early aphid buildup increases disease risk.
- Pay particular attention to late-planted fields and to sparse or gappy areas of the crop.
- Track regional outbreak reports where available, since aphid populations and disease can build up rapidly.

## When Farmers Should Consult an Agricultural Extension Officer or Plant Health Specialist
- If rosette symptoms appear on young plants or across a significant part of a field, to confirm the diagnosis and decide on management for the current and future crops.
- If symptoms appear across a wide area, suggesting a regional outbreak that may benefit from coordinated action.
- Before selecting a variety or planting strategy for future crops, to confirm which resistant or tolerant varieties and planting dates perform well locally.
- If uncertain whether symptoms are due to groundnut rosette disease, nutrient deficiency, or another cause, since visual diagnosis alone can be inconclusive in mild cases.
