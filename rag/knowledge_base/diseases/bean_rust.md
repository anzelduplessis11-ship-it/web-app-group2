# Bean Rust

## Disease Name
Bean Rust

## Scientific Name
Caused by the fungus *Uromyces appendiculatus* (synonym *Uromyces phaseoli*), an autoecious rust fungus capable of completing its entire life cycle on a single host species.

## Crops Affected
- Common bean (*Phaseolus vulgaris*) is the primary host and the crop of main economic concern (see `crops/common_beans.md`).
- Lima bean (*Phaseolus lunatus*) and some other *Phaseolus* species can be affected, generally to a lesser degree than common bean.
- The fungus is not known to cause significant disease on unrelated legumes such as cowpea, soybean, or groundnut, each of which has its own distinct rust pathogen.

## Cause
Bean rust is caused by infection of bean foliage, pods, and stems by the fungus *Uromyces appendiculatus*. The fungus can produce up to five different spore stages during its life cycle, but the reddish-brown urediniospores are responsible for the rapid, repeating infection cycles that drive epidemics within and between seasons.

## Symptoms
- Small, round, white to pale yellow raised spots appear first on leaves, usually on the underside surface, and enlarge over several days.
- These spots develop into powdery, reddish-brown to cinnamon-brown pustules (uredinia) that rupture the leaf surface and release masses of spores; a narrow yellow halo often surrounds each pustule.
- Pustules occur mainly on the underside of leaves but can also appear on the upper surface, on leaf stalks (petioles), on stems, and occasionally on pods in severe cases.
- Numerous pustules can merge, causing large areas of leaf tissue to turn yellow, dry out, and die.
- Heavily rusted leaves drop prematurely, and severe, early defoliation reduces the leaf area available for pod filling, resulting in smaller pods, fewer seeds, and lighter seed weight.
- Late in the season, or as pustules age, the fungus may switch to producing dark brown to black teliospores (telia), giving older pustules a darker appearance.
- Symptom severity depends on variety susceptibility, weather conditions, and how early in the crop cycle infection begins.

## Conditions Favouring Infection
- Moderate temperatures, generally in the range of 17 to 27 degrees Celsius, with an optimum around 20 to 25 degrees Celsius.
- High relative humidity and the presence of free moisture, such as dew, light rain, or overhead irrigation, on leaf surfaces for roughly six to eight hours, which is needed for spores to germinate and infect.
- Dense plant canopies and close spacing that restrict airflow and prolong leaf wetness and humidity within the crop.
- Overhead irrigation, which wets foliage and can substitute for rainfall in maintaining favourable humidity levels.
- Susceptible bean varieties grown without a protective fungicide programme in humid or highland growing areas.

## How It Spreads
- The main mechanism of spread is windborne urediniospores, which can travel considerable distances between fields and even between regions, allowing the disease to appear in fields with no obvious local source of infection.
- Rain splash and wind-driven rain move spores over shorter distances within and between neighbouring plants.
- The fungus survives between cropping seasons on volunteer bean plants, infected crop debris, and, in some regions, on living bean plants grown continuously or out of the main season.
- Bean rust is generally not considered an important seed-borne disease, unlike bean anthracnose or halo blight, so seed is a minor pathway compared with airborne spread.
- Movement of infected plant material, workers, and equipment through wet, rusted foliage can spread spores locally, but this is secondary to wind dispersal as the main driver of epidemics.

## Risk Level
- Moderate to high in humid tropical, subtropical, and highland bean-growing regions where conditions favour repeated infection cycles.
- Can develop into damaging epidemics under sustained warm, humid weather, particularly on susceptible varieties.
- Yield losses of 20 to 30 percent are commonly reported under severe, poorly managed epidemics, with losses considerably higher in extreme cases where infection occurs early and defoliation is severe.
- Risk is lower in dry growing seasons and regions, and on resistant varieties.

## Treatment Options
- Foliar fungicides can give effective control when applied early, at the first sign of pustules, and continued at appropriate intervals through the susceptible growth stages.
- Effective active-ingredient classes include triazole (DMI) fungicides, strobilurin (QoI) fungicides, and multi-site protectants such as chlorothalonil; rotating between different modes of action helps manage the risk of fungicide resistance developing.
- Fungicide applications are most effective as a preventive or early-curative measure; once a crop has widespread heavy defoliation, spraying gives little further benefit.
- Removing and destroying severely infected crop debris after harvest helps reduce carryover of inoculum for future crops in the same area.
- Confirm currently registered fungicide active ingredients, rates, timing, and pre-harvest intervals for bean rust in your country with a national agricultural regulatory authority or extension officer before purchasing or applying any product.

## Prevention
- Grow resistant or tolerant bean varieties where available and suited to local conditions, since host resistance is one of the most reliable and economical controls available.
- Rotate away from beans for at least one to two seasons where rust has been a recurring problem, to reduce local carryover of the fungus.
- Remove and destroy volunteer bean plants and infected crop debris after harvest.
- Avoid excessively dense planting and, where practical, avoid overhead irrigation to reduce leaf wetness duration.
- Plant on time according to local planting calendars to avoid the crop's most susceptible growth stages coinciding with peak humid periods.
- Where multiple bean plantings overlap across a district, coordinate timing with neighbours where possible, since wind-borne spores move readily between nearby fields.

## Monitoring Recommendations
- Scout fields regularly from flowering onward, checking the underside of lower and middle leaves first, since pustules typically appear there before spreading upward through the canopy.
- Walk a zig-zag path through the field, examining plants at multiple points rather than only at the edges.
- Estimate the proportion of leaf area affected and monitor whether pustules are spreading toward the upper canopy, which signals rising risk to yield.
- Monitor weather conditions, since warm, humid spells with extended leaf wetness indicate an elevated risk period requiring closer scouting.
- Watch for regional rust outbreak reports where available, since windborne spread means rust can appear rapidly across a wider area.
- Keep records of which varieties and fields showed heavy rust pressure to guide future variety selection and field rotation.

## When to Consult an Agricultural Extension Officer or Plant Health Specialist
- When rust pustules are first observed, to confirm the diagnosis and to help decide whether a fungicide programme is warranted given the variety, growth stage, and weather outlook.
- Before purchasing or applying any fungicide, to confirm the locally registered active ingredient, correct dose, application timing, and pre-harvest interval.
- Before selecting a bean variety for planting, to confirm which varieties have documented resistance suited to the local area.
- If rust develops rapidly or reaches high severity despite management, to reassess the control programme.
- If uncertain whether the symptoms are bean rust or another bean leaf disease, such as angular leaf spot or anthracnose, since some lesions can be confused without close inspection.
