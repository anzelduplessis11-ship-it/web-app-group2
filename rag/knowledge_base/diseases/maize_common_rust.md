# Common Rust of Maize

## Overview
Common rust is a widespread fungal disease of maize (*Zea mays*) recognised by the many small, reddish-brown, powdery pustules that break out on both surfaces of the leaves. It occurs across Africa but is most damaging in the cooler, higher-altitude and highland maize-growing zones of eastern and southern Africa, where mild temperatures and heavy dew favour the fungus. In most seasons and on most modern varieties common rust causes only minor to moderate leaf damage and limited yield loss. However, on susceptible varieties, on late-planted crops, and under prolonged cool, moist, cloudy weather, rust can build up heavily before grain fill and cause yield losses that in bad cases reach 20 percent or more. Because resistant varieties are widely available and effective, and because most crops tolerate low levels of rust, chemical control is only occasionally justified for smallholders. Common rust is often confused with the more damaging and quarantine-significant tropical rusts, so correct identification matters. See `crops/maize.md` for general maize agronomy.

## Scientific Name
Common rust of maize is caused by the fungus *Puccinia sorghi*. Despite the species name, its main economic host is maize; the "sorghi" name reflects its historical association with an alternate host and not sorghum as a major maize-rust host. It should not be confused with *Puccinia polysora* (southern or tropical rust), which produces lighter orange pustules mainly on the upper leaf surface and can be more damaging in hot lowland areas, nor with *Physopella zeae* (tropical rust). Correct identification of the rust species affects how much concern is warranted.

## Crops Affected
- Maize (*Zea mays*) is the primary host (see `crops/maize.md`).
- Both grain maize and green maize are affected, as well as sweetcorn, which is often more susceptible.
- *Puccinia sorghi* requires an alternate host (species of *Oxalis*, wood sorrel) to complete its full sexual life cycle, but in warm maize regions it survives and spreads mainly by repeatedly infecting maize, so the alternate host is of limited practical concern to most farmers.

## Cause
Common rust is caused by infection of maize leaves by the fungus *Puccinia sorghi*. In tropical and subtropical Africa the fungus survives between crops on volunteer maize and living maize plants, and its spores (urediniospores) are carried long distances on the wind. When a rust spore lands on a maize leaf that stays wet from dew or rain, and temperatures are mild, the spore germinates and the fungus penetrates the leaf. About one to two weeks later, pustules break through the leaf surface and release thousands of new reddish-brown spores, which are blown to other leaves and plants to start new infections. Repeated cycles through the season can cover leaves with pustules under favourable weather.

## Symptoms
Symptoms progress with plant growth and with the age of each infection:

- **Early infection (vegetative stage):** The first sign is small, chlorotic (pale yellow) flecks on the leaf, which are easy to overlook. These appear on both the upper and lower leaf surfaces.
- **Pustule formation:** Within days the flecks develop into raised, oval to elongated pustules (uredinia) that rupture the leaf surface. Mature pustules are cinnamon- to reddish-brown and powdery; rubbing a leaf leaves a rust-coloured stain on the fingers or on clothing. A key diagnostic feature of common rust is that pustules appear roughly equally on **both** the upper and lower leaf surfaces, and they are scattered over the whole leaf.
- **Mid-season:** Pustules may be numerous on the lower and middle leaves. On susceptible plants they can be very dense, sometimes arranged in bands. Heavily infected leaves may turn yellow and dry prematurely.
- **Late season (grain fill):** As the crop matures, the reddish-brown pustules are gradually replaced by darker, brownish-black pustules (telia) as the fungus switches to producing its overwintering spores. Severely rusted leaves die early, reducing the leaf area available for grain filling and, in bad cases, leading to lighter grain.

Look-alike caution: southern (tropical) rust caused by *Puccinia polysora* produces smaller, more circular, lighter orange to tan pustules that are concentrated on the **upper** leaf surface, and it is favoured by hot, humid lowland conditions. Distinguishing the two matters because tropical rust can be more aggressive; when in doubt, consult a specialist.

## Conditions Favouring Infection
- Cool to mild temperatures, generally 16 to 25 degrees Celsius; common rust is favoured by cooler weather than most other maize foliar diseases.
- High humidity and long periods of leaf wetness from dew, mist, or light rain (six or more hours of leaf wetness strongly favours infection).
- Cloudy, overcast weather and cool nights with heavy morning dew, typical of highland and high-altitude maize zones.
- Late-planted crops and off-season irrigated maize that grow during cooler, moister periods.
- Susceptible varieties and sweetcorn.
- Continuous availability of green maize and volunteer plants that let the fungus persist and provide a spore source.

## How It Spreads
- The main means of spread is airborne urediniospores carried by wind, often over long distances between fields and even between regions.
- Rain splash and physical contact move spores over short distances within a field.
- The fungus persists between seasons on living maize, off-season and irrigated maize, and volunteer plants, providing the source of spores for the next crop.
- Because spread is by wind-blown spores rather than by seed or residue, rust can appear suddenly in a field even where maize was not grown the previous season.
- Overlapping maize plantings across a district maintain a "green bridge" that keeps the fungus alive and spreading year-round.

## Risk Level
**Low to Moderate** in most situations, rising to **High** only under specific conditions. Justification: on the widely grown, moderately resistant maize varieties, and under normal weather, common rust usually stays at levels the crop tolerates with little yield effect, so it is generally a lower-risk disease than grey leaf spot or northern leaf blight. Risk becomes High when a susceptible variety or sweetcorn is grown, when planting is late so that grain fill coincides with cool, wet, cloudy weather, or when heavy rust develops before flowering. The disease is rarely of Very High risk in maize when resistant varieties are used. Farmers should nonetheless watch it closely because misidentification (confusing it with the more damaging tropical rust) can lead to either under- or over-reaction.

## Treatment Options
- **Usually no chemical control is needed.** For most smallholder crops grown from resistant or moderately resistant varieties, common rust does not reach levels that justify spraying, and the cost of fungicide would exceed the benefit.
- **When control is justified** (a susceptible variety or sweetcorn, heavy early rust before tasselling, high-value crop, and favourable cool-moist weather), foliar fungicides give good control. Effective active-ingredient classes include triazole (DMI) fungicides and strobilurin (QoI) fungicides, and pre-mixes of the two. Applied preventively or at first appearance, they protect the upper leaves through grain fill.
- **Timing:** protect the crop from before tasselling through early grain fill, focusing on keeping the ear leaf and the leaves above it healthy. Late applications on already-blighted leaves give little return.
- **Resistance management:** avoid repeated sole use of strobilurins; alternate modes of action as advised locally.
- **Safety and legal note:** do not rely on product brand names. Confirm the currently registered and approved fungicide active ingredients for maize in your country, the correct dose, the pre-harvest interval, and safe handling with a qualified agricultural extension officer before purchasing or applying anything.

## Prevention
- **Grow resistant or tolerant varieties.** This is the primary and most economical control. Many maize hybrids and open-pollinated varieties carry effective rust resistance; ask extension services for varieties suited to your altitude.
- **Plant at the recommended time.** Avoid very late planting that pushes grain fill into cool, wet, cloudy conditions ideal for rust. Follow local planting calendars.
- **Remove volunteer maize** and manage overlapping plantings to reduce the "green bridge" that keeps the fungus alive between crops in a district.
- **Avoid growing highly susceptible sweetcorn** in cool, humid, high-altitude sites without a plan to manage rust.
- **Maintain balanced plant nutrition and spacing** so plants are vigorous and the canopy is not excessively humid.
- **Coordinate with neighbours where possible**, since wind-blown spores move between fields; district-wide use of resistant varieties reduces the overall spore load.

## Monitoring Recommendations
- Begin scouting from the mid-vegetative stage, and watch more closely during cool, cloudy, dewy weather.
- Walk a zig-zag or "W" path through the field and check plants at several points, not only the edges.
- Examine both the upper and lower surfaces of the lower and middle leaves for the small reddish-brown, powdery pustules; the presence of pustules on both surfaces helps confirm common rust.
- Estimate how much of the leaf area is covered and whether rust is climbing toward the ear leaf before flowering, which is the main trigger for considering action.
- Distinguish common rust (reddish-brown, both surfaces, scattered) from tropical rust (lighter orange, mainly upper surface, hot lowlands); if the rust looks unusual or very aggressive, get it identified.
- Note weather trends: prolonged cool, moist, cloudy spells raise the risk and warrant closer scouting.
- Keep season notes on which varieties and fields showed heavy rust to inform variety choice next season.

## When to Consult an Agricultural Extension Officer or Plant Health Specialist
- When you are unsure whether the rust is common rust (*Puccinia sorghi*) or the more damaging tropical/southern rust (*Puccinia polysora*), since the two need different levels of concern.
- When heavy rust develops before tasselling on a susceptible or high-value crop and you are deciding whether spraying is justified.
- Before buying or applying any fungicide, to confirm the locally registered active ingredient, dose, timing, pre-harvest interval, and safe handling.
- Before selecting a variety, to confirm which rust-resistant maize varieties are recommended for your altitude and season.
- If rust is unusually severe or appears alongside other leaf diseases, to plan an integrated response and rule out a more serious problem.
- If the same fields suffer heavy rust year after year, to review planting dates, variety choice, and volunteer-maize management.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: My maize leaves have reddish-brown powder that rubs off on my hands. Is this serious?**
A: That powder is rust spores, most likely common rust. On its own, moderate common rust on a resistant variety is usually not serious and rarely needs spraying. It becomes a concern if it develops heavily before flowering, on a susceptible variety, or during prolonged cool, wet weather. Estimate how much leaf is affected and whether it is reaching the ear leaf, and consult an extension officer if it is spreading fast.

**Q: How is common rust different from the tropical rust people warn about?**
A: Common rust (*Puccinia sorghi*) produces cinnamon to reddish-brown pustules scattered on both the upper and lower leaf surfaces and prefers cooler, high-altitude conditions. Tropical or southern rust (*Puccinia polysora*) produces smaller, lighter orange pustules mostly on the upper leaf surface and prefers hot, humid lowlands, and it can be more aggressive. If your rust is orange and mostly on top of the leaf in a hot area, get it identified by a specialist.

**Q: Will common rust spread to my sorghum?**
A: The maize common rust fungus does not cause significant rust on sorghum; sorghum has its own rust caused by a different fungus. So common rust in your maize is not a direct threat to a neighbouring sorghum crop (see `crops/sorghum.md`).

**Q: Do I need to remove infected leaves or plants?**
A: Removing individual rusted leaves is not practical or necessary, because the disease spreads by wind-blown spores from many sources. The more useful actions are growing a resistant variety, planting on time, and removing volunteer maize that keeps the fungus alive between crops.

**Q: Can I still eat and sell maize from a rust-infected crop?**
A: Yes. Common rust affects the leaves, not the grain directly, and does not make the grain unsafe to eat or sell. Its only effect on yield is through loss of leaf area, which can make grain lighter in severe cases. It does not produce the toxins associated with some ear rots.

**Q: Why did rust appear even though I did not grow maize here last season?**
A: Common rust spreads mainly by wind-blown spores that can travel long distances from other fields and regions. Because it does not depend on local residue or seed, it can appear in a field regardless of what was grown there before, especially when there is maize elsewhere in the district and the weather turns cool and moist.

**Q: Should I spray at the first sign of a few pustules?**
A: Usually no. A few pustules on the lower leaves of a resistant variety are normal and tolerable. Spraying is only worth considering when rust builds up heavily before flowering on a susceptible or high-value crop under favourable cool, wet conditions. Discuss the decision, and the correct registered product, with an extension officer rather than spraying automatically.
