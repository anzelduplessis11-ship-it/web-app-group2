# Maize Streak Virus Disease

## Disease Name
Maize Streak Disease (MSD)

## Scientific Name
Caused by Maize streak virus (MSV), a *Mastrevirus* in the family Geminiviridae.

## Crops Affected
- Primarily maize (*Zea mays*).
- Also affects other grasses and cereals including sorghum, sugarcane, and wild grass species, which serve as alternative hosts/reservoirs.

## Cause
Maize streak virus, transmitted exclusively by leafhopper insects of the genus *Cicadulina* (notably *Cicadulina mbila*). The virus is not seed-borne and cannot spread through mechanical contact, soil, or seed.

## Symptoms
- Narrow, broken, pale yellow to white streaks running parallel to leaf veins.
- Streaks are most visible on younger leaves and become more pronounced as the plant matures.
- Severely affected plants show stunted growth, reduced cob size, and poor grain fill.
- Infection at an early growth stage (seedling to 4-leaf stage) causes the most severe yield loss; later infection has a milder effect.

## Conditions Favouring Infection
- High populations of *Cicadulina* leafhopper vectors, often linked to warm, dry conditions following rain.
- Continuous maize cropping or proximity to grass weed hosts that harbour the virus and vector between seasons.
- Early-season leafhopper migration into young maize fields, especially where planting is staggered across a region, providing continuous host availability for the vector.

## How It Spreads
- Spread exclusively by leafhopper vectors; the virus is acquired when leafhoppers feed on infected plants and is transmitted persistently when they feed on healthy plants.
- Not spread through seed, soil, water, or mechanical means (e.g., tools, hands).
- Regional spread is influenced by leafhopper migration patterns, wind, and the presence of grass weed reservoirs.

## Risk Level
- High in regions with a history of leafhopper outbreaks and susceptible maize varieties.
- Can cause total crop loss in severe early-season outbreaks on susceptible varieties.
- Risk varies significantly by season and is difficult to predict without local monitoring of leafhopper populations.

## Treatment Options
- There is no curative treatment for virus-infected plants; management is entirely preventive.
- Insecticide seed treatments or early-season foliar insecticides can reduce leafhopper populations and delay/reduce infection, but do not cure already-infected plants.
- Remove and destroy severely infected plants early in the season where practical, to reduce the source of infection for vectors.

## Prevention
- Use maize varieties with documented tolerance or resistance to maize streak virus, especially in high-risk regions.
- Plant early and uniformly within a community/region to reduce the window of vulnerable young plants available to migrating leafhoppers.
- Control grass weeds in and around fields that serve as alternative hosts for both the virus and the vector.
- Consider insecticide seed treatment in areas with a known history of severe outbreaks.

## Monitoring Recommendations
- Scout young maize plants (up to 6 weeks old) weekly for characteristic streak symptoms and for leafhopper presence.
- Monitor field margins and grass weed populations as potential vector reservoirs.
- Track regional outbreak reports where available, since leafhopper populations can build up rapidly following favourable weather.

## When Farmers Should Consult an Agricultural Extension Officer or Plant Health Specialist
- If more than 10-15% of young plants show streak symptoms, especially before the 6-leaf stage.
- If symptoms appear across a wide area, suggesting a regional outbreak that may require coordinated vector management.
- If uncertain whether symptoms are due to maize streak virus versus nutrient deficiency or another disease, since visual diagnosis alone can be inconclusive in early or mild cases.
- Before selecting a resistant variety for future planting, to confirm which varieties have documented resistance performance in the local area.
