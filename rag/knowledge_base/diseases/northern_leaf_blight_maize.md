# Northern Leaf Blight of Maize

## Overview
Northern leaf blight (NLB), also called northern corn leaf blight or turcicum leaf blight, is a major fungal foliar disease of maize (*Zea mays*) across the moderate-temperature, higher-rainfall maize zones of Africa. It is recognised by large, elongated, cigar- or canoe-shaped grey-green to tan lesions on the leaves. When these lesions develop heavily on the upper leaves before and during grain fill, the loss of green leaf area sharply reduces photosynthesis and grain filling, and yield losses of 30 to 50 percent are possible on susceptible varieties in favourable seasons. NLB is especially important in the mid-altitude and highland maize belts of eastern and southern Africa, where mild temperatures, frequent rain, and heavy dew occur. Like grey leaf spot, it survives on maize residue and is worsened by continuous maize and reduced tillage. It is managed most reliably through resistant varieties, crop rotation, and residue management, with fungicides used only when a high-value, susceptible crop faces heavy pressure. See `crops/maize.md` for general agronomy.

## Scientific Name
Northern leaf blight is caused by the fungus *Exserohilum turcicum* (asexual stage; also written *Helminthosporium turcicum*). Its sexual stage is known as *Setosphaeria turcica*. The fungus exists as different races, which vary in their ability to overcome specific resistance genes in maize; this is why a variety that resists NLB in one area may show some disease where a different race is present.

## Crops Affected
- Maize (*Zea mays*) is the primary economic host (see `crops/maize.md`).
- Both grain and green maize are affected, and sweetcorn is often quite susceptible.
- *Exserohilum turcicum* also infects sorghum (`crops/sorghum.md`) and some other grasses, but the races that attack maize and sorghum are largely specialised, so maize NLB is chiefly a maize concern. Nonetheless, grassy hosts and volunteer maize can help the fungus survive.

## Cause
NLB is caused by infection of maize leaves by *Exserohilum turcicum*. The fungus survives between seasons in and on infected maize residue (leaves and stalks) left on the soil surface, and on some grassy hosts. In warm, humid, wet weather it produces dark spores (conidia) on this residue. Rain splash and wind carry the spores onto the lower leaves of a new crop. On a leaf that stays wet for many hours, the spore germinates and the fungus enters the leaf, then grows within it and kills the tissue, producing the characteristic long lesions. New spores form on these lesions, especially on their undersides, and are spread to higher leaves and neighbouring plants, driving repeated cycles of infection up the plant and across the field through the season.

## Symptoms
Symptoms progress with plant age and the age of each lesion:

- **Early infection (vegetative stage):** The first symptoms are small, oval, grey-green, water-soaked spots on the lower leaves. These are subtle and can resemble the early stage of other leaf diseases.
- **Characteristic lesions (from late vegetative through flowering):** The spots enlarge into the diagnostic NLB lesions: long, elliptical, cigar- or canoe-shaped lesions, typically 3 to 15 cm long, grey-green at first and becoming tan or straw-coloured as they mature. Unlike grey leaf spot, NLB lesions are **not** confined by the leaf veins; they spread across veins and have rounded, tapering ends. This large, boat-shaped, vein-crossing form is the key identification feature.
- **Sporulation:** As lesions mature, their surfaces (especially the undersides) may take on a dusty, dark grey-green to olive appearance during humid weather as the fungus produces spores. In the field a "dirty" grey cast on the lesion under morning dew is a useful clue.
- **Grain-filling stage (the damaging phase):** Under favourable weather, lesions multiply and merge, blighting large sections of leaf and moving up toward the ear leaf and above. When the upper leaves are killed during grain fill, the plant cannot fill grain properly. Severely blighted plants look scorched or "burned," dry prematurely, and produce light, poorly filled ears.
- **Late stage:** Heavy leaf loss can trigger the plant to remobilise stalk reserves into the grain, weakening the stalk and increasing the risk of stalk rot and lodging (plants falling over).

Scouting tip: distinguish NLB (large, cigar-shaped, grey-green to tan, crossing veins) from grey leaf spot (narrow, rectangular, vein-limited) and from common rust (small raised pustules). NLB lesions are the largest of the common maize leaf lesions.

## Conditions Favouring Infection
- Moderate temperatures, generally 18 to 27 degrees Celsius; NLB prefers slightly cooler and wetter conditions than some other foliar diseases.
- Long periods of leaf wetness (six or more hours, and ideally longer) from dew, mist, or rain, together with high humidity.
- Frequent rain, overcast weather, and heavy dew, typical of mid-altitude and highland maize zones.
- Fields with a history of maize and abundant surface residue (minimum or zero tillage, continuous monocropping).
- Susceptible varieties, and races of the fungus able to overcome the resistance in the variety being grown.
- Dense, humid canopies and sheltered, low-lying fields where leaves stay wet.

## How It Spreads
- The fungus survives from season to season mainly in infected maize residue on the soil surface, and to a lesser extent on grassy hosts and volunteer maize; this residue is the primary source of first infections.
- Spores produced on residue and on lesions are carried by wind and rain splash to nearby leaves and plants; wind can move spores between neighbouring fields and over longer distances.
- The disease progresses up the plant and across the field over several weeks as each generation of lesions produces more spores.
- Continuous maize cropping and retention of infected residue on the surface allow the pathogen to build up over successive seasons.
- The fungus is not an important seed-borne pathogen, so residue and continuous cropping, not seed, are the key drivers of spread.

## Risk Level
**High** in the mid-altitude and highland maize belts of eastern and southern Africa, and **Very High** where susceptible varieties are grown continuously under reduced tillage in cool, wet seasons. Justification: NLB attacks during the yield-critical grain-fill period, kills large areas of leaf quickly under favourable weather, builds up strongly in continuous-maize systems, and can remove more than a third of the yield on susceptible varieties. Risk is lower (Moderate to Low) in hot, dry areas, in dry seasons with little leaf wetness during grain fill, and where resistant varieties are grown in rotation with good residue management.

## Treatment Options
- **Integrated management first.** For smallholders the most reliable and affordable control is a resistant variety combined with rotation and residue management; fungicides are a supplement for high-value, susceptible crops under heavy pressure.
- **Fungicide classes.** Where use is justified and affordable, foliar fungicides protect the upper leaves. Active-ingredient classes effective against NLB include triazole (DMI) fungicides, strobilurin (QoI) fungicides, and pre-mixes combining the two. They work best applied preventively or at the first sign of disease.
- **Timing.** The key window is from just before tasselling to early grain fill, protecting the ear leaf and the leaves above it. Fungicide applied after the upper canopy is already heavily blighted gives little return.
- **Resistance management.** Avoid repeated sole use of strobilurins; alternate or mix modes of action as advised locally, and note that varietal (genetic) resistance is the most durable tool.
- **Safety and legal note.** Never rely on brand names. Confirm the currently registered and approved fungicide active ingredients for maize in your country, the correct dose, the pre-harvest interval, and safe handling with a qualified agricultural extension officer before buying or spraying.

## Prevention
- **Grow resistant varieties.** This is the most cost-effective control. Many maize hybrids and open-pollinated varieties carry NLB resistance, including both major-gene (race-specific) and partial (quantitative) resistance; partial resistance tends to be more durable. Ask extension services for varieties suited to your altitude and known to resist local races.
- **Rotate crops.** Rotate maize with non-host crops such as beans (`crops/common_beans.md`), groundnuts (`crops/groundnut.md`), or other legumes for at least one season to reduce the residue-borne spore source.
- **Manage residue.** Where soil conservation allows, incorporate, bury, or compost infected maize residue to speed decomposition. Where residue must stay on the surface for erosion control, rely more heavily on resistant varieties and rotation.
- **Avoid continuous maize** on the same land, especially under minimum tillage.
- **Control volunteer maize and grassy hosts** in and around fields.
- **Use appropriate plant spacing** to avoid an overly dense, humid canopy, following local recommendations.
- **Plant at the recommended time** and maintain balanced fertility so plants are vigorous through grain fill.

## Monitoring Recommendations
- Begin scouting from the mid-vegetative stage and increase frequency from tasselling onward, the critical period for yield.
- Walk a "W" or zig-zag pattern and inspect plants at several points, not just field edges.
- Examine lower and middle leaves first for the early grey-green oval spots, then track how far up the plant the large cigar-shaped lesions have reached.
- Estimate the number of lesions per leaf and whether they have reached the ear leaf; the ear leaf and the two leaves above it contribute most to grain filling and are the priority to protect.
- Watch the weather: extended cool, wet, cloudy, dewy spells raise the risk and should trigger closer scouting and earlier decisions.
- Scout most intensively in fields with a maize history, heavy surface residue, and susceptible varieties.
- Keep simple season notes on which fields and varieties suffered most, to guide rotation and variety choices next season.

## When to Consult an Agricultural Extension Officer or Plant Health Specialist
- When you find large cigar-shaped lesions and are unsure whether it is northern leaf blight, grey leaf spot, or another problem (see `diseases/grey_leaf_spot_maize.md` and `diseases/maize_common_rust.md`).
- Before selecting a variety, to confirm which NLB-resistant maize varieties are recommended for your altitude and are effective against the races in your area.
- Before buying or applying any fungicide, to confirm the locally registered active ingredient, dose, timing, pre-harvest interval, and safe handling.
- When lesions reach the ear leaf before or early in grain fill on a susceptible, high-value crop, to decide whether a fungicide application is economically justified.
- If a variety that was resistant begins to show significant NLB, since this may indicate a shift in the local fungal race and a need to change varieties.
- If severe leaf blighting is followed by stalk rot and lodging, to plan an early harvest and adjust next season's management.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: How do I tell northern leaf blight from grey leaf spot?**
A: Northern leaf blight lesions are large (often 3 to 15 cm), cigar- or canoe-shaped, grey-green to tan, with rounded tapering ends, and they cross the leaf veins. Grey leaf spot lesions are narrow, rectangular, and strictly limited by the veins, giving a boxy look. If the lesion is a big boat-shaped patch that crosses veins, it is likely NLB; if it is a narrow rectangle bounded by veins, it is likely grey leaf spot.

**Q: A variety that used to resist blight is now getting it. Why?**
A: *Exserohilum turcicum* has several races that differ in their ability to overcome specific resistance genes. A new race in your area can overcome the major resistance gene in your variety, so the variety shows disease. Varieties with partial (quantitative) resistance are more durable. Ask extension services about varieties resistant to the races present locally.

**Q: Will northern leaf blight spread from my maize to my sorghum?**
A: The fungus can infect sorghum, but the races that attack maize and sorghum are largely specialised, so maize NLB does not usually cause serious disease in a neighbouring sorghum crop. Both crops do share the general principle that clean residue management and rotation reduce leaf diseases (see `crops/sorghum.md`).

**Q: The lesions look "dirty" and dusty in the morning. What is that?**
A: Under humid, dewy conditions the fungus produces dark spores on the surface of mature lesions, especially on the underside, giving them a dusty grey-green to olive cast. Seeing this "dirty" sporulation on large cigar-shaped lesions is a helpful confirmation of northern leaf blight and a sign the disease is actively producing spores.

**Q: Is it worth spraying if blight is only on the lower leaves at tasselling?**
A: It can be. Blight on the lower leaves at tasselling, on a susceptible high-value crop during wet, cool weather, is an early warning that it may move up. Protecting the ear leaf and upper leaves around tasselling to early grain fill can preserve yield. Once the upper leaves are already heavily blighted, spraying gives little benefit. Confirm the decision and the registered product with an extension officer.

**Q: Does the disease make the maize grain unsafe to eat?**
A: No. Northern leaf blight attacks the leaves, not the grain, and does not make the grain unsafe. Its damage is through loss of green leaf area, which reduces grain filling and can lower yield. It is not one of the mould diseases that produce grain toxins; for those see `diseases/maize_ear_rots.md`.

**Q: What is the single best step to reduce blight next season?**
A: Grow a variety with good, ideally partial, resistance to NLB, and rotate away from maize for a season while managing the infected residue from this crop. This low-cost combination removes the host and reduces the residue-borne spore source, and it does not depend on spraying.
