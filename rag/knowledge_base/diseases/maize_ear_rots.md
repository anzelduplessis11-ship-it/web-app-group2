# Maize Ear Rots

## Overview
Maize ear rots are fungal diseases that rot the developing and mature cob (ear) of maize (*Zea mays*). They matter for two reasons: they reduce the quantity and quality of grain, and, most seriously, several of the fungi produce mycotoxins, poisonous chemicals that make grain unsafe for people and livestock. This is a critical food-safety issue for African smallholders, who often store and consume their own maize. The most important ear rots in Africa are Fusarium ear rot and Gibberella ear rot (both caused by *Fusarium* species, producing fumonisins and other toxins), Aspergillus ear rot (caused by *Aspergillus flavus*, producing aflatoxins, the most dangerous mycotoxin group), and Diplodia ear rot (caused by *Stenocarpella* species, producing diplodiatoxin). Aflatoxin contamination in particular has been linked to acute poisoning outbreaks and to chronic liver damage, stunting in children, and immune suppression. Ear rots are managed through resistant varieties, good agronomy, insect control, timely harvest, rapid drying, and clean, dry storage. See `crops/maize.md`, and for storage safety see `storage/` and `crops/maize.md` harvest guidance.

## Scientific Name
Maize ear rots are caused by several unrelated fungi:
- **Fusarium ear rot:** *Fusarium verticillioides* (formerly *F. moniliforme*) and *Fusarium proliferatum*, which produce fumonisin toxins.
- **Gibberella ear rot:** *Fusarium graminearum* (sexual stage *Gibberella zeae*), which produces deoxynivalenol (DON, "vomitoxin") and zearalenone.
- **Aspergillus ear rot:** *Aspergillus flavus* and *Aspergillus parasiticus*, which produce aflatoxins.
- **Diplodia ear rot:** *Stenocarpella maydis* (formerly *Diplodia maydis*) and *Stenocarpella macrospora*, which produce diplodiatoxin.

## Crops Affected
- Maize (*Zea mays*) is the principal host for all of these ear rots (see `crops/maize.md`).
- *Aspergillus flavus* also infects groundnuts and produces aflatoxin in them, so aflatoxin management is shared across maize and groundnut systems (see `crops/groundnut.md` and `diseases/groundnut_leaf_spots.md`).
- *Fusarium* species affect other cereals and can be associated with stalk rots in maize, but the cob is the focus for food safety.

## Cause
Ear rots are caused by fungi that infect the ear during and after flowering. Infection routes include:
- Fungal spores from crop residue, soil, and air landing on the silks and growing down into the ear during and after silking.
- Fungi entering through wounds made by insects (such as maize stalk borer and other ear-feeding pests; see `pests/maize_stalk_borer.md` and `pests/fall_armyworm.md`), by birds, or by hail and mechanical damage.
- Fungi already present on the plant colonising the kernels, especially when the crop is stressed.
Once inside the ear, the fungi grow on and between the kernels, rotting them and, for the toxin-producing species, contaminating the grain with mycotoxins. Toxin production is greatly increased by stress: *Aspergillus* and *Fusarium* toxins build up under hot, dry, drought-stressed conditions around and after flowering, while *Gibberella* and *Diplodia* are favoured by cool, wet conditions during grain fill and a wet period before harvest. Delayed harvest, poor drying, and damp storage allow further fungal growth and toxin accumulation after the crop is physiologically mature.

## Symptoms
Symptoms differ by fungus, and recognising which rot is present helps judge the toxin risk:

- **Fusarium ear rot:** Scattered or grouped kernels with a white to pinkish, cottony mould, often on kernels damaged by insects or on scattered kernels across the ear rather than a single block. Infected kernels may show whitish streaks called "starburst" symptoms radiating over the kernel cap. The rot is often patchy.
- **Gibberella ear rot:** A distinctive reddish-pink to red mould that usually starts at the tip of the ear and grows down toward the base, often affecting a continuous block of kernels. In severe cases the husk sticks tightly to the ear and the whole tip is rotted red-pink. This red colour is a warning of DON and zearalenone.
- **Aspergillus ear rot:** An olive-green to yellow-green powdery mould on and between kernels, often near the ear tip or where insects have fed, and favoured by hot, dry, drought conditions. This green mould is the visible sign of possible aflatoxin, the most dangerous toxin, but dangerous aflatoxin levels can be present with little visible mould, so appearance alone is not a safe guide.
- **Diplodia ear rot:** A dense, white to greyish-white mould that typically starts at the base of the ear (near where it attaches to the stalk) and grows upward, often with tiny black fruiting bodies (pycnidia) later visible on the husk, kernels, or cob. Infected ears are lightweight, and the husks may be bleached and stuck to the ear.

Progression and field signs: ear rots become visible from the mid grain-fill stage onward and are most obvious near maturity. Early external clues include husks that are bleached, discoloured, or prematurely dry, ears that droop early, and mould visible when husks are peeled back. Insect tunnelling and bird damage on the ear are strongly associated with rot and toxins.

## Conditions Favouring Infection
- **For Aspergillus (aflatoxin) and much Fusarium (fumonisin):** hot, dry, drought-stressed conditions during and after flowering, high temperatures, and heat- and moisture-stressed plants; late-season drought is especially dangerous for aflatoxin.
- **For Gibberella and Diplodia:** cool, wet, humid conditions during grain fill, and prolonged wet or humid weather in the weeks before harvest.
- **Common to all:** insect damage to ears (borers, armyworm, earworms), bird and hail damage, upright ears that trap water, poor husk cover, high plant stress (drought, poor fertility, weed competition), delayed harvest, slow or inadequate drying, and damp storage.
- Continuous maize and heavy infected residue increase the inoculum of *Fusarium* and *Diplodia*.

## How It Spreads
- The fungi survive between seasons in maize residue and in the soil, and their spores are spread by wind, rain splash, and insects onto silks and ears.
- Insects that bore into stalks and ears both wound the ear and physically carry spores inside, so insect infestation strongly increases ear rot and toxin risk.
- Within storage, fungi already on the grain continue to grow and spread from kernel to kernel whenever the grain is too moist, and toxins accumulate further.
- Contaminated seed and infected residue can carry *Fusarium* and *Diplodia* into new crops.
- Aflatoxin and fumonisin risk moves through the whole chain from field to store; mixing mouldy grain with clean grain spreads contamination.

## Risk Level
**Very High**, primarily because of the food-safety hazard from mycotoxins, not only because of yield loss. Justification: aflatoxins from *Aspergillus flavus* are among the most toxic natural substances and are linked to acute, sometimes fatal, poisoning outbreaks in Africa as well as chronic liver disease, childhood stunting, and immune suppression; fumonisins and DON also cause serious human and animal health effects. Because smallholders often eat their own maize and store it under difficult conditions, contaminated grain poses a direct risk to family health. Even when visible rot is limited, invisible toxins can be present, and toxins are not destroyed by ordinary cooking. For yield alone the risk would be Moderate to High; the mycotoxin hazard raises the overall risk to Very High and makes prevention essential.

## Treatment Options
- **There is no cure for a rotted ear or for grain that already contains mycotoxins.** Management is prevention plus careful handling of the harvest; toxins cannot be removed by normal cooking, boiling, or fermentation.
- **In the field**, fungicides are generally not a practical or reliable control for ear rots in smallholder maize, and are not the main tool; some foliar fungicide classes used near silking (for example triazole fungicides) may reduce certain ear rots in high-value systems, but results are variable and this is not a substitute for good agronomy. Confirm any product with an extension officer.
- **Biological control** using non-toxin-producing (atoxigenic) strains of *Aspergillus flavus* applied to the soil has been developed and deployed in several African countries to reduce aflatoxin. Where such a product is officially available and recommended locally, use it strictly according to national guidance and only in combination with good agronomy.
- **At and after harvest** (the most important intervention): harvest promptly at maturity, do not leave the crop standing in wet or humid conditions, sort out and discard rotted, discoloured, insect-damaged, and mouldy cobs and kernels, dry grain quickly to a safe moisture (about 13 percent or lower for storage, and lower still for long storage), and store in clean, dry, well-ventilated, pest-proofed conditions. Hermetic (airtight) storage bags reduce insect activity and further mould growth.
- **Never feed heavily mouldy grain to livestock or eat it**, and keep suspect grain separate; consult authorities about testing where an aflatoxin problem is suspected.

## Prevention
- **Grow resistant or tolerant varieties** where available; some maize varieties have better husk cover and lower ear-rot and aflatoxin susceptibility. Ask extension services.
- **Control ear- and stalk-feeding insects** (stalk borer, fall armyworm, earworms) through integrated pest management, because insect wounds are a major entry point for rot fungi and toxins (see `pests/maize_stalk_borer.md` and `pests/fall_armyworm.md`).
- **Reduce plant stress:** plant on time, maintain balanced fertility, control weeds, and use appropriate spacing so that plants, especially at flowering and grain fill, are not drought- or nutrient-stressed. Where irrigation is available, avoiding drought stress around flowering strongly reduces aflatoxin.
- **Choose varieties with good husk cover and tight ears** and, where relevant, ears that dry facing downward, to shed rain and reduce ear rots.
- **Rotate with non-host crops** and manage infected residue to lower *Fusarium* and *Diplodia* inoculum.
- **Harvest promptly and dry rapidly** once the grain is mature; do not delay harvest into wet weather, and dry on clean, raised surfaces or cribs, never on bare soil.
- **Store clean, dry, sorted grain** in clean, dry, ventilated stores or hermetic bags, protect from insects and re-wetting, and monitor stored grain regularly. See storage guidance and `pests/grain_weevils_and_borers.md`.
- **Sort ruthlessly:** remove and discard mouldy, discoloured, and damaged cobs before shelling and before storage, and do not mix them with sound grain.

## Monitoring Recommendations
- Scout the crop through grain fill for early external signs: bleached or discoloured husks, ears drooping early, insect entry holes and frass on ears, and bird damage.
- From the dough stage to maturity, peel back husks on a sample of ears in several parts of the field and inspect for mould, noting its colour (pink/red, green, white) and where on the ear it starts, to judge which rot and toxin risk is present.
- Pay special attention to fields that suffered drought stress around flowering (aflatoxin/fumonisin risk) or wet weather during grain fill and before harvest (Gibberella/Diplodia risk).
- Track the number and proportion of ears with mould; a rising proportion signals a need to harvest and sort urgently.
- At harvest and during storage, keep monitoring for mould, heating, off-smells, insect activity, and re-wetting; check grain moisture where possible before and during storage.
- Keep records of which fields and seasons had ear-rot problems to guide variety choice, planting date, and insect management next season.

## When to Consult an Agricultural Extension Officer or Plant Health Specialist
- Whenever you see green mould (possible aflatoxin) or extensive red-pink or white ear rot, to get advice on safe handling and whether the grain should be tested or discarded.
- Before deciding whether maize with visible mould is safe for family food or livestock feed, since toxins can be present even where mould looks limited and are not removed by cooking.
- If people or animals become ill after eating maize (symptoms such as vomiting, feed refusal, jaundice, or, in severe acute aflatoxin poisoning, serious liver illness), seek medical or veterinary help urgently and report the suspected contamination.
- Before selecting varieties and planning insect control, to reduce ear-rot and toxin risk for the coming season.
- To ask about officially recommended aflatoxin biocontrol products or grain-testing services available in your country.
- If ear rots recur despite good practice, to review varieties, planting dates, drought stress, insect control, and storage.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: The tips of some cobs have a red-pink mould. Is the grain safe to eat?**
A: A reddish-pink mould, especially starting at the ear tip, suggests Gibberella ear rot, which can carry the toxins DON and zearalenone. Do not eat visibly mouldy grain and do not feed it to animals. Sort out and discard affected cobs, keep them separate from clean grain, and ask an extension officer about handling and testing. Toxins are not destroyed by cooking, so a mouldy cob cannot be made safe by boiling or roasting.

**Q: I saw green powdery mould on a few cobs. How dangerous is that?**
A: Olive-green to yellow-green mould points to *Aspergillus flavus*, which produces aflatoxins, the most dangerous mycotoxins. This is a serious food-safety concern. Remove and discard those cobs, do not mix them with sound grain, and seek advice about testing. Importantly, dangerous aflatoxin can be present even when there is little visible mould, so a clean look does not guarantee safety, especially after a drought-stressed season.

**Q: Can I cut off the mouldy part and eat the rest of the cob?**
A: This is not safe. Mycotoxins spread beyond the visible mould into apparently sound kernels, and they are invisible and not removed by cooking. It is far safer to discard the whole affected cob and any nearby grain. Rely on prevention, prompt harvest, thorough drying, and careful sorting rather than trying to salvage mouldy ears.

**Q: Why is aflatoxin worse in drought years?**
A: *Aspergillus flavus* thrives when maize is stressed by heat and drought around and after flowering, and stressed plants are less able to resist infection, so toxin builds up. This is why avoiding drought stress at flowering (through timely planting and, where possible, irrigation), controlling insects, and harvesting and drying promptly are so important for keeping aflatoxin low.

**Q: How dry must my maize be for safe storage?**
A: Grain should be dried to about 13 percent moisture or lower for storage, and lower still for long-term storage, then kept dry. Moist grain lets storage moulds keep growing and toxins keep accumulating. Dry on clean, raised surfaces or in a well-ventilated crib, never on bare soil, and store in clean, dry, pest-proofed conditions or hermetic bags. See storage guidance for details.

**Q: Do insects really cause ear rot?**
A: Yes, strongly. Insects such as stalk borers, fall armyworm, and earworms wound the ear and carry fungal spores inside, opening the door for *Fusarium*, *Aspergillus*, and *Diplodia* and greatly increasing toxin risk. Controlling these pests through integrated pest management is one of the most effective ways to reduce ear rots and mycotoxins (see `pests/maize_stalk_borer.md` and `pests/fall_armyworm.md`).

**Q: Is there a spray that cures ear rot?**
A: No spray reliably cures ear rot in smallholder maize, and no treatment removes toxins from contaminated grain. Management is prevention: resistant varieties with good husk cover, insect control, reduced plant stress, prompt harvest, rapid drying, careful sorting, and clean dry storage. Where officially available, aflatoxin biocontrol using atoxigenic *Aspergillus* strains can help, but only alongside good agronomy and under local guidance.
