# Maize Lethal Necrosis

## Disease Name
Maize Lethal Necrosis (MLN)

## Scientific Name
Caused by a co-infection of two viruses: Maize chlorotic mottle virus (MCMV, genus *Machlomovirus*) together with a cereal virus of the family Potyviridae, most commonly Sugarcane mosaic virus (SCMV). MLN develops when both viruses infect the same plant.

## Crops Affected
- Maize (*Zea mays*) is the primary and most seriously affected crop (see `crops/maize.md`).
- Other grasses and cereals (such as sorghum, sugarcane, and certain wild grasses) can host the individual viruses and act as reservoirs, though severe necrosis is mainly seen in maize.

## Cause
Simultaneous infection of maize by MCMV and a potyvirus (commonly SCMV). Neither virus alone causes the severe "lethal necrosis" syndrome; it is the combination that produces the most damaging disease.

## Symptoms
- Yellowing and mottling (chlorotic mottle) of leaves, often starting on younger leaves near the whorl.
- Necrosis (browning and death) of leaf tissue, sometimes progressing from leaf margins inward.
- Dead heart (death of the growing point) in severely affected young plants.
- Stunting, poor cob formation, and malformed or partially filled cobs.
- Premature drying and plant death in severe cases; symptom severity can vary with variety, growth stage at infection, and environmental conditions.

## Conditions Favouring Infection
- Presence of both viruses in an area together with their insect vectors.
- Continuous maize cropping and short or absent rotation, which maintain virus sources and vector populations across seasons.
- High populations of vector insects, including certain beetles, thrips, and aphids that can transmit the component viruses.
- Use of infected or poor-quality seed and proximity to infected fields or grass reservoirs.

## How It Spreads
- Spread by insect vectors: MCMV can be transmitted by several beetle and thrips species, while the potyvirus component is transmitted by aphids (see `pests/aphids.md`).
- Can be introduced or spread through contaminated seed for at least one of the component viruses, making seed source important.
- Mechanical transmission through contaminated tools, hands, and plant-to-plant contact is possible for some components.
- Regional spread is influenced by vector movement, cropping patterns, and the exchange of seed and green plant material between areas.

## Risk Level
- Very high. MLN is one of the most destructive maize diseases where it occurs and can cause severe to total yield loss on susceptible varieties under high disease pressure.
- Risk is greatest where continuous maize cropping, susceptible varieties, and active vectors coincide.
- Losses can range from moderate to near-total crop failure depending on variety, time of infection, and disease pressure.

## Treatment Options
- There is no curative treatment for virus-infected plants; management is entirely preventive.
- Insecticides may reduce vector populations but do not cure infected plants and are not a reliable standalone control.
- Remove and destroy infected plants early where practical, to reduce the source of infection for vectors.
- In severe outbreaks, coordinated area-wide management is more effective than the actions of a single farmer alone.

## Prevention
- Use certified, disease-free seed from a reputable source, since seed can carry component viruses.
- Plant varieties with documented tolerance or resistance to MLN where available and suited to local conditions.
- Rotate maize with non-cereal crops (for example legumes) to break the disease and vector cycle, and avoid continuous maize cropping.
- Practice a maize-free break period across a community where recommended locally, to reduce virus carryover.
- Control grass weeds in and around fields that can host the component viruses and vectors.
- Manage vector insects through field sanitation and, where necessary, approved insecticides, in coordination with local extension guidance.

## Monitoring Recommendations
- Scout maize fields regularly from the seedling stage onward for chlorotic mottle, necrosis, and stunted or dying plants.
- Pay particular attention to young plants and to plants near field margins and previous maize plots.
- Monitor for vector insects (aphids, thrips, and beetles) as part of routine scouting.
- Track regional outbreak reports where available, since MLN can build up and spread rapidly under favourable conditions.

## When Farmers Should Consult an Agricultural Extension Officer or Plant Health Specialist
- As soon as MLN is suspected, given the speed and severity of the disease and the importance of confirming the diagnosis.
- If symptoms appear across a wide area, suggesting a regional outbreak that may require coordinated management.
- Before sourcing seed for future planting, to identify reputable certified seed sources and resistant or tolerant varieties available locally.
- If uncertain whether symptoms are due to MLN versus nutrient deficiency, maize streak virus (see `diseases/maize_streak_virus.md`), or another cause, since visual diagnosis alone can be inconclusive.
