# Banana Xanthomonas Wilt

## Disease Name
Banana Xanthomonas Wilt (BXW), also known as banana bacterial wilt

## Scientific Name
Caused by the bacterium *Xanthomonas vasicola* pv. *musacearum* (previously referred to as *Xanthomonas campestris* pv. *musacearum*).

## Crops Affected
- Banana and plantain (*Musa* species and hybrids), including cooking, dessert, and beer types, are the hosts of concern.
- Enset (*Ensete ventricosum*), a close relative grown in some highland areas, can also be affected.

## Cause
Infection by the bacterium *Xanthomonas vasicola* pv. *musacearum*, which colonises the plant's water- and sap-conducting tissue and can enter through the male flower (following insect visits), through cutting tools, and through wounds.

## Symptoms
- Premature and uneven yellowing and wilting of leaves, which may collapse and hang down along the pseudostem.
- Yellowing or wilting of the bracts and shrivelling of the male bud on the flower stalk.
- Uneven, premature ripening of fruit within a bunch, with fruit often showing yellow-orange, discoloured internal flesh.
- A yellowish bacterial ooze that appears when the pseudostem, fruit stalk, or fruit is cut.
- Internal yellow to brown discolouration of the vascular tissue when the pseudostem is cut across. Whole mats (clumps of plants connected underground) can eventually be lost.

## Conditions Favouring Infection
- Presence of the bacterium in an area together with flowering plants that can be visited by insects.
- Use of contaminated cutting tools moved from plant to plant without cleaning.
- Warm conditions that favour bacterial multiplication and insect activity around the flowers.
- Densely grown, continuously cropped banana stands where the bacterium can move readily between plants and mats.

## How It Spreads
- Spread by insects that visit the male flowers, carrying bacteria between plants (an important reason to manage the male bud).
- Spread very readily on contaminated cutting tools (knives, machetes, pruning tools) used for de-suckering, de-budding, and harvesting.
- Spread through infected planting material (suckers) taken from diseased mats.
- Can also move in infected plant debris and, over distance, through the movement of infected suckers and fruit between areas.

## Risk Level
- Very high. BXW is one of the most serious threats to banana and plantain where it occurs, because it can kill entire mats and there is no chemical cure.
- Whole plantations can be severely affected if the disease is not managed quickly and consistently.
- Losses can range from the loss of individual plants to devastation of a farm's banana production under uncontrolled spread.

## Treatment Options
- There is no chemical cure for infected plants; management is based on sanitation, removing diseased material, and preventing spread.
- Cut down and destroy affected plants; a common recommendation is to remove diseased plants (and manage the surrounding mat) and leave the material to decompose in place rather than moving it, in line with local guidance.
- Remove the male bud (de-budding) using a forked stick rather than a cutting tool, to avoid tool contamination while reducing insect-borne spread.
- Disinfect all cutting tools between plants and between mats (for example by flaming or using a disinfectant), since contaminated tools are a major route of spread.

## Prevention
- Use clean planting material (suckers) only from healthy, disease-free mats, or use tissue-cultured plants where available.
- Remove the male bud early with a forked stick to reduce insect transmission through the flower.
- Disinfect cutting tools before moving from one plant or mat to the next.
- Promptly identify and remove diseased plants to stop spread within and between mats.
- Avoid moving suckers, fruit, or plant material from affected areas into disease-free areas.
- Practice good field sanitation and, where recommended locally, coordinate management across neighbouring farms, since the disease spreads between fields.

## Monitoring Recommendations
- Inspect banana mats regularly for early leaf yellowing and wilting, wilting of the male bud, and premature or uneven fruit ripening.
- Use the cut-stem test to check for yellowish bacterial ooze when the disease is suspected.
- Pay particular attention to plants at and after flowering, when insect-borne infection risk is high.
- Monitor neighbouring fields and be alert to disease reports in the surrounding area, since spread between farms is common.

## When Farmers Should Consult an Agricultural Extension Officer or Plant Health Specialist
- As soon as BXW is suspected, given how quickly and severely it can spread, to confirm the diagnosis and set up a control plan.
- If the disease is found, to get guidance on correct removal of diseased plants, tool disinfection, and de-budding practices for local conditions.
- If the disease appears in an area not previously affected, since early coordinated action is important to contain spread.
- Before sourcing suckers or tissue-cultured plants, to identify clean planting material sources and any resistant or tolerant options available locally.
