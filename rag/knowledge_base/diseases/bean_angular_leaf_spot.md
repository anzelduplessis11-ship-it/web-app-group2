# Bean Angular Leaf Spot

## Disease Name
Bean Angular Leaf Spot

## Scientific Name
Caused by the fungus *Pseudocercospora griseola* (synonym *Phaeoisariopsis griseola*).

## Crops Affected
- Common bean (*Phaseolus vulgaris*) is the primary host and the crop of main economic concern (see `crops/common_beans.md`).
- Lima bean (*Phaseolus lunatus*) can also be affected.
- The disease is of particular importance in humid tropical and subtropical bean-growing regions, including much of sub-Saharan Africa and Latin America.

## Cause
Angular leaf spot results from infection of bean tissue by the fungus *Pseudocercospora griseola*. The fungus is carried on and in seed and survives in infected crop debris between seasons, with disease developing and intensifying as favourable warm, humid weather persists through the growing season.

## Symptoms
- On leaves, lesions are typically angular in outline because their expansion is restricted by the leaf veins, giving a distinctive geometric, patchwork appearance rather than the rounder lesions typical of many other bean leaf spots.
- Lesions start as small, pale to grey-green spots that enlarge and turn grey-brown to brown as they mature.
- In humid conditions, a fine grey, fuzzy fungal growth (sporulation) can be seen on the underside of lesions, particularly in the early morning when humidity is highest.
- On pods, lesions are more rounded to irregular, reddish-brown with a darker margin, and can affect seed quality if pods are infected while developing.
- Lesions can also occur on stems and leaf stalks (petioles), appearing as elongated, dark streaks.
- Severely affected leaves turn yellow, dry out, and drop prematurely, and heavy defoliation, particularly if it occurs before or during pod fill, reduces both yield and seed quality.
- Symptom severity tends to increase through the season as successive infection cycles occur under favourable weather, often becoming most visible from mid-season onward.

## Conditions Favouring Infection
- Moderate to warm temperatures, generally in the range of 20 to 28 degrees Celsius.
- High relative humidity and extended periods of leaf wetness, whether from rainfall, heavy dew, or overhead irrigation.
- Alternating wet and dry conditions through the season, which can favour repeated infection cycles as the fungus sporulates during humid spells and disperses during drier, breezier periods.
- Dense canopies and close plant spacing that restrict airflow and prolong leaf wetness.
- Use of infected seed and the presence of infected crop debris from previous bean crops.
- Continuous or frequent bean cropping in the same fields without rotation.

## How It Spreads
- The fungus can be carried on and in seed, making infected seed an important pathway for introducing the disease into new fields and new growing areas.
- Airborne and rain-splashed conidia (asexual spores) spread the fungus from infected to healthy plants within and between fields during the growing season.
- The fungus survives between seasons in infected crop debris left on or near the soil surface.
- Volunteer bean plants emerging from the previous season's crop can also carry the fungus into a new planting.
- Movement of people, animals, tools, and equipment through wet, infected foliage can spread spores locally, though airborne and rain-splash dispersal remain the main drivers of within-field spread.

## Risk Level
- Moderate to high in humid tropical and subtropical bean-growing regions, including many parts of sub-Saharan Africa, where it is considered one of the more economically important bean foliar diseases.
- Losses vary widely depending on variety susceptibility, timing of infection, and weather, but severe epidemics can cause substantial yield loss through defoliation and reduced pod fill.
- Risk is lower in drier growing regions and seasons, and on resistant varieties grown from clean seed.

## Treatment Options
- Foliar fungicides can help limit disease spread when applied early and repeated according to a protective schedule during favourable, humid weather, though they are most effective as a preventive measure rather than a cure for heavily infected crops.
- Effective active-ingredient classes commonly used against fungal leaf spots of beans include chlorothalonil (a multi-site protectant) and triazole (DMI) fungicides.
- Seed treatment with an appropriate fungicide active ingredient can help reduce the introduction of the fungus via infected seed, where locally approved treated seed or seed-treatment products are available.
- Removing and destroying infected crop debris after harvest reduces carryover of inoculum to the next season.
- Confirm currently approved and locally registered fungicide active ingredients, seed treatments, rates, and timing with a national agricultural regulatory authority or extension officer, since registered products vary by country.

## Prevention
- Use certified, disease-free seed, since infected seed is an important source of the disease, particularly when introducing beans to a new field or area.
- Grow resistant or tolerant varieties where available and suited to local conditions.
- Rotate away from beans for at least two seasons in fields with a history of angular leaf spot, to allow infected debris to break down.
- Remove and destroy bean crop debris after harvest rather than leaving it on the soil surface.
- Ensure adequate plant spacing to promote airflow and reduce canopy humidity.
- Avoid overhead irrigation where practical, or irrigate in ways that allow foliage to dry quickly.
- Do not save seed from visibly infected crops for future planting.

## Monitoring Recommendations
- Scout fields regularly from mid-season onward, when symptoms often become most apparent, checking the underside of leaves for the characteristic angular lesions and grey sporulation in humid conditions.
- Walk a zig-zag path through the field, inspecting plants at multiple points rather than only at the field edges.
- Inspect seed lots before planting for any discoloration or blemishes that could indicate infection.
- Pay particular attention to fields with a history of angular leaf spot or where beans have been grown repeatedly without rotation.
- Monitor weather patterns, since alternating wet and humid spells signal periods of higher infection risk.
- Keep season records of severity by field and variety to guide future seed sourcing, variety choice, and rotation planning.

## When to Consult an Agricultural Extension Officer or Plant Health Specialist
- When characteristic angular lesions are observed and appear to be spreading, to confirm the diagnosis and determine whether a fungicide programme is warranted.
- Before selecting seed sources or planning a fungicide or seed-treatment programme, to confirm certified seed availability and locally registered products.
- If the disease recurs season after season despite management, to review seed sourcing, rotation practices, and variety choice.
- If uncertain whether symptoms are due to angular leaf spot or another bean disease, such as bean rust or anthracnose, since some leaf and pod lesions can appear similar without close inspection.
