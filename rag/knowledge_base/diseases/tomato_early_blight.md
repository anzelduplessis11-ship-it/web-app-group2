# Early Blight of Tomato

## Overview
Early blight is one of the most common and economically damaging fungal diseases of tomato grown by smallholder farmers across sub-Saharan Africa. Despite its name, it is not necessarily an early-season disease; the "early" refers to it appearing earlier in the season than late blight in some regions. It is primarily a disease of the leaves, stems, and fruit, and it is most destructive on plants that are already stressed by drought, poor nutrition, or a heavy fruit load. The disease reduces yield in two main ways: it destroys leaf area needed for photosynthesis, causing defoliation from the bottom of the plant upwards, and it causes rot on the fruit near the stem end, making produce unmarketable.

Early blight is favoured by the warm, humid conditions that are typical of many African tomato-growing seasons, especially where rain-fed and irrigated crops overlap and where tomato is grown intensively season after season on the same land. Because the pathogen survives in soil and crop debris, and because susceptible varieties are widely grown, early blight is a persistent problem rather than an occasional one. Effective control depends on an integrated approach that combines resistant or tolerant varieties, crop rotation, good field sanitation, balanced plant nutrition, and, only when justified, well-timed protectant fungicide use. This document should be read together with the tomato crop guide (`crops/tomato.md`) and the related disease guides for late blight (`diseases/tomato_late_blight.md`) and Septoria leaf spot (`diseases/septoria_leaf_spot_tomato.md`), which can look similar in the field.

## Scientific Name
Early blight of tomato is caused by the fungus *Alternaria solani*. A closely related species, *Alternaria tomatophila* (sometimes considered part of the *Alternaria* complex on tomato), causes very similar symptoms in some regions. For practical smallholder management, both are treated identically. The fungus belongs to a group known for producing dark, multi-celled spores (conidia) and for surviving well in dry plant debris.

## Crops Affected
- Tomato (*Solanum lycopersicum*) is the principal host and the crop of main concern (see `crops/tomato.md`).
- Irish potato (*Solanum tuberosum*) is also affected by *Alternaria solani* and can share inoculum where the two crops are grown near each other (see `crops/irish_potato.md`).
- Other members of the nightshade family, including eggplant (aubergine), sweet pepper, and hot pepper, can be affected to a lesser degree.
- Several solanaceous weeds, such as black nightshade, can host the fungus and act as a reservoir between crops.

Because tomato and potato share the pathogen, farmers should avoid planting the two crops next to each other or in immediate rotation.

## Cause
Early blight is caused by infection with the fungus *Alternaria solani*. The fungus survives between seasons in infected crop debris left in or on the soil, in the soil itself, on volunteer tomato and potato plants, on solanaceous weeds, and occasionally on or in seed. When conditions become warm and moist, the fungus produces large numbers of dark spores. These spores land on tomato leaves, stems, or fruit, and if the surface stays wet long enough, they germinate and penetrate the plant tissue, producing the characteristic spots. The fungus is a "weak" pathogen in the sense that it attacks stressed, ageing, or nutrient-deficient tissue most aggressively, which is why older lower leaves are affected first and why poorly nourished crops suffer more.

## Symptoms
Early blight symptoms appear on leaves, stems, and fruit, and they progress in a recognisable pattern:

- **Leaf spots:** The first and most diagnostic symptom is small, dark brown to black spots on the older, lower leaves. As the spots enlarge, they develop a pattern of dark concentric rings, giving a "target-board" or "bullseye" appearance. This concentric ringing is the key feature that distinguishes early blight from other leaf diseases.
- **Yellow halo:** A yellow zone (chlorotic halo) often surrounds each spot as the fungus releases a toxin that kills surrounding tissue.
- **Defoliation:** Heavily spotted leaves turn yellow, then brown, dry up, and die. Leaf loss progresses from the bottom of the plant upwards. Severe defoliation exposes fruit to sunburn (sunscald) and greatly reduces yield.
- **Stem lesions (collar rot):** On seedlings and young transplants, dark, sunken lesions can girdle the stem at or near the soil line, sometimes killing the plant. On older plants, elongated dark lesions with concentric rings can form on stems and leaf stalks.
- **Fruit rot:** Dark, leathery, sunken spots form at the stem end of the fruit, often with the same concentric ring pattern and sometimes with a velvety black covering of spores. Affected fruit may drop early.

## Conditions Favouring Infection
- Warm temperatures, typically between 24°C and 29°C, which are ideal for spore production and infection.
- Alternating wet and dry periods; heavy dew, light rain, fog, or overhead irrigation followed by warm days.
- Prolonged leaf wetness (several hours), which allows spores to germinate and penetrate.
- High humidity within a dense, poorly ventilated canopy.
- Plant stress from drought, waterlogging, heavy fruit load, or poor nutrition, especially nitrogen and potassium deficiency.
- Continuous or repeated cultivation of tomato or potato on the same land, which builds up soil and debris inoculum.

## How It Spreads
- Spores are carried by wind over short and moderate distances, allowing the disease to move within and between fields.
- Rain splash and splashing from overhead irrigation move spores from the soil and lower leaves onto upper foliage and neighbouring plants.
- Infected crop debris, volunteer plants, and solanaceous weeds provide the initial source of spores at the start of each season.
- People, animals, and tools moving through a wet crop can carry spores from plant to plant.
- The fungus can be carried on and in seed, so infected seed or seedlings can introduce the disease into a clean field or nursery.

## Risk Level
Moderate to high. Early blight rarely destroys a crop as suddenly as late blight, but under warm, humid conditions on susceptible varieties it can cause severe defoliation and fruit rot, with yield losses commonly ranging from 20 percent to more than 50 percent when uncontrolled. Because the fungus survives readily in soil and debris and thrives on stressed plants, it is a chronic, recurring threat wherever tomato is grown intensively. The risk is highest in the warm, wet parts of the season, in fields with a history of the disease, and where crop rotation and sanitation are neglected.

## Treatment Options
Management of an active outbreak should combine cultural measures with targeted, justified chemical use.

- **Remove affected tissue:** As soon as spots appear, pick off and remove badly infected lower leaves and destroy them by burial or burning away from the field. Do not leave infected material in the field or add it to compost that will return to the crop.
- **Reduce plant stress:** Ensure the crop is adequately and evenly watered and well nourished, because vigorous plants resist the fungus far better than stressed ones. Correct any nitrogen or potassium deficiency, guided by the soil and fertiliser guidance.
- **Improve airflow and reduce leaf wetness:** Stake and prune plants to open the canopy, and switch overhead watering to drip or furrow irrigation where possible so leaves dry quickly.
- **Protectant fungicides:** When the disease is developing and weather favours it, protectant (contact) fungicides can slow spread. Relevant active-ingredient classes include the multi-site protectant contact fungicides (for example the dithiocarbamate group and chlorothalonil-type contact fungicides) and copper-based products. Where disease pressure is high, systemic classes such as the strobilurins (QoI group) and triazoles (DMI group) are sometimes used, often in mixtures or rotations to reduce the risk of the fungus developing resistance. Fungicides work best applied preventively or at the very first signs, not once the crop is heavily infected.
- **Confirm products locally:** Farmers must confirm which specific products are currently registered, approved, and legal in their country, along with correct dosage, spray interval, and pre-harvest interval, with a qualified agricultural extension officer or plant health specialist. Product registration and recommendations vary between countries and change over time.
- **Rotate chemical groups:** If sprays are used repeatedly, alternate between different active-ingredient classes to reduce the chance of the fungus becoming resistant.

## Prevention
Prevention is far more effective and affordable than trying to cure a severe outbreak.

- **Resistant or tolerant varieties:** Choose tomato varieties described as tolerant or resistant to early blight where they are available and suited to local conditions and market preferences. This is the single most cost-effective measure.
- **Crop rotation:** Rotate away from tomato, potato, pepper, and eggplant for at least two to three seasons in affected fields, using unrelated crops such as cereals or legumes, to allow soil and debris inoculum to decline.
- **Field sanitation:** Remove and destroy crop debris after harvest, control volunteer tomato and potato plants, and remove solanaceous weeds that host the fungus.
- **Healthy seed and seedlings:** Use certified, disease-free seed and raise strong, healthy seedlings; inspect nursery plants and discard any showing collar rot or leaf spots before transplanting.
- **Spacing and staking:** Plant at the recommended spacing and stake plants to keep foliage off the ground and improve airflow, reducing leaf wetness.
- **Mulching:** Apply mulch to reduce rain splash of spores from the soil onto lower leaves and to conserve even soil moisture, which reduces plant stress.
- **Balanced nutrition and watering:** Maintain balanced fertility, especially adequate nitrogen and potassium, and provide even moisture to keep plants vigorous.
- **Watering practice:** Water early in the day and at the base of plants so foliage dries quickly, and avoid working among plants while they are wet.

## Monitoring Recommendations
- Scout fields at least once or twice a week, and more often during warm, humid weather, starting from the earliest growth stages.
- Focus first on the older, lower leaves, where the target-board spots appear first.
- Distinguish early blight (dark spots with concentric rings and a yellow halo) from Septoria leaf spot (many small spots with grey centres and dark borders, see `diseases/septoria_leaf_spot_tomato.md`) and from late blight (large water-soaked lesions with white growth on the underside, see `diseases/tomato_late_blight.md`).
- Check developing fruit, especially near the stem end, for early dark lesions.
- Keep simple records of where and when symptoms first appear, so that action can be timed and repeated problem areas identified.
- Watch weather patterns; periods of warm days with heavy dew or intermittent rain signal higher risk and the need for closer scouting.

## When to Consult an Agricultural Extension Officer or Plant Health Specialist
- When leaf spots first appear and the farmer is unsure whether the cause is early blight, Septoria leaf spot, late blight, or a nutritional problem, since management differs.
- Before buying or applying any fungicide, to confirm which products are locally registered and approved, and to obtain correct dosage, timing, spray interval, and pre-harvest interval.
- When the disease continues to spread despite spraying, which may indicate poor spray timing, the wrong product, or fungicide resistance.
- When planning crop rotation, variety selection, or nursery management to prevent recurring problems season after season.
- When symptoms appear alongside other problems, such as wilting or fruit disorders, that may indicate more than one disease is present.

## Frequently Asked Questions
**Q: How can I tell early blight apart from late blight?**
A: Early blight causes small dark spots with distinct concentric rings ("target-board" pattern) and yellow halos, appearing first on the older lower leaves during warm, humid weather. Late blight causes larger, irregular, water-soaked greyish-green lesions, often with white fuzzy growth on the leaf underside, and it spreads and destroys plants far more rapidly during cool, wet weather. See `diseases/tomato_late_blight.md` for details.

**Q: Does early blight stay in the soil?**
A: Yes. The fungus survives in infected crop debris, in the soil, on volunteer plants, and on related weeds between seasons. This is why crop rotation, removing debris, and controlling volunteers and solanaceous weeds are so important.

**Q: My lower leaves are dying but the top of the plant looks healthy. Is this early blight?**
A: It may well be. Early blight characteristically starts on the oldest, lowest leaves and moves upward. However, lower-leaf yellowing can also come from nitrogen shortage or natural ageing, so look closely for the dark concentric-ring spots that confirm early blight.

**Q: Can I save seed from an infected crop?**
A: It is not recommended. The fungus can be carried on and in seed, so saving seed from an infected crop risks introducing the disease into your next planting. Use certified, disease-free seed instead.

**Q: Will spraying fungicide cure my badly infected plants?**
A: No. Fungicides mainly protect healthy tissue and slow further spread; they do not repair leaves and fruit already damaged. They work best applied preventively or at the very first symptoms. Always confirm locally approved products and correct use with an extension officer.

**Q: Is early blight dangerous to eat?**
A: Early blight affects plant health and fruit quality but is not a human health hazard. However, badly rotted fruit should not be eaten or sold, and fruit treated with fungicide must observe the correct pre-harvest interval before harvest and consumption.

**Q: Why is early blight worse on my stressed plants?**
A: *Alternaria solani* attacks weakened, ageing, and poorly nourished tissue most aggressively. Drought, waterlogging, heavy fruit load, and nutrient deficiency all lower the plant's defences, so keeping plants well watered and well fed is a genuine control measure, not just good general practice.
