# Coffee Berry Disease

## Disease Name
Coffee Berry Disease (CBD), sometimes referred to as coffee anthracnose when leaf and blossom phases are involved.

## Scientific Name
Caused by the fungus *Colletotrichum kahawae* (formerly classified as a specialised, aggressive strain of *Colletotrichum coffeanum* or *Colletotrichum gloeosporioides*). It belongs to the family Glomerellaceae and is closely related to other *Colletotrichum* species that cause anthracnose diseases on many crops, but *C. kahawae* is distinguished by its ability to attack immature, green coffee berries directly.

## Crops Affected
- Arabica coffee (*Coffea arabica*) is the principal and most severely affected host; the disease is of major economic importance wherever Arabica is grown at moderate to high altitude.
- Robusta coffee (*Coffea canephora*) is generally far less susceptible and is rarely affected under field conditions.
- Related wild and ornamental *Coffea* species can occasionally show infection but are not of commercial concern.

## Cause
Coffee berry disease is caused by direct infection of green (unripe) coffee berries by the fungus *Colletotrichum kahawae*. The fungus penetrates the berry skin directly, without requiring a wound, and colonises the outer tissue before spreading into the pulp and, in advanced cases, the bean itself. The same fungus can also infect blossoms, young shoots, and leaves, although berry infection is what causes the greatest economic damage.

## Symptoms
- **Green (immature) berries**: small, sunken, dark brown to black lesions that expand rapidly, often covering large portions of the berry within days under favourable conditions.
- **Sporulation**: under humid conditions, lesions may develop pinkish-orange to salmon-coloured masses of spores (acervuli) on the surface, which is a useful diagnostic feature.
- **Berry mummification**: infected berries frequently shrivel, blacken, and remain attached to the tree as hardened "mummies," or they may drop prematurely.
- **Ripe berry infection**: a less common but still damaging form ("brown blight") can occur on nearly ripe or ripe berries, producing sunken brown lesions that reduce quality even where berries are harvested.
- **Blossom blight**: infected flowers turn brown, wither, and fail to set fruit, reducing the following crop.
- **Leaf and shoot lesions**: occasional dark, irregular lesions on young leaves and green shoots, generally of secondary importance compared with berry damage.
- Multiple lesions on a single berry, or lesions appearing at several points across a tree, are typical of an active outbreak rather than isolated, incidental damage.

## Conditions Favouring Infection
| Factor | Effect on disease risk |
|---|---|
| Cool, wet weather (moderate temperatures, roughly 18-22°C) | Strongly favours spore germination and infection |
| High-altitude growing zones (approximately 1,400-2,000 m) | Correlates with the cooler, humid conditions the fungus prefers |
| Prolonged leaf and berry wetness (mist, dew, frequent rain) | Provides the moisture film needed for spore germination and penetration |
| Dense canopy and poor air circulation | Prolongs wetness duration and humidity around berries |
| Heavy, continuous rainfall during berry expansion | Coincides with the most vulnerable berry growth stage |
| Poor field sanitation (retained mummified berries, litter) | Maintains a reservoir of fungal inoculum between seasons |

Infection risk rises sharply when wet, cool weather coincides with the period when berries are pin-head to pea-sized, as this stage is the most susceptible to fungal penetration.

## How It Spreads
- Rain-splashed and wind-driven spores are the principal means of local spread, moving inoculum from mummified berries, lesions, and infected debris onto healthy green berries.
- Spores can also be moved short distances by wind alone during humid, misty conditions, and over longer distances during wind-driven rain events.
- Contaminated hands, tools, and pickers' clothing can carry spores from infected to healthy trees during field operations, although this is a secondary route compared with rain splash.
- Infected plant debris and retained mummified berries left on trees or on the ground act as a carry-over source of inoculum from one season to the next.
- The disease is not known to be transmitted through true coffee seed used for planting, but nursery seedlings can become infected if raised in or near disease-affected areas with the same wet, cool conditions.

## Risk Level
- High to very high in traditional high-altitude Arabica-growing regions with a history of cool, wet weather during berry development.
- Under favourable weather and without control measures, crop losses have been reported ranging from significant partial loss up to near-total loss of the susceptible crop in severe epidemic years.
- Risk is lower, though not absent, in warmer, drier, lower-altitude Arabica zones and in areas planted predominantly to Robusta.
- Because the fungus can persist on mummified berries and in plant debris, risk tends to recur year after year in the same location once established, particularly where sanitation is poor.

## Treatment Options
- No treatment reverses damage already present in infected berries; management is aimed at protecting healthy, developing berries from new infection.
- Protectant copper-based fungicides (copper oxychloride, copper hydroxide, and related copper compounds) are widely used as a first line of defence, applied before and during periods of high infection risk.
- Systemic and translaminar fungicides from the triazole and strobilurin chemical classes are used in many CBD-endemic areas, often alternated or combined with copper protectants to improve control and to reduce the risk of the fungus developing resistance to any single mode of action.
- Fungicide programmes are typically timed to coincide with flowering and early berry development, since protecting berries during the pin-head to pea stage gives the greatest benefit.
- Farmers should always confirm which specific fungicide products are currently registered for coffee berry disease control in their country with a local agricultural extension officer or agro-input supplier, since registration status, application rates, and pre-harvest intervals vary by location and change over time.
- Removal and destruction of infected and mummified berries at each harvest and during off-season sanitation reduces the amount of fungicide needed by lowering the starting inoculum load.

## Prevention
- Plant coffee varieties bred for tolerance or resistance to coffee berry disease where these are available and suited to the local growing zone; several Arabica varieties with documented CBD resistance have been developed specifically for high-risk regions.
- Maintain adequate spacing and regular pruning of coffee trees and shade cover to improve air circulation and reduce the length of time foliage and berries remain wet.
- Manage shade levels carefully: excessive shade increases humidity and wetness duration, while some shade can moderate temperature extremes, so shade management should reflect local recommendations.
- Practice thorough field sanitation by removing and destroying mummified berries, fallen berries, and diseased plant debris, ideally before the start of the rainy season.
- Apply a well-timed, locally recommended fungicide programme during periods of highest risk (cool, wet weather during flowering and early berry set), rather than only reacting after symptoms appear.
- Avoid establishing new coffee plantings using seedlings or planting material sourced from severely CBD-affected areas without appropriate precautions.
- Coordinate spray timing with local weather forecasts and extension advisories where available, since infection periods are closely linked to rainfall and temperature patterns.

## Monitoring Recommendations
- Scout fields at least weekly during the flowering and berry development period, focusing on berries at the pin-head and pea-sized stages, which are most vulnerable.
- Inspect a representative sample of trees across the field, not just those at the edge, since disease pressure can vary with microclimate (for example, shaded or low-lying areas may show earlier symptoms).
- Check for early, small sunken lesions on green berries and for pinkish-orange spore masses on lesions during humid weather, which indicate active sporulation and rising risk to neighbouring berries.
- Keep simple records of local rainfall, temperature, and humidity where possible, since periods of cool, wet weather should trigger closer scouting and consideration of protective fungicide applications.
- Monitor the effectiveness of sanitation by checking whether mummified berries and debris have been adequately removed after each round of cleaning.

## When to Consult an Agricultural Extension Officer or Plant Health Specialist
- As soon as sunken lesions with pinkish-orange spore masses are found on green berries, to confirm the diagnosis and to plan a timely fungicide response before the disease spreads further.
- Before starting or revising a fungicide spray programme, to confirm which products are currently locally registered for coffee berry disease, correct rates, and safe pre-harvest intervals.
- When selecting new coffee varieties or planting material, to identify which CBD-tolerant or resistant varieties are recommended and available for the specific growing zone.
- If losses appear to exceed what is typical for a normal season despite following standard sanitation and spray practices, since this may indicate resistance development in the local fungal population or gaps in the control programme that need expert assessment.
- When establishing a new coffee planting in an area with a known history of severe CBD outbreaks, to get tailored advice on variety choice, spacing, and shade management before planting.
