# Cassava Brown Streak Disease

## Disease Name
Cassava Brown Streak Disease (CBSD)

## Scientific Name
Caused by two related RNA viruses in the genus *Ipomovirus* (family Potyviridae): Cassava brown streak virus (CBSV) and Ugandan cassava brown streak virus (UCBSV). The two viruses often occur together or in overlapping areas.

## Crops Affected
- Cassava (*Manihot esculenta*) is the primary and effectively only significant host crop (see `crops/cassava.md`).
- A small number of wild plant species can act as alternative hosts, but these play a limited role compared with infected cassava planting material.

## Cause
Infection by cassava brown streak viruses, transmitted by the whitefly (*Bemisia tabaci*) and, most importantly, through infected stem cuttings used as planting material.

## Symptoms
- Yellowing (chlorosis) along the veins or in blotches on leaves, which can be mistaken for other disorders in the early stages.
- Brown, dead streaks on green stems, giving the disease its name.
- The most damaging symptom is a dry, brown, corky rot within the storage roots, which often develops without obvious symptoms on the leaves above ground.
- Roots may appear healthy on the outside while being extensively damaged inside, making the disease difficult to detect before harvest.
- Symptom severity can vary with variety, virus strain, and environmental conditions, and root symptoms can worsen the longer roots are left in the ground.

## Conditions Favouring Infection
- High whitefly vector populations, favoured by warm temperatures and the presence of susceptible host plants nearby.
- Continuous cultivation of cassava in the same area without a break, allowing disease and vector carryover.
- Use of infected planting material from previous seasons, which is the primary means of disease introduction into new fields.
- Lower and mid-altitude areas have historically been associated with higher disease pressure, though distribution can shift over time.

## How It Spreads
- Primarily spread through the use of infected stem cuttings as planting material (vegetative propagation directly transmits the virus).
- Secondarily spread by whitefly vectors moving between infected and healthy plants within and between fields.
- Not spread through soil, and not transmitted through true botanical seed.
- Movement of infected cuttings between regions is a major driver of long-distance spread.

## Risk Level
- High in areas with established infection and where certified disease-free planting material is not used.
- Particularly damaging because root losses can be severe even when above-ground symptoms appear mild, and losses may only become apparent at harvest.
- Yield and quality losses can range from minor to near-total loss of usable roots, depending on variety, virus strain, and time of infection.

## Treatment Options
- No curative treatment exists for virus-infected plants.
- Management relies entirely on prevention: removing infected plants and using clean planting material.
- Roguing (removal and destruction) of infected plants, especially early in the season, can reduce the source of infection for whitefly spread within a field.
- Harvesting affected fields earlier than usual can limit the progressive worsening of root rot, though this does not reverse existing damage.

## Prevention
- Use certified, disease-free planting material as the single most effective prevention measure.
- Select and propagate cuttings only from visibly healthy mother plants, recognising that root symptoms may not be visible when selecting stems.
- Plant resistant or tolerant varieties where available and suited to local conditions.
- Practice roguing: promptly remove and destroy plants showing symptoms.
- Control whitefly populations through field sanitation and, where necessary, approved insecticides, in coordination with local extension guidance.
- Avoid moving cuttings from areas with known brown streak disease into disease-free areas.

## Monitoring Recommendations
- Inspect fields monthly for leaf yellowing and brown stem streaks, particularly in the first 3-4 months after planting when whitefly transmission risk is highest.
- Check a sample of roots before general harvest to detect internal brown rot, since roots can be affected without obvious leaf symptoms.
- Monitor whitefly populations on the underside of leaves, especially during warm periods.
- Track the source and health status of planting material used each season.

## When Farmers Should Consult an Agricultural Extension Officer or Plant Health Specialist
- If leaf symptoms are seen, or if root rot is found when checking sample roots, to confirm the diagnosis and decide whether to harvest early or replant with clean material.
- Before sourcing planting material from an unfamiliar source, to verify it is certified disease-free where certification schemes exist.
- If unsure whether symptoms are due to cassava brown streak disease, cassava mosaic disease (see `diseases/cassava_mosaic_disease.md`), nutrient deficiency, or another cause, since symptoms can overlap.
- When selecting resistant varieties, to confirm which varieties have documented resistance to the virus strains present in the local area.
