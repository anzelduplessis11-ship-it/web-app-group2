# Cotton Verticillium Wilt

## Disease Name
Cotton Verticillium Wilt.

## Scientific Name
Caused by the soil-borne fungus *Verticillium dahliae* (occasionally *Verticillium albo-atrum* is implicated in some regions, though *V. dahliae* is by far the more common and important species affecting cotton).

## Crops Affected
- Cotton (*Gossypium hirsutum* and other cultivated *Gossypium* species) is a major host of economic concern.
- *Verticillium dahliae* has an unusually wide host range and can also infect many other crops, including tomato, potato, okra, sunflower, and various vegetables, as well as numerous weed species; these alternative hosts can maintain and build up soil populations of the fungus even when cotton is not being grown.

## Cause
Cotton verticillium wilt is caused by infection of the plant's root system by *Verticillium dahliae*, which then colonises the vascular (water-conducting) tissue of the stem. The fungus produces microscopic resting structures called microsclerotia that survive for extended periods in the soil, from which the fungus germinates to infect roots directly, generally without needing a wound.

## Symptoms
- **Interveinal chlorosis and necrosis**: a characteristic mottled, "tiger-stripe" or "scorch-like" pattern of yellowing and browning between the leaf veins, most often first noticeable on lower and older leaves.
- **Wilting**: leaves and, in severe cases, whole branches wilt, though wilting in verticillium wilt is often less sudden and dramatic than in some other vascular wilts.
- **Leaf drop**: affected leaves eventually die and drop, leading to progressive defoliation from the base of the plant upward.
- **Vascular discoloration**: when the stem is cut lengthwise or across, a light tan to brown discoloration of the vascular tissue is typically visible, though usually less intensely dark than in Fusarium wilt.
- **Stunting and reduced boll set**: infected plants often show reduced overall vigour, smaller size, and fewer or smaller bolls, directly reducing lint yield.
- **Symptom timing**: symptoms frequently become more pronounced during cooler periods of the growing season or after a period of cool weather following warm conditions, which distinguishes this disease from Fusarium wilt of cotton, which tends to be more severe in warm conditions.
- Symptom severity and onset vary with cotton variety, soil fungal population levels, and weather, and can sometimes be confused with nutrient deficiency, moisture stress, or other wilt diseases without closer inspection of the vascular tissue.

## Conditions Favouring Infection
- Cool soil and air temperatures, generally more favourable to disease development than the warm conditions preferred by some other vascular wilt pathogens of cotton.
- Moist soil conditions, particularly following irrigation or rainfall, which favour microsclerotia germination and root infection.
- High populations of microsclerotia in the soil, built up through repeated cotton cropping or cultivation of other susceptible host crops and weeds.
- Root stress or damage from nematodes or other soil pests, which can increase the plant's vulnerability to infection and worsen disease severity.
- Imbalanced soil fertility, particularly excessive nitrogen relative to potassium, which has been associated with increased disease severity in some studies.
- Continuous or frequent cotton cultivation without rotation to non-host crops, allowing soil populations of the fungus to increase over successive seasons.

## How It Spreads
- The fungus survives in soil as microsclerotia for many years, making infested soil itself the primary long-term reservoir of the disease.
- Movement of infested soil on farm equipment, tools, footwear, or irrigation water is a major way the disease spreads from field to field.
- Wind and water erosion of infested topsoil can carry microsclerotia to previously uninfested areas.
- Infected crop debris left in the field after harvest can contribute microsclerotia to the soil population for future seasons.
- The disease can also spread through use of contaminated seed to a limited extent, and through movement of infected transplants or plant material of alternative host crops.
- Unlike many foliar diseases, verticillium wilt does not require wounds, rain splash, or insect vectors for infection, since the fungus infects directly through the root system in infested soil.

## Risk Level
- Moderate to high in fields with a history of the disease or of other susceptible host crops, and it is considered a chronic, difficult-to-manage soil-borne problem once established.
- Because microsclerotia persist in soil for many years, the disease is very difficult to eradicate from an infested field, and risk tends to increase with continued cultivation of susceptible crops.
- Yield and fibre quality losses vary with the level of soil infestation, cotton variety susceptibility, and seasonal weather, ranging from minor effects in tolerant varieties under low inoculum pressure to significant yield reduction in susceptible varieties under high inoculum pressure and favourable cool, moist conditions.

## Treatment Options
- There is no effective curative treatment for cotton plants already showing verticillium wilt symptoms in the field; management is focused on reducing soil inoculum levels and limiting further spread rather than treating infected plants directly.
- Soil fumigation can reduce microsclerotia populations in the treated zone, but it is costly, requires specialised equipment, and is generally impractical for most smallholder cotton production; where used, it should follow product label instructions and local regulatory requirements exactly.
- Some seed treatments and biological control products marketed for suppression of soil-borne pathogens may offer partial protection to young seedlings, but these generally do not eliminate an established soil infestation.
- Farmers should confirm with a local agricultural extension officer or agro-input supplier whether any seed treatments, soil amendments, or biological control products are currently locally registered and recommended for verticillium wilt suppression in cotton, since options and their effectiveness vary by region.
- Because chemical control options are limited and often uneconomical at the field scale, cultural and resistance-based prevention measures are generally the most practical and effective approach to managing this disease over time.

## Prevention
- Plant cotton varieties with documented tolerance or resistance to verticillium wilt where these are available and suited to local growing conditions, since variety choice is one of the most effective and accessible management tools.
- Rotate cotton with non-host crops, particularly cereals such as maize, sorghum, or small grains, for several seasons where possible to allow soil microsclerotia populations to decline over time.
- Avoid over-irrigation and manage soil moisture carefully, since excess soil moisture favours fungal survival and root infection.
- Manage plant-parasitic nematodes and other root-stress factors, since root damage can increase susceptibility to verticillium wilt.
- Maintain balanced soil fertility, avoiding excessive nitrogen without adequate potassium, in line with local soil testing and extension recommendations.
- Clean soil, plant debris, and residues from equipment, tools, and footwear before moving between fields, particularly when moving from a known-infested field to a clean one.
- Remove and manage susceptible weed hosts within and around cotton fields, since these can help maintain soil populations of the fungus even during non-cotton growing periods.
- Avoid introducing infested soil or plant material into previously uninfested fields, and be cautious when sourcing seedlings, transplants, or soil-associated inputs from areas with known verticillium wilt problems.

## Monitoring Recommendations
- Scout fields regularly through the growing season, paying particular attention to the mid-to-late season and to periods following cool weather, when symptoms often become most visible.
- Look for the characteristic interveinal chlorosis and "tiger-stripe" leaf pattern, starting with lower, older leaves and progressing upward in more severe cases.
- Where wilt is suspected, cut stems lengthwise to check for vascular discoloration and compare symptom patterns with other possible causes, such as Fusarium wilt or moisture stress, since management differs.
- Keep field records of disease incidence, location within the field, and cotton variety planted, to build a picture of soil infestation levels and inform future rotation and variety decisions.
- Where soil testing services for *Verticillium* microsclerotia levels are available locally, consider testing fields with a history of the disease before deciding on cotton variety and rotation plans for the coming season.

## When to Consult an Agricultural Extension Officer or Plant Health Specialist
- When wilt symptoms are observed and there is uncertainty about whether the cause is verticillium wilt, Fusarium wilt, or another stress factor, since accurate diagnosis is important for selecting the correct long-term management approach.
- Before selecting cotton varieties for a field with a known history of verticillium wilt, to identify locally recommended tolerant or resistant varieties.
- When planning crop rotations for fields with confirmed or suspected verticillium wilt infestation, to receive guidance on suitable rotation crops and appropriate rotation length for local conditions.
- If disease severity appears to be increasing over successive seasons despite standard rotation and variety choices, since this may warrant soil testing or a review of the overall field management programme.
- Before considering soil fumigation or any specialised soil treatment, to confirm whether such measures are practical, registered, and appropriate for the specific field and farming system.
