# Sorghum Anthracnose

## Disease Name
Sorghum Anthracnose

## Scientific Name
Caused by *Colletotrichum sublineola* (previously classified as *Colletotrichum graminicola* f. sp. *sorghi*), an ascomycete fungus. It is considered one of the most economically important fungal diseases of sorghum in warm, humid production regions, including much of sub-Saharan Africa.

## Crops Affected
- Sorghum (*Sorghum bicolor*), including grain sorghum, forage sorghum, and sweet sorghum types.
- Sudangrass and sorghum-sudangrass hybrids, which are also susceptible.
- Johnsongrass (*Sorghum halepense*) and some other wild *Sorghum* relatives can serve as alternative hosts, helping the pathogen persist in the landscape between cultivated sorghum crops.
- Note: maize is affected by a closely related but host-specialised pathogen (*Colletotrichum graminicola*, causing maize anthracnose), which is generally considered a separate host-adapted population from the sorghum-infecting fungus.

## Cause
Infection by the fungus *Colletotrichum sublineola*, which can attack the leaves, stalks, and grain heads (panicles) of sorghum at different growth stages, causing distinct disease phases often referred to as leaf anthracnose, stalk (red) rot, and grain/panicle anthracnose. The fungus produces asexual spores (conidia) in small fruiting structures called acervuli, which often bear characteristic dark, stiff, hair-like setae; these structures form on infected leaves, stalks, and residue, and are a useful diagnostic feature under magnification. The pathogen can survive between seasons within infected crop residue and on or in infected seed, allowing it to persist in a field or be introduced to a new area even when conditions are temporarily unfavourable for active disease development.

## Symptoms
- **Leaf anthracnose**: small, circular to oval spots that are tan, straw-coloured, or reddish in the centre with a darker reddish-purple to black border; spots may have tiny black dots (acervuli) in the centre, sometimes bearing short black hair-like structures (setae) visible with magnification.
- Numerous leaf spots can coalesce under severe infection, causing large areas of leaf tissue to die and giving affected leaves a scorched appearance.
- **Stalk (red) rot**: infected stalks show a reddish discoloration of the internal pith tissue when split open, along with external black lesions on the stalk surface; severely infected stalks weaken and are prone to lodging (falling over) and premature death.
- **Panicle/grain anthracnose**: infected grain heads show dark, sunken lesions on the panicle branches (rachis) and glumes, and infected grain may be discoloured, shrivelled, or covered with dark fungal fruiting structures, reducing both yield and grain quality.
- Premature plant death can occur under severe stalk rot infection, cutting the grain-filling period short and reducing yield.

| Disease Phase | Plant Part Affected | Key Field Sign |
|---|---|---|
| Leaf anthracnose | Leaves | Tan/reddish spots with dark borders and black fruiting specks |
| Stalk (red) rot | Stalks/internal pith | Reddish internal discoloration; external black lesions; lodging |
| Panicle/grain anthracnose | Grain heads, glumes, grain | Dark sunken lesions on rachis; discoloured, shrivelled grain |

## Conditions Favouring Infection
- Warm temperatures, generally in the range of 25–30°C, favour rapid disease development.
- High humidity and frequent rainfall, particularly during the growing season, strongly favour infection and spread of all disease phases.
- Extended leaf wetness from rain, dew, or overhead irrigation provides the moisture needed for spore germination.
- Continuous sorghum cropping without rotation, which allows inoculum to build up in crop residue over successive seasons.
- Susceptible sorghum varieties or hybrids grown without adequate resistance in high-rainfall or humid zones.
- Dense planting and vigorous, tall canopies that restrict airflow and prolong leaf wetness.
- Grain heads that mature and remain in the field during periods of wet weather are especially vulnerable to panicle and grain infection.

## How It Spreads
- Spores (conidia) are spread short to moderate distances by wind-driven rain splash, moving the fungus from infected residue and lower leaves onto healthy tissue.
- Infected crop residue and stubble left in or near the field is a major source of inoculum carried over between seasons.
- Infected seed can carry the fungus and introduce the disease into new fields or areas, making seed source an important consideration for disease management.
- Volunteer sorghum plants and wild *Sorghum* relatives such as Johnsongrass can harbour the pathogen between cultivated cropping cycles.
- Movement of infected plant material or contaminated equipment between fields can also contribute to local spread.

## Risk Level
- High in warm, humid sorghum-growing regions with frequent rainfall, where the disease is a major constraint on both grain and forage sorghum production.
- Risk is comparatively lower in drier sorghum production areas, though localised wet conditions or irrigation can still create favourable outbreaks.
- Yield losses can be substantial when infection occurs early and progresses through all three disease phases (leaf, stalk, and panicle), particularly on susceptible varieties, and quality losses from grain infection can further reduce market value.
- Forage and fodder sorghum crops affected by leaf anthracnose can also suffer reduced palatability and feed quality, which is an additional economic consideration in mixed crop-livestock systems.
- Because the pathogen can survive on residue and in seed, fields with a history of severe anthracnose tend to carry elevated risk into subsequent seasons unless residue and seed sources are actively managed.

## Treatment Options
- Foliar fungicides from the triazole (DMI) and strobilurin (QoI) chemical classes can reduce leaf anthracnose severity when applied preventively or at early disease onset, particularly on high-value seed or forage crops where fungicide use is economically justified.
- Fungicide seed treatments can help protect young seedlings from early infection originating from infected seed, particularly where seed source or history is uncertain.
- Fungicide use for the stalk rot and panicle phases is generally less practical and less effective once infection is established internally in the stalk; management for these phases relies more heavily on resistant varieties and cultural practices.
- Remove and destroy severely infected crop residue after harvest where practical, to reduce the amount of inoculum carried into the next season.
- Confirm currently locally registered fungicide active ingredients, application rates, and timing with a national agricultural authority or extension officer, as registered products and recommendations vary by country.

## Prevention
- Plant resistant or tolerant sorghum varieties or hybrids, which is the most effective and sustainable approach given the limitations of curative fungicide treatment for stalk and panicle phases.
- Use certified, disease-free seed from a reputable source, since infected seed is a known pathway for introducing the disease into new fields.
- Practice crop rotation away from sorghum and sudangrass for at least one to two seasons where practical, to reduce residue-borne inoculum.
- Manage and destroy infected crop residue after harvest, through appropriate incorporation or removal practices consistent with local soil conservation guidance.
- Control Johnsongrass and other wild *Sorghum* relatives in and around fields, as these can serve as alternative hosts and inoculum reservoirs.
- Avoid excessive plant density and manage nitrogen fertilisation to reduce canopy density and prolonged leaf wetness.
- Harvest grain promptly at maturity where possible, since delayed harvest in wet conditions increases the risk of panicle and grain infection.

## Monitoring Recommendations
- Scout fields regularly from early vegetative growth through grain maturity, checking lower leaves first for early leaf spot symptoms.
- Split a sample of stalks periodically, especially later in the season or if lodging is observed, to check for internal reddish discoloration indicating stalk rot.
- Inspect grain heads closely as they mature, particularly during or after wet weather, for dark lesions on the rachis or discoloured, shrivelled grain.
- Pay closer attention during and after periods of warm, humid, or rainy weather, which favour rapid disease development across all three phases.
- Keep records of disease severity by field and variety across seasons to inform future variety selection and rotation planning.
- Where forage or fodder sorghum is grown, also assess harvested material for leaf spotting before use, since heavily affected forage may be of reduced feeding quality.
- In seed production fields, inspect panicles and harvested seed lots closely for signs of infection, since visibly healthy-looking seed can still carry the fungus internally.

## When to Consult an Agricultural Extension Officer or Plant Health Specialist
- If leaf spot symptoms are spreading rapidly or coalescing across a significant portion of the leaf canopy, particularly during warm, wet weather.
- If stalk lodging or unexplained premature plant death is observed, to confirm whether stalk rot is the cause and assess the extent of the problem.
- Before selecting a sorghum variety or hybrid for planting, to confirm which options have documented anthracnose resistance suited to local conditions.
- If grain heads show dark lesions or discoloured grain near harvest, to assess quality impact and discuss appropriate harvest timing or handling.
- When planning crop rotation or residue management strategies for fields with a history of severe anthracnose outbreaks.
- If seed intended for planting comes from a field or lot with a known history of anthracnose, to discuss seed testing or treatment options before use.
- When unsure whether stalk lodging is due to anthracnose stalk rot, another stalk rot pathogen, or an unrelated cause such as wind damage, since the appropriate management response differs.
