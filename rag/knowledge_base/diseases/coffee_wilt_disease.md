# Coffee Wilt Disease

## Disease Name
Coffee Wilt Disease (CWD), also known as tracheomycosis of coffee.

## Scientific Name
Caused by the fungus *Gibberella xylarioides* (asexual/anamorph stage known as *Fusarium xylarioides*). It is a vascular wilt pathogen belonging to the same broad group of *Fusarium*-related fungi responsible for wilt diseases in numerous other crops, though the strains attacking coffee are host-specialised.

## Crops Affected
- Robusta coffee (*Coffea canephora*) has historically suffered the most severe and widespread damage from this disease, particularly in parts of East and Central Africa.
- Arabica coffee (*Coffea arabica*) is also susceptible and has experienced serious outbreaks in some producing areas.
- Other *Coffea* species and some related plants can occasionally be affected, but Robusta and Arabica are the hosts of primary economic concern.

## Cause
Coffee wilt disease results from infection of the plant's water-conducting (xylem) tissue by *Gibberella xylarioides*. Once inside the vascular system, the fungus grows and produces substances that block water movement through the stem, leading to the wilting, dieback, and eventual death that give the disease its name. The fungus typically enters through wounds, including those made during pruning, harvesting, and other field operations, as well as through root damage.

## Symptoms
- **Early wilting**: initial yellowing and wilting of leaves, often starting on one side of the tree or on individual branches rather than the whole plant at once.
- **Leaf browning and drop**: affected leaves progress from yellowing to browning and eventually drop, leading to visible defoliation on affected branches.
- **Vascular discoloration**: when an affected stem, branch, or root is cut across, a blue-black to dark brown discoloration of the internal vascular tissue is typically visible, a key diagnostic sign.
- **Bark symptoms**: darkening, cracking, or dieback of the bark near the base of the stem or on affected branches in more advanced cases.
- **Dieback and death**: progressive dieback of branches, followed by decline of the whole tree, with death commonly occurring within one to a few seasons after infection depending on the vigour of the tree and severity of the attack.
- **Fungal fruiting structures**: small, dark, pimple-like fungal fruiting bodies (perithecia) may develop on the bark of dead or dying wood under humid conditions, contributing to further spore production.
- Symptoms can sometimes resemble drought stress, root damage, or other wilt-causing problems, so cutting into the stem to check for vascular discoloration is important for a reliable field diagnosis.

## Conditions Favouring Infection
- Presence of fresh wounds on stems, branches, or roots created during pruning, de-suckering, harvesting, or cultivation, which provide entry points for the fungus.
- Use of contaminated tools moved between trees without cleaning, spreading the fungus from infected to healthy plants during routine field work.
- Continuous cultivation of susceptible coffee varieties in the same area, allowing the pathogen to persist and inoculum to build up over time.
- Stress factors such as poor soil fertility, drought, or root damage that may make trees more vulnerable to establishment of infection once the fungus is present.
- Movement of infected planting material (seedlings or cuttings) into previously unaffected areas, introducing the disease to new locations.

## How It Spreads
- Contaminated cutting tools (pruning knives, pangas, harvesting implements) are one of the most significant means of spread between trees and across farms.
- Infected planting material, including seedlings and cuttings taken from diseased or symptomless-but-infected mother plants, can introduce the disease into previously clean areas.
- Root-to-root contact between neighbouring infected and healthy trees can allow the fungus to spread underground in densely planted stands.
- Airborne and insect- or bird-assisted movement of fungal spores from fruiting bodies on dead wood can contribute to local spread, although tool- and planting material-based spread are generally considered more important in driving outbreaks.
- Infected plant debris, including diseased stumps and prunings left in or near the field, can serve as a long-term source of inoculum.
- The disease is not known to spread significantly through soil alone in the absence of root contact, wounds, or contaminated material, but infested debris in the soil can remain a source of infection for extended periods.

## Risk Level
- Very high. Coffee wilt disease has caused severe historical epidemics in East and Central Africa, in some cases destroying a large proportion of Robusta coffee holdings in affected regions over a period of years.
- There is no chemical cure, and infected trees generally die, making prevention and early removal of infected material critical to limiting losses.
- Once established in an area, the disease tends to persist and can re-emerge in replanted susceptible varieties if source material and field sanitation are not carefully managed.

## Treatment Options
- There is no effective chemical or biological treatment that cures an infected tree once vascular symptoms are established; management is entirely preventive and sanitation-based.
- Confirmed infected trees should be uprooted, including as much of the root system as practical, and destroyed (commonly by burning) to eliminate the source of infection and prevent further spore production from dead wood.
- All tools used on or near an infected tree should be thoroughly disinfected before use on any other tree, using an appropriate disinfectant recommended by local extension services.
- Farmers should confirm with a local agricultural extension officer which sanitation protocols and, where relevant, any locally registered tool disinfectants or soil treatments are currently recommended, since practices can be updated as research on the disease progresses.

## Prevention
- Plant coffee varieties with documented tolerance or resistance to coffee wilt disease where these are available and appropriate for the local growing area, particularly in regions with a history of outbreaks.
- Source planting material (seedlings and cuttings) only from certified, disease-free nurseries or mother plants with no history of wilt symptoms.
- Disinfect pruning, harvesting, and other cutting tools between trees, and especially before moving from a suspect or known-infected tree to a healthy one.
- Avoid unnecessary wounding of stems, branches, and roots during field operations, since wounds are a primary entry point for the fungus.
- Promptly identify, remove, and destroy infected trees, including root material where feasible, rather than leaving them in the field to serve as a continuing source of spores.
- Avoid replanting susceptible coffee varieties immediately into soil where infected trees have recently been removed without guidance from a local extension officer on appropriate waiting periods or precautions.
- Maintain general tree vigour through balanced fertilisation and good agronomic practices, since stressed trees may be more vulnerable to disease establishment.
- Where the disease is present in a district, coordinate with neighbouring farms and local coffee authorities on area-wide sanitation and replanting strategies, since the disease can move readily between adjoining farms.

## Monitoring Recommendations
- Inspect coffee trees regularly, at least monthly, for early signs of one-sided wilting, yellowing, or branch dieback, which can indicate early-stage infection.
- When suspicious wilting is observed, cut into a small section of the affected stem or branch to check for the characteristic blue-black to brown vascular discoloration.
- Pay close attention to trees near known infection points or along field boundaries shared with affected neighbouring farms.
- Inspect new seedlings and planting material carefully before planting, and avoid introducing material with any unexplained wilting, discoloration, or poor vigour.
- Keep a simple field record of any trees removed due to suspected wilt disease, including their location, to help track patterns of spread over time.

## When to Consult an Agricultural Extension Officer or Plant Health Specialist
- Immediately upon noticing one-sided wilting, unexplained yellowing, or vascular discoloration in a coffee tree, given how destructive and fast-moving outbreaks of this disease can become if not addressed early.
- Before sourcing coffee seedlings, cuttings, or other planting material from any source, to confirm it is free of coffee wilt disease and, where available, obtain certified disease-free stock.
- When deciding whether and how soon to replant coffee in an area where infected trees have been removed, to receive guidance on soil and site management before replanting.
- If the disease is found for the first time in an area with no prior history of outbreaks, since early reporting and coordinated action can help limit further spread to neighbouring farms.
- When selecting coffee varieties for a new planting in a region with known coffee wilt disease pressure, to identify locally recommended tolerant or resistant varieties.
