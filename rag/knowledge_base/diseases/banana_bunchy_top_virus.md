# Banana Bunchy Top Virus

## Disease Name
Banana Bunchy Top Disease (caused by Banana bunchy top virus, BBTV)

## Scientific Name
Caused by Banana bunchy top virus (BBTV), a virus in the genus *Babuvirus*, family Nanoviridae.

## Crops Affected
- Banana and plantain (*Musa* species and hybrids) are the principal hosts affected (see `crops/banana_and_plantain.md`).
- Some related ornamental and wild Musa-family plants can also become infected and may act as sources of the virus near cultivated banana.
- The disease does not affect unrelated crop families.

## Cause
Infection by Banana bunchy top virus, transmitted from plant to plant by the banana aphid (*Pentalonia nigronervosa*) in a persistent, circulative manner, meaning the aphid remains capable of transmitting the virus for an extended period after acquiring it, and by the movement of infected suckers used as planting material.

## Symptoms
- Dark green streaks running along the veins of leaves, leaf petioles, and the pseudostem, sometimes described as a "Morse code" pattern of dots and dashes; this is often the earliest and most reliable field sign.
- Newly produced leaves become progressively narrower, more upright, and chlorotic (yellow) at the margins compared with older leaves.
- Leaves become increasingly brittle, stunted, and bunched together at the top of the plant, giving the disease its common name.
- Severely affected plants are markedly stunted and may fail to flower or produce fruit; if a bunch does form, it is typically small, distorted, and of poor quality.
- Plants infected at a young age are usually the most severely affected and may never produce a usable crop, while plants infected later may show milder symptoms in the current cycle but decline progressively.
- Infected mats do not recover and typically deteriorate further with each subsequent sucker generation if not removed.

## Conditions Favouring Infection
- Presence of the banana aphid vector in and around the planting, since the aphid is required for plant-to-plant spread within a field.
- Proximity to already infected banana plants, abandoned or wild banana stands, and ornamental Musa plants, which act as sources of the virus.
- Warm conditions that favour aphid activity and reproduction.
- Continued use of suckers taken from an area with unknown or unmanaged disease status.
- Dense plantings that allow aphids to move readily between plants.

## How It Spreads
- Primarily spread by the banana aphid, which acquires the virus by feeding on infected plants and transmits it to healthy plants during subsequent feeding; once infective, aphids can transmit the virus for an extended period.
- Spread over longer distances mainly through the movement of infected suckers and other vegetative planting material.
- Not transmitted through true botanical seed, and not spread mechanically through sap on tools or by simple contact, unlike some other plant viruses.
- Abandoned banana plantations, backyard plants, and wild or ornamental Musa plants left unmanaged near production fields can serve as a continuing source of both the virus and the aphid vector.

## Risk Level
- High to very high where the virus and its aphid vector are established; the disease has historically caused severe damage to banana industries in various regions once introduced.
- Particularly serious because there is no cure, infected plants do not recover, and yield potential is permanently reduced or eliminated in affected mats.
- The risk of rapid spread is elevated wherever infected planting material is distributed without inspection, since a single infected sucker can introduce the virus into a previously clean area.

## Treatment Options
- There is no chemical or other cure for virus-infected plants; the virus cannot be eliminated from an infected mat.
- Management relies entirely on early detection, removal of infected plants, and control of the aphid vector.
- Roguing: promptly remove and destroy entire infected mats, including the underground corm, since new suckers from an infected corm will also be infected; treating the cut stump with an approved herbicide can help prevent regrowth from surviving tissue.
- Control banana aphid populations on remaining and neighbouring plants using recommended insecticides as a supporting measure, applied in line with local extension guidance, since reducing the vector population slows further spread.
- Any insecticide or herbicide used for roguing and vector control should be confirmed as locally registered and appropriate with an agricultural extension officer.

## Prevention
- Plant only certified, virus-tested (indexed) tissue-cultured plantlets, or suckers verified as coming from disease-free mother plants.
- Inspect all planting material carefully for the characteristic dark green streaking before planting, and reject any suspect material.
- Remove and destroy wild, abandoned, or ornamental banana plants near production fields that could harbour the virus or its vector.
- Control banana aphid populations through field sanitation and, where necessary, approved insecticides.
- Establish new plantings away from known infected areas where possible, and avoid moving suckers from regions with known bunchy top outbreaks.
- Coordinate roguing and vector control with neighbouring farms, since the disease spreads readily between adjoining plantings.

## Monitoring Recommendations
- Inspect banana plants regularly, especially young suckers and newly emerging leaves, for dark green streaking on veins, petioles, and pseudostems.
- Watch for progressive narrowing, upright growth, and chlorotic margins on new leaves, and for stunted, bunched growth at the crown.
- Check the underside of leaf sheaths and the base of the pseudostem for banana aphid colonies, particularly in warm periods.
- Survey surrounding areas, including abandoned plots and homestead plantings, for potential sources of infection.
- Keep records of the source of all suckers or plantlets brought onto the farm.

## When to Consult an Agricultural Extension Officer or Plant Health Specialist
- As soon as the characteristic dark green streaking or bunchy, stunted growth is observed, to confirm the diagnosis and begin roguing before the virus spreads further.
- Before sourcing suckers or plantlets from any new location, to verify that virus-free planting material is being used.
- If bunchy top is confirmed in a new area, since prompt, coordinated action across neighbouring farms is important to prevent wider establishment.
- When planning aphid control measures, to confirm which insecticide options are locally registered and appropriate.
