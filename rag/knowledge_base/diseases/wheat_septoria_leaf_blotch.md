# Septoria Leaf Blotch of Wheat

## Disease Name
Septoria Leaf Blotch (also known as Septoria Tritici Blotch)

## Scientific Name
Caused by *Zymoseptoria tritici* (formerly classified as *Septoria tritici*), an ascomycete fungus. A related disease, Septoria nodorum blotch (also called glume blotch), is caused by *Parastagonospora nodorum* (formerly *Septoria nodorum*) and can occur alongside or be confused with Septoria tritici blotch, though it more commonly affects glumes and upper leaves later in the season.

## Crops Affected
- Bread wheat (*Triticum aestivum*) and durum wheat (*Triticum durum*), which are both susceptible.
- Triticale can also be affected.
- Barley and some other cereals and grasses can host related *Septoria*-complex species, though *Zymoseptoria tritici* is primarily a wheat pathogen.

## Cause
Infection by the fungus *Zymoseptoria tritici*, which survives on infected wheat residue and stubble between seasons and produces spores that infect new wheat crops. The fungus has an unusually long latent period (the time between infection and visible symptoms), often around 2–4 weeks, which means the disease can be spreading internally within a crop well before it becomes obvious to a farmer scouting the field. During this latent phase the fungus grows between the plant's leaf cells without immediately killing them, which is part of why symptoms are often first noticed only after substantial internal colonisation has already occurred, making early-season risk assessment based on weather and residue history more useful than relying on visual symptoms alone.

## Symptoms
- Initial symptoms appear as small, irregular, pale green to yellow spots or lesions, usually starting on the lower, older leaves first.
- Lesions expand into elongated, irregularly shaped brown to tan blotches, often bounded by leaf veins, giving them a rectangular or lens-like shape.
- Within mature lesions, tiny black specks called pycnidia (fungal fruiting bodies) become visible, often arranged in rows; these are a key diagnostic feature distinguishing Septoria leaf blotch from other leaf diseases.
- In humid conditions, pycnidia release masses of thread-like spores that can be seen as a whitish or cream-coloured ooze under magnification or high humidity.
- Disease progresses upward through the canopy over time, from older lower leaves toward the flag leaf, with the flag leaf being the most critical for yield since it contributes the most to grain fill.
- Severely affected leaves yellow, die, and can be shed prematurely, reducing the photosynthetic area available during grain filling.
- Symptoms on glumes (if Septoria nodorum blotch is also present) appear as brown blotches with less distinct pycnidia arrangement, often confused with tan spot or other diseases without close examination.

## Conditions Favouring Infection
- Cool to moderate temperatures, generally in the range of 15–20°C, favour disease development, although infection can occur across a broader range under sustained wet conditions.
- Extended periods of leaf wetness (rain, dew, fog, or high humidity) lasting many hours are required for spore germination and infection; wetter regions and seasons see much higher disease pressure.
- Frequent rainfall throughout the growing season, which both promotes infection and physically splashes spores from lower to upper leaves.
- Dense crop canopies with poor airflow, which prolong leaf wetness duration.
- Continuous wheat cropping without rotation, and retention of infected wheat residue on the soil surface, which maintains a local source of inoculum between seasons.
- Susceptible varieties grown without adequate fungicide protection in high-rainfall zones.

## How It Spreads
- Spores (pycnidiospores) are spread short distances primarily by rain splash, moving the fungus from infected lower leaves and crop residue upward onto newly emerging leaves and to neighbouring plants.
- Wind-driven rain can extend the distance of local splash dispersal within and between nearby fields, but long-distance wind dispersal (as seen with rust diseases) is much less significant for this pathogen.
- Infected crop residue and stubble left on or near the soil surface is a major source of initial infection at the start of a new season, as spores are produced on decomposing debris and splashed onto young wheat plants.
- The long latent period means the fungus can be present and spreading within a crop for several weeks before visible symptoms are widespread, making early-season inoculum sources especially important to manage.
- Seed transmission is not considered a significant pathway for this disease compared with residue-borne and rain-splashed inoculum.

## Risk Level
- Moderate to high in wheat-growing regions with frequent rainfall or high humidity during the growing season, and comparatively lower risk in drier production areas.
- Left unmanaged in favourable wet seasons, yield losses can be substantial, particularly when infection reaches the flag leaf before or during grain fill.
- The long latent period and the risk of fungicide resistance developing in some *Zymoseptoria tritici* populations (notably reduced sensitivity to certain strobilurin and, in some regions, triazole fungicides) add to the management challenge and overall risk.
- Risk tends to be highest in fields with a recent history of wheat-on-wheat cropping and surface-retained residue, since these conditions provide a ready local source of inoculum at the start of the season, compounding the risk posed by prevailing weather.

| Risk Factor | Effect on Disease Pressure |
|---|---|
| Frequent rainfall/high humidity | Strongly increases risk |
| Continuous wheat cropping | Increases local inoculum carryover |
| Surface-retained infected residue | Provides early-season spore source |
| Dense canopy/high nitrogen | Prolongs leaf wetness, increases risk |
| Crop rotation away from wheat | Reduces local inoculum |

## Treatment Options
- Foliar fungicides from the triazole (DMI) chemical class remain a primary tool, often used in combination with other modes of action such as succinate dehydrogenase inhibitors (SDHIs), since reliance on a single mode of action increases the risk of resistance development.
- Strobilurin (QoI) fungicides have reduced effectiveness against this pathogen in many regions due to documented resistance, so their use against Septoria leaf blotch specifically should be guided by current local resistance data rather than assumed effective.
- Because of the long latent period, fungicide timing based on crop growth stage and weather-based risk forecasting (where available) is generally more effective than waiting for visible symptoms, since substantial infection may already be present internally by the time lesions appear.
- Protecting the upper leaves, particularly the flag leaf and the leaf below it, is the priority during fungicide programmes, as these contribute most to grain yield.
- Always confirm currently registered fungicide active ingredients, approved tank-mix partners, application timing, and resistance-management guidance with a national agricultural authority or local extension officer, as recommendations vary by country and evolve as resistance patterns change.

## Prevention
- Use resistant or tolerant wheat varieties where available, as genetic resistance reduces both disease severity and reliance on fungicides.
- Rotate away from wheat (and other susceptible cereals) for at least one season where practical, to allow infected residue to break down and reduce local inoculum levels.
- Manage infected crop residue through appropriate tillage or residue incorporation practices consistent with local soil conservation guidance, since surface-retained infected stubble is a key source of early-season infection.
- Avoid excessively dense planting and manage nitrogen fertilisation to avoid overly lush canopies that prolong leaf wetness and canopy humidity.
- Where fungicides are used, rotate or combine different modes of action according to local resistance-management guidance to reduce the risk of the pathogen developing resistance.
- Plan planting dates, where flexibility exists, to reduce the overlap between highly susceptible early growth stages and periods of consistently wet weather typical for the region.

## Monitoring Recommendations
- Scout fields regularly from early tillering onward, checking lower and older leaves first, since this is where symptoms typically appear before moving up the canopy.
- Look for the characteristic irregular, vein-bounded tan to brown lesions with small black pycnidia specks, using a hand lens if needed to confirm pycnidia presence.
- Because of the long latent period, treat early lower-leaf symptoms as an indicator that infection may already be progressing upward, and factor recent and forecast rainfall/humidity into decisions rather than relying on visible symptoms alone.
- Pay close attention as the crop approaches flag leaf emergence and heading, since protecting the flag leaf is critical for yield.
- Keep records of disease progression and weather conditions across seasons to help anticipate risk in future years on the same land.
- Consider marking or tracking a few representative plants through the season to observe how quickly disease moves from lower leaves toward the flag leaf, which can help calibrate scouting frequency and fungicide timing on the same farm in future seasons.

## When to Consult an Agricultural Extension Officer or Plant Health Specialist
- If lower-leaf lesions are found spreading rapidly up the canopy toward the flag leaf, especially during sustained wet weather.
- Before finalising a fungicide programme, to confirm currently effective active ingredients and resistance-management recommendations for the local area, given known regional resistance concerns with some fungicide classes.
- If disease control appears poor despite fungicide application, as this may indicate reduced fungicide sensitivity in the local pathogen population requiring a different management strategy.
- When deciding on variety selection or crop rotation plans for future seasons, to confirm which varieties have documented resistance performance under local conditions.
- If uncertain whether symptoms are due to Septoria leaf blotch, Septoria nodorum blotch, tan spot, or another leaf disease, since accurate diagnosis affects fungicide choice and timing.
