# Coffee Leaf Rust

## Disease Name
Coffee Leaf Rust (CLR)

## Scientific Name
Caused by *Hemileia vastatrix*, a basidiomycete (rust) fungus and an obligate biotroph, meaning it can only grow and reproduce on living coffee leaf tissue. Numerous physiological races of the pathogen exist, and new races capable of overcoming previously resistant coffee varieties have emerged periodically.

## Crops Affected
- Arabica coffee (*Coffea arabica*), which is generally the most susceptible major coffee species to severe epidemics.
- Robusta coffee (*Coffea canephora*), which shows greater general tolerance than Arabica but is not immune and can still be affected, particularly by certain races or under highly favourable conditions.
- Other cultivated and wild *Coffea* species can also be infected to varying degrees, and some have served as sources of resistance genes used in coffee breeding programmes.

## Cause
Infection by the fungus *Hemileia vastatrix*, which produces orange-yellow urediniospores on the underside of infected coffee leaves. These are the repeating spore stage responsible for rapid disease build-up and spread within and between coffee plantations during favourable conditions.

## Symptoms
- Small, pale yellow spots on the upper surface of leaves that enlarge over time, corresponding to fungal growth developing on the underside of the same leaf area.
- On the underside of affected leaves, powdery orange-yellow pustules (uredinia) develop, which is the most distinctive and diagnostic sign of the disease; the powder rubs off easily onto fingers when touched.
- As lesions age, the centre of the spot often turns necrotic (brown to grey-black) while a yellow-orange ring of active sporulation persists at the margin, giving older lesions a "target" or ring-like appearance.
- Severely affected leaves yellow and drop prematurely, a process known as defoliation, which can occur extensively under severe epidemics.
- Repeated and severe defoliation over one or more seasons weakens the plant, reduces its capacity for photosynthesis, and leads to dieback of branches, especially in already-stressed or overbearing trees.
- Yield impact is often delayed: severe defoliation in one season frequently reduces the following season's crop due to weakened plant reserves and reduced capacity to develop and fill the next crop of berries, in addition to any impact on the current season.
- Chronic, repeated severe infection over successive years can lead to plant decline and, in extreme untreated cases, tree death.

## Conditions Favouring Infection
- Warm temperatures, with an optimum range generally cited around 21–25°C; disease development slows at cooler high-altitude temperatures and can also be suppressed at very high temperatures.
- Free moisture on the leaf surface (dew, mist, or rain) for several hours is required for spore germination and infection, making humid environments and cloud-affected growing areas particularly favourable.
- Lower to mid-altitude coffee growing zones have historically experienced more severe epidemics than cooler high-altitude zones, although rising temperatures associated with climate change have been linked to increased disease pressure at higher altitudes than previously typical in some regions.
- Overbearing coffee trees (carrying a very heavy berry crop) tend to be more susceptible, since the physiological stress of heavy fruit load reduces the plant's capacity to resist infection.
- Dense planting, excessive shade, or poor canopy management that restricts airflow and prolongs leaf wetness duration.
- Nutrient-stressed plants, particularly those deficient in potassium and nitrogen, tend to show greater susceptibility and more severe disease progression.
- Susceptible coffee varieties grown without confirmed resistance in areas with a history of the disease.

## How It Spreads
- Urediniospores are dispersed primarily by wind over short to moderate distances within and between neighbouring plantations, and rain splash contributes to more localised spread within a plantation.
- Heavy wind-driven rain and storms can spread spores over greater distances than still, humid conditions alone.
- Long-distance spread between regions or countries has historically been associated with movement of infected plant material, and potentially with wind currents and human activity (such as movement of workers, equipment, or clothing carrying spores) between growing areas.
- The fungus survives between favourable periods on infected leaves that remain on the tree, allowing it to persist through less favourable weather and resume rapid spread once conditions become favourable again.
- Because the pathogen is an obligate biotroph, it does not survive for long in the absence of living coffee leaf tissue, so infected leaf material and living, symptomatic trees are the primary source of ongoing inoculum.

## Risk Level
- High to very high in most coffee-growing regions, as coffee leaf rust is one of the most economically significant diseases of coffee worldwide and has caused major historical epidemics, including severe regional outbreaks that substantially reduced production and farmer incomes.
- Risk has increased in some higher-altitude areas previously considered lower risk, linked to warming temperatures that make conditions more favourable for the pathogen at altitudes where it was historically less severe.
- Because yield impact often carries over into the following season due to weakened plant reserves, the effective economic risk of a severe epidemic extends beyond the season in which defoliation occurs.
- Emergence of new pathogen races capable of overcoming resistance in previously resistant varieties adds ongoing risk even where resistant planting material has been used.

## Treatment Options
- Copper-based protectant fungicides are a widely used foundation for coffee leaf rust management, applied preventively to protect healthy foliage before infection occurs; they act mainly as a protectant rather than a curative treatment.
- Systemic fungicides from the triazole (DMI) chemical class provide curative and protectant activity and are commonly used, often in rotation or combination with copper-based products, to manage resistance risk and improve control under high disease pressure.
- Fungicide application timing is important and is often guided by monitoring of disease incidence, weather conditions, and the plant's phenological stage (such as before major flushes of new growth or ahead of predicted favourable weather), since preventive timing is generally more effective than reactive treatment after severe defoliation has already occurred.
- Pruning and removal of severely affected branches or heavily infected leaves can help reduce inoculum load in a plantation, alongside chemical control.
- Confirm currently locally registered fungicide active ingredients, application rates, timing, and pre-harvest intervals with a national agricultural authority or extension officer, as registered products and recommended programmes vary by country and region.

## Prevention
- Plant resistant or tolerant coffee varieties where available and suited to local growing conditions and market requirements, including varieties bred from resistance sources such as Timor Hybrid-derived lines (e.g., Catimor and Sarchimor type varieties) that have been used in various breeding programmes, while recognising that new pathogen races can eventually reduce the effectiveness of some resistance sources over time.
- Manage shade levels appropriately for the local growing system, since both excessive shade (which increases humidity and leaf wetness) and complete lack of shade (which can stress plants) can influence disease pressure; locally appropriate shade management balances multiple factors, not rust risk alone.
- Maintain balanced plant nutrition, with particular attention to adequate potassium and nitrogen, as well-nourished plants generally show greater tolerance to infection and better recovery from defoliation.
- Avoid practices that lead to chronic overbearing without adequate plant nutrition and management, since heavily stressed, overbearing trees are more susceptible to severe rust outbreaks.
- Ensure appropriate plant spacing and regular pruning to maintain good airflow through the canopy and reduce leaf wetness duration.
- Apply preventive fungicide programmes ahead of periods known locally to favour disease development, based on historical patterns and monitoring, rather than waiting for visible severe symptoms.
- Remove and manage severely infected leaf and branch material where practical to reduce local inoculum sources.

## Monitoring Recommendations
- Scout plantations regularly, ideally at least monthly during lower-risk periods and more frequently (every one to two weeks) during periods of warm, humid weather known to favour the disease.
- Check the underside of leaves, particularly in the lower and inner canopy where humidity is often highest, for the characteristic powdery orange-yellow pustules.
- Assess both disease incidence (proportion of plants or leaves affected) and severity (extent of leaf area affected per leaf) to track epidemic development and inform fungicide timing decisions.
- Monitor for premature leaf drop and overall canopy density as an indicator of cumulative disease impact, since defoliation severity strongly influences yield in the current and following season.
- Track local weather conditions, particularly temperature and leaf wetness duration, as an early indicator of periods of elevated infection risk.

## When to Consult an Agricultural Extension Officer or Plant Health Specialist
- If pustules are found on a significant proportion of leaves or are spreading rapidly through the plantation, particularly during warm, humid weather.
- Before establishing a new planting or replanting a plot, to confirm which currently available coffee varieties have documented resistance suited to the local area and market.
- If disease control appears poor despite an established fungicide programme, as this may indicate a resistance-breaking pathogen race, incorrect timing, or an application/coverage issue requiring specialist diagnosis.
- When significant defoliation has occurred and there is uncertainty about appropriate post-epidemic management (such as pruning, nutrition, or crop load management) to support plant recovery.
- Before starting or adjusting a fungicide programme, to confirm currently locally registered products, rates, and timing recommendations for the region.
