# Fusarium Wilt of Tomato

## Overview
Fusarium wilt is a serious soil-borne fungal disease of tomato that blocks the plant's water-conducting (vascular) system, causing yellowing, wilting, and death. It is particularly troublesome for smallholder farmers in warm regions of Africa because the fungus lives in the soil, survives there for many years even without a tomato crop, and cannot be eliminated once a field is infested. As a result, Fusarium wilt is managed almost entirely through prevention: resistant varieties, clean planting material, crop rotation, and soil and water management. There is no effective spray that cures an infected plant, because the fungus lives inside the plant's vascular tissue where sprays cannot reach.

Fusarium wilt is often confused with bacterial wilt (see `diseases/tomato_bacterial_wilt.md` and `diseases/potato_bacterial_wilt.md`) because both cause wilting, and with Verticillium wilt, but the management and the diagnostic tests differ. Correct identification matters, because resistant varieties are available for Fusarium wilt and are the cornerstone of control. This document should be read together with the tomato crop guide (`crops/tomato.md`) and the related wilt and soil-borne disease guides in this knowledge base.

## Scientific Name
Fusarium wilt of tomato is caused by the fungus *Fusarium oxysporum* f.sp. *lycopersici*. The term "f.sp." stands for *forma specialis*, meaning a specialised form of the fungus that attacks only a specific host, in this case tomato. There are several races of this pathogen (commonly numbered race 1, race 2, and race 3), which matters because tomato varieties bred to resist one race may still be susceptible to another. A related pathogen, *Fusarium oxysporum* f.sp. *radicis-lycopersici*, causes Fusarium crown and root rot with somewhat different symptoms.

## Crops Affected
- Tomato (*Solanum lycopersicum*) is the specific host of this specialised form of the fungus (see `crops/tomato.md`).
- The tomato form does not attack unrelated crops, because it is host-specific. However, the wider *Fusarium oxysporum* species contains many other specialised forms, each attacking different crops such as banana, common bean, and onion, so a "Fusarium wilt" affecting another crop is caused by a different specialised form.
- This host specificity is helpful for management: rotating tomato with unrelated crops such as cereals starves the tomato-specific fungus of its host.

## Cause
Fusarium wilt is caused by infection with *Fusarium oxysporum* f.sp. *lycopersici*. The fungus lives in the soil and in infected crop debris, where it can survive for many years as tough resting spores (chlamydospores) even when no tomato is grown. It enters young tomato plants through the roots, especially where roots are damaged by cultivation, transplanting, or root-knot nematodes (see `diseases/root_knot_nematodes.md`). Once inside, it grows into the plant's water-conducting vessels (the xylem) and multiplies. The fungus, and the plant's own defensive reactions, plug these vessels, so water can no longer move up to the leaves. The plant responds by wilting, even when the soil is moist, and eventually dies.

## Symptoms
The symptoms reflect the blockage of the water-conducting system and are often one-sided, which is a useful diagnostic clue.

- **Yellowing (chlorosis):** The first sign is usually yellowing of the older, lower leaves. This frequently begins on one side of the plant or even one side of a single leaf, giving a distinctly lopsided appearance.
- **Wilting:** Leaves and shoots wilt during the heat of the day and may recover overnight at first, then wilt permanently as the disease progresses. Wilting occurs even though the soil has adequate moisture.
- **Progressive collapse:** Yellowing and wilting spread upward and around the plant; leaves turn brown and die but often remain attached. The whole plant becomes stunted and eventually dies.
- **Vascular browning:** If an affected stem is cut lengthwise near the base, a brown discolouration is visible in the vascular ring just under the skin. This internal browning is a key feature separating Fusarium wilt from many other problems.
- **No ooze:** Unlike bacterial wilt, a cut Fusarium-infected stem placed in clear water does not produce milky bacterial streaming, and the pith does not turn slimy. This simple water test helps tell the two wilts apart.
- **Root and crown symptoms:** Roots may be discoloured; in crown and root rot forms, a dark rot appears at the crown and on major roots.

## Conditions Favouring Infection
- Warm soil temperatures, roughly 25°C to 30°C, which are ideal for the fungus.
- Warm air temperatures and long, sunny days.
- Acidic soils, which tend to favour the disease more than alkaline soils.
- Sandy soils and light-textured soils where the disease often develops more readily.
- Presence of root-knot nematodes, which wound the roots and greatly increase infection and severity.
- Excess nitrogen fertiliser in ammonium form and low potassium, which can worsen the disease.
- Continuous cropping of tomato on the same land, which builds up the soil population of the fungus.

## How It Spreads
- The fungus persists and spreads within a field through infested soil, where its resting spores survive for years.
- It is moved between fields on anything that carries contaminated soil: farm tools, boots, hooves of animals, vehicle tyres, and irrigation or flood water.
- Infected transplants and seedlings raised in contaminated nursery soil are a major way the disease is introduced into clean fields.
- The fungus can be carried on the surface of and occasionally within seed, so seed from infected crops can introduce it.
- It does not spread appreciably through the air; it is fundamentally a soil- and water-borne disease.

## Risk Level
High and long-lasting in infested fields. Because the fungus survives for many years in the soil and cannot be eradicated, once a field is infested the risk remains high indefinitely unless resistant varieties are used. On susceptible varieties in warm, infested soils, losses can be severe, sometimes exceeding half the crop, and individual plants that show symptoms almost always die. The risk is greatly reduced where resistant varieties are grown, which is why the disease is considered manageable rather than catastrophic where such varieties are available and adopted.

## Treatment Options
There is no cure for an individual plant once it is infected, and no spray reaches the fungus inside the vascular system. Management therefore concentrates on reducing spread and protecting future crops.

- **Remove and destroy infected plants:** Rogue out wilting plants together with as much of their root system as possible and destroy them by burial or burning away from the field, to reduce the fungus returned to the soil.
- **Avoid moving contaminated soil:** Clean soil off tools, boots, and equipment before moving between fields, and avoid letting irrigation or run-off water flow from infested to clean areas.
- **Manage soil conditions:** Where practical, raising soil pH towards neutral by liming (guided by a soil test) and using nitrate rather than ammonium nitrogen sources can reduce disease severity. Maintaining adequate potassium also helps.
- **Manage nematodes:** Because root-knot nematodes make Fusarium wilt much worse, controlling them (see `diseases/root_knot_nematodes.md`) is an important part of managing the disease.
- **Soil solarisation:** In hot regions, covering moist soil with clear plastic for several weeks during the hottest part of the year can reduce the fungus in the top layer of soil, which is helpful especially in nursery beds.
- **Chemical limits:** Fungicide sprays do not control Fusarium wilt effectively. Any soil treatments or biological products should be discussed with a qualified agricultural extension officer, who can confirm what is locally registered, approved, and appropriate; farmers must not rely on chemicals as a primary control.

## Prevention
- **Resistant varieties:** Grow tomato varieties bred for resistance to Fusarium wilt, choosing ones that resist the races present in the local area. Resistance is the single most effective and economical control. Variety labels sometimes indicate resistance to Fusarium races (for example F, FF, or FFF designations); confirm suitability with an extension officer.
- **Crop rotation:** Rotate tomato with unrelated crops such as cereals, maize, or grasses for at least three to four seasons in infested fields. Because the fungus is host-specific to tomato, rotation gradually reduces its soil population.
- **Clean planting material:** Use certified, disease-free seed and raise seedlings in clean or sterilised (for example solarised) nursery soil, so transplants do not carry the fungus into the field.
- **Nematode management:** Keep root-knot nematodes in check through rotation, resistant varieties, and soil health measures, since nematode damage opens the door to Fusarium infection.
- **Soil health and drainage:** Maintain good soil structure, organic matter, and drainage; healthy, unstressed root systems resist infection better.
- **Sanitation:** Remove and destroy crop debris after harvest, control volunteer tomato plants, and clean tools and equipment to avoid spreading contaminated soil.
- **Balanced nutrition:** Avoid excess ammonium nitrogen, maintain adequate potassium, and follow balanced fertility practices to keep plants vigorous and less susceptible.

## Monitoring Recommendations
- Scout regularly from the seedling stage onward, watching for one-sided yellowing and daytime wilting that are the hallmark early signs.
- When a plant wilts, cut the lower stem lengthwise to check for brown vascular discolouration, and do the water-streaming test to rule out bacterial wilt.
- Note the pattern of affected plants; Fusarium wilt often appears in patches that expand over seasons in the same field.
- Check roots of removed plants for the galls caused by root-knot nematodes, which indicate a linked problem.
- Keep records of which fields and areas are affected so that resistant varieties and rotation can be planned for those fields.

## When to Consult an Agricultural Extension Officer or Plant Health Specialist
- When tomato plants wilt and the farmer needs to determine whether the cause is Fusarium wilt, bacterial wilt, Verticillium wilt, or another problem, since management and variety choice depend on the correct diagnosis.
- Before selecting varieties, to identify resistant options matched to the races present locally.
- When planning rotations, soil treatments, or liming, to confirm appropriate practices and, for any products, the locally registered and approved options.
- When wilt appears in a new field or spreads across the farm, to plan sanitation and prevent movement of contaminated soil.
- When Fusarium wilt occurs together with root-knot nematodes, to design a combined management plan.

## Frequently Asked Questions
**Q: How do I tell Fusarium wilt from bacterial wilt?**
A: Cut the lower stem of a wilting plant and place it in a glass of clear water. Bacterial wilt produces milky white threads streaming from the cut within minutes; Fusarium wilt does not. Fusarium wilt also shows brown discolouration in the vascular ring and often one-sided yellowing, while bacterial wilt causes rapid, complete wilting with a slimy stem. See `diseases/tomato_bacterial_wilt.md`.

**Q: Can I get rid of the fungus from my soil?**
A: Not fully. The fungus forms tough resting spores that survive in soil for many years, so infested fields cannot be cleared. Management relies on resistant varieties, long rotations with unrelated crops, and avoiding spread of contaminated soil.

**Q: Will a fungicide spray save my wilting plants?**
A: No. The fungus lives inside the plant's water-conducting vessels where sprays cannot reach, and there is no effective curative spray. Prevention through resistant varieties and clean soil is the only reliable approach.

**Q: Why do only some of my plants wilt while others stay healthy?**
A: Infection depends on where the fungus is concentrated in the soil, on root wounds, and on individual plant susceptibility. Fusarium wilt typically appears in patches and spreads outward over seasons, and resistant plants growing in the same soil may remain healthy.

**Q: Does planting tomato after maize help?**
A: Yes. Because the tomato form of the fungus attacks only tomato, rotating with unrelated crops such as maize or other cereals for several seasons gradually reduces the fungus in the soil and is a valuable control measure.

**Q: My variety is labelled resistant but some plants still wilted. Why?**
A: Resistance is usually specific to certain races of the fungus. If a different race is present in your soil, a variety resistant only to another race can still be affected. Also, severe nematode damage can partly overcome resistance. Consult an extension officer about which races occur locally.

**Q: Is fruit from an infected plant safe to eat?**
A: The fungus is not a human health hazard, but severely infected plants produce little usable fruit, and any fruit should be judged on its own condition. The main loss from Fusarium wilt is the death of the plant and the resulting drop in yield.
