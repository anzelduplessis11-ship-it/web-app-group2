# Cotton Bacterial Blight

## Disease Name
Cotton Bacterial Blight (CBB), also known as angular leaf spot or, in its stem-girdling form, blackarm disease of cotton.

## Scientific Name
Caused by the bacterium *Xanthomonas citri* pv. *malvacearum* (previously classified as *Xanthomonas campestris* pv. *malvacearum* or *Xanthomonas axonopodis* pv. *malvacearum* in older literature).

## Crops Affected
- Cotton (*Gossypium hirsutum*, *Gossypium barbadense*, and other cultivated *Gossypium* species) is the primary and economically significant host.
- Some wild *Gossypium* relatives and other malvaceous plants can host the bacterium and may act as reservoirs in some regions, though cultivated cotton is the crop of concern for farmers.

## Cause
Cotton bacterial blight is caused by infection of cotton plants with the bacterium *Xanthomonas citri* pv. *malvacearum*. The bacterium enters plant tissue through natural openings such as stomata and hydathodes, as well as through wounds caused by wind-blown sand, insect feeding, or mechanical damage, and it multiplies within leaf, stem, and boll tissue.

## Symptoms
- **Seedling stage**: dark, water-soaked lesions on cotyledons and young leaves; severe seedling infection can cause damping-off or death of young plants, sometimes called seedling blight.
- **Leaf lesions**: small, angular, water-soaked spots bounded by leaf veins, which later turn dark brown to black; lesions often appear first on the undersides of leaves and can be seen as translucent when leaves are held up to light in early stages.
- **Vein and midrib infection**: dark streaking along leaf veins and the midrib, which can extend into the petiole.
- **Blackarm (stem) phase**: black, elongated lesions on stems, branches, and petioles that can girdle the stem, causing the branch or, in severe cases, the whole plant above the lesion to wilt and break off, giving the "blackarm" appearance.
- **Boll lesions**: circular to irregular, water-soaked spots on bolls that become sunken and black as they mature; internal boll rot can follow, especially when secondary organisms invade damaged tissue, reducing lint yield and quality.
- **Defoliation**: in severe infections, extensive leaf lesions lead to premature leaf drop, reducing the plant's ability to support boll development.
- A bacterial ooze may sometimes be visible on fresh lesions in humid conditions, which can help distinguish bacterial blight from purely fungal leaf spots.

## Conditions Favouring Infection
| Factor | Effect on disease risk |
|---|---|
| Warm, humid weather | Favours bacterial multiplication and spread |
| Rainfall, especially wind-driven rain | Splashes bacteria onto healthy tissue and creates infection-favourable wetness |
| Overhead or furrow irrigation that wets foliage | Provides moisture needed for infection, similar to rainfall |
| Dense plant canopy with poor air movement | Prolongs leaf wetness and humidity around foliage |
| Wind-blown sand or mechanical injury | Creates wounds that allow bacterial entry |
| Contaminated or infected seed used for planting | Introduces inoculum directly into a new crop from the outset |
| Continuous cotton cropping without rotation | Allows carry-over of the bacterium in crop residue between seasons |

## How It Spreads
- Contaminated seed is the primary means by which the disease is introduced into a new field or region, since the bacterium can survive on and within the seed coat and, to a lesser extent, internal seed tissue.
- Once present in a field, the bacterium spreads readily via rain splash and wind-driven rain, moving bacteria from infected leaves, stems, and bolls onto healthy tissue.
- Contaminated tools, equipment, and workers' hands and clothing moving through wet foliage can spread the bacterium mechanically between plants and across a field.
- Infected crop residue left in or on the soil after harvest can harbour the bacterium and serve as a source of infection for the following season's crop if susceptible cotton is replanted without adequate residue management or rotation.
- Insects that feed on cotton tissue can occasionally contribute to spread by creating wounds or, less commonly, by carrying bacteria between plants, though this is considered secondary to seed-borne introduction and rain-splash spread.

## Risk Level
- High in regions with warm, humid growing seasons and significant rainfall during the growing period, particularly where planting relies on farm-saved or uncertified seed.
- Yield losses vary with the severity and timing of infection but can be substantial in years with sustained wet weather, through combined effects of defoliation, boll rot, and reduced lint quality.
- Risk is comparatively lower in drier growing regions or seasons, though seed-borne inoculum means the disease can still appear even in areas with historically low pressure if infected seed is planted.

## Treatment Options
- There is no curative treatment that reverses lesions already present on leaves, stems, or bolls; management is focused on protecting healthy tissue from further infection and reducing the overall bacterial population in the field.
- Copper-based bactericides (such as copper hydroxide or copper oxychloride) can be applied as a protectant spray to slow the spread of the disease once it appears, particularly ahead of forecast rain or irrigation events.
- Seed treatment before planting, using appropriate seed-applied bactericidal or antibacterial treatments, can reduce the introduction of seed-borne inoculum, and is generally more effective as a preventive measure than foliar treatment after symptoms appear.
- Farmers should confirm with a local agricultural extension officer or agro-input supplier which specific copper-based or seed-treatment products are currently registered for cotton bacterial blight control in their country, along with correct rates and timing, since registrations and recommendations vary by location.
- Once the stem-girdling (blackarm) phase is established on a plant, chemical treatment has limited ability to save the affected branch or plant, and management shifts toward preventing further spread within the field.

## Prevention
- Use certified, pathogen-tested cotton seed, ideally acid-delinted and treated, as the single most effective step in preventing introduction of the disease into a new planting.
- Plant cotton varieties with documented resistance or tolerance to bacterial blight where these are available and suited to local growing conditions.
- Practise crop rotation with non-host crops for at least one to two seasons to reduce carry-over of the bacterium in crop residue and soil.
- Destroy or thoroughly incorporate cotton crop residues after harvest rather than leaving infected plant debris on the soil surface into the next season.
- Avoid working in or irrigating fields when foliage is wet, since this increases the risk of mechanically spreading bacteria between plants.
- Where irrigation is used, favour methods that minimise prolonged leaf wetness, such as furrow irrigation, over overhead sprinkler systems in high-risk areas.
- Maintain adequate plant spacing to improve air circulation and reduce the duration of leaf wetness within the canopy.
- Avoid saving and replanting seed from fields that showed bacterial blight symptoms in the previous season, since the bacterium can be carried on the seed coat.

## Monitoring Recommendations
- Inspect seedlings soon after emergence for water-soaked lesions on cotyledons and young leaves, since early seedling infection can indicate seed-borne inoculum requiring attention for future seasons.
- Scout fields regularly through the growing season, particularly after rainfall or irrigation events, checking the undersides of leaves for angular, water-soaked spots.
- Examine stems and branches for early blackening or lesions that could indicate the onset of the blackarm phase, especially in fields with a history of the disease.
- Check bolls periodically for water-soaked or sunken black spots, particularly in wet seasons, since boll infection directly affects yield and lint quality.
- Keep records of disease incidence by field and season to inform future decisions on variety choice, rotation planning, and seed sourcing.

## When to Consult an Agricultural Extension Officer or Plant Health Specialist
- When angular leaf lesions, blackarm stem symptoms, or boll lesions are first observed, to confirm the diagnosis and receive guidance on an appropriate response for the current season.
- Before selecting cotton seed for planting, particularly when considering farm-saved seed, to confirm whether it is likely to carry bacterial blight inoculum and whether certified alternatives are available.
- When planning crop rotations or residue management after a season with notable bacterial blight symptoms, to receive guidance on appropriate rotation length and residue handling for local conditions.
- If disease severity appears unusually high despite following standard preventive practices, since this may indicate a need for revised variety selection, seed sourcing, or field management.
- When choosing cotton varieties for planting in an area with a known history of bacterial blight, to identify locally recommended resistant or tolerant varieties.
