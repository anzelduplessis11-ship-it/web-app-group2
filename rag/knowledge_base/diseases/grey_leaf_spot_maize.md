# Grey Leaf Spot of Maize

## Overview
Grey leaf spot (GLS) is one of the most economically important foliar diseases of maize (*Zea mays*) in sub-Saharan Africa. It is a fungal disease that destroys leaf tissue, reducing the green leaf area the plant needs for grain filling. Where it becomes severe before or during grain fill, it can cause yield losses of 20 to 60 percent, and in highly susceptible varieties under continuous maize cultivation losses can be even higher. The disease is favoured by warm, humid, cloudy conditions and is strongly linked to minimum-tillage and continuous-maize systems where infected crop residue is left on the soil surface. GLS is a mid- to late-season disease: it usually appears around or after flowering (tasselling and silking) and does its greatest damage during the grain-filling period. Because it thrives in the mid-altitude and highland maize belts of eastern and southern Africa, it is a priority disease for smallholder farmers in Kenya, Ethiopia, Uganda, Tanzania, Rwanda, Zambia, Zimbabwe, Malawi, and South Africa. Integrated management built around resistant hybrids and open-pollinated varieties, crop rotation, and residue management is the foundation of control. See `crops/maize.md` for general agronomy.

## Scientific Name
Grey leaf spot of maize is caused by fungi in the genus *Cercospora*. Two closely related species are recognised: *Cercospora zeae-maydis* and *Cercospora zeina*. In much of eastern and southern Africa the dominant pathogen is *Cercospora zeina*, whereas *Cercospora zeae-maydis* is more common in North America. For practical field management the two species behave similarly and are managed the same way. The fungus is an ascomycete that reproduces mainly by producing asexual spores called conidia.

## Crops Affected
- Maize (*Zea mays*) is the principal and economically most important host (see `crops/maize.md`).
- Both grain maize and green (fresh) maize are affected, as well as sweetcorn.
- The fungi are highly specialised to maize and do not cause significant disease on unrelated crops such as beans or groundnuts, which is why rotation away from maize is effective.

## Cause
Grey leaf spot is caused by infection of maize leaves by *Cercospora zeina* or *Cercospora zeae-maydis*. The fungus survives between seasons in and on infected maize residue (leaves, stalks, and cobs) left on or near the soil surface. When warm, humid conditions return, the fungus produces conidia on this residue. These spores are carried by wind and rain splash onto the lower leaves of a new maize crop. Spores that land on a wet leaf germinate and the fungus enters the leaf mainly through the stomata (natural breathing pores). Inside the leaf it grows in the tissue between the leaf veins, killing cells and producing the characteristic rectangular lesions. The disease then moves up the plant from older, lower leaves to younger, upper leaves as the season progresses and as spores are produced on new lesions.

## Symptoms
Symptom development follows a clear progression tied to crop growth stage:

- **Early leaf stage (before tasselling):** The first symptoms are small, pinpoint tan to light-brown spots, often surrounded by a faint yellow halo, usually on the lowest, oldest leaves nearest the ground. At this stage the spots are easy to miss and can be confused with other leaf spots or minor injury.
- **Lesion expansion (around tasselling and silking):** As lesions mature, they take on the diagnostic form of GLS: long, narrow, rectangular lesions that are grey to tan, running parallel to the leaf veins and sharply limited by the veins on either side. This "boxy," vein-limited shape is the single most useful feature for identifying the disease. Lesions are typically 3 to 7 cm long and a few millimetres wide. When you hold a mature lesion up to the light, it often appears grey because of the mat of spores forming on its surface.
- **Grain-filling stage (the damaging phase):** Under favourable weather, lesions multiply rapidly and merge (coalesce), causing large areas of the leaf to die and turn grey-brown. Severe blighting moves up the plant toward the ear leaf and above. When the ear leaf and the leaves above it are killed during grain fill, the plant cannot fill the grain properly.
- **Late stage:** Heavily infected plants show extensive leaf death (blighting), premature drying, weak stalks, and poor grain fill. Because photosynthesis is lost, the plant may draw reserves from the stalk, leading to stalk rot and lodging (plants falling over), which further reduces harvestable yield.

Field scouting tip: examine lesions on both sides of the leaf and against the light. GLS lesions are typically flat, do not have concentric rings, and are strictly bounded by the veins.

## Conditions Favouring Infection
- Warm temperatures, generally in the range of 22 to 30 degrees Celsius.
- High relative humidity (above 90 percent) and long periods of leaf wetness from dew, mist, or light rain; extended dew periods of 11 to 13 hours strongly favour infection.
- Overcast, cloudy weather that keeps the canopy humid and slows leaf drying.
- Fields with a history of maize and abundant maize residue on the soil surface (minimum or zero tillage, continuous monocropping).
- Dense, closed canopies and low-lying, sheltered fields where humidity stays high and air movement is poor.
- Mid-altitude to highland zones with mild temperatures and frequent morning dew are especially prone.

## How It Spreads
- The fungus survives from one season to the next primarily in infected maize residue on the soil surface; this residue is the main source of the first infections each season.
- Conidia (spores) produced on residue and on lesions are spread short distances by wind and by rain splash to nearby leaves and plants.
- Wind can carry spores between neighbouring fields, so a heavily infected field can seed disease in adjacent maize.
- The disease progresses up the plant and across the field over several weeks as each new generation of lesions produces more spores.
- Movement of infected residue or soil, and continuous cropping of maize on the same land, help the pathogen build up over successive seasons.
- The fungus is not efficiently carried on seed, so seed is not an important means of spread; residue and continuous cropping are the key drivers.

## Risk Level
**High** in the mid-altitude and highland maize belts of eastern and southern Africa, and **Very High** where susceptible varieties are grown continuously under reduced tillage with residue left on the surface. The justification: the disease attacks during grain fill, directly reducing the leaf area that fills the grain; it builds up strongly in continuous-maize, minimum-till systems that are increasingly common; and susceptible varieties combined with humid highland weather can lose more than half of their yield. Risk is lower (Moderate to Low) in hot, dry lowlands, in seasons with little dew and rain during grain fill, and where resistant varieties are grown in rotation with clean tillage.

## Treatment Options
- **Integrated approach first:** The most reliable, affordable control for smallholders is genetic resistance combined with rotation and residue management. Chemical control is a supplement, justified only when a susceptible crop with good yield potential faces high disease pressure before grain fill is complete.
- **Fungicide classes:** Where fungicide use is justified and affordable, foliar fungicides can protect the upper leaves. Active-ingredient classes used against GLS include triazole (DMI) fungicides, strobilurin (QoI) fungicides, and pre-mixed products combining a triazole with a strobilurin. These are most effective when applied preventively or at the very first sign of disease, targeting protection of the ear leaf and the leaves above it.
- **Timing:** The key window is from just before tasselling to early grain fill, when protecting the upper canopy gives the greatest yield benefit. Fungicide applied after the upper leaves are already heavily blighted gives little return.
- **Resistance management:** To slow fungicide resistance, avoid repeated use of strobilurin-only products; alternate or mix modes of action as advised locally.
- **Important safety and legal note:** Never rely on brand names. Farmers must confirm which fungicide active ingredients are currently registered and approved for maize in their country, the correct dose, the pre-harvest interval, and safe handling, with a qualified agricultural extension officer or plant health specialist before buying or spraying.

## Prevention
- **Grow resistant varieties:** This is the single most cost-effective control. Many maize hybrids and open-pollinated varieties released by national programmes carry good levels of GLS resistance. Ask extension services for varieties bred for GLS resistance suited to your altitude.
- **Rotate crops:** Break the maize cycle by rotating with non-host crops such as beans (`crops/common_beans.md`), groundnuts (`crops/groundnut.md`), soybean, or other legumes for at least one season. Rotation starves the fungus by removing its host and letting residue decompose.
- **Manage residue:** Where feasible, incorporate or bury infected maize residue, or remove and compost it, to speed decomposition and reduce the spore source. Balance this against soil-conservation needs; where residue must be retained on the surface for erosion control, lean more heavily on resistant varieties and rotation.
- **Avoid continuous maize on the same land**, especially under minimum tillage.
- **Use appropriate plant spacing** to avoid an overly dense, humid canopy, following local recommendations for your variety and rainfall.
- **Manage nitrogen sensibly:** balanced fertility keeps plants vigorous; avoid practices that create an excessively dense, humid canopy.
- **Field siting and sanitation:** where possible, avoid planting susceptible maize in low, sheltered, humid fields immediately downwind of last season's heavily infected maize.

## Monitoring Recommendations
- Begin scouting from the mid-vegetative stage, and increase the frequency from tasselling onward, which is the critical period.
- Walk a "W" or zig-zag pattern across the field and examine plants at several points rather than only along the edges.
- On each plant, inspect the lower and middle leaves first for the earliest tan flecks, then track how far up the plant lesions have reached.
- Record roughly how many lesions are present and whether they have reached the ear leaf. The ear leaf and the two leaves above it are the most important to protect, because they contribute most to grain filling.
- Note the weather: extended humid, cloudy, dewy spells signal rising risk and should trigger closer scouting and earlier decisions.
- Scout more intensively in fields with a maize history, heavy surface residue, and susceptible varieties.
- Keep simple season-to-season notes on which fields and varieties suffered most, to guide rotation and variety choices next season.

## When to Consult an Agricultural Extension Officer or Plant Health Specialist
- When you first find rectangular, vein-limited grey lesions and are unsure whether it is grey leaf spot or a look-alike such as northern leaf blight, common rust, or a nutrient problem (see `diseases/northern_leaf_blight_maize.md` and `diseases/maize_common_rust.md`).
- Before choosing a variety, to confirm which GLS-resistant hybrids or open-pollinated varieties are recommended and available for your altitude and season.
- Before buying or applying any fungicide, to confirm the locally registered active ingredient, correct dose, timing, pre-harvest interval, and safe handling.
- When the disease reaches the ear leaf before or early in grain fill, to decide whether a fungicide application is economically justified.
- If GLS returns severely year after year despite your efforts, to review rotation, residue management, and variety selection.
- If you observe stalk rot and lodging following severe leaf blighting, to plan an early harvest and adjust management for next season.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: How do I tell grey leaf spot apart from northern leaf blight?**
A: Grey leaf spot lesions are long, narrow, and rectangular, with straight sides that are strictly limited by the leaf veins, giving a "boxy" look. Northern leaf blight lesions are larger, cigar- or canoe-shaped, grey-green to tan, and are not confined by the veins. If lesions are boxy and vein-bounded, suspect grey leaf spot; if they are large elongated ovals crossing veins, suspect northern leaf blight. When unsure, consult an extension officer.

**Q: Why did grey leaf spot get worse after I stopped ploughing and left residue on the surface?**
A: The fungus survives on maize residue left on the soil surface. Leaving infected residue exposed keeps a large spore source close to the new crop, so reduced tillage combined with continuous maize increases disease. If you retain residue for soil conservation, rely more on resistant varieties and crop rotation to compensate.

**Q: Will grey leaf spot spread from my maize to my beans or groundnuts?**
A: No. The fungus is specialised to maize and does not cause significant disease on beans, groundnuts, or other unrelated crops. This is exactly why rotating maize with legumes is an effective way to reduce the disease.

**Q: Is it too late to spray once I see grey lesions on the lower leaves?**
A: Not necessarily. A few lesions low on the plant before tasselling is an early warning. If you have a susceptible, high-potential crop and humid weather, protecting the upper leaves around tasselling to early grain fill can still pay off. Once the ear leaf and the leaves above are already heavily blighted, spraying gives little benefit. Discuss timing with an extension officer.

**Q: Can I save seed from an infected crop?**
A: The fungus does not spread efficiently on seed, so grey leaf spot itself is not primarily a seed-borne problem. However, saving seed from a diseased, poorly filled crop can carry over low vigour and other issues. For maize, using certified seed of a resistant variety is strongly preferred.

**Q: Does grey leaf spot cause the plant to fall over?**
A: Indirectly, yes. When the disease kills a large part of the leaf area during grain fill, the plant draws stored sugars from the stalk to fill the grain. This weakens the stalk and makes it prone to stalk rot and lodging (falling over), which adds to yield loss and makes harvesting harder.

**Q: Which is the most important control for a smallholder with a limited budget?**
A: Growing a GLS-resistant variety, combined with rotating away from maize for a season and managing infected residue. This combination is low-cost, does not require spraying equipment, and addresses the source of the disease rather than only treating symptoms.
