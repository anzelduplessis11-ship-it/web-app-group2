# Banana Fusarium Wilt (Panama Disease)

## Disease Name
Banana Fusarium Wilt, widely known as Panama Disease

## Scientific Name
Caused by the soil-borne fungus *Fusarium oxysporum* f. sp. *cubense* (Foc), a specialised form of a common soil fungus that attacks only banana and plantain (*Musa* species). Several races are recognised, differing in the varieties they attack:

- Race 1 devastated the once-dominant Gros Michel export variety in the mid-20th century.
- Race 2 mainly affects some cooking bananas and plantain-type cultivars, such as Bluggoe.
- Race 4 includes a "subtropical race 4," which attacks Cavendish bananas only when plants are stressed (for example by cold or waterlogging), and Tropical Race 4 (TR4), an especially aggressive strain that attacks Cavendish and many other cultivars, including some previously resistant to earlier races, without needing additional stress.

TR4 is considered one of the most serious threats to banana production worldwide and has been confirmed in parts of Africa, including Mozambique, in addition to Asia, Australia, and the Middle East.

## Crops Affected
- Banana and plantain (*Musa* species and hybrids), across cooking, dessert (including Cavendish), and beer/brewing types (see `crops/banana_and_plantain.md`).
- Susceptibility differs strongly by cultivar and by which race of the fungus is present; a variety resistant to one race may be fully susceptible to another, particularly TR4.
- Enset (*Ensete ventricosum*), a related crop grown in some highland areas, belongs to the same plant family but is not the primary concern for this disease.

## Cause
Infection by the soil-borne fungus *Fusarium oxysporum* f. sp. *cubense*, which survives in soil as thick-walled resting spores (chlamydospores) for decades, even in the absence of banana plants. The fungus enters through the roots, often at points of wounding, and grows into the plant's water-conducting tissue (xylem). As the fungus multiplies and the plant reacts to the infection, the vascular vessels become blocked, cutting off the movement of water to the leaves and causing the wilting and yellowing that give the disease its common name.

## Symptoms
- Yellowing that typically begins at the margins of the oldest (lowest) leaves and progresses inward and upward through younger leaves as the disease advances.
- Leaves wilt, collapse at the petiole, and hang down around the pseudostem ("skirting"), eventually drying to a brown colour.
- Splitting or cracking at the base of the pseudostem is a characteristic external sign in many cases.
- When the pseudostem is cut across near the base, the internal vascular tissue shows yellow-brown to dark brown or reddish-brown discolouration and streaking; the corm (underground stem) may show similar discolouration.
- Younger, newly emerging leaves may remain green longer but are eventually affected, and plants often become progressively stunted.
- Infected mats rarely recover; plants may die before producing a bunch, or may produce small, poorly filled bunches.
- Symptoms can resemble those of banana Xanthomonas wilt (see `diseases/banana_xanthomonas_wilt.md`) and moko disease, but Fusarium wilt does not produce the yellowish bacterial ooze seen in bacterial wilts.

## Conditions Favouring Infection
- Presence of the fungus in the soil, often introduced through infected planting material in the past.
- Growing susceptible cultivars; Cavendish varieties are highly vulnerable to TR4, while susceptibility of other cultivars depends on which race is present locally.
- Root wounding from cultivation, nematode damage, or waterlogging, which creates entry points for the fungus.
- Poorly drained, waterlogged soils and low soil pH, which favour fungal survival and plant stress.
- Continuous banana cultivation on the same land without rotation, allowing the fungus to build up.
- Movement of water, soil, or planting material from infested areas into clean fields.

## How It Spreads
- Primarily spread through infected planting material, particularly suckers taken from diseased mats; even tissue-cultured plants can carry the fungus if not properly produced and indexed from clean sources.
- Spread within and between fields through contaminated soil carried on tools, footwear, vehicle tyres, and animals.
- Moved by surface water, irrigation water, and floodwater draining from infested fields.
- Spread through infected plant debris left in the field or moved to new areas.
- Not spread through the air; this is fundamentally a soil-borne and planting-material-borne disease, which is why biosecurity around soil and plant movement is central to its control.
- Long-distance spread between regions and countries has historically occurred through movement of infected planting material and contaminated soil, which is how TR4 has expanded its range internationally.

## Risk Level
- Very high, and among the most serious diseases affecting banana production globally.
- No cure exists once a plant or field is infected, and the fungus can persist in soil for 20 years or more, making infested land effectively unusable for susceptible cultivars indefinitely.
- Where TR4 is present, the risk is especially severe because it can overcome resistance that protects against other races, threatening Cavendish and many other widely grown cultivars.
- Losses range from the decline of individual mats to the complete loss of banana production on infested land.

## Treatment Options
- There is no chemical or biological cure for a plant once infected; fungicides do not reach the fungus inside the vascular tissue and are not an effective treatment.
- Management is based entirely on containment, sanitation, and prevention of further spread.
- Where a plant is confirmed infected, the recommended approach in many programmes is to kill the plant in place (for example by herbicide injection) rather than cutting it down, since cutting can spread contaminated sap and soil to unaffected mats.
- Restrict all movement of soil, water, tools, and people out of confirmed infected areas until cleaned, following local quarantine guidance.
- Do not attempt to replant susceptible cultivars in a confirmed infested field.
- Any specific disinfectant, herbicide, or containment product should be confirmed as locally approved and appropriate with an agricultural extension officer or plant health authority.

## Prevention
- Use only certified, pathogen-free planting material, preferably tissue-cultured plantlets sourced from a reputable, properly indexed laboratory.
- Never move suckers from an area of unknown or suspect disease status into a clean field.
- Where available and suited to local markets, grow cultivars with documented resistance or tolerance to the races present locally; be aware that resistance to earlier races does not guarantee resistance to TR4.
- Practise strict farm biosecurity: clean and disinfect footwear, tools, and vehicle tyres before moving between fields or farms, and restrict visitor access to production areas.
- Improve field drainage and avoid waterlogging, and minimise root wounding during cultivation and pest management.
- Avoid using irrigation or run-off water sourced from fields of unknown disease status.
- Report suspected new outbreaks, especially of TR4, promptly to plant health authorities to support containment.

## Monitoring Recommendations
- Inspect banana mats regularly for the characteristic pattern of leaf yellowing beginning at the margins of older leaves, progressive wilting, and pseudostem splitting.
- When disease is suspected, cut the pseudostem or corm of a suspect plant across to check for internal vascular discolouration, ideally with guidance from a trained officer given the biosecurity implications of handling suspect TR4 material.
- Pay close attention to any newly planted material, and to plants grown from suckers rather than certified tissue culture.
- Monitor field drainage and water flow patterns, since spread often follows the movement of water.
- Maintain records of the source and health status of all planting material introduced onto the farm.
- Stay informed about the disease status of neighbouring farms and the wider region, particularly regarding TR4.

## When to Consult an Agricultural Extension Officer or Plant Health Specialist
- Immediately when Fusarium wilt or Panama disease is suspected, given the severity of the disease and the importance of correct diagnosis and containment.
- Before introducing new planting material onto the farm, to verify it is certified and pathogen-free.
- If symptoms appear in an area or on a cultivar not previously known to be affected, since this could indicate the presence of a new race, including TR4, which requires urgent reporting and coordinated action.
- When choosing resistant or tolerant cultivars, to confirm which options are suited to the races confirmed in the local area.
- When planning biosecurity measures for a farm bordering an area with known infestation.
