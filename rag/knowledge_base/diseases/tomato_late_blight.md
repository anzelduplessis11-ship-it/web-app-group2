# Tomato Late Blight

## Disease Name
Late Blight

## Scientific Name
Caused by *Phytophthora infestans*, an oomycete pathogen (water mould, not a true fungus, though often grouped with fungal diseases).

## Crops Affected
- Tomato (*Solanum lycopersicum*).
- Potato (*Solanum tuberosum*), which shares the same pathogen and can serve as a source of infection when grown nearby.

## Cause
Infection by the oomycete pathogen *Phytophthora infestans*, which produces spores (sporangia) that spread rapidly under cool, wet conditions.

## Symptoms
- Water-soaked, irregular dark green to brown lesions on leaves, often starting at leaf edges or tips.
- White, fuzzy fungal-like growth (sporulation) visible on the underside of leaves in humid conditions.
- Brown-black lesions on stems that can girdle and collapse them.
- Firm, brown, greasy-looking lesions on fruit, which can expand to cover large areas.
- Rapid plant collapse and death under favourable disease conditions; entire fields can be destroyed within days under severe outbreaks.

## Conditions Favouring Infection
- Cool temperatures (15-20°C) combined with high humidity (above 90%) or prolonged leaf wetness (more than 10-12 hours).
- Frequent rain, fog, or heavy dew.
- Dense planting that restricts airflow and prolongs leaf wetness.
- Overhead irrigation, especially in the late afternoon or evening.

## How It Spreads
- Spores are spread by wind and splashing water (rain, irrigation) over short to moderate distances.
- Can spread rapidly across a field and to neighbouring fields under favourable weather.
- Infected potato tubers or volunteer potato plants can serve as an initial source of infection at the start of a season.
- Does not survive well in dry conditions or in the absence of living host tissue, but can survive in infected plant debris or tubers.

## Risk Level
- Very high. Late blight is one of the most destructive tomato and potato diseases worldwide and can cause complete crop loss within 1-2 weeks under favourable conditions if untreated.

## Treatment Options
- Preventive fungicide programs (protectant fungicides applied before infection) are the primary management tool, as curative options are limited once infection is established.
- Remove and destroy infected plant material promptly to reduce spore load, ideally by burial or burning away from the field, not composting.
- In severe outbreaks, it may be necessary to remove and destroy entire sections of a field to prevent spread to unaffected areas.
- Confirm currently approved and locally registered fungicide active ingredients with a national agricultural regulatory authority or extension officer, since registered products vary by country.

## Prevention
- Use resistant or tolerant varieties where available.
- Avoid overhead irrigation, especially late in the day; use drip irrigation where possible.
- Ensure adequate plant spacing and staking to improve airflow and reduce leaf wetness duration.
- Avoid planting tomato near or after potato crops in the same season.
- Remove volunteer potato and tomato plants and destroy crop debris after harvest.
- Apply preventive fungicide sprays during periods of cool, wet weather known to favour the disease, following locally approved products and label instructions.

## Monitoring Recommendations
- Scout fields at least twice weekly during cool, humid weather, focusing on lower and inner canopy leaves where humidity is highest.
- Check the underside of leaves for early water-soaked lesions and white sporulation.
- Monitor local weather forecasts for conditions favourable to blight development (cool, wet periods) to time preventive action.

## When Farmers Should Consult an Agricultural Extension Officer or Plant Health Specialist
- As soon as suspected late blight symptoms are observed, given the speed at which the disease can destroy a crop.
- Before selecting a fungicide program, to confirm locally registered and effective products and correct application timing.
- If disease appears to be spreading despite fungicide application, as this may indicate resistance to the product used or a need for a different management approach.
- When deciding whether to destroy a section of an affected field, to weigh the risk of further spread against remaining crop value.
