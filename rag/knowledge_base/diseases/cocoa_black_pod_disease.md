# Cocoa Black Pod Disease

## Disease Name
Cocoa Black Pod Disease (also called pod rot or Phytophthora pod rot).

## Scientific Name
Caused mainly by *Phytophthora palmivora* and, in parts of West Africa, by the more aggressive species *Phytophthora megakarya*. Other related *Phytophthora* species, including *Phytophthora capsici*, can also cause black pod symptoms in some regions. These are oomycetes (water moulds), a group of fungus-like organisms rather than true fungi, though they are managed with similar general principles to fungal diseases.

## Crops Affected
- Cacao (*Theobroma cacao*) is the primary host and the crop of economic concern.
- Some *Phytophthora* species involved in black pod also have a wide host range and can affect other crops (for example rubber, coconut, and various fruit and ornamental species in the case of *P. palmivora*), which can serve as alternative sources of the pathogen near cocoa farms.

## Cause
Black pod disease is caused by infection of cacao pods (and sometimes flowers, flower cushions, chupons, and stems) by *Phytophthora* species. These pathogens produce swimming spores (zoospores) that require free water to move and infect plant tissue, and they can also spread as resistant resting spores that survive in soil and plant debris between seasons.

## Symptoms
- **Pod lesions**: small, water-soaked, brownish spots on pods that enlarge rapidly, often turning the entire pod surface dark brown to black within a matter of days under humid conditions.
- **Sporulation**: a whitish, powdery fungal growth frequently develops on the surface of blackened pods during humid weather, representing active spore production capable of infecting nearby healthy pods.
- **Internal damage**: beans inside severely affected pods are typically shrivelled, discoloured, and unusable, even if the pod is harvested before it fully rots.
- **Flower cushion and chupon infection**: infected flower cushions can blacken and die, reducing future pod set, while infected chupons (young vertical shoots) show similar blackening and can die back.
- **Stem canker**: in some cases, particularly with *P. megakarya* and *P. palmivora*, the pathogen can infect the trunk, producing a canker with a reddish-brown, gummy or watery exudate; severe trunk cankers can girdle and kill the tree.
- Pods of any age can be infected, though rapidly expanding lesions on young, actively growing pods often lead to total pod loss, while infection closer to maturity may allow partial bean recovery if pods are harvested promptly.

## Conditions Favouring Infection
| Factor | Effect on disease risk |
|---|---|
| High rainfall and prolonged leaf/pod wetness | Provides the free water zoospores need to swim and infect |
| High relative humidity | Favours spore production and survival on pod surfaces |
| Warm temperatures (roughly 20-30°C) | Speeds up pathogen development and lesion expansion |
| Dense shade and poor canopy ventilation | Prolongs humidity and wetness within the canopy |
| Close tree spacing and unpruned canopies | Increases pod-to-pod and pod-to-soil contact, aiding spread |
| Pods touching soil or leaf litter | Exposes pods to soil-borne inoculum and splash-borne spores |
| Poor drainage in the plantation | Maintains prolonged soil and canopy moisture favourable to the pathogen |

## How It Spreads
- Rain splash is the main short-distance mechanism, moving zoospores and spores from infected pods, cankers, and soil debris onto healthy pods and flower cushions.
- Soil-borne inoculum, including resting spores in leaf litter and old infected pod husks left on the ground, provides a persistent reservoir that is re-activated and splashed upward during rain.
- Direct contact between diseased and healthy pods, particularly where pods are clustered or touching, allows the disease to move within a tree.
- Ants and other insects that move between pods can carry spores incidentally, contributing to localised spread.
- Contaminated tools used during pruning or harvesting can spread the pathogen between trees if not cleaned between uses.
- Movement of infected pod husks, plant material, or contaminated soil between farms can introduce the pathogen into previously unaffected plantations.

## Risk Level
- High. Black pod disease is one of the most economically damaging diseases of cocoa worldwide and is present in essentially all major cocoa-growing regions.
- Typical annual losses are commonly reported in the range of 20-30% of potential yield, but losses can rise to 60-90% in severe outbreaks under sustained wet weather and poor management.
- Where stem canker develops in addition to pod rot, individual trees can be killed, adding a further dimension of loss beyond reduced pod yield.
- Risk is generally higher where *P. megakarya* is present, as this species tends to be more aggressive than *P. palmivora* in the regions where both occur.

## Treatment Options
- Copper-based fungicides (such as copper oxychloride, copper hydroxide, and related copper compounds) are widely used as protectant sprays applied to pods and, where relevant, to the lower trunk to reduce new infections.
- Systemic fungicides from the phosphonate group (including phosphorous acid and related formulations) are used in some cocoa-growing regions for their activity against *Phytophthora* and their ability to move within the plant, complementing protectant copper sprays.
- Spray programmes are generally timed to coincide with the rainy season and periods of active pod development, since protecting young, susceptible pods gives the greatest benefit.
- Farmers should confirm with a local agricultural extension officer or agro-input supplier which specific fungicide products are currently registered for black pod control in their country, along with correct rates, timing, and any pre-harvest intervals, since registrations and recommendations vary by location.
- Removing and destroying infected pods (see prevention) reduces the amount of inoculum present and improves the effectiveness of any fungicide programme.
- Where stem cankers are found, careful excision of the diseased bark and underlying tissue, followed by treatment of the wound as locally recommended, may help limit canker spread on valuable trees, but severely girdled trees usually cannot be saved.

## Prevention
- Practise regular, thorough field sanitation by removing and destroying all diseased pods (a practice often called "pod removal" or "sanitary harvesting") at least weekly during periods of high disease pressure, since infected pods are a major source of ongoing infection.
- Prune cacao trees and manage shade to improve air circulation and light penetration, reducing the length of time pods and foliage remain wet.
- Avoid excessive planting density and maintain adequate spacing between trees to reduce canopy humidity and pod-to-pod contact.
- Improve field drainage to prevent standing water and prolonged soil moisture that favour the pathogen.
- Where available, plant cacao varieties or hybrids with documented tolerance to black pod disease, particularly in areas with a history of severe outbreaks.
- Avoid allowing pods to remain in contact with soil or leaf litter, and clear fallen pod husks and debris from the base of trees regularly.
- Disinfect pruning and harvesting tools between trees, particularly when moving from visibly diseased to healthy trees.
- Apply a well-timed, locally recommended fungicide programme ahead of and during the rainy season, rather than only reacting once symptoms are already widespread.

## Monitoring Recommendations
- Inspect pods at least weekly during the rainy season, checking pods of all sizes and ages across the tree canopy, including those in the lower and inner canopy where humidity tends to be highest.
- Check flower cushions and chupons periodically for blackening or dieback, particularly in trees with a recent history of black pod infection.
- Examine the lower trunk and major branches for early signs of canker, such as discoloured, sunken bark or gummy exudate.
- Track weather patterns, particularly periods of sustained rainfall, since these should prompt closer scouting and consideration of protective fungicide applications.
- Monitor the effectiveness of sanitation practices by confirming that removed pods are being properly destroyed (rather than left near the tree) and that debris is being cleared regularly.

## When to Consult an Agricultural Extension Officer or Plant Health Specialist
- When black pod symptoms are found on a significant proportion of pods despite regular sanitation, to review whether a fungicide programme should be started, adjusted, or intensified.
- Before starting a fungicide spray programme, to confirm which products are currently locally registered for black pod control, correct rates, and safe pre-harvest intervals.
- If stem cankers with gummy or watery exudate are observed on the trunk, to assess the severity and determine whether excision, felling, or other measures are needed to protect the tree and surrounding plantation.
- When selecting new planting material or replanting after severe outbreaks, to identify locally recommended tolerant varieties or hybrids suited to the area.
- If losses appear unusually severe compared with a typical season despite following standard control practices, since this may indicate the presence of a more aggressive *Phytophthora* species or gaps in the management programme that warrant expert assessment.
