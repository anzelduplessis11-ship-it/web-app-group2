# Rice Blast

## Disease Name
Rice Blast

## Scientific Name
Caused by the fungus *Magnaporthe oryzae* (also known by the name of its asexual stage, *Pyricularia oryzae*).

## Crops Affected
- Rice (*Oryza sativa* and *Oryza glaberrima*) is the primary host (see `crops/rice.md`).
- Some other grasses can host related forms of the fungus, but cultivated rice is the crop of main concern.

## Cause
Infection by the fungus *Magnaporthe oryzae*, which produces spores that infect leaves, stems (nodes), and the panicle, and spreads under humid conditions with leaf wetness.

## Symptoms
- Leaf blast: diamond- or spindle-shaped lesions on leaves, typically greyish or white in the centre with brown to reddish-brown margins.
- Lesions can enlarge and join together, killing large areas of leaf tissue and, in severe cases, whole leaves and seedlings.
- Node blast: blackening and weakening of the stem nodes, which can cause the stem to break.
- Neck blast (panicle blast): a brown to black lesion on the neck of the panicle that can cause it to break or prevent grain filling, leading to empty or partially filled grain ("whiteheads").
- Neck and panicle infection is often the most damaging stage; severity varies with variety, growth stage, and weather.

## Conditions Favouring Infection
- Prolonged leaf wetness from dew, rain, or mist, together with high humidity.
- Moderate temperatures with warm days and cooler nights that promote long dew periods.
- High nitrogen fertilisation, which produces soft, lush growth that is more susceptible to infection.
- Dense planting and conditions that keep the canopy humid; upland and rainfed rice can be especially affected where dew is heavy.

## How It Spreads
- Spread mainly by airborne spores carried by wind and splashing water over short to long distances.
- Survives and carries over in infected crop residue and on infected seed, which can introduce the disease into a new crop.
- Spread within and between fields is favoured by successive plantings and by warm, humid, dewy weather.
- Reduced under dry conditions with short dew periods, which limit spore production and infection.

## Risk Level
- High to very high under humid, dewy conditions and on susceptible varieties, particularly when neck blast affects the panicle.
- One of the most important rice diseases worldwide, capable of causing severe yield loss in favourable seasons.
- Losses can range from minor leaf damage to major yield loss when neck and panicle blast are severe.

## Treatment Options
- Protectant and systemic fungicides can help manage blast when applied preventively and timed to protect the crop during high-risk periods, particularly around panicle emergence for neck blast.
- Fungicides are most effective as part of an integrated programme rather than as a rescue treatment for a heavily infected crop.
- Avoid excessive nitrogen, which worsens the disease, and correct management practices alongside any spraying.
- Confirm currently approved and locally registered fungicide active ingredients with a national agricultural regulatory authority or extension officer, since registered products vary by country.

## Prevention
- Plant resistant or tolerant varieties where available and suited to local conditions, as resistance is a central management tool.
- Use certified, disease-free seed and treat seed where recommended locally.
- Apply nitrogen fertiliser at recommended rates and split timings rather than in large single doses, to avoid excessive lush growth.
- Maintain even, adequate plant spacing to reduce canopy humidity.
- Manage water carefully; consistent flooding in irrigated systems can reduce blast pressure compared with intermittent drying, in line with local recommendations.
- Remove and destroy infected crop residue after harvest to reduce carryover.

## Monitoring Recommendations
- Scout fields regularly from the seedling stage onward, focusing on leaves for early diamond-shaped lesions, especially during humid, dewy weather.
- Increase monitoring around panicle emergence and check nodes and panicle necks for neck blast, which is the most damaging phase.
- Pay particular attention to heavily fertilised, dense, or upland/rainfed fields where dew persists.
- Monitor weather for extended dew and humid periods so that preventive action can be timed.

## When Farmers Should Consult an Agricultural Extension Officer or Plant Health Specialist
- If blast lesions are spreading on leaves, or if neck or node blast is found, to confirm the diagnosis and decide whether and when to apply a fungicide.
- Before selecting seed, varieties, or a fungicide programme, to confirm certified seed sources, resistant varieties, and locally registered products and correct timing.
- If the disease recurs season after season despite management, to review variety choice, nitrogen management, and water management.
- If uncertain whether symptoms are due to rice blast versus another rice disease or a nutrient problem, since some leaf symptoms can look similar.
