# Cocoa Swollen Shoot Virus

## Disease Name
Cocoa Swollen Shoot Disease (CSSD), caused by Cocoa Swollen Shoot Virus (CSSV).

## Scientific Name
Cocoa swollen shoot virus, a member of the genus *Badnavirus* (family Caulimoviridae). Multiple genetically distinct strains of CSSV exist across West Africa, and they can differ in how severely and how quickly they affect infected trees.

## Crops Affected
- Cacao (*Theobroma cacao*) is the only crop of major economic concern affected by this virus.
- Some related and ornamental plant species can host the virus without necessarily showing strong symptoms, and certain non-cacao shade or forest trees can act as alternative hosts and reservoirs for the mealybug vectors and, in some cases, the virus itself.

## Cause
Cocoa swollen shoot disease is caused by infection of cacao trees with cocoa swollen shoot virus. The virus is transmitted by several species of mealybugs (small, sap-sucking insects in the family Pseudococcidae) that feed on cacao tissue and acquire the virus from infected plants, then transmit it to healthy trees during subsequent feeding.

## Symptoms
- **Shoot and stem swelling**: characteristic swelling of young shoots and stems, particularly at nodes, which gives the disease its name.
- **Root swelling**: swelling can also occur on roots, contributing to overall decline in tree vigour.
- **Leaf symptoms**: red vein banding or vein clearing on young leaves, along with mosaic patterns, chlorotic mottling, and sometimes distortion of leaf shape.
- **Pod symptoms**: pods on infected trees are frequently smaller, rounded, or otherwise misshapen compared with healthy pods, and bean yield and quality are reduced.
- **General decline**: infected trees typically show a progressive decline in vigour, reduced flowering and pod set, dieback of branches, and, for aggressive virus strains, tree death within one to a few years of infection.
- Symptom severity and speed of decline vary considerably depending on the specific virus strain present, the cacao variety, and the age and health of the tree at the time of infection; some strains cause relatively mild, chronic decline while others cause rapid death.

## Conditions Favouring Infection
- Presence of mealybug vector populations on or near cacao trees, since the virus depends entirely on these insects for transmission between plants.
- Presence of ant species that protect and "farm" mealybugs in exchange for the sugary honeydew mealybugs produce, since these ants shield mealybugs from natural predators and help sustain vector populations.
- Warm conditions that favour mealybug reproduction and activity.
- Proximity to existing infection foci, since new infections typically radiate outward from already-infected trees or nearby alternative host plants harbouring both the virus and its mealybug vectors.
- Presence of shade or forest-edge trees that can host mealybug populations and, in some documented cases, the virus itself, providing a source from which mealybugs move into adjacent cacao.

## How It Spreads
- The virus is transmitted from tree to tree exclusively by mealybug vectors; it is not spread by wind, rain, soil, or mechanical contact between plants in the way many other cacao diseases are.
- Mealybugs acquire the virus while feeding on an infected tree and can transmit it to a healthy tree during subsequent feeding, though the efficiency and persistence of transmission vary by mealybug species and virus strain.
- Because mealybugs have limited mobility on their own, new infections tend to spread gradually outward in expanding rings from an initial infected tree, often assisted by ants that move mealybugs between neighbouring plants.
- The virus is not known to be transmitted through true cacao seed, but vegetative planting material (budwood or cuttings) taken from an infected tree will carry the virus into new plantings.
- Longer-distance spread within a growing region is generally attributed to the gradual, continuous movement of mealybug populations between adjoining farms, rather than any single dramatic long-range mechanism.

## Risk Level
- Very high in West African cocoa-growing regions where the disease is endemic, and it is considered one of the most economically destructive diseases of cacao historically recorded, having been responsible for the loss of very large numbers of cacao trees over past decades in some countries.
- There is no cure for infected trees, and aggressive virus strains can kill trees within a relatively short period, making early detection and removal critical to limiting spread.
- Even where trees are not killed outright, chronic infection substantially reduces pod yield and bean quality over the productive life of the tree.

## Treatment Options
- There is no chemical or biological treatment that cures a tree once infected with cocoa swollen shoot virus; management is entirely based on preventing new infections and removing sources of the virus.
- The standard control approach in affected areas is "cutting-out": removing and destroying infected trees, and in many programmes also removing a buffer ring of surrounding apparently healthy trees, since these are likely to be either already infected but not yet showing symptoms, or at very high risk of imminent infection.
- Insecticide treatments targeting mealybug vectors can help reduce the rate of new infections in surrounding trees, but insecticides alone do not eliminate the virus from already-infected plants and should be used as a supporting measure alongside cutting-out and sanitation.
- Farmers should confirm with a local agricultural extension officer or cocoa authority which insecticide products are currently registered locally for mealybug control on cacao, along with correct rates and application timing, since recommendations and product registrations vary by location.
- Cutting-out programmes are frequently coordinated at an area-wide or community level by cocoa extension services, since isolated action on a single farm is generally far less effective at controlling spread than coordinated regional efforts.

## Prevention
- Plant cacao varieties or hybrids bred for tolerance to cocoa swollen shoot virus where these are available and recommended for the local growing area, since tolerant material can substantially reduce losses even where the virus is present.
- Participate in or support coordinated cutting-out and area-wide control programmes where these are organised by local cocoa authorities, since individual farm action alone is rarely sufficient to control a vector-borne, spreading disease of this kind.
- Monitor for and manage mealybug populations, including control of ant species that protect mealybugs, since reducing vector numbers lowers the rate of new virus transmission.
- Avoid taking budwood, cuttings, or other vegetative planting material from trees showing any swelling, vein banding, mosaic, or other suspicious symptoms.
- Where replanting after cutting-out, follow locally recommended waiting periods and site preparation guidance before establishing new cacao in the same area.
- Maintain vigilance along farm boundaries shared with neighbouring plantations, particularly where those neighbouring areas have a known history of the disease, since new infections commonly enter a farm from adjoining infected trees.
- Remove or manage alternative host plants and shade trees known to harbour mealybug populations near young or valuable cacao plantings, in line with local extension guidance.

## Monitoring Recommendations
- Inspect cacao trees regularly, ideally monthly, for shoot and stem swelling, red vein banding, mosaic leaf patterns, and misshapen pods, paying particular attention to young, actively growing shoots where symptoms often first appear.
- Pay close attention to trees near farm boundaries, shade trees, and any known previous infection points, since new cases typically appear first in these locations.
- Monitor for mealybug colonies on stems, leaf axils, and the undersides of leaves, and note the presence of attending ants, which is a strong indicator of an active mealybug population.
- Keep a simple map or record of any trees removed due to suspected swollen shoot symptoms, to help track the spread pattern over successive seasons and inform future cutting-out decisions.
- Report suspected new outbreaks, particularly in areas with no prior history of the disease, to local extension or cocoa authority contacts promptly, since early detection significantly improves the chances of containing spread.

## When to Consult an Agricultural Extension Officer or Plant Health Specialist
- As soon as shoot or stem swelling, red vein banding, or unusual mosaic patterns are noticed on any cacao tree, given how important early detection and coordinated cutting-out are for limiting the spread of this disease.
- Before removing suspected infected trees, to confirm the diagnosis and to follow correct cutting-out and buffer-zone procedures recommended for the area.
- When planning to replant cacao after a cutting-out operation, to receive guidance on timing, site preparation, and choice of tolerant planting material.
- If the disease appears for the first time in an area with no prior record of it, since rapid reporting and coordinated regional action are important to prevent wider establishment.
- When selecting new cacao planting material or hybrids, to confirm which locally available varieties have documented tolerance to the virus strains present in the region.
