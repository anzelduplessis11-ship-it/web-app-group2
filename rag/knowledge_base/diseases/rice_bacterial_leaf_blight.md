# Rice Bacterial Leaf Blight

## Disease Name
Rice Bacterial Leaf Blight (BLB)

## Scientific Name
Caused by *Xanthomonas oryzae* pv. *oryzae*, a gram-negative bacterium. A related pathovar, *Xanthomonas oryzae* pv. *oryzicola*, causes bacterial leaf streak, a distinct but sometimes co-occurring disease with different symptom patterns.

## Crops Affected
- Rice (*Oryza sativa* and *Oryza glaberrima*), affecting both irrigated lowland and, to a lesser extent, upland and rainfed rice systems.
- Wild rice relatives and certain grass weeds in and around rice fields can host the bacterium, potentially serving as a reservoir between rice crops.

## Cause
Infection by the bacterium *Xanthomonas oryzae* pv. *oryzae*, which typically enters rice plants through natural openings such as hydathodes (water pores at leaf tips and margins) or through wounds, including those created during transplanting when leaf tips are clipped. Once inside, the bacterium multiplies in the vascular (water-conducting) tissue, which explains the characteristic wilting seen in severe seedling infections.

## Symptoms
- Water-soaked, pale green to grey-green lesions typically starting at the leaf tip or margin, often visible as streaks running along the veins.
- Lesions expand and turn yellow, then straw-coloured to greyish-white, with a distinctive wavy or irregular margin separating diseased from healthy tissue.
- In humid conditions, small beads or droplets of milky bacterial ooze may be visible on the surface of young lesions, particularly in the early morning; this ooze dries to form small yellowish crusts.
- **Kresek phase**: a severe, systemic wilting phase that can affect young seedlings and plants up to about 30–40 days after transplanting, in which infected plants wilt rapidly, curl, and may die; this phase is associated with high seedling mortality when conditions are favourable.
- **Pale yellow leaf phase**: a chronic phase in older plants where infected leaves develop a uniform pale yellow to whitish discoloration rather than distinct lesions, sometimes confused with nutrient deficiency.
- Severely affected fields can show widespread leaf drying and a scorched appearance, particularly toward the later part of the season, leading to poor grain fill and reduced yield.

| Symptom Phase | Typical Timing | Key Feature |
|---|---|---|
| Leaf blight (classic) | Any growth stage, common from tillering onward | Water-soaked lesions from leaf tip/margin, wavy border, greyish-white with age |
| Kresek (seedling wilt) | Seedling to early tillering | Rapid wilting and death of young plants |
| Pale yellow leaf | Later growth stages | Uniform pale yellow leaf discoloration without distinct lesion margins |

## Conditions Favouring Infection
- Warm temperatures, generally in the range of 25–34°C, combined with high humidity favour rapid disease development.
- Standing water and prolonged leaf wetness, common in flooded/irrigated lowland rice systems, favour bacterial multiplication and spread.
- Heavy or wind-driven rain and storms, which create leaf wounds and facilitate bacterial entry and splash dispersal.
- High nitrogen fertilisation, particularly excessive or poorly timed applications, produces lush, susceptible growth and is strongly associated with more severe outbreaks.
- Deep floodwater and continuous flooding without adequate drainage.
- Practices that wound leaves, such as clipping seedling tops before transplanting (traditionally done in some areas to ease handling), which create entry points for the bacterium.
- Cultivation of susceptible varieties without confirmed resistance genes in areas with a history of the disease.

## How It Spreads
- The bacterium spreads locally via wind-driven rain and splashing water, which move bacterial ooze from infected leaves onto healthy plants.
- Irrigation water is a significant pathway for spread within and between fields, since the bacterium can survive and move in floodwater and irrigation channels connected across fields.
- Infected seed can carry the bacterium, contributing to introduction of the disease into new areas or seasons, making seed source and any available seed health testing or treatment important considerations.
- Infected crop residue, ratoon (regrowth) rice, and volunteer rice plants can serve as a source of inoculum carried over between seasons.
- Wounds created during transplanting (especially leaf-tip clipping) or from wind/rain damage, insect feeding, or mechanical injury provide entry points that facilitate infection and localised spread.
- Contaminated tools, equipment, and floodwater shared between fields can move the bacterium between locations.

## Risk Level
- High in irrigated lowland rice systems, particularly during the wet season or in areas with frequent storms, high humidity, and standing water.
- One of the most economically damaging bacterial diseases of rice worldwide, with yield losses that can range from moderate to severe (in some reported severe outbreaks, losses have reached very high levels) depending on the growth stage at infection, variety susceptibility, and weather conditions.
- Early infection (kresek phase) causing seedling death is particularly damaging since it can require replanting or result in significant stand loss.
- Risk is lower in drier upland rice systems without standing water, though it can still occur under sufficiently humid, rainy conditions.

## Treatment Options
- There is no effective in-season chemical cure for bacterial leaf blight once plants are infected; management is primarily preventive, relying on resistant varieties and cultural practices.
- Copper-based bactericidal products have shown some suppressive effect in certain contexts but generally provide limited and inconsistent control against this disease compared with resistant varieties and sound field management, and should not be relied upon as a primary control method.
- Some antibiotic-based treatments (e.g., streptomycin-containing products) have been used in some regions for bacterial diseases of rice historically, but their use is restricted or discouraged in many countries due to resistance development and food safety/regulatory concerns; any such product must be locally registered and used strictly according to label and regulatory guidance.
- Draining standing water from severely affected fields where feasible can help reduce further spread within the field by limiting the movement of bacteria in floodwater.
- Remove and destroy severely infected plant debris and volunteer/ratoon rice after harvest to reduce carryover inoculum for the next season.
- Confirm any currently locally registered bactericide products, application guidance, and regulatory restrictions with a national agricultural authority or extension officer before use, since options and restrictions vary significantly by country.

## Prevention
- Plant resistant or tolerant rice varieties carrying confirmed resistance genes (commonly referred to as *Xa* genes in rice breeding) effective against locally present bacterial strains, which is the most effective and sustainable management approach.
- Use certified, pathogen-free seed, and consider seed treatment options recommended locally, since seed-borne inoculum is a known pathway for introducing the disease.
- Avoid clipping seedling leaf tips before transplanting where this practice is used, since it creates wounds that facilitate bacterial entry; if clipping cannot be avoided, minimise the extent of leaf damage.
- Apply nitrogen fertiliser in balanced, split applications based on crop growth stage and soil testing rather than large single doses, since excessive nitrogen strongly favours disease severity.
- Manage field water levels to avoid prolonged deep flooding where practical, and ensure fields have adequate drainage to reduce standing water duration, particularly during high-risk weather periods.
- Remove and destroy infected crop residue, volunteer rice, and ratoon growth after harvest to reduce inoculum carryover between seasons.
- Avoid moving irrigation water, tools, or equipment from known infected fields to healthy fields without appropriate cleaning.

## Monitoring Recommendations
- Scout fields regularly from the seedling stage through grain fill, checking particularly after storms, heavy rain, or periods of high humidity, since these conditions favour rapid disease onset and spread.
- Examine leaf tips and margins for early water-soaked lesions, and check for the characteristic wavy lesion margin as lesions mature.
- Watch closely for wilting symptoms in young seedlings (kresek phase), particularly in the weeks following transplanting in fields with a history of the disease.
- Inspect fields for the pale yellow leaf phase in older plants, distinguishing it from nutrient deficiency by checking for the presence of other classic blight symptoms elsewhere in the field.
- Monitor irrigation water sources and shared canals for signs of disease in upstream fields, since floodwater can carry the bacterium between connected fields.

## When to Consult an Agricultural Extension Officer or Plant Health Specialist
- As soon as water-soaked lesions with a wavy margin or seedling wilting (kresek) are observed, given the potential for rapid spread under favourable warm, humid, and wet conditions.
- Before selecting a rice variety for planting, to confirm which resistance genes are effective against bacterial strains present in the local area.
- If pale yellowing is observed and it is unclear whether this is due to bacterial leaf blight or a nutrient deficiency, since accurate diagnosis affects the appropriate management response.
- If disease appears to be spreading rapidly through shared irrigation water, to discuss water management options and potential coordination with neighbouring farmers.
- Before using any bactericide or antibiotic-based product, to confirm current local registration status and regulatory guidance.
