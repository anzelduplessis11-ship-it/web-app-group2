# Cassava Bacterial Blight

## Disease Name
Cassava Bacterial Blight (CBB)

## Scientific Name
Caused by the bacterium *Xanthomonas phaseoli* pv. *manihotis* (previously classified as *Xanthomonas axonopodis* pv. *manihotis* and *Xanthomonas campestris* pv. *manihotis*).

## Crops Affected
- Cassava (*Manihot esculenta*) is the principal and economically important host (see `crops/cassava.md`).
- Other *Manihot* species can be affected, but cultivated cassava is of primary concern to farmers.

## Cause
Infection by the bacterium *Xanthomonas phaseoli* pv. *manihotis*, which enters the plant through natural openings such as stomata and water pores (hydathodes) on leaves, as well as through wounds caused by wind damage, insects, tools, or storms. Once inside, the bacterium multiplies in the leaf tissue and can move into the vascular system, causing systemic infection that leads to wilting and dieback.

## Symptoms
- Angular, water-soaked spots on leaves that are bounded by the leaf veins; these spots later turn brown and necrotic, often with a yellow halo.
- Spots can enlarge and merge, leading to blighted patches of dead leaf tissue.
- Wilting of leaves and young shoots, which gives the disease its common name; wilting can progress rapidly under favourable conditions.
- A gummy, sticky bacterial exudate (ooze) may appear on infected leaves, petioles, and stems, particularly in humid conditions.
- Dieback of shoot tips, sometimes producing a distinctive "candlestick" appearance where the growing point collapses and dies while surrounding leaves wilt.
- Vascular discolouration can be seen when infected stems are cut, reflecting systemic spread of the bacterium.
- Severe infections cause extensive defoliation, stunting, and dieback of stems, which can significantly reduce both the quality of planting material for the following season and current root yield.

## Conditions Favouring Infection
- Warm, humid weather with frequent rainfall, which favours both bacterial multiplication and its spread by rain splash.
- Wind-driven rain, which physically carries bacteria from infected to healthy plants and creates wounds through which the bacterium can enter.
- High humidity and prolonged leaf wetness, which support bacterial survival and infection on the leaf surface.
- Wounding of plants by wind, hail, insects, or field tools, which provides entry points in addition to natural leaf openings.
- Growing susceptible varieties, and dense planting that increases humidity within the canopy and facilitates plant-to-plant contact.
- Use of infected stem cuttings as planting material, which introduces the bacterium directly into a new planting.

## How It Spreads
- Primarily spread through the use of infected stem cuttings as planting material, which can carry the bacterium even when cuttings appear outwardly healthy.
- Spread within and between plants by wind-driven rain and splashing water, which carries bacteria from infected leaves and stems to healthy tissue.
- Spread on contaminated tools, such as knives and machetes used for pruning or harvesting, if not cleaned between plants.
- Can be spread short distances by insects that move between plants, although rain splash and infected planting material are the dominant routes.
- Long-distance spread between farms and regions occurs mainly through the movement of infected cuttings.

## Risk Level
- High in humid, rainy tropical and subtropical lowland areas, especially during the rainy season.
- Can cause substantial yield loss on susceptible varieties, through both reduced root production and dieback of stems needed for future planting material.
- Losses vary widely depending on variety resistance, weather conditions, and how early infection occurs in the growing season; severe outbreaks under favourable wet conditions can cause serious damage to a crop.

## Treatment Options
- There is no curative bactericide treatment that reliably controls the disease once plants are infected in the field.
- Management is based on sanitation, removal of infected material, and cultural practices that reduce spread.
- Prune out and destroy severely blighted shoots and branches where practical, to reduce the amount of bacteria available for further spread within the field.
- Avoid working in fields, and especially avoid taking cuttings, when foliage is wet, since this can spread the bacterium on tools and hands.
- Any chemical or biological product considered for suppression of bacterial spread should be confirmed as locally registered and appropriate with an agricultural extension officer, since options and effectiveness vary by region.

## Prevention
- Use resistant or tolerant varieties where available and suited to local growing conditions, since variety choice is one of the most effective controls.
- Select planting material only from healthy, disease-free mother plants, ideally from fields with no history of bacterial blight.
- Practise field sanitation: remove and destroy crop debris and severely infected plants after harvest.
- Rotate cassava with non-host crops where bacterial blight has been a recurring problem, to reduce the build-up of bacteria associated with crop residues.
- Avoid excessive plant density to improve air movement through the canopy and reduce humidity around leaves.
- Disinfect cutting tools between plants, particularly when disease is present in the field.
- Avoid moving cuttings from fields with known bacterial blight into clean areas.

## Monitoring Recommendations
- Scout fields regularly during and after the rainy season for angular, water-soaked leaf spots and wilting shoots.
- Check for bacterial ooze on leaves and stems, particularly following humid or rainy periods.
- Inspect stem cuttings intended for planting material for signs of vascular discolouration or dieback before use.
- Note the pattern and extent of dieback within the field, since rapid spread following storms is a warning sign of favourable conditions for the disease.
- Keep records of which fields and varieties have shown blight symptoms to guide future variety and rotation decisions.

## When to Consult an Agricultural Extension Officer or Plant Health Specialist
- When angular leaf spots, wilting, or dieback are observed, to confirm whether the cause is bacterial blight rather than cassava mosaic disease (see `diseases/cassava_mosaic_disease.md`), cassava brown streak disease (see `diseases/cassava_brown_streak_disease.md`), or another problem, since these can produce overlapping symptoms.
- Before selecting planting material from an unfamiliar source, to verify its health status where possible.
- When choosing resistant varieties, to confirm which options are suited to the strains of the bacterium present locally.
- If the disease appears to be spreading rapidly through a field following storms or heavy rain, to plan sanitation and future rotation.
