# Organic and GAP Certification Standards

## Overview
Organic certification and Good Agricultural Practice (GAP) certification are two widely recognised, though distinct, systems that verify a farm's production practices meet a defined set of standards, providing assurance to buyers, regulators, and consumers about how food was grown. Organic certification verifies that food is produced without synthetic pesticides, synthetic fertilisers, or other prohibited inputs and practices, following a defined organic production standard. GAP certification verifies that a farm follows recognised good practices for food safety, environmental management, worker welfare, and traceability, without necessarily restricting the use of synthetic inputs in the way organic standards do. Both systems are increasingly relevant to African farmers seeking to access premium local markets, regional trade, or international export markets, particularly to buyers and retailers who require certified supply. This document explains the general principles, typical requirements, and processes for both types of certification at a pan-African level. Specific certification schemes, standard-setting bodies, accredited certifiers, and detailed technical requirements vary and evolve over time, so farmers should confirm current specific requirements and available certification options with their national agriculture ministry, an accredited certification body, or a qualified extension officer.

## Why This Document Does Not Endorse Specific Certification Schemes
- Multiple organic and GAP standards exist internationally and regionally (including private, national, and international schemes), each with its own specific technical requirements, accredited certifying bodies, and recognition in different markets.
- Which certification a farmer needs depends on the target market and buyer requirements, which vary and change over time as buyers update their sourcing requirements.
- Certification costs, available certifying bodies, and support programmes (including any group or smallholder certification options) differ by country and change over time.
- Farmers should confirm which specific certification standard is required or recognised by their target buyer or market, and identify current accredited certification bodies, through their national agriculture ministry, an export promotion agency, a farmer organisation, or a qualified extension officer.

## Organic Certification

### What Organic Certification Verifies
Organic certification confirms that a farm's production system complies with a recognised organic standard, which generally prohibits or tightly restricts synthetic pesticides, synthetic fertilisers, genetically modified organisms, and certain other inputs, while requiring practices that build soil health and biological diversity. Certified organic status allows produce to be legally marketed and labelled as "organic" (or an equivalent term) in markets that regulate use of that label, and is typically required to sell into formal organic retail or export supply chains.

### General Principles of Organic Production Standards
Although specific standards differ in technical detail, most organic standards share common underlying principles:
- **Prohibition or strict limitation of synthetic inputs**: Synthetic pesticides and synthetic mineral fertilisers are generally prohibited, with permitted alternatives limited to specified natural or approved inputs.
- **Soil health as a foundation**: Organic systems generally emphasise building soil fertility through practices such as composting, crop rotation, cover cropping, and use of organic matter, rather than relying on external synthetic nutrient inputs.
- **Prohibition of genetically modified organisms (GMOs)**: Most organic standards prohibit the use of genetically modified seed or planting material.
- **Ecological pest and weed management**: Reliance on cultural, biological, and mechanical pest and weed control methods rather than synthetic chemical control, consistent with integrated pest management principles.
- **Buffer zones and contamination prevention**: Requirements to maintain buffer distances from conventionally farmed neighbouring land to prevent spray drift or other contamination, and to prevent mixing of organic and non-organic produce during harvest, storage, and transport.
- **Record keeping and traceability**: Detailed records of all inputs used, field operations, and produce movement, allowing verification that organic integrity is maintained from field to buyer.
- **Conversion/transition period**: Land is typically required to be managed according to organic standards for a defined transition period (often measured in years) before produce from that land can be certified and sold as organic, since residual effects of previously used synthetic inputs are assumed to persist for some time.

### General Organic Certification Process
1. **Selecting an applicable standard and certifying body**: The farmer or farmer group identifies which organic standard is relevant to their target market and selects (or is guided by a buyer or exporter toward) an accredited certification body authorised to certify against that standard.
2. **Initial assessment and gap analysis**: The certifying body or a support programme typically assesses current farm practices against the standard's requirements to identify changes needed before certification can proceed.
3. **Transition/conversion period**: The farm operates under organic-compliant practices for the required transition period, during which produce generally cannot yet be sold as certified organic.
4. **Documentation and internal control system**: Farmers must maintain records of inputs, field activities, and (for group certification schemes common among smallholders) participate in an internal control system that monitors member compliance between external audits.
5. **External inspection/audit**: An independent auditor from the certifying body inspects the farm (and, for group certification, a sample of member farms) to verify compliance with the standard, generally on an annual basis for ongoing certification.
6. **Certification decision and certificate issuance**: If compliant, the certifying body issues an organic certificate, typically valid for a defined period and subject to annual re-audit to maintain certification.
7. **Ongoing compliance monitoring**: Certified farms are subject to continued record-keeping obligations and periodic re-inspection, and certification can be suspended or withdrawn for non-compliance, including for prohibited input use detected through testing or record review.

### Group Certification for Smallholders
Because individual certification costs and administrative burden can be prohibitive for small-scale farmers, many organic (and some GAP) schemes offer group certification, where a farmer group or cooperative operates a shared internal control system, and the certifying body audits the group's system plus a sample of individual member farms rather than auditing every farm individually. This significantly reduces the per-farmer cost of certification and is a common pathway for smallholder access to organic markets.

## Good Agricultural Practice (GAP) Certification

### What GAP Certification Verifies
GAP certification verifies that a farm follows recognised good practices across food safety, environmental sustainability, worker health and safety, and animal welfare (where relevant), without necessarily restricting synthetic input use in the way organic standards do. GAP certification is widely required or strongly preferred by supermarkets, retailers, and export buyers, particularly in fresh produce supply chains, as a baseline assurance of responsible production practice and food safety risk management.

### General Areas Covered by GAP Standards
- **Food safety**: Practices to minimise microbial, chemical, and physical contamination risks, covering water quality used for irrigation and washing, hygiene practices for workers handling produce, and safe post-harvest handling and storage.
- **Pesticide and input management**: Requirements to use only registered agrochemical products according to label instructions, observe pre-harvest intervals, maintain application records, and store agrochemicals safely (closely aligned with general agrochemical regulation principles).
- **Traceability**: Systems to trace produce from the specific field and batch through to the point of sale, enabling rapid identification and response if a food safety issue is later identified.
- **Worker health, safety, and welfare**: Requirements addressing safe working conditions, access to protective equipment for hazardous tasks, sanitation facilities, and fair labour practices.
- **Environmental management**: Practices addressing responsible water use, waste management, soil conservation, and protection of biodiversity and natural habitat on and around the farm.
- **Record keeping**: Comprehensive documentation of field activities, input use, water testing, worker training, and corrective actions, which auditors review as central evidence of compliance.

### General GAP Certification Process
The general process closely parallels organic certification: farmers or farmer groups assess current practices against the chosen GAP standard's checklist, address identified gaps (which may include physical infrastructure improvements such as sanitation facilities or safe chemical storage, as well as changes to practices and record-keeping), undergo an external audit by an accredited certification body, and receive certification if compliant, subject to periodic re-audit (commonly annual) to maintain certified status. As with organic certification, group certification options exist for smallholder farmers organised through a cooperative or producer group, sharing costs and a common internal control system.

## Why These Certifications Matter for Farmers
- **Market access and price premiums**: Certified organic or GAP-compliant produce often accesses markets (premium retail, export, certain institutional buyers) closed to uncertified produce, and organic certification in particular can command a price premium in many markets.
- **Buyer requirements**: An increasing number of retailers, processors, and export buyers require GAP certification as a minimum condition of doing business, regardless of whether a price premium is offered, making certification a market access requirement rather than merely an optional upgrade.
- **Improved farm management**: The record-keeping, planning, and monitoring required for certification often improve overall farm management discipline, even independent of the certification's market value.
- **Risk reduction**: GAP practices in particular reduce food safety incidents, worker safety incidents, and the risk of produce rejection due to contamination or excessive pesticide residues.
- **Environmental and social benefits**: Both systems generally encourage more sustainable and responsible farming practices, with organic standards placing particular emphasis on ecological farming methods.

## Common Mistakes
- Assuming organic and GAP certification are interchangeable, when they verify different things and are often required by different buyers for different purposes.
- Underestimating the length and practice changes required during an organic transition/conversion period before certification is possible.
- Neglecting record-keeping, which is frequently the single largest cause of audit failure even when actual field practices are largely compliant.
- Attempting individual certification without exploring available group certification options that could substantially reduce cost for smallholder farmers.
- Allowing accidental contamination (from spray drift, mixing produce, or shared equipment with non-certified neighbours) that compromises organic integrity.
- Letting certification lapse by missing the annual re-audit, losing market access that depended on continuous certification.

## When to Consult an Extension Officer, Certification Body, or Farmer Organisation
- To identify which specific organic or GAP standard is required or preferred by a target buyer or market.
- To find currently accredited certification bodies operating in the relevant country or region.
- To assess current farm practices against a chosen standard and identify the gaps that must be addressed before certification.
- To explore group certification options available through a local cooperative, farmer association, or support programme, which can substantially reduce costs for smallholder farmers.
- To understand the current transition/conversion period required before land can achieve organic certification.
- To get support setting up the record-keeping and internal control systems typically required to pass an external audit.
