# Pesticide Safety and Handling

## Overview
Pesticides (including insecticides, herbicides, and fungicides) can protect crops, but they are also toxic and can harm people, animals, water, pollinators, and the environment if handled carelessly. Safe use protects the farmer, their family, consumers, and the surrounding ecosystem. This document covers safe purchase, label reading, protective equipment, mixing, application, pre-harvest intervals, storage, disposal, and first aid. Pesticides should be one part of an integrated pest management approach, not the first or only response (see `sustainability/sustainable_farming_practices.md`). For the regulatory framework, see `regulations/agricultural_regulations_overview.md`. This guidance refers to product categories and active-ingredient classes only, never brand names; always confirm locally registered products and rules with an agricultural extension officer or the national regulatory authority.

## Safe Purchase: Registered Products Only
- Buy only pesticides that are registered and approved for use in your country and for your specific crop and pest; unregistered, counterfeit, or repackaged products may be ineffective, illegal, or dangerous.
- Purchase from reputable, licensed dealers, and check that products are in their original, sealed containers with intact, readable labels.
- Confirm with an extension officer or the regulatory authority which active-ingredient classes are currently approved for your target pest and crop, since registrations and restrictions change over time.
- Avoid products that are banned or restricted; some older or highly hazardous active ingredients are restricted precisely because of their danger to people and the environment.

## Reading and Following the Label
- The label is a legal instruction, not a suggestion. Read it fully before purchase and again before use.
- Key information includes: the target pests and approved crops, the correct dose and dilution, application method and timing, required protective equipment, the pre-harvest interval, re-entry period, first-aid instructions, and storage and disposal guidance.
- Never use a product on a crop or pest not listed on its label, and never exceed the stated dose; more is not more effective and increases risk, residues, and resistance.
- If the label is missing, unreadable, or in a language you cannot follow, do not use the product until you can obtain clear guidance.

## Personal Protective Equipment (PPE)
- Wear the protective equipment specified on the label. This commonly includes items to protect the skin (long sleeves and trousers, gloves, boots), eyes (goggles or face shield), and airways (an appropriate mask or respirator).
- Keep skin covered and avoid breathing in spray, dust, or vapour.
- Do not eat, drink, or smoke while handling or applying pesticides.
- After finishing, wash hands and any exposed skin thoroughly with soap and water, and wash contaminated clothing separately from other laundry.

## Safe Mixing
- Mix in a well-ventilated, open area away from people, animals, food, drinking water, and water sources.
- Measure carefully using dedicated equipment kept only for pesticide use; never use household or food utensils.
- Add product to water as directed and avoid splashing; mix only the quantity needed for the intended area to avoid leftover diluted product.
- Never mix products together unless the label explicitly permits it.

## Safe Application
- Apply only in suitable conditions: avoid windy weather that causes drift onto people, animals, other crops, or water, and avoid applying just before expected rain that could wash product into waterways.
- Keep people, especially children, and animals out of the treated area during and after application for the period stated on the label (the re-entry period).
- Direct the application to the target and avoid unnecessary coverage; correct calibration and timing improve effectiveness and reduce waste and risk.
- Do not apply near water sources, homes, schools, or livestock beyond label-permitted distances.

## Pre-Harvest Intervals and Residues
- The pre-harvest interval (PHI) is the minimum time that must pass between the last application and harvest, so that residues fall to safe levels.
- Always observe the PHI stated on the label. Harvesting too soon can leave unsafe residues on produce, endangering consumers and risking rejection in markets that test for residues.
- Also observe any re-entry period before people return to work in the treated area.

## Safe Storage
- Store pesticides in their original, clearly labelled containers, tightly closed, in a cool, dry, ventilated place away from direct sun.
- Keep them locked and out of reach of children, animals, and unauthorised people.
- Store separately from food, animal feed, seed, and drinking water to prevent contamination.
- Never transfer pesticides into food or drink containers, a leading cause of accidental poisoning.

## Safe Disposal
- Do not reuse empty pesticide containers for any purpose, especially not for food, water, or feed.
- Triple-rinse empty containers where the label directs, adding the rinse water to the spray tank, then puncture the container so it cannot be reused, and dispose of it according to local regulations and any collection schemes.
- Dispose of leftover product and rinse water safely and legally, away from water sources; do not pour them into streams, wells, ponds, or drains.
- Consult the regulatory authority or extension officer about approved disposal options for unwanted or expired products.

## First Aid Basics
- Keep the product label with you, as it carries specific first-aid and emergency information; show it to medical personnel.
- General measures pending professional help typically include moving the person to fresh air, removing contaminated clothing, and washing affected skin or eyes thoroughly with clean water.
- Do not induce vomiting unless the label or a medical professional specifically instructs it.
- Seek medical help immediately for any suspected poisoning, and provide the product name and label to health workers so they can identify the active ingredient. Know the location of the nearest health facility before you begin.

## Protecting Water Sources and Pollinators
- Keep pesticides and spraying well away from wells, boreholes, streams, ponds, and irrigation channels, and observe label buffer distances near water.
- Protect pollinators such as bees: avoid applying insecticides to flowering crops, and where treatment is unavoidable near flowering, apply in the late evening when fewer pollinators are active and follow all label precautions for bees.
- Protecting natural pest enemies and pollinators supports long-term pest control and reduces reliance on chemicals. For an example of integrated pest control on a major pest, see `pests/fall_armyworm.md`.

## Common Mistakes
- Using unregistered products or applying a product to a crop or pest not on its label.
- Exceeding the recommended dose in the belief that more works better.
- Skipping protective equipment because application seems quick or minor.
- Harvesting before the pre-harvest interval has passed, leaving unsafe residues.
- Storing pesticides in food or drink containers, or near food, feed, or water.
- Spraying in windy conditions or near water sources, causing drift and contamination.

## When to Consult an Agricultural Extension Officer or Regulatory Authority
- To confirm which pesticide active-ingredient classes and products are currently registered and approved for your specific crop and pest.
- To identify safer, integrated alternatives before resorting to chemical control.
- For guidance on correct dosing, application, protective equipment, and pre-harvest intervals for local conditions.
- For safe disposal of unwanted or expired pesticides and empty containers.
- Immediately, alongside medical help, in any case of suspected pesticide poisoning.
