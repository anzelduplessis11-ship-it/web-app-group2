# Seed Certification and Quality Laws

## Overview
Seed certification systems exist to give farmers confidence that the seed they buy will perform as expected: that it is genetically true to the variety named on the label, has an acceptable germination rate, is reasonably free of weed seed and disease, and has been produced and handled under quality-controlled conditions. Most African countries operate a seed law or seed regulations that establish a national seed certification system, typically overseen by a national seed authority, seed inspectorate, or department within the ministry of agriculture. The specific legal texts, certification classes, inspection procedures, and penalties for non-compliance differ from country to country and are amended periodically, so this document explains the general principles shared by most certification systems rather than any single country's current law. Farmers should confirm the specific rules, registered varieties, and list of accredited seed producers currently in force with their national seed authority or a qualified extension officer.

## Why This Document Does Not Cite Specific Country Laws
- Seed laws, certification classes, approved variety lists, and enforcement bodies are defined by national legislation, which varies between countries and is revised over time.
- Some countries participate in regional seed harmonisation frameworks that allow certified seed to move across borders, but participation and specific rules differ and change.
- Presenting a specific country's seed law as universal, or as permanently current, could mislead farmers into buying seed that is not actually certified or into missing legal requirements that apply where they farm.
- Farmers should always confirm current seed certification requirements, registered varieties, and lists of licensed seed companies or agro-dealers with the national seed authority, seed inspection service, or a local extension officer.

## What Seed Certification Is
Seed certification is a formal quality assurance process, usually backed by law or official regulation, that verifies seed offered for sale meets defined standards for genetic identity, genetic purity, physical purity, germination capacity, and freedom from specified pests, diseases, and weed seeds. Certification is typically carried out or supervised by a designated government body (a national seed certification agency, plant quarantine and seed services department, or similar authority), sometimes with accredited private inspectors operating under government oversight. A seed lot that passes certification is issued an official certificate and, in most systems, a certification label or tag that must be attached to or printed on the seed container.

## General Stages of a Seed Certification System

### 1. Variety Registration or Release
Before a crop variety can be certified and legally marketed as seed in many countries, it typically must first be evaluated and formally registered or "released" through official variety testing (commonly assessing yield, disease resistance, and distinctness from existing varieties). Only registered/released varieties are usually eligible for entry into the certification pipeline, meaning that a "new" variety being sold informally without official release may not be eligible for certification even if it performs well in trials.

### 2. Seed Classes and the Multiplication Chain
Certification systems generally track seed through a multiplication chain with distinct classes, each with progressively lower genetic purity requirements as seed is multiplied:
- **Breeder seed**: The original, highest-purity seed produced or controlled directly by the plant breeder or breeding institution.
- **Pre-basic/foundation seed**: The first multiplication generation from breeder seed, produced under strict supervision.
- **Basic/foundation seed (second stage)**: A further multiplication stage in some systems, still closely supervised.
- **Certified seed**: Seed multiplied from foundation seed under field inspection and testing, intended for sale to farmers for commercial production.
Some systems include an intermediate lower-cost category, often called Quality Declared Seed (QDS) or a similar designation, which involves a simplified quality assurance process (often self-declared by the grower with spot checks by authorities) rather than full certification, intended to expand farmer access to reasonably good-quality seed for crops or areas where full certification infrastructure is limited.

### 3. Field Inspection
Seed certification agencies (or their accredited inspectors) typically inspect seed production fields at key growth stages to verify isolation distances from other varieties or related crops (to prevent unwanted cross-pollination), check for off-types and diseased plants, and confirm that the seed grower is following approved production practices. Fields that fail inspection (for example, due to excessive off-types or inadequate isolation) are usually rejected for certification, though the resulting grain may often still be sold as ordinary grain rather than seed.

### 4. Seed Processing, Sampling, and Laboratory Testing
After harvest, seed lots are processed (cleaned, sorted, treated where applicable) and sampled for laboratory testing. Standard tests generally include:
- **Germination test**: The percentage of seeds that produce a normal seedling under standard conditions, compared against a minimum threshold set for the crop and seed class.
- **Purity analysis**: The proportion of pure seed of the stated variety versus other crop seed, weed seed, and inert matter.
- **Moisture content**: Seed moisture must generally fall within a range suitable for safe storage without rapid quality loss.
- **Seed health testing**: Screening for seed-borne diseases or pests of regulatory concern, particularly important for seed intended for cross-border movement.
Seed lots that fail any required test are typically rejected for certification and may not legally be sold or labelled as certified seed.

### 5. Labelling, Sealing, and Traceability
Certified seed containers are generally sealed and labelled with official certification tags or labels showing information such as the crop and variety name, seed class (e.g., certified, foundation), lot number, germination percentage, purity percentage, test date, and the certifying authority. This labelling allows traceability back to the specific seed lot and production field if a quality problem is later identified, and gives farmers a documented basis for germination and purity expectations.

## Why Certified Seed Matters for Farmers
- **Predictable germination and stand establishment**: A verified minimum germination rate reduces the risk of poor emergence and the need for costly replanting.
- **Genetic purity and trueness to type**: Certification helps ensure the seed will grow into the variety described, with its expected traits (maturity period, disease resistance, yield potential, grain or fruit quality).
- **Freedom from seed-borne pests and diseases**: Reduces the risk of introducing new diseases or pests into a farmer's field or region through contaminated seed.
- **Consistency for planning**: Known germination and purity levels allow farmers to calculate seed rates and expected plant populations more accurately.
- **Recourse and accountability**: Because certified seed is traceable to a specific lot, grower, and inspection record, farmers generally have a stronger basis for complaint or compensation if certified seed fails to perform as labelled, compared with uncertified or informally sourced seed.
- **Access to markets and support**: Some formal buyers, contract farming schemes, and input-support or subsidy programmes require proof that certified seed was used.

## Certified Seed Versus Farm-Saved and Informal Seed
Many smallholder farmers rely partly or wholly on farm-saved seed (seed retained from a previous harvest) or seed obtained informally from neighbours or local markets. This is legal and common in most countries, and can be a reasonable strategy for open-pollinated varieties where farmers manage seed selection carefully. However, farmers should be aware of general limitations:
- Farm-saved seed of hybrid varieties will generally not breed true; the next generation typically shows reduced yield and inconsistent traits compared with the original hybrid, because hybrid vigour is not reliably passed on.
- Seed saved repeatedly over several seasons without careful selection can accumulate seed-borne diseases and decline in genetic purity and vigour.
- Informally traded seed usually carries no verified germination or purity guarantee and no traceability if problems arise.
- For crops or situations where disease risk is high, or where a specific variety's traits (such as resistance to a prevalent pest) are important, certified seed of an appropriate variety is generally the more reliable choice.

## Recognising Certified Seed at Point of Purchase
- Look for an official certification label or tag on the container, showing variety name, seed class, lot number, and test results, rather than relying on packaging appearance alone.
- Purchase from licensed seed companies or agro-dealers recognised by the national seed authority, since counterfeit or mislabelled seed circulates in some markets.
- Check that the container is properly sealed and has not been opened, repackaged, or mixed with other seed.
- Confirm that the seed batch has not exceeded any validity period after which germination is no longer guaranteed by the label.
- If in doubt about whether a specific product or dealer is legitimately certified, verify with the national seed authority or an extension officer before purchase.

## Common Mistakes
- Assuming any seed sold in a branded bag is automatically certified, without checking for an official certification label.
- Saving and replanting hybrid seed expecting the same performance as the original purchase.
- Buying seed from unlicensed roadside or informal vendors without any traceable certification.
- Ignoring the seed class (e.g., mistaking foundation seed intended for further multiplication for certified seed intended for grain production, or vice versa).
- Storing certified seed poorly after purchase (in damp or hot conditions), which can reduce germination below the tested level even though the seed was certified at the point of sale.
- Not retaining proof of purchase or the seed label, which can be needed to pursue recourse if a seed lot underperforms.

## When to Consult an Agricultural Extension Officer or National Seed Authority
- To confirm which crop varieties are currently officially registered/released and eligible for certification in your area.
- To obtain a current list of licensed seed companies, agro-dealers, or certified seed outlets.
- To verify whether a specific seed lot or label is genuinely certified, particularly if counterfeit seed is a known local concern.
- To understand the difference between seed classes (breeder, foundation, certified, or Quality Declared Seed) relevant to your intended use.
- Before saving seed from a hybrid or protected variety, to understand expected performance in subsequent generations.
- If certified seed fails to germinate or perform as labelled, to understand available complaint or compensation procedures.
